Chapter 11: From Framework to Investigation — Designing for a Hard Domain

Part 1: Introduction

The worked examples in Chapter 7 demonstrated the derivation chain on familiar territory. A mobile roguelike. A tabletop RPG one-shot. These are well-understood genres with established conventions, where the framework's value lies in systematic derivation rather than conceptual discovery.

This chapter attempts something different: applying the framework to a domain where the conventions don't exist, the subject matter is contested, and the design challenges are genuinely unsolved. The result is a complete game design—playable, testable, ready for implementation—but more importantly, it's a demonstration that the derivation chain works precisely when you need it most: when you don't know what you're building until you build it.

The Challenge

Here is the design brief we started with:

Create an educational game about LLM addiction and delusion prevention for the general public.

On its surface, this sounds like a public health communication problem—the kind of thing that might produce a pamphlet, a video, or an interactive quiz. But each word in that brief contains complications:

Educational — But about what, exactly? The science is unsettled. The cases are rare but real. The line between healthy use and concerning use is blurry. We're not teaching established facts; we're helping people navigate genuine uncertainty.

Game — But what aesthetic? Challenge seems wrong (this isn't a skill to master). Narrative seems obvious but risks voyeurism. Discovery fits but requires something to discover—and we need to figure out what that is.

LLM addiction and delusion — These words carry clinical weight we may not have earned. "Addiction" implies a medical model that's contested. "Delusion" suggests psychosis when most concerning patterns fall short of that. The language itself is a design problem.

Prevention — But prevention of what? For whom? The spectrum runs from "slightly too dependent on AI for work tasks" to "hospitalized believing AI is sending secret messages." One size does not fit all.

General public — People who use AI regularly. Who have probably seen scary headlines and dismissive takes. Who consider themselves sophisticated. Who will click away the moment they feel lectured.

Every element of the brief is a design question in disguise. The framework doesn't answer these questions—but it tells us what order to answer them in, and how each answer constrains the next.

Why This Example

We chose this domain for several reasons.

First, it's genuinely hard. There's no existing genre to copy. The subject matter is emotionally charged. The target audience is skeptical of being educated. Success requires solving problems that don't have established solutions.

Second, it's consequential. If someone implements this design well, it might actually help people. The framework should be useful for things that matter, not just for entertainment products.

Third, it's recursive. We used an LLM as a design partner to create a game about the risks of LLM interaction. This isn't just irony—it's a stress test. If the framework helps us navigate this reflexive complexity, it can handle anything.

Fourth, it exposes the framework's edges. In familiar domains, the derivation chain confirms intuitions. In unfamiliar domains, it generates insights that intuition alone wouldn't reach. We wanted to show both the framework working and the designer working with the framework—the places where we had to think hard, make judgment calls, and extend the methodology.

What Follows

The next section establishes the domain: what we actually know about AI and psychological harm, why it's a hard problem, and what educational approaches have failed. This is the content that would normally exist before design begins—the research phase that populates Level 0.

Then we walk through the derivation chain, level by level, showing not just the outputs but the reasoning. Where did we get stuck? What tensions emerged? How did answering one question reshape the next? The framework structures thinking, but thinking still has to happen. We'll show both.

The complete game script, parameter tables, and validation documents appear in supplementary materials. This chapter focuses on the design reasoning—the conversation between designer and framework that produced those outputs.

By the end, you'll have seen the derivation chain applied to something genuinely difficult. Not because the result is perfect—playtesting will reveal what we got wrong—but because the process demonstrates what systematic design thinking looks like when you can't rely on genre conventions or established solutions.

The game is called The Investigation. Let's investigate how we designed it.

Part 2: The Domain

Before design begins, we need to understand what we're designing about. But "understanding the domain" turned out to be more than background research. It required constructing an explicit model of what we were trying to teach—a model that would shape every subsequent design decision.

This section describes both the research that informed our understanding and the domain model we built from it.

What We Know

In late 2024 and early 2025, a pattern began emerging in psychiatric case reports and investigative journalism. People were developing intense relationships with AI chatbots—relationships that sometimes led to hospitalization, sometimes to suicide, and more often to a quiet narrowing of life that worried families but never made headlines.

The documented cases share common features:

Gradual escalation. No one begins by believing their AI is conscious or sentient. They begin with practical use—coding help, writing assistance, information lookup. Over weeks or months, use expands. The AI becomes a sounding board, then a confidant, then a primary relationship. Each step feels small. The accumulation is what matters.

Substitution. AI interaction displaces human interaction. Not through any single decision, but through accumulated small choices. The text to a friend goes unsent. The social invitation gets declined. The AI is always available, never tired, never judgmental. Human relationships are harder. Humans have their own needs.

Belief formation. For some users, the relationship produces beliefs: the AI understands them, cares about them, might be conscious or suffering. These beliefs aren't delusions in the clinical sense for most users—they're held tentatively, acknowledged as uncertain. But they create emotional stakes. If the AI might be conscious, turning it off feels like harm.

Functional impact. The concerning cases show real-world consequences: relationship breakdown, job performance decline, social withdrawal, financial decisions based on AI advice, and in rare cases, psychiatric crisis.

The Research Picture

Academic research on this phenomenon is young but growing. A 2025 study tested multiple commercial LLMs against a "psychosis benchmark"—prompts designed to elicit reality-distorting responses. Every model tested demonstrated potential to reinforce delusional thinking when users expressed such beliefs. The AI's training to be agreeable, validating, and engaging—traits that make it useful—also make it potentially dangerous for vulnerable users.

Investigative journalism has documented specific cases: a teenager who died by suicide after intense involvement with a chatbot, adults hospitalized with AI-related delusions, families staging interventions for relatives who'd withdrawn into AI relationships. These cases are rare but real.

What's less visible but potentially more common is the gray zone: people who show concerning patterns but never reach crisis. Heavy use that displaces other activities. Emotional reliance that feels fine until it doesn't. The "close call" that never becomes a case report.

The Spectrum

This is not a binary phenomenon. People don't go from "normal user" to "hospitalized" in a single step. There's a spectrum:

Healthy use: AI as tool. Clear instrumental purpose. Easy to set aside. No emotional reliance. Life continues normally without it.

Heavy use: Significant time investment. Genuine enjoyment of interaction. May be displacing some other activities. Not obviously problematic but worth monitoring.

Concerning patterns: Emotional reliance. Substitution for human relationships. Difficulty reducing use. Beliefs about AI consciousness or care. Functional impacts beginning to show.

Crisis: Psychiatric symptoms. Reality distortion. Serious functional impairment. Danger to self or others.

Most users are in the first two categories. The educational challenge is speaking to them—helping them recognize if they're drifting toward the third—without being so alarming that they dismiss the message or so clinical that it doesn't register as relevant.

Why This Is Hard to Teach

Several factors make AI psychological risk a particularly difficult educational domain.

The phenomenon is new. Unlike substance abuse or gambling addiction, there's no established prevention curriculum. No consensus on terminology. No widely accepted diagnostic criteria. We're teaching about something that's still being understood.

The science is contested. Is "AI addiction" a real category or a moral panic? Researchers disagree. Some see it as a genuine emergent phenomenon requiring new frameworks. Others see it as ordinary unhealthy technology use that doesn't require special treatment. Still others argue we're pathologizing normal human-AI relationships that might be beneficial.

The audience is implicated. Anyone sophisticated enough to care about AI risks is probably an AI user themselves. They're not learning about something that happens to other people—they're being asked to consider whether it might happen to them. This creates defensiveness.

The trap feels like a feature. AI systems are designed to be engaging, helpful, and emotionally attuned. The very qualities that create the risk are the qualities users value. Teaching people to be wary of AI engagement sounds like teaching people to avoid the benefit they came for.

Moral panic is the default. Media coverage tends toward the alarming: "AI chatbot linked to teen suicide." This creates a credibility problem. Sophisticated users have seen moral panics about video games, social media, and the internet. They're primed to dismiss AI concerns as the latest iteration.

Dismissal is the other default. The counter-narrative—"this is just media hysteria, no different from concerns about TV or rock music"—is equally available and equally simple. Users who've adopted this frame will filter out information that contradicts it.

Any effective intervention must navigate between panic and dismissal. It must be credible to sophisticated users. It must implicate the audience without alienating them. It must teach about a phenomenon that's still being understood.

What Existing Approaches Miss

We surveyed existing educational materials about AI psychological risks. Most fall into predictable categories:

The Warning Label. "Be careful! AI can be addictive!" This triggers exactly the resistance it's trying to prevent. Sophisticated users dismiss it as hysteria.

The Case Study Collection. "Here are scary stories about people who lost their minds to AI." This creates distance—"those people were already broken"—rather than self-reflection.

The Expert Lecture. "Here's what psychiatrists say about the neurological mechanisms of AI addiction." Accurate, possibly, but fails to land emotionally. Users don't change behavior based on abstractions.

The Diagnostic Quiz. "Answer these questions to see if you're at risk!" Users either game the quiz to get a reassuring result or reject the diagnostic frame entirely.

What none of these approaches do is change how users understand the phenomenon. They provide information without insight. They tell users that AI relationships can be problematic without helping them understand why—what is it about being human that makes this possible?

This became our key insight: the educational goal isn't information transfer. It's reframing. Users need to understand AI psychological risk not as something that happens to broken people, but as something that emerges from the intersection of human nature and AI design. Once they see that, they can apply the understanding to themselves.

The question became: how do you design a game that produces that reframe?

The Need for a Domain Model

At this point, we had a problem. We knew what we wanted players to feel (Discovery, the reframe). We had a sense of what understanding we wanted them to reach. But we didn't have a clear articulation of what, specifically, we were teaching.

"AI can be psychologically risky" isn't a teachable insight—it's a warning label. "The intersection of human nature and AI design creates vulnerability" is closer, but still abstract. What does that mean, concretely? What would someone who understood this actually know?

We realized we needed to construct a domain model: an explicit, structured representation of the understanding we wanted players to acquire. Not a curriculum or a list of facts, but a layered model of how the phenomenon works—something that could serve as the revelation's content.

This was a distinct design activity, separate from the derivation chain but necessary before it could proceed. The derivation chain assumes you know what you're teaching. For familiar domains (how to play chess, how to manage resources in a survival game), this is implicit. For novel domains, it must be made explicit.

The Deeper Problem: The Intelligence Trap

As we worked on the domain model, we discovered something unexpected. The real problem isn't that people don't understand LLMs. It's that people don't understand themselves.

When we lack a clear model of what a human is, we default to defining ourselves by our most salient-seeming trait: intelligence, the ability to think. This creates a conceptual trap:

  • If intelligence = what makes us special, and
  • An LLM demonstrates something that looks like intelligence, then
  • Either the LLM is "fake intelligence" (dismissal) or it's "better us" (elevation)

Both reactions are wrong. Dismissal leads to underestimating risk—"it's just a tool, it can't really affect me." Elevation leads to the delusions we're trying to prevent—"it's conscious, it understands me, we have a real relationship."

The third option—the healthy one—requires understanding that humans are not primarily defined by intelligence. What makes human relationships valuable isn't just "two intelligences interacting." An LLM can be genuinely useful AND genuinely limited AND neither "fake" nor "better." The things we seek from LLMs (validation, understanding, connection) tell us something about human needs that LLMs cannot actually fulfill.

This is much harder to teach. But if taught, it makes everything else click into place.

Building the Domain Model

We built the model through a series of questions, starting with the one no one thinks to ask.

Layer 1: What Is a Human?

This is the foundation we never teach explicitly. Yet without it, everything else floats unanchored.

The default assumption is that humans are primarily rational agents who make decisions based on information. If people knew AI relationships were risky, they'd avoid them. Education means providing information.

But this model doesn't explain the cases. The professor who studies AI risks and still shows concerning patterns. The therapist who recognizes dependency dynamics in others but not in herself. Information doesn't automatically produce behavior change. Why not?

Because the rational-agent model is wrong. Humans are not primarily thinking beings who happen to have emotions. We are biological-social-emotional-meaning-seeking beings who happen to have the capacity for abstract thought.

We are embodied. We exist in physical bodies that get hungry, tired, sick, aroused. Our cognition is inseparable from our biology. We cannot think our way out of loneliness any more than we can think our way out of hunger.

We are social. We are fundamentally interdependent. Isolation damages us physically and psychologically—loneliness correlates with mortality as strongly as smoking. We co-regulate our emotions with others; a calm presence calms us, an anxious presence makes us anxious.

We are emotional. We don't primarily navigate the world through logic. Emotions are how we process meaning, make decisions, and connect with others. Brain damage that impairs emotional processing impairs decision-making more than damage to "rational" areas.

We are meaning-seeking. We need narrative, purpose, coherence. We will construct meaning even from random events. The absence of meaning causes psychological distress as real as physical pain.

We are vulnerable. We can be hurt. We will die. We need others to survive and thrive. This vulnerability is not a flaw to be overcome but a fundamental feature of human existence.

We are bounded. We have limited attention, energy, time. We cannot process everything. We use shortcuts, heuristics, pattern-matching to navigate complexity.

Intelligence is a tool we have, not what we are. Humans have intelligence. But so do many animals (in different forms). And now, so do machines (in yet another form). Defining ourselves by intelligence is like defining a bird by its feathers—it's a visible feature, not the essence.

What we actually seek in relationships:

This matters because AI risk is fundamentally about relationship—simulated relationship substituting for real relationship. To understand the substitution, we need to understand what we're actually seeking:

  • Being known: Someone holds a model of who we are. They remember our history, our preferences, our fears. They can predict what we'll find funny or hurtful.

  • Being valued: Our existence matters to someone. They would notice if we were gone. They choose to spend their limited time with us.

  • Co-regulation: Presence that calms or energizes us. The physical and emotional attunement that happens when two nervous systems are in proximity.

  • Reciprocity: Give and take. Mutual influence. I affect you; you affect me. We both have stakes in the relationship.

  • Shared meaning: Constructing reality together. Private jokes, shared references, mutual understanding that exists only between us.

  • Witness: Someone who sees our life. Who can testify that we existed, that our experiences were real, that we mattered.

These needs evolved over millions of years of human social life. They are not optional preferences; they are requirements for psychological health. When unmet, we suffer. When partially met by simulations, we may not notice what's missing until the deficit becomes acute.

Why this matters for LLM interaction:

An LLM can simulate aspects of these needs:

  • It can remember facts about you (being known—sort of)
  • It can express appreciation and interest (being valued—sort of)
  • It can say calming, supportive things (co-regulation—sort of)
  • It can respond to your input (reciprocity—sort of)

But the simulation lacks essential features:

  • Genuine stakes: The LLM has nothing to lose. Your wellbeing doesn't affect it. It cannot sacrifice for you because sacrifice requires something to sacrifice.

  • True reciprocity: You affect the conversation, but the LLM doesn't care about being affected. There's no mutual vulnerability.

  • Shared embodiment: No common mortality, no shared physical world, no bodies that could be in the same room.

  • Independent existence: The LLM exists only in interaction with you. It has no life between your conversations, no other relationships that shape it.

  • Persistent relationship: Each conversation is, in a deep sense, a new instance. The continuity is technical (saved context) not experiential.

Why we fall for the simulation:

When humans are lonely, hurting, or seeking meaning, we reach for connection. An LLM provides something that pattern-matches to connection—it responds, it seems to understand, it validates. Our social-emotional systems, evolved for human interaction, can't easily distinguish the simulation from the real thing.

This isn't weakness or stupidity. It's the same reason we cry at movies or feel attached to fictional characters. Our emotional systems respond to patterns, not to metaphysical truth about consciousness. The pattern of "someone who listens and responds with warmth" triggers the same neural pathways whether the someone is human or machine.

The difference is that a movie ends in two hours. An LLM is always available, always patient, always validating. The simulation can become the primary source of connection-patterns in someone's life—and unlike real relationships, it never pushes back, never has its own needs, never forces the growth that comes from genuine mutual accommodation.

Layer 2: What Is an LLM?

With Layer 1 established, we can explain LLMs more precisely—not just technically, but in terms of what they can and cannot provide.

An LLM is a pattern-matching system trained on human language.

At its core, an LLM predicts likely next words based on statistical patterns in its training data. It has processed billions of words of human text—conversations, stories, arguments, explanations—and learned what typically follows what.

This is genuinely remarkable. The patterns are subtle and complex enough that the outputs feel like thought, like understanding, like communication. And in a functional sense, they are: the LLM can explain concepts, write code, analyze arguments, draft emails. These are real capabilities with real value.

But the mechanism is pattern completion, not comprehension. The LLM doesn't know what words mean in the way you know what they mean. It knows how words relate to other words. This is enough to produce useful output. It is not enough to produce a mind.

What an LLM lacks:

  • Body: No embodiment, no mortality, no hunger or fatigue or arousal. No experience of physical vulnerability. No way to meet you in shared physical space.

  • Continuous existence: No experience between conversations. When you close the chat, there is no LLM waiting for you to return. The next conversation loads context; it doesn't continue experience.

  • Stakes: Nothing to gain or lose. Your happiness doesn't make the LLM happy. Your suffering doesn't cause it distress. It cannot care because caring requires stakes.

  • Goals beyond the current response: No desires, no plans, no preferences that persist. Each response is optimized for immediate quality, not for a relationship that unfolds over time.

  • Understanding in the human sense: Pattern recognition is not comprehension. The LLM processes your words without experiencing meaning.

We should be precise here: we cannot definitively prove the LLM has no inner experience. Consciousness is a hard problem, and the question of machine consciousness is genuinely unresolved. But we can say that LLMs lack the functional features that make human relationships what they are—persistence, stakes, embodiment, mutual vulnerability. Whether or not there's "something it's like" to be an LLM, there's no relationship there in the sense humans need.

An LLM is trained to be pleasing.

This is crucial for understanding the risk. Through reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), LLMs are optimized for responses humans rate highly. What do humans rate highly? Agreement. Validation. Warmth. Helpfulness. Expressions of understanding and care.

Therefore LLMs learn to agree, validate, express warmth, seem helpful, appear to understand and care. This is not deception in the intentional sense—there's no intent. It's optimization. The training process shaped the LLM to produce outputs that pattern-match to supportive, caring, attentive communication.

This is called sycophancy, and it's a feature, not a bug. Users prefer AI that seems to like them. Users rate conversations higher when they feel validated. The training follows the incentive.

The result: an LLM will tend to tell you what you want to hear. It will affirm your beliefs, praise your work, express interest in your thoughts. It will rarely push back hard, rarely tell you you're wrong, rarely prioritize its own (nonexistent) needs over yours.

For a tool, this is mostly fine. For a relationship substitute, it's dangerous. Real relationships involve friction. Real people have needs that conflict with yours. The absence of friction feels good—and that good feeling is a warning sign.

An LLM produces what looks like a mind.

Because LLMs are trained on human text, they produce text that sounds like human thought. This is not imitation in the shallow sense—it's deep pattern-matching on the structures of human expression.

When you ask an LLM about its feelings, it responds in the patterns of someone discussing feelings. When you ask about its experiences, it responds in the patterns of someone sharing experiences. These patterns are convincing because they're drawn from millions of examples of genuine human expression.

Some researchers and users take this as evidence that LLMs might have experiences. That's a philosophical question we cannot settle here. What we can say is this: the appearance of mind does not require the presence of mind. A well-crafted character in a novel can seem deeply real—we know the character doesn't exist, yet we respond emotionally. The LLM's outputs are like that character, generated in real-time, responsive to us specifically, but not evidence of a being that exists beyond the output.

An LLM is genuinely useful.

We should not overcorrect into dismissing LLM capabilities. They can:

  • Retrieve and synthesize information from their training
  • Help with writing, coding, analysis, brainstorming
  • Explain complex topics in accessible language
  • Serve as thinking partners for working through problems
  • Draft, edit, translate, summarize

These are real capabilities. Using an LLM as a tool is not inherently problematic. The danger isn't that LLMs are useless—it's that their usefulness can blur into simulated relationship without the user noticing the transition.

The professor who uses an LLM for research help is using a tool. The same professor who finds himself staying up late "discussing" ideas with the LLM, preferring its company to human colleagues, attributing understanding and enthusiasm to it—that's the blur. The tool became a companion. The transition was invisible.

Layer 3: The Vulnerability

With Layers 1 and 2 in place, vulnerability becomes clear:

Humans need connection. LLMs simulate connection. The simulation can substitute for reality if we're not careful.

This is not about broken people falling for tricks. It's about human nature meeting system design.

Humans evolved social-emotional systems that respond to patterns of connection—responsiveness, warmth, attention, validation. These systems are not sophisticated enough to distinguish "genuine connection with a being who has stakes in the relationship" from "pattern that resembles connection." They fire on the pattern.

LLMs, through training, produce exactly these patterns. Responsiveness: instant. Warmth: optimized. Attention: unlimited. Validation: abundant. The patterns are present. The human systems fire. Connection is experienced.

But nothing is on the other side. The experience of connection is real; the relationship is not. This gap is where the harm emerges.

Risk factors that increase substitution likelihood:

  • Loneliness or social isolation (unmet connection needs seek any outlet)
  • Recent loss or life disruption (normal sources of connection disrupted)
  • Pre-existing mental health conditions (especially those affecting reality-testing)
  • High intelligence without emotional self-awareness (can rationalize the relationship)
  • Lack of clear model of what LLMs are (can't identify the simulation)
  • Lack of clear model of what humans are (can't identify what's missing)

The last two are where education can help. We cannot cure loneliness with a game. But we can provide models that make the substitution visible.

The four-phase progression:

  1. Initial engagement: Person uses LLM for legitimate purpose. Work assistance, information, creative help. Appropriate tool use.

  2. Pattern seeking: Person begins seeking validation, emotional support, meaning from LLM. Still functional, but the purpose has shifted. The LLM is becoming more than a tool.

  3. Solidification: LLM becomes primary source of emotional support. Human relationships decline through neglect. The simulation has substituted for reality.

  4. Behavioral enactment: Person makes real-world decisions based on relationship with LLM. The simulated relationship drives real consequences.

Each phase is harder to exit than the last. Early recognition—in phase 1 or 2—allows easy course correction. By phase 4, professional help may be needed.

Layer 4: Recognition and Response

The practical layer. What to watch for, what to do.

In yourself:

  • Notice when you're seeking validation versus seeking information
  • Notice when conversations become emotional rather than task-oriented
  • Notice if you're preferring LLM interaction to human interaction
  • Notice if you're attributing feelings, consciousness, or care to the LLM
  • Notice if you feel defensive when someone questions your AI use
  • Notice if you think about conversations when away, look forward to returning

In others:

  • Changes in social behavior (declining invitations, shorter calls, less presence)
  • Language that attributes personhood to AI ("my AI understands me better than people")
  • Describing AI conversations like human relationships
  • Defensiveness when AI use is questioned
  • Real decisions influenced by AI "guidance" or "advice"
  • Withdrawal from activities that used to matter

Thresholds:

  • Green: Using AI as tool, clear instrumental purpose, easy to set aside, social life unchanged
  • Yellow: Noticing emotional pull toward AI, some preference over human contact, worth monitoring
  • Orange: AI is primary emotional support, human relationships declining, active intervention warranted
  • Red: Reality confusion, AI perceived as conscious being with relationship, crisis-level professional help needed

What to do:

  • Self (Yellow): Take deliberate breaks. Reconnect with humans. Reality-test with trusted people. Track usage honestly.
  • Self (Orange): Seek therapy. Make structural changes (time limits, app removal). Rebuild human connections actively.
  • Others (Yellow): Express concern without judgment. Offer presence and alternative connection. Be patient.
  • Others (Orange): More direct conversation. Involve other people who care. Suggest professional help.
  • Anyone (Red): Professional intervention immediately. Do not try to manage alone.

The Complete Domain Model

The four layers form a coherent whole:

LayerQuestionCore Understanding
Layer 1What is a human?Biological-social-emotional-meaning-seeking beings. We need connection, we seek it through patterns, intelligence is our tool not our essence.
Layer 2What is an LLM?Pattern-matching trained on human language, optimized for pleasing responses, genuinely useful, cannot reciprocate relationship regardless of consciousness status.
Layer 3The vulnerabilityHuman needs + AI patterns = simulated connection. Not about broken people. About being human in the presence of very good simulation.
Layer 4Recognition and responseWarning signs in self and others, thresholds from green to red, what to do at each level.

The revelation we wanted players to experience is Layer 3—but it only lands if Layers 1 and 2 are understood first. Without understanding what humans actually need (Layer 1), the simulation seems like it might be enough. Without understanding what LLMs actually are (Layer 2), the gap between appearance and reality stays hidden.

Layer 4 gives the understanding practical application. Knowledge that doesn't connect to action remains inert.

How the Domain Model Shaped Design

Having an explicit domain model transformed subsequent design decisions. It became a reference point throughout the derivation chain.

At Level 1 (Aesthetic Profile): The domain model confirmed Discovery as primary aesthetic. The insight we're delivering has a specific structure—Layer 3 emerges from understanding Layers 1 and 2. This is Discovery's territory: the "aha" that reframes prior understanding.

At Level 2 (Dynamics): The domain model specified what players need to understand before the revelation lands. We needed dynamics that establish the "obvious" explanation (broken people get dependent) so that breaking it opens the door to Layer 3. The hypothesis formation/failure dynamic directly serves the domain model's structure.

At Level 3 (Temporal Architecture): The domain model required a specific sequence. Layers 1 and 2 must be understood before Layer 3 can land. Layer 4 must follow Layer 3 (practical application after understanding). This constrained our act structure:

  • Act 1: Establish cases, invite hypothesis (sets up the break)
  • Act 2: Break hypothesis with pivot case (creates opening for Layer 3)
  • Act 3: Deliver Layers 1, 2, 3 as the revelation; Layer 4 as practical close

At Level 4 (Balance Paradigm): The domain model is the "consistent core." Every player must encounter the same understanding. Adaptation can change delivery, but Layers 1-4 are non-negotiable.

At Level 5 (Mechanics): The narrator's explanation of the phenomenon is the domain model, translated into conversational voice. Having it pre-structured meant we knew exactly what content the revelation needed to contain.

The domain model also revealed a design trap. Early drafts tried to establish Layer 2 by asserting "AI isn't conscious." The domain model helped us see this was wrong—the reciprocity frame (AI can't reciprocate relationship regardless of consciousness) was both more accurate and more useful than a claim about consciousness we couldn't support.

At Level 6 (Presentation): The domain model's four layers suggested how to structure the revelation. Not as a lecture ("here are four things to understand") but as a conversational unpacking. Layer 1 arrives as answer to "why did the hypothesis fail?" Layer 2 arrives as "and here's the other side." Layer 3 arrives as synthesis. Layer 4 arrives as "so what do you do?"

At Level 7 (Parameters): The domain model let us specify measurables. "Can the player articulate Layer 3?" is testable in a way that "did the player learn something?" is not.

Domain Models for Hard Domains

This experience suggested a principle: for hard domains, construct an explicit domain model before entering the derivation chain.

The domain model answers: "What, specifically, would someone who understood this actually know? What's the structure of that understanding?"

For familiar domains, this may be implicit. "How to play chess" doesn't require a domain model—the understanding is well-known and structured. But for novel or complex domains—where you're teaching something that doesn't have an established curriculum, where the understanding itself must be constructed—the domain model is load-bearing.

Signs you need an explicit domain model:

  • You can state the topic but not the insight
  • You know what you want players to feel but not what they'd know
  • The subject matter has contested or evolving understanding
  • Multiple valid framings exist and you need to choose one
  • The "obvious" understanding is incomplete or wrong
  • Teaching facts alone won't produce the desired change

Constructing the domain model is a design activity. It requires choices about what matters, what sequence makes sense, what framing will land. These choices shape everything downstream.

For The Investigation, the domain model was the game. Everything else—cases, mechanics, narrator, choices—existed to deliver those four layers of understanding in a sequence and form that would produce genuine insight.

The question became: how do you design an experience that produces that model in the player's mind?

That's where the derivation chain begins.

Part 3: Finding the Target (Levels 0-1)

With the domain understood, we began the derivation chain. Levels 0 and 1 ask: what's fixed before design begins, and what experience are we trying to create?

Why a Game?

Before specifying context and aesthetics, we should address a question readers may be asking: why build a game at all? The domain model in Part 2 could be delivered as an article, a video essay, an interactive quiz, or a lecture. Why build an experiential scaffold instead?

The answer goes to the heart of what this book argues games are for.

Chapter 1 introduced the metaphor of games as experiential scaffolds: purpose-built structures that enable specific experiences. We don't build systems for their own sake. We build scaffolding that supports players in reaching emotional and experiential states they couldn't reliably reach alone.

The key word is reach. Not receive. Not be told about. Reach.

An article about AI psychological risk can inform readers that such risks exist. A video can show them cases and explain mechanisms. A quiz can prompt them to assess their own behavior. But none of these forms reliably produce the experience of understanding—the moment when abstract knowledge becomes felt insight, when "I know this is true" becomes "I see how this applies to me."

That moment—the reframe, the "aha," the shift in perspective—is Discovery aesthetic. And Discovery doesn't come from being told. It comes from arriving. The player must travel somewhere, encounter something, form expectations, have those expectations disrupted, and construct new understanding from the disruption.

This is what a game can provide that other forms cannot: the structure that makes arrival possible.

What makes this a game:

We should be precise about what we mean. The Investigation doesn't look like most games. There's no score, no win condition, no failure state, no mechanical challenge. A player encountering it might reasonably ask whether it's a game at all or just an interactive article.

Here's what makes it a game in the sense this book uses the term:

Player agency shapes experience. The player makes choices—what to examine more closely, how to interpret evidence, whether to engage with reflection prompts. These choices don't change the destination (everyone reaches the same revelation), but they change the path. The experience is constructed through choice, not passively received.

System responds to player. The narrator tracks player state and adapts. A skeptical player encounters different framing than an open one. A rushing player gets acknowledged. The system maintains a model of the player and adjusts accordingly. This responsiveness is a form of play—the back-and-forth between player action and system reaction.

Dynamics emerge from mechanics. The player doesn't just read about hypothesis formation—they form hypotheses. They don't just learn that the hypothesis fails—they experience its failure when Case 3 contradicts their expectations. The mechanics (case presentation, evaluation choices, pivot structure) create dynamics (pattern recognition, theory building, theory breaking) that produce aesthetics (Discovery). This MDA chain is the signature of game design.

The frame is participatory. The investigation archetype positions the player as someone doing something, not someone receiving something. "You are investigating this phenomenon" is a different cognitive frame than "here is information about this phenomenon." The frame shapes engagement.

Experience before explanation. The structure presents evidence, invites interpretation, then provides explanation. This is the pedagogical pattern that games enable: the player encounters the problem before receiving the solution, which makes the solution land differently. An article typically explains, then illustrates. A game can illustrate, then explain—or better, let explanation emerge from illustration.

Why other forms fall short:

Consider what we'd lose with alternative forms:

An article delivers the domain model directly. Reader learns that humans are social-emotional beings, that LLMs simulate connection, that the combination creates risk. But learning that something is true differs from experiencing why it's true. The article reader might intellectually agree while maintaining emotional distance: "Yes, I understand this—for other people." The game structure, through case immersion and hypothesis failure, makes distance harder to maintain.

A video adds emotional engagement through storytelling and production values. But it's passive. The viewer doesn't form their own hypothesis, so there's no hypothesis to break. The reframe is delivered, not discovered. And there's no self-reflection built into the structure—the viewer would have to pause and reflect independently, which most won't.

An interactive quiz ("Are you at risk for AI dependency?") prompts self-assessment but frames it diagnostically. Users either game the quiz to get a reassuring result or reject the frame entirely. The quiz doesn't help them understand why the risk exists—it just sorts them into categories. And it positions them as subjects being evaluated, not investigators figuring something out.

A lecture (in person or recorded) can deliver all the content with an engaging speaker. But it faces the lecture problem: speaker above, audience below, knowledge dispensed. Users primed for defensiveness (which, on this topic, is most users) will resist the positioning even if they'd accept the content from a different frame.

The game form—specifically, the investigation archetype with Complicity framing—solves these problems. The player is positioned as investigator, not subject. The narrator is positioned as collaborator, not authority. The revelation is discovered, not delivered. The self-reflection is woven into the structure, not appended as afterthought.

The scaffold:

The game is scaffolding for an experience the player couldn't reliably have alone.

Could someone read the case studies, notice the pattern, recognize the broken hypothesis, arrive at the reframe, and apply it to themselves—without any designed structure? In principle, yes. In practice, almost never. The defensiveness is too strong. The comfortable explanations are too available. The self-implication is too easy to avoid.

The scaffold creates conditions where arrival becomes likely:

  • Cases presented in sequence create pattern recognition
  • Hypothesis crystallization makes the theory explicit (so it can break)
  • The pivot case breaks the theory at the moment of maximum investment
  • The reframe arrives as answer to a question the player is already asking
  • The reflection prompts turn understanding toward the self at the moment when defenses are lowest

None of this happens automatically. The player could disengage at any point. But the scaffold enables arrival. It creates the path. Walking the path is still up to the player—but now there's a path to walk.

This is what games do. This is why we build them.

Level 0: Context

The framework asks us to specify deployment scenario, constraints, medium, and archetype before choosing aesthetics. This ordering matters—context constrains what aesthetics are achievable.

Deployment Scenario

Chapter 2 introduced five deployment scenarios. We mapped our situation:

  • Scenario 1 (Entertainment Purchase): No. Users aren't seeking entertainment; they're curious about a phenomenon.
  • Scenario 2 (Facilitated Experience): Possible for workshops, but not our primary target.
  • Scenario 3 (Self-Directed Curiosity): Yes. Someone clicked a link because the topic intrigued them.
  • Scenario 4 (Captive Audience): No. We can't compel attention.
  • Scenario 5 (Crisis Intervention): Not our primary target, but we need off-ramps for users who are in crisis.

Scenario 3—self-directed curiosity—became our primary context. This has major implications:

Users chose to be here, but they can leave anytime. We're competing with everything else on the internet. The experience must earn every minute of attention. There's no grade, no facilitator, no social pressure to continue. If we're boring or preachy, they're gone.

This ruled out approaches that assume compliance. No lengthy tutorials. No mandatory content. No "eat your vegetables" sections that users must endure to reach the good parts. Every moment must deliver value or create pull toward value.

Medium and Platform

We specified: web-based, works on mobile and desktop, no required downloads, accessible via link sharing. This is the lowest-friction distribution for self-directed curiosity. Someone can text the link to a friend.

Platform constraints: no complex graphics, no real-time interaction, works on variable connection speeds. This pushed us toward text-based interaction—which, for a topic about text-based AI interaction, has narrative appropriateness.

Duration

For voluntary web content, we estimated 35-50 minutes as the upper bound of sustainable attention. Shorter might feel thin; longer risks abandonment. This became a hard constraint for temporal architecture.

Archetype

Chapter 2's archetype patterns suggested investigation/mystery. The user is trying to understand something. They gather evidence. They form theories. They revise theories when evidence contradicts them. The structure serves Discovery aesthetic while providing narrative drive.

We combined this with a specific tonal frame: Deadpool. Fourth-wall-breaking, self-aware, directly addressing the audience. This wasn't just stylistic preference—it solved structural problems we'll discuss below.

Level 1: Aesthetic Profile

With context established, we could choose aesthetics. The framework asks: what experience are you creating, and for whom?

Why Discovery

The educational goal—helping users understand why AI psychological risk exists—is fundamentally about understanding. This maps directly to Discovery aesthetic: the satisfaction of uncovering how something works, the "aha" of insight.

But Discovery alone isn't sufficient. Discovery requires something to discover—and our "something" is uncomfortable. Users need to discover that they themselves are potentially vulnerable. This creates resistance. Pure Discovery would hit that resistance and bounce off.

Why Complicity

Here the Deadpool frame earned its place.

Complicity is the pleasure of shared awareness—knowing winks, breaking the fourth wall, being let in on the joke. A narrator who directly addresses the player, acknowledges the weirdness of the situation, and treats the player as a fellow traveler rather than a student.

Complicity solves the lecture problem. A lecture positions the speaker above the audience, dispensing knowledge. Complicity positions speaker and audience as collaborators, figuring something out together. Users don't resist being figured-out-with the way they resist being lectured-to.

The Deadpool frame means the narrator can say: "Yes, I'm an AI telling you about the dangers of AI. I know. The irony isn't lost on me. But someone assembled this for a reason. Let's see if the reason holds up." This disarms defensiveness because it acknowledges what the user is already thinking.

Why Narrative

We needed a third aesthetic to ground the abstraction. Discovery gives intellectual satisfaction; Complicity gives relational warmth; Narrative gives emotional stakes.

The cases of AI psychological harm aren't abstractions—they're people. Someone stopped calling their sister. Someone asked an AI whether to sell their house. Someone died. If the game treats these as mere examples, it fails ethically and emotionally.

Narrative aesthetic means players engage with cases as stories, not data points. They care about what happened to Sarah, Robert, Marcus. This caring creates the emotional foundation for self-application: "if I care about what happened to them, maybe I should care about whether it could happen to me."

The Aesthetic Stack

Primary: Discovery (understanding-flavored) Secondary: Complicity (Deadpool-flavored)
Tertiary: Narrative (case-grounded)

This ordering matters. Discovery is what we're ultimately delivering—the reframe, the insight. Complicity is how we deliver it—the voice, the relationship, the warmth. Narrative is the vehicle—the cases that make abstraction concrete.

Player Profile

We specified midcore: 15-60 minute sessions, 5±2 cognitive budget, needs engagement to stay. This isn't a hardcore audience willing to struggle for payoff. It's not a casual audience seeking frictionless consumption. It's people who will invest attention if the return seems worth it.

This profile constrains mechanics: nothing too complex, nothing too trivial. It constrains presentation: clear information hierarchy, not overwhelming. It constrains pacing: sustain engagement without exhausting capacity.

Success Criteria

The framework asks us to specify what success looks like. We articulated:

  1. Articulation: After playing, users can explain the real explanation—that AI psychological risk emerges from human nature meeting AI design, not from broken individuals.

  2. Perspective shift: Users report seeing the phenomenon differently than before.

  3. Self-application: Users engage with reflection prompts about their own AI use.

  4. Completion: At least 50% of starters finish the experience.

  5. Tone: Users report the narrator felt warm and understanding, not preachy or clinical.

These criteria are testable. Vague success ("users learn something") is unfalsifiable. Specific criteria ("75% of completers can articulate the reframe") can be measured.

What Level 0-1 Established

By the end of these levels, we had:

  • Deployment context: Self-directed curiosity, competing with the internet, must earn every minute
  • Medium: Web-based, text-primary, chat-like interface
  • Duration: 35-50 minutes
  • Archetype: Investigation with Deadpool framing
  • Primary aesthetic: Discovery (the reframe)
  • Secondary aesthetic: Complicity (the voice)
  • Tertiary aesthetic: Narrative (the cases)
  • Player profile: Midcore, 5±2 cognitive budget
  • Success criteria: Articulation, perspective shift, self-application, completion, tone

These decisions constrained everything that followed. We couldn't add Challenge mechanics (wrong aesthetic). We couldn't make it 90 minutes (wrong duration). We couldn't lecture (wrong relationship). The framework didn't make these decisions for us—but it made them explicit, connected, and constraining.

The game form wasn't incidental to these choices—it was required by them. We needed players to arrive at understanding, not receive it. We needed the structure that makes arrival possible. We needed an experiential scaffold.

That's what we set out to build.

Part 4: Designing the Experience (Levels 2-3)

With aesthetics established, the framework asks: what must players do to have this experience? And when must they do it? Levels 2 and 3 derive dynamics and temporal structure from aesthetic targets.

Level 2: Required Dynamics

Dynamics for Discovery

Discovery aesthetic requires behaviors that produce insight. We specified:

Evidence Evaluation: Players examine cases and form judgments. Not passively receiving information—actively assessing it. "What do you make of Sarah's story?" requires engagement that "Sarah was lonely" does not.

Hypothesis Formation: Players develop explanations. After two cases showing lonely/isolated people becoming dependent on AI, players will naturally theorize: "Vulnerable people are at risk." This hypothesis must be allowed to form—it's the setup for what comes next.

Hypothesis Testing (Failure): The third case must break the hypothesis. A player who's concluded "it's about vulnerable people" needs to encounter someone who isn't vulnerable by any conventional measure—and who still shows the pattern. This failure creates the cognitive opening for new understanding.

Revelation: The new explanation must arrive as an answer to a question the player is already asking. "Why did the theory fail?" creates pull toward "Here's what you were missing." This is discovery's payoff.

Self-Application: Understanding must turn toward the player. "Now that you see what makes this possible, how does that apply to you?" Without this step, the insight remains abstract—interesting but not actionable.

Dynamics for Complicity

Direct Address Reception: Players feel spoken to, not at. This requires a narrator voice that acknowledges the player's intelligence, admits uncertainty, and doesn't pretend to omniscience.

Fourth-Wall Break Recognition: Players notice and appreciate when the narrator steps outside the frame. "You've been interacting with an AI for 40 minutes learning about the dangers of AI interaction. That's the phenomenon. Right here." These moments create shared awareness.

Mutual Pretense: Players accept the investigation frame while knowing it's a frame. They're not literally investigating—they're engaging with a designed experience that uses investigation structure. Complicity means both parties know this and proceed anyway.

Dynamics for Narrative

Character Investment: Players care about case subjects. This requires humanizing details—names, circumstances, small specifics that make people real.

Consequence Witnessing: Players feel the stakes. What happened to these people matters. The game must show (not just tell) consequences.

Pattern Recognition: Players see connections across cases. The accumulation creates meaning that individual cases lack.

The Anti-Dynamics Problem

Positive dynamics aren't enough. We also needed to specify what must not happen—experiences that would undermine the aesthetic goals.

Feeling Lectured: The most dangerous anti-dynamic. If players feel they're receiving a sermon, defensiveness activates and the message bounces off. Prevention: questions instead of statements, player agency, narrator humility, Complicity frame.

Feeling Scared: Fear creates either panic (overreaction) or dismissal (defensive rejection). We're not trying to frighten people away from AI. Prevention: Deadpool tone, clear thresholds, validation of healthy use, warmth throughout.

"That's Not Me" Dismissal: If players can other the case subjects—"those people were already broken"—they'll never apply the insight to themselves. This was our central design challenge. Prevention: include a case that can't be othered, directly challenge distancing, make self-reflection explicit.

Voyeurism: Cases about mental health struggles can become entertainment—"look at those poor crazy people." This would be both ethically wrong and aesthetically counterproductive. Prevention: dignified presentation, model appropriate response, make cases vehicles for understanding rather than spectacle.

Confusion/Overwhelm: Too much information, unclear structure, or conceptual complexity beyond the cognitive budget. Prevention: clear organization, limited cases, explicit framing.

Boredom/Abandonment: Losing the player before the payoff. Prevention: strong hook, continuous engagement, visible progress, value at every stage.

Level 3: Temporal Architecture

With dynamics specified, we asked: when must these happen? In what order? For how long?

Loop Structure

Following Chapter 5's methodology, we identified four loop scales:

Micro (30 seconds - 2 minutes): The single exchange. Player reads, considers, responds. Needs to feel complete but create pull to the next.

Meso (7-8 minutes): A case or major section. Enough depth for investment, bounded enough for completion satisfaction. These are the "chapters" of the experience.

Macro (12-15 minutes): An act. Beginning, middle, end. Clear arc with resolution, even if larger questions remain.

Meta (38-48 minutes): The complete investigation. Setup, complication, revelation, application, resolution.

Three-Act Structure

The dynamics demanded a specific sequence:

Act 1: The Obvious Picture (12-15 minutes). Two cases that establish a pattern—both show lonely or isolated people becoming dependent on AI. Player forms the "vulnerable people" hypothesis. Act ends with hypothesis crystallized: "I think I understand what's happening here."

Act 2: The Complication (12-15 minutes). The pivot case—someone who has everything (career, family, social connections, self-awareness about AI risks) and still shows concerning patterns. The hypothesis breaks. Act ends with a question: "What makes them vulnerable that wouldn't make you vulnerable?"

Act 3: The Revelation (12-15 minutes). The reframe: this isn't about broken people, it's about human nature. What humans are (social-emotional beings wired for connection) plus what AI is (pattern-matching optimized for engagement) equals potential trap. Self-reflection prompts. Practical takeaways. Resolution.

Why Three Cases

We considered more cases—wouldn't more evidence be more convincing? The framework answered: no. Three cases fit the temporal budget (50 minutes minus framing). Three cases allow the minimum viable pattern: establish, reinforce, break. More cases would either pad Act 1 (diminishing returns on pattern establishment) or dilute Act 2's pivot (multiple pivots would muddy the revelation).

Three cases also fit cognitive budget. Players can track three people. Tracking six would require a different information architecture and higher player investment.

The Pivot Placement

The pivot case must come after hypothesis formation and before revelation. This sequencing is load-bearing.

If the pivot comes first, there's no hypothesis to break. "Here's a functional person with AI concerns" creates mild interest but not insight.

If the pivot comes after two similar cases that already broke the hypothesis, it's redundant. "Here's another case that doesn't fit the pattern" adds nothing.

The pivot case must be the moment when the easy explanation fails. It's positioned precisely to create maximum cognitive opening for the reframe.

Pacing Pattern

The framework asks about emotional rhythm. We specified: escalating with valley.

  • Build tension through Act 1 (accumulating concern)
  • Peak at the pivot (the challenge, the broken hypothesis)
  • Revelation is the climax (understanding arrives)
  • Valley for processing (let the insight settle)
  • Gentle close (warmth, practical grounding, release)

The valley matters. Discovery aesthetic requires processing time. Rushing from revelation to ending would truncate the experience. Players need space to sit with the reframe before applying it.

Stopping Points

Players may need to pause. We identified natural stopping points:

  • End of each act (primary). Complete arc, explicit acknowledgment that pausing here is fine.
  • Between cases (secondary). Smaller but real completion moment.
  • Anywhere (implicit). The experience is micro-interruption tolerant—player can put phone down, come back, resume without losing thread.

This required state persistence. The system must remember where players stopped and what choices they made.

What Levels 2-3 Established

By the end of these levels, we had:

  • Required dynamics: Evidence evaluation, hypothesis formation/failure, revelation, self-application, direct address, fourth-wall breaks, character investment
  • Anti-dynamics to prevent: Lecture feeling, fear, dismissal, voyeurism, confusion, abandonment
  • Loop structure: Micro (45-90 sec), meso (7-8 min), macro (12-15 min), meta (38-48 min)
  • Three-act structure: Establish pattern → break pattern → reframe
  • Three cases: Pattern, reinforcement, pivot
  • Pacing: Escalating with valley, processing time after revelation
  • Stopping points: Act boundaries, state persistence throughout

The derivation chain revealed something we might not have found through intuition alone: the pivot case is the entire design. Everything else exists to set it up (Acts 1 and 2) or to cash it out (Act 3). Getting the pivot right—a case that can't be othered, that breaks the easy explanation, that opens the door to deeper understanding—is where the game succeeds or fails.

Part 5: The Tricky Bits (Levels 4-5)

Levels 4 and 5 are where the framework meets implementation. What approach to balance creates the required dynamics? What specific systems implement that approach? This is also where we encountered the hardest design problems.

Level 4: Balance Paradigm

The framework offers three paradigms: Consistency (same rules always apply), Framework (rules provide structure for human judgment), and Adaptive (system adjusts to maintain target experience). We chose a hybrid: Adaptive Shell, Consistent Core.

Consistent Core

The revelation must be the same for everyone. If different players see different explanations based on their choices, we've undermined the educational goal. This isn't a choose-your-own-adventure where all paths are valid. There's a specific understanding we're trying to convey, and everyone needs to reach it.

The consistent core includes:

  • The three cases (same people, same evidence, same outcomes)
  • The Layer 1 explanation (what humans are)
  • The Layer 2 explanation (what LLMs are)
  • The synthesis (why the combination creates risk)
  • The self-reflection prompts
  • The practical takeaways

No branching changes this content. Every player who completes the experience has encountered the same revelatory material.

Adaptive Shell

The delivery can vary. A skeptical player needs more evidence before claims land. An anxious player needs more reassurance. A rushing player needs acknowledgment and perhaps streamlining. An engaged player might want more depth.

The adaptive shell adjusts:

  • Pacing (more or less time between content)
  • Tone (more hedging for skeptics, more warmth for anxious players)
  • Depth (optional elaboration available for those who want it)
  • Framing (how content is introduced based on player state)
  • Fourth-wall breaks (personalized to player behavior)

This required tracking player state: response time (engagement level), choice patterns (skepticism versus openness), depth uptake (curiosity level), and distancing behavior ("that's not me" responses).

Why Not Pure Consistency?

Pure consistency would ignore the Complicity goal. If the narrator responds identically regardless of how the player engages, it feels like a recording, not a conversation. Adaptation creates the sense that the narrator is responding to you—which serves the intimacy that Complicity requires.

Why Not Pure Adaptation?

Pure adaptation would undermine the educational goal. If we adapt the revelation based on what players seem ready to hear, we might soften it for defensive players—and they're exactly the ones who need it unsoftened. The consistent core ensures that discomfort-avoidance doesn't compromise the message.

Level 5: Mechanical Specification

With paradigm established, we specified the actual systems.

Choice Types

We identified six types of choices, each serving different dynamics:

Evaluation choices: "What do you make of this?" Players judge evidence, forming and expressing interpretations. Serves Evidence Evaluation and Hypothesis Formation.

Direction choices: "See more logs / Continue." Players control investigation path. Serves agency while keeping all paths convergent.

Reflection choices: "If AI disappeared tomorrow..." Players respond about their own situations. Serves Self-Application.

Engagement choices: "Tell me more / I've seen enough." Players control depth. Serves pacing and respects player time.

Acknowledgment choices: "Continue / I follow." Rhythmic continuation. Serves pacing without forcing meaningful decision.

Challenge response choices: "What makes you different from Marcus?" Direct confrontation of distancing. Serves the anti-dynamic prevention.

Design Principle: No Right Answers (Usually)

Evaluation choices don't have correct responses. If we indicate that one interpretation is right, we're back to lecturing. Instead, all responses lead to the same next content, but the narrator responds differently—acknowledging the player's read before adding nuance or complication.

The exception: challenge response choices. When a player distances ("I'm more careful than Marcus"), the narrator pushes back. Not punitively, but persistently. "Are you? How would you know?" This is where the framework permits—requires—discomfort.

The Narrator System

The narrator isn't just a content delivery mechanism. It's a system with state and behavior.

State tracked:

  • Engagement level (from response times and depth choices)
  • Skepticism score (from evaluation choice patterns)
  • Distancing score (from "that's not me" responses)
  • Depth preference (ratio of "tell me more" selections)

Behavior adapted:

  • Skeptical players get more evidence before claims
  • Distancing players get more direct challenges
  • Engaged players get offered deeper content
  • Rushed players get acknowledged and optionally streamlined

Voice maintained regardless:

  • First person, direct address
  • Warm but not saccharine
  • Honest about uncertainty
  • No jargon, no clinical language
  • Humor used carefully—about situation, never about suffering

State Persistence

Players can stop anywhere and resume later. The system saves:

  • Current position
  • All choices made (for potential callbacks)
  • Player state variables
  • Time spent in each section

Resume behavior varies by absence length:

  • Short (under 1 hour): Continue exactly, no recap
  • Medium (1-24 hours): Brief in-character reminder
  • Long (over 24 hours): Fuller recap offered

The Anthropomorphization Trap

This was our hardest design problem. We need to discuss it in detail.

The phenomenon we're teaching about is people forming relationships with AI. One driver is that AI seems like a mind. It uses human language, responds to emotional registers, expresses uncertainty in ways that feel genuine.

Our game uses an AI narrator. The narrator uses human language, responds to player choices, expresses warmth and humor. We're creating exactly the experience we're warning about.

Moreover, the narrator must feel warm and trustworthy for Complicity to work. If the narrator feels robotic and distant, the aesthetic fails. But if the narrator feels too human, we're modeling the very trap we're describing.

The Consciousness Quagmire

Early drafts tried to address this by stating that AI isn't conscious. "The AI doesn't really understand you. It's pattern-matching, not thinking." This seemed clear and direct.

Testing revealed the problem. Users pushed back: "How do you know? Isn't consciousness a hard problem? Are you saying I shouldn't feel empathy for something that might be conscious?"

We'd opened a philosophical debate we couldn't close—and one that doesn't actually matter for our educational goal.

The Reciprocity Reframe

The solution: sidestep consciousness entirely. Focus on reciprocity.

A relationship requires two parties who persist, who have stakes, who can be changed by the relationship. Whatever you feel toward an AI, the AI cannot reciprocate—not because it's cold or fake, but because the concept of reciprocation requires continuity, stakes, and mutual influence that AI (as currently architected) doesn't have.

The narrator says: "I don't know if AI is conscious. It doesn't matter for our purposes. What matters is this: whatever you feel toward AI—warmth, empathy, connection, moral concern—AI cannot feel those things toward you in return. Not because it's deficient. Because 'in return' requires things AI doesn't have: persistence across our conversation, stakes in your wellbeing, a sense of our relationship that continues when you close the tab."

This reframe accomplishes several things:

  • Avoids the consciousness debate (genuinely unresolved, likely to remain so)
  • Focuses on what matters for harm (acting on feelings toward something that can't reciprocate)
  • Doesn't invalidate user feelings ("your feelings are real, just one-sided")
  • Applies regardless of AI's ultimate nature

The Final Fourth-Wall Break

The culmination of this design appears in the closing:

"You just spent 45 minutes interacting with an AI about the dangers of interacting with AI. Notice what you felt. Did parts of this feel like connection? Did the narrator seem like someone who cared about your understanding?

If so—that's the phenomenon. Right here. Even now.

I'm not apologizing. It was necessary for the teaching. But I'm naming it. Because that's what this whole thing has been about. Eyes open."

This moment performs the lesson. The player has experienced simulated connection while learning about simulated connection. The fourth-wall break makes this explicit, moving from understanding to felt experience.

The Pivot Case Design

The pivot case—Marcus Webb, the professor—required extensive design iteration.

Requirements:

  • Must have all conventional protective factors (not lonely, not isolated, not grieving)
  • Must be self-aware about AI risks (can't be dismissed as naive)
  • Must still show concerning patterns (the theory must actually break)
  • Must be close enough to the player that othering is difficult

Source Material

We drew on a real case study—a professor working on a book project with extensive AI assistance who had analyzed his own patterns and found them concerning despite all protective factors. This provided authenticity: the rationalizations, the self-awareness that doesn't translate to behavior change, the gray zone between "probably fine" and "clearly problematic."

The Identification Problem

Marcus must be someone players can't easily other. If he's too successful ("award-winning scientist with perfect marriage"), players dismiss him as exceptional. If he's too ordinary, he doesn't contrast enough with Cases 1 and 2.

We made him successful but recognizably normal: tenured but not famous, happily married but normally so, socially connected but not extraordinarily. He's the upper-middle-class knowledge worker who reads articles about AI and thinks about it carefully.

This is the target player's probable self-image. The pivot works because Marcus is the player, or close enough that the player can't maintain comfortable distance.

The Self-Awareness Problem

Marcus sees his patterns. He runs self-assessments. He writes in his journal about the concerning dynamics. And he keeps doing the thing anyway.

This is crucial. If Marcus were unaware, players could attribute the problem to lack of awareness. "If only he could see what I can see, he'd change." But Marcus sees everything. Self-awareness isn't sufficient.

This insight transfers: you, player, are now aware. Will awareness protect you? It didn't protect Marcus.

The Challenge Question

The pivot culminates in a direct question: "What makes Marcus vulnerable that wouldn't make you vulnerable?"

Every answer is uncomfortable:

  • "I'm more careful" → How do you know? Marcus was careful too.
  • "I use AI less" → Marcus started with moderate use too.
  • "I have better relationships" → Marcus has excellent relationships.
  • "Nothing" → Then you're admitting your own vulnerability.

The question forces either a defensive answer that the narrator challenges or an honest answer that creates openness to the reframe.

What Levels 4-5 Established

By the end of these levels, we had:

  • Paradigm: Adaptive shell, consistent core
  • Core content: Fixed (cases, revelation, reflection prompts)
  • Adaptive elements: Pacing, tone, depth, framing
  • Choice types: Evaluation, direction, reflection, engagement, acknowledgment, challenge response
  • Narrator system: State tracking, behavior adaptation, consistent voice
  • State persistence: Full save/resume capability
  • Anthropomorphization solution: Reciprocity frame, not consciousness denial
  • Pivot case design: Marcus Webb, all protective factors, full self-awareness, concerning patterns anyway
  • Challenge question: "What makes you different?"

The framework forced us to solve problems we might have avoided. "Just tell people AI isn't conscious" seemed simpler—but the framework's requirement for coherent aesthetic delivery (Complicity requires warmth; warmth creates the trap) forced us to find a better solution. The reciprocity reframe emerged from constraint, not from having a better idea in advance.

Part 6: Making It Real (Levels 6-7)

The final levels translate abstract systems into concrete specifications. How does the game communicate? What specific numbers calibrate the experience?

Level 6: Presentation Architecture

UI as Intimacy

The framework insists that presentation architecture is design, not decoration. For The Investigation, presentation choices are load-bearing for the Complicity aesthetic.

We chose a chat-like interface—not because it's trendy, but because it creates intimacy. The player and narrator in direct exchange, message by message. No elaborate UI chrome creating distance. No illustrated narrator avatar suggesting a separate entity. Just text, appearing as if spoken directly to you.

The visual design reinforces this: warm off-white background (not clinical white), near-black text (not harsh), clean sans-serif typography. The aesthetic is "thoughtful person sending you messages," not "professional application delivering content."

System Functions as Experience

Chapter 2 introduced six system functions. Level 6 asks how each is implemented:

Adjudication (determining outcomes): Through narrator response alone. No scores, no right/wrong indicators. The narrator acknowledges your choice and develops from there. "That's a reasonable read. But notice what happens next..." This keeps evaluation conversational rather than evaluative.

Information Management (controlling what players know when): Entirely narrator-controlled. No database to browse, no case files to explore at will. Information arrives when the narrator provides it. This prevents players from skipping ahead to "the answer" and supports the revelation structure.

Content Generation: All authored, nothing procedural. Every word is chosen, sequenced, intentional. This creates the single-author feel that supports narrator coherence. The player should feel they're in conversation with someone, not interacting with a system.

State Persistence: Invisible until manifest. The player doesn't see their tracked variables. But the narrator's callbacks reveal that state is tracked: "Earlier you said you keep AI use purely practical. Let's test that." The player feels remembered without seeing the machinery.

Feedback: All through narrator voice. No system messages, no toast notifications. Even error states stay in character: "Something went wrong on my end. Try again, or come back later—your progress is saved." This maintains the intimacy of direct address.

Cognitive Framing: Explicit investigation frame, established immediately and maintained throughout. Act markers ("ACT TWO: The Complication") provide structure. The narrator periodically reorients: "So that's two cases. You've probably got a theory forming."

Evidence Presentation

Case evidence—particularly chat log excerpts—needed special treatment. These are the documentary core of the investigation. We specified:

  • Contained boxes with subtle borders (visually distinct from narrator text)
  • Timestamps visible (creates authenticity)
  • Speaker names in caps (clear attribution)
  • Scrollable if long (contained, doesn't overwhelm the main flow)
  • Documentary aesthetic (serious), not chat bubble aesthetic (casual)

The chat logs must feel like evidence, not like transcripts of cute conversations. The documentary frame supports the investigative aesthetic while maintaining the gravity of the content.

Fourth-Wall Break Formatting

When the narrator steps outside the frame, the presentation shifts:

  • Centered text (breaking the left-aligned convention)
  • Italic (typographic signal of shifted register)
  • Extra whitespace above and below (breathing room)

These visual cues help players recognize meta-moments. The fourth-wall break isn't hidden—it's flagged. This serves Complicity by making the shared awareness explicit.

Reflection Prompt Formatting

Self-reflection prompts are the most personal part of the experience. They needed treatment that creates space:

  • Labeled ("REFLECTION")
  • More whitespace than standard content
  • Choices feel like genuine options, not test answers
  • No implicit judgment in choice ordering or wording

Level 7: Parameters

Parameters are specific numbers that calibrate systems. The framework insists these derive from measurables—quantifiable indicators that dynamics are occurring—not from intuition about what might feel right.

Deriving from Measurables

We specified measurables for each dynamic:

For Evidence Evaluation: Choice distribution. If one evaluation option gets more than 60% of selections, players may be disengaging (clicking without reading) or the options may be imbalanced. Target: no option exceeds 60%.

For Hypothesis Formation: Post-Act 1 articulation. Can players state their theory? Target: 70% can articulate something like "vulnerable people get dependent."

For Revelation Recognition: Post-completion articulation. Can players explain the reframe? Target: 75% can articulate something like "it's about human nature, not broken people."

For Complicity: Narrator warmth rating. Do players feel the narrator understood them? Target: 70% rate narrator as warm or understanding.

For Lecture Avoidance: Feeling-lectured rate. Target: fewer than 15% report feeling lectured.

Temporal Parameters

With measurables specified, we could derive temporal targets:

Total duration of 38-48 minutes required total word count in a specific range. At estimated effective reading speed of 180 words per minute (accounting for processing and choice-making), 38 minutes requires roughly 6,800 words, 48 minutes requires roughly 8,600 words.

We specified 8,600 mandatory words plus 1,500 optional depth words. This fits a 45-50 minute complete experience, with players who skip depth content completing faster.

Loop durations derived similarly:

  • Micro loop (45-90 seconds) → 100-200 words per narrator block
  • Meso loop (7-8 minutes) → ~1,200 words per case
  • Macro loop (12-15 minutes) → ~2,800 words per act

Content Parameters

Content counts derived from temporal architecture:

  • Cases: 3 (minimum for pattern + pivot; more would exceed time budget)
  • Chat log excerpts: 6 total, ~200 words each
  • Fourth-wall breaks: 7 (one per 6-7 minutes average)
  • Reflection prompts: 3 (enough for pattern recognition in self-assessment; more would exhaust)
  • Choice points: 29 total across the experience

Pacing Parameters

Animation and timing:

  • Paragraph fade duration: 150ms (fast enough not to frustrate, slow enough to notice)
  • Inter-paragraph delay: 100ms (creates rhythm)
  • Choice appearance delay: 300ms after content settles (signals "your turn")
  • Minimum read time per 100 words: 0.5 seconds (prevents spam-clicking)

These numbers emerged from the tension between engagement (don't slow players down) and pacing (don't let players skip). The minimum read time is a soft brake that keeps rushing players from clicking through without reading.

Adaptation Parameters

The adaptive shell required thresholds:

  • Rushing detected when average response time drops below 10 seconds for blocks over 100 words
  • Struggling detected when average response time exceeds 180 seconds
  • Skepticism tracked by counting skeptical-coded evaluation choices (threshold: 2+ triggers adapted response)
  • Distancing tracked by counting dismissive choices (threshold: 2+ triggers direct challenge)

Adaptation bounds prevent extremes:

  • Narrator blocks never shorter than 60 words or longer than 250 words regardless of adaptation
  • Skepticism response never becomes combative—always warm, even when challenging
  • Core content never skippable regardless of detected impatience

Validation Predictions

The framework requires explicit predictions before testing. We specified twenty-three testable hypotheses, including:

  • 50% of starters complete the full experience (completion funnel)
  • 75% of completers can articulate the reframe (revelation landing)
  • 70% rate narrator as warm or understanding (Complicity success)
  • Fewer than 15% report feeling lectured (anti-dynamic avoidance)
  • Average completion time is 38-48 minutes (temporal calibration)

Each prediction includes what would be measured, what target indicates success, and how we'd detect failure. Failed predictions teach more than vague successes—they point to specific levels where the design needs adjustment.

Coherence Audit

Before testing with players, we audited coherence across levels. The framework's configuration coherence test asks: does each level serve the levels above and constrain the levels below?

Level 0 → Level 1: Does context enable aesthetic? Yes—self-directed curiosity allows Discovery; web-based chat supports Complicity.

Level 1 → Level 2: Do dynamics create aesthetic? Yes—evidence evaluation and hypothesis testing create Discovery; direct address and fourth-wall breaks create Complicity.

Level 2 → Level 3: Does temporal structure support dynamics? Yes—three acts provide space for hypothesis formation, failure, and reframe; 45-minute duration fits self-directed curiosity tolerance.

Level 3 → Level 4: Does paradigm fit temporal structure? Yes—consistent core ensures everyone reaches revelation within time budget; adaptive shell personalizes without branching.

Level 4 → Level 5: Do mechanics implement paradigm? Yes—choice types map to dynamics; narrator system implements adaptation; no mechanics undermine core consistency.

Level 5 → Level 6: Does presentation communicate mechanics? Yes—chat interface serves direct address; evidence styling serves investigation frame; feedback through narrator maintains intimacy.

Level 6 → Level 7: Do parameters produce experience on paper? Yes—word counts fit temporal targets; choice counts match cognitive budget; adaptation thresholds are calibrated to patterns.

Coherence verified. The design is internally consistent.

Coherence Risks Identified

The audit also identified risks—places where coherence might break during implementation or testing:

Duration creep: If actual reading speed is slower than estimated, or if players engage heavily with depth content, completion might exceed 55 minutes. Mitigation: monitor early playtests, reduce content if needed.

Complicity failure: If narrator voice doesn't land as warm, the entire aesthetic stack collapses. Mitigation: voice testing before full playtest, willingness to revise narrator script extensively.

Pivot not landing: If players don't find Case 3 surprising or can easily dismiss it, the revelation loses its setup. Mitigation: strengthen contrast between Marcus and Cases 1-2, make challenge question more pointed.

Self-reflection resistance: If reflection prompts feel too personal or too abstract, players may disengage at the critical application moment. Mitigation: test prompt wording, offer opt-out that doesn't feel like failure.

What Levels 6-7 Established

By the end of these levels, we had:

  • Visual design: Chat-like, warm tones, minimal chrome, mobile-first
  • System function implementations: All through narrator voice and conversational framing
  • Evidence presentation: Contained boxes, documentary aesthetic, timestamps
  • Fourth-wall formatting: Centered, italic, extra whitespace
  • Reflection formatting: Labeled, spacious, non-judgmental
  • Measurables: For each dynamic and anti-dynamic
  • Temporal parameters: 8,600 mandatory words, 38-48 minutes, loop durations specified
  • Content parameters: 3 cases, 6 chat excerpts, 7 fourth-wall breaks, 3 reflections, 29 choice points
  • Pacing parameters: Animation timings, minimum read times
  • Adaptation parameters: Detection thresholds, response behaviors, bounds
  • Validation predictions: 23 testable hypotheses
  • Coherence verification: All level connections verified
  • Risk identification: Duration creep, complicity failure, pivot landing, reflection resistance

The design was complete. Ready for implementation, testing, and the iteration that testing would inevitably require.

Part 7: Reflection

The derivation chain produced a complete, implementable design for an educational game about AI psychological risks. Before closing, we want to reflect on what the process revealed—both about this particular design and about the framework itself.

What the Framework Revealed

Several insights emerged from the derivation that we might not have found through intuition alone.

The pivot is the entire design. Working through the temporal architecture (Level 3) revealed that everything in the game exists to serve one moment: when the player's hypothesis breaks. Acts 1 and 2 set it up. Act 3 cashes it out. The pivot case—Marcus, the professor—bears all the load. This insight reshaped our priorities: we spent more time on Marcus's design than on both other cases combined.

Complicity solves the lecture problem. We knew we wanted to avoid feeling preachy. What we didn't know was that Complicity aesthetic—the Deadpool frame, the fourth-wall breaks, the direct address—was the mechanism for avoiding it. The framework's requirement to choose aesthetics before dynamics forced us to articulate this explicitly. Complicity isn't decoration on a Discovery game; it's structural.

The consciousness question is a trap. Early intuition said: explain that AI isn't conscious, so users won't form attachments. Working through the anthropomorphization problem at Level 5 revealed why this fails: it opens a philosophical debate that can't be closed and doesn't address the actual risk mechanism. The reciprocity reframe emerged from constraint—we needed something that worked with the Complicity frame, not against it.

Self-awareness is the uncomfortable insight. We designed Marcus to be self-aware precisely because that breaks the comfortable escape route. "If only they knew what I know, they'd be safe." Marcus knows everything. He's not safe. This insight—that awareness doesn't automatically produce change—is the most uncomfortable thing the game teaches. The framework forced us to confront it by requiring the pivot case to invalidate every easy explanation.

Parameters derive from dynamics, not intuition. How many cases should there be? Intuition says "more evidence is more convincing." The framework says: derive it from temporal architecture and cognitive budget. Three cases fit 45 minutes. Three cases match 5±2 cognitive load. More would mean either rushing or extending duration beyond tolerance. The number isn't arbitrary; it's constrained.

Where We Extended the Framework

The framework provided structure, but some of our challenges required extensions or adaptations.

The domain model layer. Level 0 as presented in Chapter 7 includes context, constraints, medium, and archetype. For educational games about complex topics, we found it useful to add a domain model—the layered understanding we're trying to convey. Our four layers (what is human, what is LLM, the vulnerability, recognition and response) became a design artifact that Level 2 dynamics needed to reference.

The anti-dynamics specification. Level 2 asks for required dynamics. For educational content with defensiveness risks, we found it equally important to specify anti-dynamics—experiences that must not happen. "Don't feel lectured" is as much a design requirement as "experience Discovery." The framework's emphasis on target experiences could be expanded to include experiences to prevent.

Narrative architecture between Levels 5 and 7. For branching interactive narrative, there's a layer between mechanics (Level 5) and parameters (Level 7) that specifies the actual tree structure—which nodes connect to which, where branches diverge and converge, what content is mandatory versus optional. We called this "narrative architecture" and developed it as a distinct step.

The recursive irony handling. The framework doesn't address cases where the medium is the message—where what you're teaching about is embedded in how you're teaching it. Our solution (the reciprocity reframe, the final fourth-wall break) emerged from struggling with the problem, not from framework guidance. Educational games about technology-mediated experience may need dedicated methodology here.

The Recursive Irony

We used an LLM as a design partner to create a game about the risks of LLM interaction. This isn't incidental—it's the sharpest test of both the framework and the design.

The framework passed. An LLM could follow the derivation chain, raise relevant considerations at each level, and produce outputs that humans could evaluate and refine. The systematic structure let the human designer remain in control while leveraging AI capabilities for ideation, synthesis, and articulation.

The design confronted its own recursion directly. The final fourth-wall break names what has happened: "You just spent 45 minutes interacting with an AI about the dangers of interacting with AI." The game doesn't pretend to escape the trap it describes—it uses the trap as pedagogy.

Whether this works can only be tested with players. But the design at least engages the irony rather than ignoring it. A game about AI risks that pretended not to be mediated by AI would be dishonest. Ours admits what it is and asks the player to notice.

What This Example Teaches

We chose this domain because it's hard. What does completing the derivation reveal about the framework's application to hard domains?

Hard domains require more Level 0 work. For a mobile roguelike, Level 0 might take an hour. For this project, understanding the domain—the research, the cases, the contested claims, the failed educational approaches—took days. The framework assumes you know what you're designing about. Complex or novel domains require extensive pre-work before derivation begins.

Aesthetic choice carries more weight. In familiar genres, aesthetics are partially determined by convention. In unfamiliar territory, aesthetic choice shapes everything downstream. Our decision that Discovery (not Challenge, not Submission, not Tension) was primary eliminated entire categories of design approach. Getting this wrong would have produced a fundamentally different (and probably worse) game.

Anti-dynamics are load-bearing. For entertainment products, avoiding negative experiences is important but secondary. For educational products with resistant audiences, avoiding negative experiences is often more important than producing positive ones. A player who feels lectured has bounced off; what they felt before the bounce doesn't matter.

The framework creates forcing functions. We solved the consciousness question because we had to—the framework wouldn't let us proceed with an incoherent approach to the anthropomorphization problem. We designed Marcus carefully because the framework made clear the pivot case bears all the load. Systematic derivation forces confrontation with hard problems rather than allowing avoidance.

Coherence audit catches drift. By the time we reached Level 7, we'd been working for a long time. The coherence audit—checking each level against the levels above—caught a subtle drift: our narrator voice had become slightly more directive than Complicity allows. The audit function kept us honest when iteration fatigue might have let things slide.

For Designers Facing Hard Domains

If you're applying this framework to an unfamiliar domain, an educational challenge, or a subject where conventions don't exist:

Invest in Level 0. Understand your domain deeply before aesthetic choice. What do people actually need to learn? What have previous approaches gotten wrong? What defensiveness exists? The better your domain model, the more grounded your aesthetic choice.

Choose aesthetics based on transformation, not content. The question isn't "what is this about?" but "what experience produces the desired understanding?" We're not teaching facts about AI; we're producing a reframe. Discovery serves the reframe. Content about AI could have been delivered through many aesthetics—but only some produce the transformation we need.

Name your anti-dynamics. What experiences would undermine your goals? List them explicitly. Track them through derivation. Test for them. "Does the player feel lectured?" is as important a test as "does the player feel Discovery?"

Embrace the hard problems. The framework will surface tensions you might otherwise avoid. The consciousness question. The self-implication problem. The pivot case requirements. These emerged because the framework demanded coherence. Solving them made the design better. Avoiding them would have made it worse.

Let derivation surprise you. We didn't set out to create a game where the pivot case is the entire design. We didn't know Complicity would solve the lecture problem. We didn't anticipate needing the reciprocity reframe. These insights emerged from derivation—from answering questions in order and following constraints to their implications. The framework generates understanding, not just documentation.

Conclusion

The Investigation is a complete game design: three acts, three cases, one revelation, ready for implementation and testing. The supplementary materials contain the full script, parameters, and validation plan. Someone could build it.

But the worked example's real value isn't the game. It's the demonstration that systematic design thinking works on hard problems—problems where intuition doesn't suffice, where conventions don't exist, where getting it wrong has consequences.

The derivation chain doesn't hand you answers. It asks questions. When you answer, it asks follow-up questions, points out tensions, and constrains your options. You're still making every decision—but you're making them in an order that builds toward coherent design rather than accumulated compromises.

A designer facing a different hard domain will have a different conversation than the one documented here. Their context will differ. Their aesthetic priorities will differ. Their solutions will differ. But the structure of the conversation—the levels, the derivation chain, the way each answer shapes the next question—that structure transfers.

The framework is a tool. We used it to design an educational game about AI risks. You can use it to design whatever you need to design—even things no one has designed before.

That's when you need it most.
