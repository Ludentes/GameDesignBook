Part 2: From Aesthetic to Genre

Prerequisites: You've Chosen Your Aesthetics

This chapter assumes you've read Chapter 1: Understanding Game Systems (Section 2: The Twelve Aesthetics) and identified your aesthetic goals using the MDA framework plus our four expansions.

Quick reminder - The 12 Aesthetics:

MDA's Original Eight:

  1. Sensation - Sensory pleasure, beauty, visceral immersion
  2. Fantasy - Make-believe, imagining yourself in another world
  3. Narrative - Story and drama
  4. Challenge - Obstacle course, overcoming difficulty
  5. Fellowship - Social framework, playing with others
  6. Discovery - Exploration, finding new things
  7. Expression - Self-discovery, creativity
  8. Submission - Pastime, relaxation, "turning brain off"

Our Four Expansions: 9. Mastery - Deep system understanding and optimization (distinct from Challenge: not just overcoming but understanding deeply) 10. Tension - Adrenaline, time pressure, high-stakes decisions (distinct from Sensation: psychological pressure not sensory pleasure) 11. Progression - Growth, becoming more powerful over time (distinct from Expression: advancement not creativity) 12. Collection - Accumulation, completionism, "gotta catch 'em all" (distinct from Discovery: possessing not finding)

If you haven't chosen your primary aesthetic yet, stop here and read Chapter 1 first.

The Two-Layer Framework

Context has two layers that designers often confuse:

Layer 1: GENRE (Load-Bearing)

  • The structural type of experience you're creating
  • Mystery investigation, horror survival, political thriller, power fantasy
  • Determines what mechanics you need
  • Determines how systems should work
  • Cannot be easily swapped without redesign

Layer 2: DRESSING (Cosmetic)

  • The surface aesthetics and setting flavor
  • Star Wars, medieval fantasy, cyberpunk, Victorian London
  • Provides player attachment and recognition
  • Creates visual/tonal consistency
  • Can often be swapped without changing core experience

The critical insight: Most designers think "I'm making a Star Wars game" tells them what to build. It doesn't. "I'm making a political thriller (genre) in Star Wars dressing" tells them what to build.

The Star Wars Proof

Consider three productions in the same universe:

Star Wars: A New Hope (1977)

Genre: Fantasy hero's journey (Campbell's monomyth)

What this genre needs:

  • Mentor relationship (Obi-Wan teaching Luke)
  • Power growth through trials (Force training)
  • Moral choice (temptation of dark side)
  • Destiny/prophecy elements (Chosen One narrative)
  • Coming of age arc (farm boy to hero)

Dressing: Space opera (starships, lightsabers, aliens)

Could be reskinned as:

  • Medieval fantasy (wizard training apprentice)
  • Eastern martial arts film (master/student)
  • Superhero origin story (great power/great responsibility)

The genre is load-bearing. The dressing is cosmetic.

Rogue One (2016)

Genre: War movie (suicide mission, ensemble cast)

What this genre needs:

  • Squad-based tactics (different specialists)
  • High lethality (characters die)
  • Resource scarcity (desperate odds)
  • Mission objectives > character survival
  • Gritty, grounded combat

Dressing: Star Wars universe (same as above)

Could be reskinned as:

  • WWII mission (The Dirty Dozen)
  • Modern military (Black Hawk Down)
  • Heist gone wrong (Heat)

Different genre, same universe. Needs completely different mechanics.

Andor (2022)

Genre: Political thriller/espionage

What this genre needs:

  • Information gathering (spy networks)
  • Social maneuvering (alliances, betrayals)
  • Consequences of choices (rebellion has costs)
  • Slow-burn tension (paranoia, surveillance)
  • Systemic corruption (fighting the system)

Dressing: Star Wars universe (same as above)

Could be reskinned as:

  • Cold War espionage (The Americans)
  • Cyberpunk corporate resistance (Mr. Robot)
  • Historical resistance movement (any occupied nation)

Same dressing, three completely different genres, three completely different mechanics needed.

Why This Matters for Design

Wrong approach (most designers): "I want to make a Star Wars game!" → Builds space battles, lightsabers, Force powers → Doesn't know what experience to create → Ends up with incoherent mix or space coffee

Right approach: "I want Discovery + Narrative aesthetics" → These serve mystery investigation genre → Mystery investigation could be Star Wars dressing → Need investigation mechanics, clue systems, deduction → Apply Star Wars flavor to those mechanics

The genre determines what you build. The dressing determines what it looks like.

The Complete Decision Process

Step 1: Identify Your Aesthetics

Question 1: What's your PRIMARY aesthetic?

Pick ONE. This is your north star. Everything serves this.

Quick self-test: □ Players should primarily feel sensory pleasure and beauty → Sensation □ Players should primarily feel immersed in another identity → Fantasy □ Players should primarily feel engaged with dramatic story → Narrative □ Players should primarily feel satisfaction from overcoming obstacles → Challenge □ Players should primarily feel connected with other players → Fellowship □ Players should primarily feel joy of discovering new things → Discovery □ Players should primarily feel creative self-expression → Expression □ Players should primarily feel relaxed and unwinding → Submission □ Players should primarily feel deep understanding and optimization → Mastery □ Players should primarily feel adrenaline and high stakes → Tension □ Players should primarily feel character growth and power → Progression □ Players should primarily feel completionist satisfaction → Collection

Question 2: What are your SECONDARY aesthetics?

Pick 1-2 that support your primary. Most good games serve 2-3 aesthetics.

Common pairings that work:

  • Challenge + Mastery (Dark Souls, Into the Breach)
  • Narrative + Discovery (Disco Elysium, Outer Wilds)
  • Fellowship + Challenge (Left 4 Dead, Deep Rock Galactic)
  • Submission + Progression (idle games, casual RPGs)
  • Discovery + Narrative (mystery games, Return of the Obra Dinn)
  • Tension + Challenge (horror survival, permadeath roguelikes)

Pairings that conflict:

  • ❌ Submission + Tension (can't relax AND be tense)
  • ❌ Challenge + Submission (can't be difficult AND turn-brain-off)
  • ⚠️ Fellowship + Mastery (social time vs optimization - possible but requires careful balance)

Step 2: Filter Genres by Aesthetic

For each aesthetic, certain genres naturally create that experience.

Use this matrix to find candidate genres:

Aesthetic-Genre Compatibility Matrix

SENSATION (Sensory pleasure, beauty, flow)

Genres that naturally serve:

  • Rhythm/Music games (Guitar Hero, Beat Saber)
  • Action spectacle (Devil May Cry, Bayonetta)
  • Atmospheric exploration (Journey, ABZÛ)
  • Sensory puzzles (Tetris Effect, Lumines)

Why these work:

  • Direct audiovisual feedback to every action
  • Immediate gratification (beautiful response NOW)
  • Flow state through rhythm
  • Synaesthetic design (sound + visuals unified)

Key mechanics:

  • Responsive audiovisual feedback
  • Rhythm/timing as core
  • Minimal cognitive load (attention on sensation)
  • Aesthetic coherence (every element reinforces beauty)

Dressing flexibility: VERY HIGH - works in any setting

FANTASY (Make-believe immersion)

Genres that naturally serve:

  • Power fantasy (God of War, Destiny)
  • Role-playing (D&D, Mass Effect)
  • World simulation (The Sims, Animal Crossing)
  • Escapist adventure (Zelda, Skyrim)

Why these work:

  • Player agency (you ARE the character)
  • Consistent internal logic (world feels real)
  • Wish fulfillment (be something you're not)
  • Character identity mechanics (make them yours)

Key mechanics:

  • First/second person perspective
  • Character customization and identity
  • World reactivity (NPCs acknowledge you)
  • Progression tied to character growth

Dressing flexibility: MEDIUM - needs specific world to inhabit

NARRATIVE (Story and drama)

Genres that naturally serve:

  • Dramatic arc stories (The Last of Us, Disco Elysium)
  • Branching narratives (Telltale games, Detroit: Become Human)
  • Mystery investigation (Obra Dinn, Her Story)
  • Political intrigue (Crusader Kings, Blades in the Dark)
  • Tragedy/Loss (That Dragon Cancer, To the Moon)

Why these work:

  • Consequences matter (choices affect story)
  • Character development through play
  • Dramatic structure (setup, conflict, resolution)
  • Emotional stakes (care about outcomes)

Key mechanics:

  • Choice with narrative consequences
  • Character-driven conflicts
  • Story state tracking (remembers what happened)
  • Pacing that serves dramatic beats

Dressing flexibility: MEDIUM-HIGH

  • Genre (drama, tragedy, intrigue) is load-bearing
  • Dressing (space, medieval, modern) is cosmetic

Critical insight for this aesthetic:

  • Andor and Game of Thrones = same genre (political intrigue), different dressing
  • This is WHERE genre/dressing distinction becomes clearest

CHALLENGE (Overcoming obstacles)

Genres that naturally serve:

  • Tactical combat (XCOM, Into the Breach)
  • Precision platforming (Celeste, Super Meat Boy)
  • Puzzle solving (Portal, The Witness)
  • Strategic conquest (Civilization, Total War)
  • Survival resource management (Don't Starve, Frostpunk)
  • Boss rush (Shadow of the Colossus, Cuphead)

Why these work:

  • Clear obstacles (know what to overcome)
  • Skill-testing mechanics (execution matters)
  • Fair failure (understand why you lost)
  • Mastery curve (getting better feels good)

Key mechanics:

  • Clear win/loss conditions
  • Consistent rules (pattern learning possible)
  • Difficulty calibration
  • Low-friction failure (quick retry)

Dressing flexibility: VERY HIGH

  • Chess (abstract), Dark Souls (gothic), Portal (sci-fi) all work
  • Genre determines challenge structure
  • Dressing is almost entirely cosmetic

FELLOWSHIP (Social interaction)

Genres that naturally serve:

  • Cooperative survival (Left 4 Dead, Deep Rock Galactic)
  • Social deduction (Among Us, Werewolf/Mafia)
  • Party games (Mario Party, Jackbox)
  • MMO raiding (WoW, Final Fantasy XIV)
  • Collaborative storytelling (Fiasco, The Quiet Year)
  • Trading/Negotiation (Catan, Diplomacy)

Why these work:

  • Interdependence (need each other)
  • Communication is gameplay (talking matters)
  • Shared goals or competition (binding force)
  • Social dynamics emerge naturally

Key mechanics:

  • Simple individual mechanics (brainspace for socializing)
  • Communication channels enabled
  • Clear roles or asymmetry
  • Visible game state (enables discussion)

Dressing flexibility: HIGH

  • Sci-fi crew vs medieval villagers creates different social dynamics
  • But Fellowship works in many settings

DISCOVERY (Finding new things)

Genres that naturally serve:

  • Mystery investigation (Outer Wilds, Return of the Obra Dinn)
  • Exploration (Subnautica, Metroidvania)
  • Archaeological discovery (Heaven's Vault, 80 Days)
  • Scientific experimentation (Opus Magnum, SpaceChem)
  • Interconnected revelation (Her Story, Telling Lies)

Why these work:

  • Hidden information (something to discover)
  • Reward for curiosity (exploration pays off)
  • "Aha!" moments (revelation satisfaction)
  • Interconnected discoveries (fragments form whole)

Key mechanics:

  • Non-linear exploration (player-driven)
  • Environmental storytelling (clues in world)
  • Knowledge accumulation (discoveries persist)
  • Internal consistency (investigation yields truth)

Dressing flexibility: MEDIUM

  • Needs mysterious/unknown elements
  • Works best with: Ruins, alien worlds, historical mysteries, scientific frontiers
  • Generic modern settings harder (less mystery)

EXPRESSION (Creativity and self-discovery)

Genres that naturally serve:

  • Creative building (Minecraft, Terraria)
  • Character customization (Fashion Souls, Sims)
  • Deck building (Slay the Spire, Dominion)
  • Base/City design (Factorio, Cities: Skylines)
  • Artistic creation (Dreams, Super Mario Maker)

Why these work:

  • Open-ended tools (multiple valid approaches)
  • Player-driven goals (no "correct" answer)
  • Exhibition of creativity (show others)
  • Identity through choices (build reflects you)

Key mechanics:

  • Rich option space (many combinations)
  • No optimal solution (preference matters)
  • Sharing/exhibition mechanics (optional)
  • Aesthetic feedback (creations look/feel different)

Dressing flexibility: VERY HIGH

  • Medieval castle building = Sci-fi base = Abstract deck construction
  • Genre (building, customization) is load-bearing
  • Dressing is cosmetic

SUBMISSION (Relaxation and pastime)

Genres that naturally serve:

  • Idle/Incremental (Cookie Clicker, Adventure Capitalist)
  • Cozy life sim (Animal Crossing, Stardew Valley)
  • Meditative puzzles (Flow, Monument Valley)
  • Casual match-3 (Bejeweled, Candy Crush)
  • Peaceful exploration (Book of Hours, A Short Hike)

Why these work:

  • Low stakes (can't really fail)
  • Simple mechanics (minimal cognitive load)
  • Forgiving pacing (no pressure)
  • Pleasant feedback (satisfying not stressful)

Key mechanics:

  • No failure states or very forgiving
  • Gentle progression (always moving forward)
  • Optional optimization (can engage more if wanted)
  • Calm aesthetic (soothing presentation)

Dressing flexibility: HIGH

  • Pastoral farming, space gardening, abstract zen
  • Tone matters more than specific setting

MASTERY (Deep optimization)

Genres that naturally serve:

  • Optimization puzzles (Slay the Spire, Into the Breach, Opus Magnum)
  • Competitive skill games (Fighting games, RTS, MOBA)
  • Deep strategy (Chess, Go, Poker)
  • Routing/Speedrunning (any game with skill ceiling)
  • Build crafting (Path of Exile, Factorio)
  • Pattern mastery (Dark Souls, Sekiro, rhythm games at high level)

Why these work:

  • High skill ceiling (always room to improve)
  • Learnable systems (study yields understanding)
  • Optimization space (multiple approaches, some better)
  • Measurable improvement (track getting better)
  • Depth reveals itself (beginners see surface, experts see complexity)

Key mechanics:

  • Consistent rules (pattern learning possible)
  • Transparent cause-effect (understand why)
  • Scalable difficulty (challenges grow with skill)
  • Optimization metrics (DPS, win rate, time)
  • Punishment for suboptimal play (incentive to master)

Key distinction from Challenge:

  • Challenge = overcoming difficulty once
  • Mastery = understanding deeply, optimizing, getting better over time

Dressing flexibility: VERY HIGH

  • Chess (abstract), StarCraft (sci-fi), Street Fighter (martial arts)
  • Genre is load-bearing, dressing is cosmetic

TENSION (Adrenaline and high stakes)

Genres that naturally serve:

  • Horror survival (Alien: Isolation, Resident Evil, Dead Space)
  • Real-time pressure (StarCraft, They Are Billions, FTL)
  • Permadeath roguelikes (XCOM Ironman, Hades, Slay the Spire)
  • Thriller/Suspense (Hitman stealth, Dishonored)
  • Battle royale (PUBG, Escape from Tarkov)
  • Ticking clock scenarios (This War of Mine, Frostpunk)

Why these work:

  • High stakes (meaningful consequences)
  • Limited time (can't deliberate forever)
  • Permanent or costly failure (death matters)
  • Information pressure (threat present but not fully known)
  • Resource scarcity (can't afford mistakes)

Key mechanics:

  • Time pressure (real-time OR turn limits)
  • Permanent/slow-recovering losses
  • Threat escalation (gets worse over time)
  • Limited safety nets
  • Imperfect information (can't see everything)

Key distinction from Sensation:

  • Sensation = sensory pleasure (feels good)
  • Tension = psychological pressure (feels intense)

Dressing flexibility: MEDIUM

  • Needs contexts that support stakes
  • Works: Horror, survival, warfare, heists, disasters
  • Doesn't work: Cozy pastoral, peaceful exploration

PROGRESSION (Character growth and power)

Genres that naturally serve:

  • Skill-based progression (RuneScape, Old School RuneScape)
  • RPG leveling (most RPGs, Diablo, Destiny)
  • Incremental/Idle (Cookie Clicker, Clicker Heroes)
  • Empire building (Civilization, 4X games)
  • Metroidvania (gaining abilities that open world)
  • Roguelite (meta-progression between runs)

Why these work (RuneScape player perspective):

  • Visible mastery of disciplines (99 in a skill means something)
  • Multiple parallel goals (don't like combat? Train fishing)
  • Permanent milestones (every level stays achieved)
  • Clear long-term projects (quest cape, max cape)
  • Self-directed pacing (grind when you want)
  • Community recognition (everyone knows what 99 Runecraft means)
  • Meditation through repetition (grinding is zen)

Key mechanics:

  • Multiple progression tracks (not just one "level")
  • Exponential time investment (later levels harder = meaningful)
  • Visible status symbols (skill capes, achievements)
  • Activity variety within grind
  • Prestige tiers (99 → 120 → 200M XP)

Key distinction from Mastery:

  • Mastery = player improves (execution skill)
  • Progression = character improves (time investment)
  • RuneScape has BOTH: Getting 99s (progression) + tick manipulation (mastery)

Dressing flexibility: VERY HIGH

  • Fantasy skills, space progression, idle bakery all work
  • But context must support multiple progression tracks

COLLECTION (Completionism)

Genres that naturally serve:

  • Monster/Creature collecting (Pokemon, Temtem, Cassette Beasts)
  • Achievement hunting (most modern games with achievements)
  • Card collecting (Magic: The Gathering, Hearthstone)
  • Collectathon platformers (Banjo-Kazooie, Super Mario Odyssey)
  • Catalogue completion (Breath of the Wild shrines/Korok seeds)
  • Set completion (Destiny exotics, loot games)

Why these work:

  • Finite set (can be completed)
  • Clear checklist (know what's missing)
  • Rarity tiers (common to legendary)
  • Hunting satisfaction (finding the rare one)
  • Completion reward (100% achievement)
  • Exhibition (show off to others)

Key mechanics:

  • Catalogue/collection tracking (see what you have/need)
  • Variety in obtainment (different methods)
  • Rarity system (some harder than others)
  • Completionist rewards (bonus for getting all)
  • Trading/showcase (optional but enhances)

Key distinction from Discovery:

  • Discovery = finding something new and learning about it
  • Collection = possessing all instances of known types
  • Discovery = knowledge, Collection = ownership

Dressing flexibility: HIGH

  • Creatures, cards, weapons, achievements, locations
  • But context must support collectible identity (distinct items)

Step 3: Apply Your Constraints

You now have candidate genres for your aesthetics. Filter by real-world constraints.

Time Constraints

Monthly, 2-hour sessions:

  • ✅ Episodic genres (each session = complete story)
  • ✅ Mystery (one case per session)
  • ❌ Long tactical combat (can't finish)
  • ❌ Empire building (needs continuity)

Weekly, 4-hour sessions:

  • ✅ Most genres work
  • ✅ Campaign structures
  • ✅ Tactical combat

Daily, 30-minute sessions:

  • ✅ Incremental/Idle
  • ✅ Quick puzzles
  • ❌ Deep strategy (setup too long)
  • ❌ Long narrative arcs

Decision rule:

Player Count Constraints

Solo play:

  • ✅ Discovery, Challenge, Mastery, Progression, Submission
  • ⚠️ Fellowship (need AI or solo procedures)
  • ✅ Narrative (solo investigation, personal story)

2-4 players:

  • ✅ All genres work
  • ✅✅ Fellowship genres excel
  • ✅ Cooperative or competitive

5+ players:

  • ✅ Party games, social deduction
  • ✅ MMO raiding, large cooperative
  • ⚠️ Deep strategy (analysis paralysis)
  • ❌ Intimate narrative (too many voices)

Decision rule:

Format Constraints

Digital (computer/console):

  • ✅ All genres
  • ✅✅ Real-time pressure (automation handles speed)
  • ✅✅ Complex tracking (no manual bookkeeping)
  • ✅ LLM integration possible

Tabletop (pen & paper):

  • ✅ Most genres
  • ⚠️ Real-time pressure (hard without computer)
  • ⚠️ Complex tracking (GM burden)
  • ✅ Social interaction easier

Hybrid (VTT + humans):

  • ✅ Best of both
  • ✅ Automation + social
  • ✅ LLM integration natural

Decision rule:

Cognitive Load Budget

From Chapter 3 - How much thinking can players handle?

High load budget:

  • ✅ Mastery (optimization requires thinking)
  • ✅ Deep strategy
  • ✅ Complex puzzles
  • Players expect to think hard

Medium load budget:

  • ✅ Tactical combat
  • ✅ Narrative choices
  • ✅ Discovery (investigation)

Low load budget:

  • ✅ Submission (by definition)
  • ✅ Progression (grinding is meditation)
  • ✅ Simple mechanics
  • Players want to relax

Decision rule:

Step 4: Choose Your Genre

You now have:

  • ✅ Aesthetics identified (primary + secondary)
  • ✅ Candidate genres filtered by aesthetics
  • ✅ Constraints applied (time/players/format/load)

Result: 1-3 candidate genres remain

If multiple candidates: Choose based on:

  • Personal preference/passion (which excites you?)
  • Understanding (which do you know best?)
  • Fewer pitfalls (which is less risky?)
  • Player feedback (which do they want?)

If zero candidates: Your aesthetics or constraints conflict.

Options:

  • Adjust aesthetics (pick different secondary)
  • Adjust constraints (if possible)
  • Accept hybrid complexity (harder but possible)

If one clear candidate: Proceed confidently. The decision tree worked.

Worked Example: Discovery + Narrative

Designer input:

  • Primary: Discovery
  • Secondary: Narrative
  • Constraints: Solo play, 2-hour sessions, monthly
  • Load budget: Medium

Step 1: Genres for Discovery

  • Mystery investigation ✓
  • Exploration ✓
  • Archaeological discovery ✓
  • Scientific experimentation ✓
  • Interconnected revelation ✓

Step 2: Which also serve Narrative?

  • Mystery investigation ✓✓ (serves both well)
  • Archaeological discovery ✓ (can serve both)
  • Exploration ⚠️ (Discovery yes, Narrative weak)
  • Scientific experimentation ⚠️ (Discovery yes, Narrative weak)
  • Interconnected revelation ✓ (can serve both)

Step 3: Apply constraints (solo, 2 hours, monthly)

  • Mystery investigation ✓ (solo works, 2-hour cases possible, episodic)
  • Archaeological discovery ✓ (solo works, episodic discoveries)
  • Interconnected revelation ✓ (solo investigation, episodic clues)
  • Exploration ❌ (needs longer sessions)

Step 4: Final candidates

  • Mystery investigation
  • Archaeological discovery
  • Interconnected revelation

Step 5: Choose based on preference "I love detective stories" → Mystery investigation

Result: Mystery investigation genre selected

Step 5: Separate Genre from Dressing

You have chosen a GENRE (load-bearing).

Now choose DRESSING (cosmetic).

Critical distinction:

  • Genre = what creates the experience
  • Dressing = what it looks like

Mystery Investigation genre can wear many dressings:

  • Victorian London (Sherlock Holmes)
  • Modern forensics (CSI)
  • Fantasy magic (Dresden Files)
  • Space station (sci-fi murder mystery)
  • Historical period (Name of the Rose)

All are MYSTERY INVESTIGATION at core. Dressing is interchangeable.

How to choose dressing:

Question 1: What excites you/players?

  • "We love Star Wars" → Space opera dressing
  • "We just read Foundation" → Asimov-style sci-fi
  • "We want hobbits" → Tolkien-style fantasy

Question 2: Does dressing support genre?

Good fit:

  • Mystery + Victorian London ✓ (established trope)
  • Horror + haunted house ✓ (natural fit)
  • Power fantasy + superhero ✓ (genre expectation)

Possible but requires work:

  • Mystery + cozy pastoral ⚠️ (unusual, needs careful framing)
  • Horror + bright cheerful ⚠️ (tonal conflict)
  • Tragedy + cartoon animals ⚠️ (possible but hard)

Actively conflicts:

  • Submission aesthetic + grimdark dressing ❌
  • Cozy life sim + apocalyptic wasteland ❌
  • Relaxation + violent combat dressing ❌

Question 3: Is dressing actually cosmetic here?

Test: "Could I swap dressing without changing genre?"

Example: Rogue One

  • Genre: War movie
  • Dressing: Star Wars
  • Test: Could be WWII, Vietnam, modern warfare
  • Result: Dressing is cosmetic ✓

Example: Disco Elysium

  • Genre: Political thriller
  • Dressing: Revachol (specific poverty, specific politics)
  • Test: Could be... different city? Maybe, but politics matter
  • Result: Dressing is partially load-bearing ⚠️

When in doubt: Assume dressing is cosmetic until proven otherwise.

Complete Decision Summary

The process (one page):

1. IDENTIFY AESTHETICS

  • Primary: [one aesthetic]
  • Secondary: [1-2 supporting aesthetics]

2. FILTER GENRES

  • List genres for primary aesthetic (use matrix)
  • Filter to those serving secondary
  • Result: [candidate genres]

3. APPLY CONSTRAINTS

  • Time: [hours per session, frequency]
  • Players: [solo, 2-4, 5+]
  • Format: [digital, tabletop, hybrid]
  • Load: [high, medium, low]
  • Result: [viable genres]

4. CHOOSE GENRE

  • Pick from viable genres
  • Based on: passion, understanding, fewer pitfalls
  • Result: [chosen genre]

5. SEPARATE GENRE/DRESSING

  • Genre = load-bearing (creates experience)
  • Dressing = cosmetic (looks)
  • Choose dressing: preference + genre support
  • Result: [genre + dressing]

6. PROCEED TO CONTEXT DESIGN

  • Next: Parts 3-6 (building actual context)
  • You know WHAT to build
  • Need to decide HOW MUCH to build

Validation Checklist

Before proceeding, verify:

□ Aesthetic-Genre Fit

  • Does this genre naturally create my primary aesthetic?
  • Does it also serve secondary aesthetics?

□ Constraint Compatibility

  • Can we actually run this in available time?
  • Does it work with our player count?
  • Is it feasible in our format?

□ Cognitive Load Match

  • Does this genre's natural load fit our budget?
  • Are we asking for right amount of thinking?

□ Genre-Dressing Separation

  • Do I know what's load-bearing vs cosmetic?
  • Could I explain genre without mentioning dressing?
  • Is my dressing choice actually cosmetic or partially load-bearing?

□ No Conflicting Aesthetics

  • Am I trying to serve contradictory aesthetics?
  • Do my aesthetics actually work together?

If all checked: Proceed to Part 3

If any unchecked: Revisit that step

What You've Accomplished

You've systematically moved from abstract aesthetic goals to concrete genre choice:

✅ Identified aesthetics (primary + secondary) ✅ Filtered genres using aesthetic compatibility ✅ Applied constraints (time/players/format/load) ✅ Chosen genre (load-bearing structure) ✅ Separated dressing (cosmetic layer) ✅ Validated choices (everything aligns)

You now know:

  • What experience you're creating (aesthetic)
  • What structural type serves it (genre)
  • What surface flavor you want (dressing)
  • That your choice is feasible (constraints satisfied)

Next step: Validate your context actually matters.
