Supplementary Material: Decision Matrices

This document provides reference matrices for common design decisions. These matrices encode relationships established throughout the book; they're lookup tools, not substitutes for understanding the underlying principles.

Use these matrices when:

  • You need a quick reference during active design work
  • You want to check your derivation against common patterns
  • You're exploring option spaces before committing to a direction

Do not use these matrices as:

  • Rigid prescriptions (your specific design may diverge)
  • Substitutes for reading the relevant chapters
  • Shortcuts past genuine design thinking

Using Decision Matrices

Matrices are maps, not territories. They summarize patterns observed across many games. Your specific design may have good reasons to diverge. The value of matrices is rapid lookup and pattern recognition, not prescription.

Cross-reference matrices. A single decision often requires consulting multiple matrices. Designing resources? Check Matrix 5 (aesthetic → resources) AND Matrix 4 (player profile → constraints) AND Matrix 6 (cognitive load distribution).

When matrices conflict with your instinct, investigate. Either your instinct is catching something the matrix misses (your design has unique requirements), or the matrix is surfacing something your instinct overlooked. Both are valuable signals.

Matrices encode the book's frameworks. If a matrix entry is unclear, refer to the relevant chapter. Matrix 1's dynamics come from Chapters 1 and 6. Matrix 4's parameters come from Chapters 4 and 5. Matrix 8's constraints are discussed throughout.

Update matrices as you learn. These matrices represent current understanding. As you design and playtest, you may discover patterns that refine or contradict them. Your experience is data.
