Designing Game Systems

Part 1: Introduction

Every chapter in this book has examined a piece of game design: aesthetics and what players seek from games, context and how it shapes mechanical needs, resources and how they create meaning through scarcity, cognitive load and the limits of player attention, temporal architecture and the rhythm of play, balance and the calibration of experience. Each piece matters. But pieces alone don't make a game.

This chapter is about assembly. Not in the mechanical sense of snapping components together, but in the architectural sense: understanding how each element constrains and enables the others, how decisions at one level ripple through every subsequent choice, and how the whole must serve a unified experiential purpose.

The core insight is simple: game design follows a derivation chain. Each level of design constrains the next. You cannot determine parameters without first establishing mechanics. You cannot select mechanics without understanding what temporal structure they must support. You cannot design temporal structure without knowing what player behaviors you need. And you cannot identify required behaviors without clarity on what experience you're creating.

This isn't a claim about how designers should work—creativity strikes from any direction. It's a claim about how constraints flow. Wherever you start, you'll eventually need to answer questions in a specific logical order, because each answer depends on answers that come before it.

The Experiential Scaffold

Chapter 1 introduced the metaphor of games as experiential scaffolds: purpose-built structures that enable specific experiences. We don't build systems for their own sake. We build scaffolding that supports players in reaching emotional and experiential states they couldn't reliably reach alone.

This metaphor should guide everything that follows. At every level of the derivation chain, the question isn't "is this system elegant?" or "is this mechanic interesting?" but "does this scaffolding support the experience we're building toward?" A beautiful scaffold that doesn't reach the intended destination has failed, regardless of its craftsmanship.

Investment and Payoff

Chapter 4 established that players invest cognitive resources—attention, memory, decision-making effort—and receive aesthetic payoff in return. This transaction underlies all design decisions.

The derivation chain isn't just about logical dependency; it's about ensuring that every element earns its cognitive cost. A mechanic that fits within the complexity budget but doesn't contribute to aesthetic delivery is still a failure. Conversely, a mechanic that exceeds typical budget guidelines might be justified if its aesthetic contribution is proportionally large.

Throughout this chapter, we'll frame constraints not just as limits ("can we fit this?") but as transactions ("is this worth it?"). Casual players aren't simply "lower capacity"—they're seeking efficient transactions where modest investment yields satisfying payoff. Hardcore players aren't simply "higher capacity"—they're seeking substantial payoffs that justify substantial investment.

The Chain

The derivation chain has eight levels plus a validation process:

Level 0: Context — What's fixed before design begins? Medium, platform, constraints, setting, and archetype.

Level 1: Aesthetic Profile — What experience are you creating, and for whom? Primary and secondary aesthetics, player investment capacity, success criteria.

Level 2: Required Dynamics — What must players do and perceive to have that experience? Observable behaviors, spatial expression, and how each system function serves the aesthetic.

Level 3: Temporal Architecture — When do those behaviors occur, and for how long? Loop scales, durations, pacing patterns, stopping points.

Level 4: Balance Paradigm — What approach to rules creates the required dynamics within that temporal structure? Consistency, Framework, or Adaptive paradigm.

Level 5: Mechanical Specification — What specific systems implement the paradigm and create the dynamics? After sketching alternatives, specify core mechanics, resources, and content approach.

Level 6: Presentation Architecture — How does the game communicate with the player? Information management, feedback systems, cognitive framing. UI is the game — these are design decisions as substantial as mechanics.

Level 7: Parameters — What specific numbers calibrate the systems to deliver the right intensity of experience?

Validation Process — Does the complete system actually produce the intended experience? Coherence audits, playtesting, and structured iteration.

Each level answers a specific question. Each level's answer constrains what's possible at subsequent levels. Skip a level, and you'll find yourself making arbitrary decisions that may undermine the whole.

A Note on Creativity

A framework this structured might seem to constrain creativity. It doesn't — or rather, it constrains creativity in productive ways.

Creative insight can strike anywhere: a mechanic that feels right, a theme that demands exploration, a constraint that forces innovation. The derivation chain doesn't tell you where ideas must come from. It tells you how to integrate ideas once you have them.

"I have a cool mechanic" becomes "what dynamics does this create, and what aesthetic do those dynamics serve?" The framework helps you understand your own inspiration, find its natural home in a larger system, and identify what else must be true for it to work.

The alternative — building without understanding how pieces connect — leads to systems where individual elements are interesting but the whole is incoherent. Configuration coherence (the test introduced in Chapter 1) requires that every element serve the same experiential purpose. The derivation chain is how you achieve that coherence systematically rather than accidentally.

Part 2: Entry Points

The derivation chain describes how constraints flow from aesthetics to parameters. But where do you actually begin?

Designers rarely start with a blank slate. Usually something sparks the project: a feeling to capture, a constraint to work within, a theme to explore, or a mechanic that seems promising. These entry points differ, but they all converge on the same derivation chain.

Aesthetic-First Entry

"I want players to feel the triumph of overcoming genuine challenge."

Starting with aesthetic goals is the cleanest path through the chain. You know the destination; derivation reveals the route.

From aesthetic targets, derive:

  • What dynamics create this feeling? (Level 2)
  • What temporal structure supports these dynamics? (Level 3)
  • What paradigm serves this experience? (Level 4)
  • What mechanics enable these dynamics within this structure? (Level 5)
  • How should the game communicate these systems? (Level 6)
  • What parameters tune the experience correctly? (Level 7)

Convergence point: Level 1 (Aesthetic Profile) is already complete. Proceed directly down the chain.

Constraint-First Entry

"I have six months, a solo developer, and a mobile platform."

Constraints narrow the possibility space. They don't determine aesthetics, but they eliminate options.

Mobile platform eliminates certain input schemes. Six months eliminates certain content scales. Solo development eliminates certain technical ambitions. What remains is the viable space.

From constraints, derive:

  • What aesthetics are achievable within these limits? (Level 1)
  • What temporal structures fit the platform and scope? (Level 3)
  • What content scale is producible? (Level 5)
  • What presentation complexity is viable? (Level 6)

Convergence point: Constraints populate Level 0 (Context), then inform Level 1. Once aesthetic targets are established within constraints, proceed normally.

Theme-First Entry

"I want to make a game about the costs of moral compromise."

Theme suggests aesthetics but doesn't determine them. A game "about" moral compromise could emphasize Challenge (optimize outcomes despite costs), Tension (feel the weight of choices), Narrative (experience a story of compromise), or Discovery (learn what you're capable of).

The design question isn't "how do I make a game about this theme?" but "what experience do I want players to have while engaging with this theme?"

From theme, derive:

  • What aesthetic serves this theme? (Level 1)
  • What dynamics make the theme felt rather than merely present? (Level 2)

Convergence point: Theme informs Level 0 (Setting) and Level 1 (Aesthetic), but the designer must still select specific aesthetic targets. Once selected, proceed normally.

Mechanic-First Entry

"I have this cool card-drafting mechanism I want to build around."

Starting with a mechanic means working backward to aesthetics and forward to parameters simultaneously.

Backward: What experiences does this mechanic naturally support? Card drafting creates anticipation, planning, and competition for scarce options. What aesthetic does that serve? Challenge? Mastery? Tension?

Forward: Given the aesthetic this mechanic serves, what temporal structure supports it? What other mechanics complement it? How should it communicate to players? What parameters tune it correctly?

Convergence point: The mechanic enters at Level 5. Derive Levels 0-4 backward (what context, aesthetic, dynamics, temporal structure, and paradigm does this mechanic serve?), then proceed forward through Levels 6-7.

Setting-First Entry

"I want to make a game set in a dying space station."

Setting provides vocabulary and suggests dynamics, even when it's theoretically cosmetic (could be reskinned without changing core play).

A dying space station immediately suggests: isolation, dwindling resources, systems failing, strangers thrown together, escape or salvation as goals. These aren't mechanics yet, but they're natural sources for dynamics.

From setting, derive:

  • What archetype fits this setting? (Level 0)
  • What aesthetics does this setting naturally evoke? (Level 1)
  • What dynamics emerge from the setting's inherent pressures? (Level 2)

Convergence point: Setting populates Level 0 and suggests Level 1-2 directions. The designer must still commit to specific aesthetic targets. Setting accelerates derivation by providing intuitive vocabulary and natural pressure sources.

Part 3: The Derivation Chain

Regardless of entry point, every design must eventually answer the same questions in the same order. This section walks through each level: what question it answers, what inputs it requires, and what constraints it imposes on subsequent levels.

Level 0: Context

Before designing, establish what you're designing within.

Questions answered:

  • What medium? (Video game, tabletop, TTRPG, hybrid)
  • What platform constraints? (Mobile, console, PC, physical components)
  • What scope constraints? (Team size, timeline, budget)
  • What business constraints? (Monetization requirements, audience expectations)
  • What content constraints? (Rating, cultural considerations)
  • What setting? (Thematic/fictional frame, even if potentially cosmetic)
  • What archetype? (Structural pattern—roguelike, colony sim, euro-game, one-shot, etc.)

Outputs:

  • Fixed constraints that cannot be designed around
  • Flexible constraints that could be challenged if necessary
  • Opportunities specific to this context
  • Setting vocabulary for clearer derivation
  • Archetype expectations to fulfill or deliberately subvert

Why Setting and Archetype Belong Here:

Setting provides natural sources for dynamics. "Sci-fi frontier crash survivors" immediately suggests isolation, scarcity, hostile environment, strangers thrown together. Even if the setting is theoretically cosmetic (the Space Coffee Test says you could reskin it), deriving with setting in mind accelerates decisions and engages intuition.

Archetype constrains mechanical direction. Choosing "roguelike" commits you to certain expectations (run-based, procedural, permadeath or similar). Choosing "euro-game" commits you to others (resource conversion, low randomness, indirect competition). Know your archetype before proceeding, or explicitly declare you're creating something without existing archetype.

The designer should know at Level 0 whether setting is load-bearing (mechanically essential) or cosmetic (reskinnable). Either is valid; uncertainty is not.

Context doesn't determine design, but it bounds it. A mobile game has different constraints than a tabletop game. A solo developer has different scope limits than a studio. A horror setting suggests different dynamics than a comedy setting. Establish these before proceeding.

For deeper treatment: Chapter 2 covers context analysis, including archetype selection, platform considerations, the Space Coffee Test for setting, and how context shapes mechanical expectations.

Level 1: Aesthetic Profile

The aesthetic profile defines what experience you're building toward.

Questions answered:

  • What primary aesthetic? (The core experience)
  • What secondary aesthetics? (Supporting experiences)
  • What excluded aesthetics? (Experiences that would conflict)
  • Who is the target player? (Casual, midcore, hardcore—and what that means for your design)
  • What defines success? (How will you know if the design works?)

Inputs required:

  • Context (Level 0)
  • Designer intention (what experience matters?)

Outputs:

  • Primary aesthetic with clear definition
  • Secondary aesthetics with priority order
  • Player profile with cognitive implications (see Chapter 4)
  • Success criteria that can be tested

Constraints imposed on subsequent levels:

  • Dynamics must create the specified aesthetics
  • Temporal structure must fit player profile
  • Complexity must fit cognitive budget
  • Presentation must serve aesthetic delivery

The cognitive budget begins here. A casual player profile means ~3 simultaneously tracked elements, short sessions, low complexity tolerance. A hardcore profile means ~7+ tracked elements, extended sessions, high complexity tolerance. This budget constrains every subsequent decision—not just mechanics, but how those mechanics are communicated.

For deeper treatment: Chapter 1 covers aesthetic categories in detail, including how aesthetics combine and conflict. Chapter 4 provides the cognitive load framework that determines budget constraints for each player profile.

Level 2: Required Dynamics

Dynamics are the observable behaviors and experiences that emerge from play. They're the bridge between aesthetics (what players feel) and mechanics (what the system does).

The MDA framework (Hunicke, LeBlanc, Zubek, 2004) illuminates this relationship: players experience games backward (Aesthetics ← Dynamics ← Mechanics), but designers build forward (Mechanics → Dynamics → Aesthetics). This creates the central design challenge: you know what aesthetic you want, but you must build mechanics. Dynamics are the missing bridge—the behaviors that mechanics must produce for aesthetics to emerge.

Questions answered:

  • What must players do to experience the target aesthetic?
  • What must players feel at key moments?
  • What must players learn or master?
  • What behaviors would undermine the aesthetic?
  • Do the target dynamics have spatial expression?
  • How will each system function serve the aesthetic?

Inputs required:

  • Aesthetic profile (Level 1)
  • Understanding of how aesthetics emerge from behavior

Deriving dynamics from aesthetics:

For Challenge aesthetic: Players must face genuine obstacles, develop skills, and experience earned triumph. Required dynamics include pattern learning (observe, hypothesize, test, refine), skill expression (expert execution demonstrably better than novice), and failure recovery (death followed by immediate retry with gained knowledge). Anti-dynamics include grinding (success through repetition, not skill) and confusion (failure without learning).

For Tension aesthetic: Players must face meaningful stakes with uncertain outcomes. Required dynamics include resource pressure (forced into hard choices by scarcity), threat awareness (constant vigilance), and close calls (near-death moments creating adrenaline). Anti-dynamics include predictability (outcomes feel predetermined) and triviality (stakes don't matter).

For Narrative aesthetic: Players must experience story progression with agency. Required dynamics include character engagement (emotional investment in fates), consequence tracking (remembering and anticipating outcomes), and agency expression (choices feel like they matter). Anti-dynamics include railroading (choices don't matter) and incoherence (story doesn't track).

For Discovery aesthetic: Players must uncover new information through exploration. Required dynamics include information gathering (actively seeking clues), theory formation (connecting dots, forming hypotheses), and revelation moments ("aha!" experiences when understanding clicks). Anti-dynamics include exhaustion (nothing new to find) and arbitrariness (discoveries feel random, not earned).

The critical principle: Every aesthetic requires specific dynamics to emerge. No dynamics = no aesthetic, regardless of parameters. If the required behaviors aren't happening, no amount of number-tuning will fix it.

Spatial Dynamics

Some dynamics require spatial expression. Others don't.

Ask: Do your target dynamics involve where things happen, not just what and when?

  • Exploration requires space to explore—unknown areas, hidden locations, traversal choices
  • Territory control requires contestable space—areas that can be owned, defended, lost
  • Positioning requires tactical space—locations with different properties, movement that matters
  • Logistics requires distance—transport time, supply lines, spatial optimization
  • Sanctuary requires safe space—areas distinct from danger, retreat possibilities

If your dynamics have spatial expression, you'll need to derive spatial structure at Level 5. If they don't, space can be minimal or abstract.

Example: A card game about political maneuvering might have no spatial dynamics—"space" is metaphorical (political positions, relationships). A dungeon crawler has essential spatial dynamics—exploration, positioning, and retreat all require physical space with properties.

System Functions as Dynamic Requirements

Chapter 1 introduced six system functions that every game must fulfill. At Level 2, these become questions about how dynamics will be supported:

Adjudication: How will players know what succeeded or failed? Challenge dynamics require clear success/failure signals. Narrative dynamics may need ambiguous outcomes. What adjudication style serves your aesthetic?

Information Management: What will players know, and when? Discovery dynamics require hidden information revealed over time. Tension dynamics require threat visibility. Mastery dynamics require learnable patterns. What information architecture serves your aesthetic?

Content Generation: What fills the possibility space? Challenge dynamics may need procedural variety. Narrative dynamics may need authored content. What content approach serves your aesthetic?

State Persistence: What matters across time? Narrative dynamics require consequence persistence. Some Challenge dynamics require fresh starts. What should the game remember?

Feedback: How will the game communicate? Challenge dynamics need immediate, clear feedback. Submission dynamics need gentle, ambient feedback. Tension dynamics need feedback that builds dread. What emotional register serves your aesthetic?

Cognitive Framing: How will players understand what they're doing? Complex aesthetics need strong mental model support. Simple aesthetics need minimal explanation. What understanding serves your aesthetic?

These questions don't get fully answered at Level 2—that happens at Levels 5 and 6. But asking them now ensures you're thinking about dynamics holistically, not just "what players do" but "how they'll perceive what they do."

Decision Forcing

For dynamics that require player decisions, consider how the design ensures decisions actually happen. Indefinite stalling undermines most aesthetics.

Solutions include:

  • Time pressure (countdown, real-time elements)
  • Escalating consequences (situation worsens while deciding)
  • Resource depletion (can't wait forever)
  • Mechanical forcing functions (must choose to proceed)
  • Social pressure (other players waiting)

Outputs from Level 2:

  • Core loop behaviors (what players do repeatedly)
  • Peak moment dynamics (what creates memorable highlights)
  • Progression dynamics (how experience develops over time)
  • Anti-dynamics (what must be prevented)
  • Spatial requirements (if any)
  • System function requirements (how each function serves the aesthetic)
  • Decision forcing approach (if needed)

Constraints imposed:

  • Temporal structure must support required dynamics
  • Mechanics must enable required behaviors
  • Presentation must support required perception
  • Parameters must tune for target feelings

For deeper treatment: Chapter 6 provides detailed aesthetic-dynamic mappings for all core aesthetics, including specific behavioral indicators that reveal whether dynamics are occurring.

Level 3: Temporal Architecture

Temporal architecture defines the rhythm and duration of play experiences.

Questions answered:

  • How long is a complete play session?
  • What loops exist within that session? (Micro, meso, macro)
  • How do loops relate to each other?
  • Where are natural stopping points?
  • How does pacing create the intended experience?

Inputs required:

  • Required dynamics (Level 2)
  • Player profile temporal constraints (from Level 1)
  • Medium constraints (from Level 0)

Deriving temporal structure from dynamics:

If the core dynamic requires "attempt, fail, learn, retry," loops must be short enough for multiple iterations per session. A learning dynamic with 30-minute loops means only 2-4 attempts in a casual session—probably too few for satisfying skill development.

If peak moments require buildup, the structure needs escalation patterns with appropriate duration. Climax without buildup falls flat; buildup without climax frustrates.

If the player profile is casual mobile, sessions are 5-15 minutes with potential interruption. Loops must complete within this window. Stopping points must allow clean exit without significant loss.

Outputs:

  • Session duration targets
  • Loop structure with specific durations
  • Pacing pattern (escalating, steady-state, episodic, wave, etc.)
  • Stopping point locations
  • Content scale requirements (derived from loops × duration)

Content scale derivation:

Temporal architecture determines content volume. Calculate:

  • Loops per session × sessions before content exhaustion = minimum unique content pieces
  • Loop duration × consumption rate = content per loop

Example: A 7-minute run with 4 floors, 4 encounters per floor, 3-5 enemy types per encounter. Player should see variety but also recognize enemies for pattern learning. 6-8 enemy types provides ~40 encounters before full rotation, allowing learning without exhaustion.

Constraints imposed:

  • Mechanics must fit within loop durations
  • Presentation must support pacing (feedback timing, information reveal rate)
  • Parameters must tune pacing correctly
  • Content must fill temporal structure without premature exhaustion

For deeper treatment: Chapter 5 covers temporal architecture in detail, including loop types, pacing patterns, stopping point design, and how temporal structure creates different aesthetic experiences.

Level 4: Balance Paradigm

The balance paradigm determines how the game maintains appropriate challenge, fairness, and engagement.

Questions answered:

  • How is difficulty managed?
  • How much player customization is supported?
  • Who or what makes judgment calls?
  • How does the system handle edge cases?

Inputs required:

  • Temporal architecture (Level 3)
  • Target aesthetic (Level 1)
  • Medium (Level 0)

The three paradigms:

Consistency Paradigm: Fixed rules, deterministic outcomes, player skill as variable. Appropriate when mastery and pattern learning are core dynamics. The system behaves identically regardless of player state—improvement comes from player adaptation.

Framework Paradigm: Rules provide structure and boundaries, but human judgment determines specific outcomes. Appropriate when narrative flexibility, social dynamics, or moral complexity are core dynamics. A GM, group consensus, or interpretive process handles what rules cannot specify.

Adaptive Paradigm: System adjusts to player state, maintaining target experience regardless of skill level. Appropriate when consistent emotional arc matters more than skill measurement. The system gets easier when you struggle, harder when you dominate.

Hybrid approaches are common. A game might use Consistency for simulation (fire always burns the same way) while using Adaptive for pacing (the storyteller modulates when threats arrive). The key is knowing which paradigm governs which aspect of the design.

Framework paradigm requires mechanical scaffolding:

Framework doesn't mean "no mechanics." It means mechanics serve judgment rather than replace it.

A Framework paradigm TTRPG still needs:

  • Resolution mechanics for uncertainty (when dice roll vs. GM decision)
  • Resource mechanics for pacing (tracking attrition, stakes, progression)
  • Character mechanics for differentiation (what makes each player's role distinct)
  • Clear triggers defining when mechanics apply vs. when judgment applies

The mechanics scaffold the GM's judgment rather than replacing it. "Roll to see if you can reach the door in time" uses mechanics. "Do you sacrifice the prisoner?" does not—that's pure decision.

[LLM TAG: Framework paradigm has traditionally required human GMs for real-time judgment. LLMs potentially enable Framework-style adaptive adjudication in solo digital games—responding to edge cases, maintaining narrative coherence, and making judgment calls that pure rule systems cannot.]

Archetype implications:

Choosing an archetype (Level 0) often commits you to paradigm expectations. Roguelikes assume Consistency — pattern learning requires predictable enemies. TTRPGs typically assume Framework — GMs adjudicate edge cases. Euro-games assume Consistency — optimization requires calculable outcomes.

Match paradigm to archetype expectations, or deliberately subvert them with clear understanding of what you're changing.

Outputs:

  • Selected paradigm with rationale
  • Edge case handling approach
  • Player expectation management strategy
  • For Framework: specific triggers for mechanics vs. judgment
  • For Adaptive: what the system observes and how it responds
  • For Hybrid: clear boundaries between paradigms

Constraints imposed:

  • Mechanics must implement chosen paradigm
  • Presentation must support paradigm (Consistency needs clear rules communication; Framework needs judgment context; Adaptive may need to hide its hand)
  • Parameters must support paradigm (Consistency needs tight tuning; Adaptive needs state detection and response curves)

For deeper treatment: Chapter 6 provides extensive coverage of all three paradigms, including when each serves specific aesthetics, how to implement them, and common failure modes.

Level 5: Mechanical Specification

Before committing to specific mechanics, explore the space. Then specify.

Level 5a: Mechanical Alternatives

Given your paradigm, what different mechanical approaches could serve your aesthetic?

Process:

  1. Sketch 2-3 different mechanical approaches
  2. Evaluate each against Level 1 (Aesthetic) and Level 2 (Dynamics)
  3. Consider Level 0 constraints (scope, team size, content requirements)
  4. Choose and justify

Why this matters: Different mechanics can serve the same aesthetics. A game about "emergent stories from colony survival" could use:

  • Fixed crew with escape goal (attachment through pre-defined characters)
  • Random colonists with sandbox play (attachment through emergent relationships)
  • Alien discovery focus (attachment split between characters and world)

Each serves the aesthetic differently. Exploring alternatives prevents premature commitment and reveals the design space you're working in.

Level 5b: Mechanical Specification

Now specify concrete systems.

Questions answered:

  • What are the core mechanics?
  • What resources exist and what functions do they serve?
  • How is content generated or structured?
  • What is the player's role and agency?
  • If spatial dynamics exist, what is the spatial structure?

Inputs required:

  • Balance paradigm (Level 4)
  • Temporal architecture (Level 3)
  • Required dynamics (Level 2)
  • Cognitive budget (from Level 1)

Deriving mechanics from dynamics:

Each required dynamic suggests mechanical implementations. Challenge dynamics need skill-testing mechanics. Tension dynamics need risk-reward mechanics. Narrative dynamics need choice-consequence mechanics. Discovery dynamics need information-revealing mechanics.

The cognitive budget constrains complexity. A casual game with 3-element budget can't support a 6-resource economy. Either reduce resources or automate some management.

Resource specification:

Resources shape possibility space (see Chapter 3). Specify:

  • What resources exist
  • What function each serves (Limiter, Currency, Tracker, Meta-resource)
  • Recovery patterns (fast/slow, predictable/variable)
  • How resources interact

Threshold-based state trackers: Some resources track internal state rather than external game state. These often have threshold effects rather than simple depletion—different mechanical effects at different levels. A "Humanity" resource might impose -1 to rolls at low levels and trigger a breaking point scene at zero. A "Heat" resource might increase enemy awareness at thresholds. These pacing resources shape emotional arc alongside mechanical arc.

Content specification:

Content type (procedural, authored, hybrid) must serve dynamics and fit production constraints.

  • Procedural: Enables variety and replayability; requires robust generation systems; risks incoherence
  • Authored: Enables precision and narrative control; requires production capacity; risks exhaustion
  • Hybrid: Procedural structure with authored key moments; balances variety with quality

Specify content categories needed:

  • Enemies/obstacles/challenges (how many, what variety)
  • Environments/settings/locations (how many, what variety)
  • Items/upgrades/options (how many, what variety)
  • Narrative content if applicable (word count, branch structure)
  • Audio/visual assets (scope estimate)

Spatial structure (if required by Level 2):

If your dynamics have spatial expression, specify:

  • Discrete or continuous space?
  • Abstract or representational?
  • Fixed or procedural layout?
  • What spatial properties matter? (Cover, height, distance, visibility)
  • How does space change over time?

Safety mechanics (for applicable aesthetics):

Games targeting Tension, Horror, or emotionally challenging Narrative may require safety mechanics as design elements:

  • Consent frameworks (what players agree to before play)
  • Opt-out mechanisms (X-card, lines and veils)
  • Debrief structures (processing after intense content)
  • Break points in temporal structure (breathing room)

These affect temporal architecture (breaks must exist), content (alternatives for intense material), and presentation (how intensity is communicated).

Outputs:

  • Complete mechanical specification
  • Resource system design
  • Content requirements with categories and quantities
  • Spatial structure (if applicable)
  • Safety mechanics (if applicable)
  • Justification tracing mechanics to dynamics to aesthetics

Constraints imposed:

  • Presentation must communicate these mechanics clearly
  • Parameters must tune these specific systems
  • Content must fill these specific categories

For deeper treatment: Chapter 3 covers resource design in detail—functions, recovery patterns, and how resources create meaning through scarcity. Chapter 4 provides the cognitive load framework for evaluating whether mechanics fit within budget.

Level 6: Presentation Architecture

How the game communicates is as designable as what the game does.

This level addresses a critical insight: the same mechanics can produce radically different player experiences based on how they're presented. A colony simulation with full information transparency creates a different game than the same simulation with limited visibility. A combat system with immediate damage numbers creates a different experience than one with delayed, ambiguous feedback.

UI is the game. Presentation architecture isn't polish applied after design — it's a design layer as substantial as mechanics themselves. Decisions made here can shift your viable audience, change which dynamics actually emerge, and determine whether your aesthetic goals are achieved.

Questions answered:

  • How does the game communicate outcomes to players? (Adjudication presentation)
  • What do players know, and when do they learn it? (Information management)
  • What content fills the systems, and how is it delivered? (Content generation details)
  • What persists across play, and how is persistence communicated? (State persistence)
  • How does the game signal what's happening? (Feedback systems)
  • How do players learn and understand the game? (Cognitive framing)

Inputs required:

  • Mechanical specification (Level 5)
  • Required dynamics, including system function requirements (Level 2)
  • Player profile and cognitive budget (Level 1)
  • Target aesthetic (Level 1)

The Six System Functions as Design Decisions

At Level 2, you identified how each system function should serve your aesthetic. Now you specify how each function is actually implemented.

1. Adjudication: How Outcomes Are Determined and Communicated

Adjudication covers both the rules that determine outcomes and how those determinations are communicated to players.

Specify:

  • Success/failure mechanics (dice, skill checks, deterministic calculation)
  • Probability transparency (visible percentages vs. hidden rolls vs. felt odds)
  • Outcome communication (immediate vs. delayed, precise vs. ambiguous)
  • Edge case handling (what happens in unusual situations)

Design space examples:

Transparent adjudication: Player sees "78% hit chance," rolls occur visibly, outcome is immediate and clear. Serves Challenge and Mastery—players can learn and optimize.

Opaque adjudication: Player acts, outcome emerges without visible calculation. "You swing. The enemy staggers back, wounded." Serves Narrative and Tension—focus on fiction, not numbers.

Delayed adjudication: Action occurs now, outcome revealed later. Serves Tension and Discovery—uncertainty persists, anticipation builds.

2. Information Management: What Players Know and When

Information architecture dramatically affects experience. The same mechanical state can feel completely different based on visibility.

Specify:

  • What information is always visible?
  • What information is hidden and how is it revealed?
  • What information is available on request vs. pushed to player?
  • How is past information retained? (Logs, history, memory aids)
  • What warnings exist for impending problems?

Design space examples:

Full transparency: Player sees colonist moods, needs, skills, relationships, threats. High information load, enables optimization, supports Mastery.

Selective transparency: Player sees some information (health, basic stats) but not internal states (exact mood, hidden relationships). Creates discovery within familiar systems.

Minimal transparency (roguelike-style): Player sees own state, enemies visible only when encountered, world revealed through exploration. Supports Discovery, creates tension through unknown.

Unreliable information: What players see isn't always accurate. Characters lie, perceptions are filtered. Serves Narrative and Discovery—truth must be earned.

The information budget: More information isn't always better. Information costs attention. A casual player drowning in data has worse experience than one with curated, relevant information. Match information density to player profile and cognitive budget.

3. Content Generation: What Fills the Systems

Level 5 specified content categories and quantities. Level 6 specifies how that content is delivered and experienced.

Specify:

  • Event/encounter pools (what can happen, relative frequency)
  • Generation rules (how procedural content is created)
  • Gating conditions (what unlocks content, what restricts it)
  • Pacing of content reveal (how quickly players see everything)
  • Content taxonomy (categories, rarities, relationships)

Design space examples:

Dense, frontloaded content: Players see most content types quickly, mastery comes from understanding interactions. Serves Challenge—quick learning, deep optimization.

Sparse, gated content: Content unlocks over time, maintaining novelty. Serves Discovery and Progression—always something new to find.

Responsive content: What appears depends on player state or choices. Serves Narrative and Adaptive paradigm—content feels personalized.

4. State Persistence: What the Game Remembers

What persists across time affects what matters. Perfect memory of every action creates different experience than selective memory or deliberate forgetting.

Specify:

  • What persists within a session?
  • What persists across sessions?
  • What resets and when?
  • How is persistence communicated to players?
  • What save/load systems exist?

Design space examples:

Total persistence: Every action, every consequence, every relationship—forever. Serves Narrative—choices have permanent weight.

Selective persistence: Some things persist (character growth, unlocks), others reset (world state, resources). Serves Progression + Challenge—meta-advancement with fresh tactical situations.

Fading persistence: Information decays over time. Old events become vague. Serves realism and prevents perfect optimization.

No persistence (roguelike): Each run is fresh. Serves Challenge—no accumulated advantage, pure skill expression.

5. Feedback: How the Game Communicates

Feedback is how the game signals what's happening, what matters, and what to pay attention to.

Specify:

  • Visual feedback (effects, animations, UI changes)
  • Audio feedback (sounds, music shifts, voice)
  • Haptic feedback (controller vibration, physical components)
  • Timing of feedback (immediate, delayed, accumulated)
  • Intensity of feedback (subtle vs. dramatic)
  • Emotional register (celebratory, ominous, neutral, urgent)

Design space examples:

Immediate, clear feedback: Action → instant visual/audio response → clear outcome. Serves Challenge—tight action-response loop, supports learning.

Ambient, gradual feedback: Changes emerge slowly, mood shifts subtly. Serves Submission and Narrative—immersion without interruption.

Escalating feedback: Intensity builds as stakes rise. Music intensifies, effects become more dramatic. Serves Tension—feedback creates the feeling.

Delayed feedback: Action now, consequence later. Plant seeds, harvest seasons later. Serves Narrative and Discovery—anticipation and payoff.

Feedback as emotional guidance: Players often feel what the game tells them to feel. Triumphant music after victory reinforces triumph. Ominous ambient sound creates dread independent of mechanical danger. Feedback design is emotional design.

6. Cognitive Framing: How Players Learn and Understand

How players build mental models of your game determines whether they can engage with it effectively.

Specify:

  • Tutorial approach (guided, discovery, hybrid, none)
  • UI hierarchy (what's emphasized, what's hidden)
  • Progressive disclosure (what's introduced when)
  • Mental model support (tooltips, help systems, documentation)
  • Metaphor and vocabulary (how the game explains itself)

Design space examples:

Guided framing: Explicit tutorial, clear UI hierarchy, comprehensive tooltips. Accessible, lower barrier. Serves casual players and complex systems.

Discovery framing: Minimal explanation, players learn through experimentation. Higher barrier, more satisfying mastery. Serves Discovery aesthetic and hardcore players.

Layered framing: Simple surface, depth revealed over time. New players engage at one level, experienced players find more. Serves broad audience range.

Metaphor-based framing: Game teaches through familiar concepts. "This works like cooking recipes" or "Think of it like managing a sports team." Reduces cognitive load by leveraging existing knowledge.

Coherence Check

After specifying presentation architecture, verify coherence with Level 1.

Ask:

  • Does cognitive load (mechanics + presentation) match target player profile?
  • Does information architecture support required dynamics?
  • Does feedback style serve aesthetic goals?
  • Has the viable audience shifted from Level 1 intentions?

If coherence fails:

Either revise Level 6 choices to serve original audience, OR acknowledge audience shift and update Level 1.

Example: A colony sim designed for midcore players might, through presentation architecture choices, become hardcore-only (too much information, too little guidance). This isn't necessarily wrong—but it must be acknowledged. Don't design for one audience at Level 1 and a different audience at Level 6.

Outputs:

  • Complete presentation architecture specification
  • For each system function: how it's implemented, how it serves the aesthetic
  • Coherence verification with Level 1
  • Any audience shift acknowledged and justified

Constraints imposed:

  • Parameters must work within this presentation architecture
  • Content must be presentable through specified systems
  • Cognitive load must remain within budget

For deeper treatment: Chapter 2 introduces the six system functions. Chapter 4 covers cognitive load implications of presentation choices.

Level 7: Parameters

Parameters are the specific numbers that tune the mechanical and presentation systems. But numbers don't emerge from intuition—they derive from measurable targets that validate whether dynamics are actually occurring.

Questions answered:

  • What are the specific values for each variable?
  • How do these values produce intended experience?
  • What measurables validate the parameters?

Inputs required:

  • Presentation architecture (Level 6)
  • Mechanical specification (Level 5)
  • Target dynamics with specific intensities (Level 2)
  • Temporal targets (Level 3)

The measurables bridge:

Between dynamics and parameters sits a critical intermediate step: measurables. A measurable is a quantifiable indicator that a dynamic is occurring.

  • Dynamic: "Pattern learning" (for Challenge aesthetic)
  • Measurable: "Player's time-to-kill improves 40% from attempt 1 to attempt 10"
  • Parameter: Boss HP, player damage, attack windows (derived to produce that improvement curve)

Without measurables, you're guessing what numbers might feel right. With measurables, you're solving for specific targets.

Measurable examples by dynamic:

For pattern learning (Challenge): TTK improvement across attempts (40% over 10 tries), prediction accuracy (70%+ by attempt 5), expert-novice performance gap (expert 50% faster than trained player).

For resource pressure (Tension): Scarcity maintenance (resources stay 30-50% of max), close call frequency (2-3 near-deaths per 10 minutes), decision frequency (5+ meaningful trade-offs per session).

For system optimization (Mastery): Strategy diversity (5+ viable builds within 20% power), optimization ceiling (expert strategies 30% more efficient than casual optimal), experimentation rate (players test multiple approaches).

For story emergence (Narrative): Event frequency (2-3 significant events per session), consequence visibility (70%+ of choices have observable results), character differentiation (each character has distinct memorable moments).

Deriving parameters from measurables:

Start with target experience, express as measurable, derive parameter from measurable.

Example chain:

  • Target: "Encounters feel tense but winnable" (experience)
  • Measurable: "Player wins 70% of encounters, feels at risk in 80%" (testable)
  • Parameter: Enemy damage = player HP ÷ 4, so player survives ~4 hits (derived)

Work backward from experience to number, not forward from arbitrary number to hoped-for experience.

Temporal integration:

Measurables must align with temporal architecture. If your meso loop is 3 minutes, TTK targets should fit within that window. If your player profile is casual with 15-minute sessions, mastery metrics should show improvement within 2-3 sessions, not 10-20.

Content parameters:

Content volume derives from temporal architecture and presentation architecture:

  • Enemy types: enough for variety across expected plays before exhaustion
  • Asset counts: sprites, sounds, words based on content type requirements
  • Upgrade/item counts: enough for meaningful choice without overwhelming
  • Information density: appropriate for presentation architecture choices

Parameter relationships:

Parameters interact. Changing one often requires adjusting others. Document relationships:

  • If enemy HP increases, encounter duration increases, loop timing changes
  • If resource recovery slows, pressure increases, cognitive load increases
  • If content volume decreases, repetition increases, engagement may decrease
  • If feedback delay increases, learning speed decreases

Outputs:

  • Complete parameter specification
  • Rationale connecting each parameter to experience target
  • Measurable targets for validation
  • Identified parameter relationships and dependencies

For deeper treatment: Chapter 6 provides the complete framework for deriving parameters from measurables, including worked examples for each balance paradigm, specific measurable targets for each aesthetic-dynamic pair, and mathematical derivation processes.

Part 4: Validation Process

Parts 2-3 described how to derive a design. But a derived design is only a hypothesis—a prediction about what will create the intended experience. The validation process tests that hypothesis.

Coherence Audit

Before testing with players, audit the design for internal coherence.

The configuration coherence test: For each level, verify that outputs serve the levels above and constrain the levels below appropriately.

  • Does context (Level 0) actually enable the target aesthetic (Level 1)?
  • Do dynamics (Level 2) actually create the stated aesthetic?
  • Does temporal structure (Level 3) support required dynamics?
  • Does paradigm (Level 4) serve the target aesthetic?
  • Do mechanics (Level 5) enable required dynamics?
  • Does presentation architecture (Level 6) communicate mechanics clearly and serve the aesthetic?
  • Do parameters (Level 7) produce intended experience (on paper)?

Common coherence failures:

  • Mechanics that don't support stated dynamics
  • Presentation that obscures mechanics players need to understand
  • Temporal structure mismatched to player profile
  • Parameters that mathematically can't produce target experience
  • Cognitive load exceeding stated budget
  • Audience shift between Level 1 and Level 6 not acknowledged

Prediction

Make hypotheses explicit before testing. "Players will feel tension during resource decisions" is testable. "The game will be fun" is not.

Strong predictions specify:

  • What will happen (behavior or response)
  • Under what conditions
  • How you'll observe it

Write predictions down. Vague predictions are unfalsifiable and teach nothing. Good predictions include:

  • "New players will complete tutorial floor in under 3 minutes"
  • "Players will express frustration at food shortage at least once per session"
  • "70% of players will correctly predict enemy attack pattern by third encounter"
  • "Players will mention specific colonist names when describing their session"

Testing

Testing validates whether the design produces intended experience.

Test by chain level:

LevelWhat to TestMethods
AestheticDoes target emotion emerge?Post-session interviews, observation
DynamicsDo intended behaviors occur?Behavioral observation, think-aloud
TemporalDoes pacing feel right?Timed observation, session logs
ParadigmDoes balance approach work?Outcome tracking, fairness perception
MechanicsDo systems function?Exploit testing, edge cases
PresentationDo players understand?Comprehension testing, confusion observation
ParametersAre numbers tuned?Measurable tracking, A/B testing

Testing formats:

  • Solo testing: Designer plays own game. Limited for aesthetic validation but useful for mechanical function.
  • Internal testing: Team or friends. Stress-tests mechanics, limited for target audience validation.
  • External testing: Target audience. Actual validation of experience. Limited iteration speed.
  • Blind testing: No designer present. Validates self-teaching and standalone function.

Iteration Protocol

When testing reveals problems, diagnose before adjusting.

Start at the lowest level that can solve the problem:

  1. Level 7 (Parameters): Can number adjustments fix it?

    • Symptoms: Too easy, too hard, wrong pacing, insufficient tension
    • Cost: Hours to days

  2. Level 6 (Presentation): Can communication/feedback changes fix it?

    • Symptoms: Confusion, missed information, wrong emotional tone, overwhelming
    • Cost: Days to weeks

  3. Level 5 (Mechanics): Do systems need redesign?

    • Symptoms: Exploits, degenerate strategies, missing dynamics, wrong interactions
    • Cost: Weeks

  4. Level 4 (Paradigm): Is balance approach wrong?

    • Symptoms: Feels unfair, too predictable, wrong kind of challenge
    • Cost: Weeks to months

  5. Level 3 (Temporal): Is pacing structure wrong?

    • Symptoms: Sessions too long/short, wrong rhythm, poor stopping points
    • Cost: Weeks to months

  6. Level 2 (Dynamics): Are we targeting wrong behaviors?

    • Symptoms: Right systems, wrong experience
    • Cost: Major restructure

  7. Level 1 (Aesthetic): Are we targeting wrong experience?

    • Symptoms: Everything works but it's not what we wanted
    • Cost: Project pivot

Principle: Higher levels are more expensive to change. Don't rebuild mechanics when parameter adjustments would suffice. But don't endlessly tweak parameters when the mechanics are fundamentally wrong.

The Six Functions as Diagnostic Lens

When dynamics don't emerge as intended, check which system function is failing:

  • Adjudication unclear → Players don't know what succeeded or failed
  • Information management broken → Players lack needed information or drown in excess
  • Content generation insufficient → Repetition, exhaustion, predictability
  • State persistence confusing → Players lose track of what matters, what persists
  • Feedback missing or wrong → Actions don't feel connected to outcomes, wrong emotional tone
  • Cognitive framing wrong → Players don't understand what they're doing or why

Documentation for Iteration

Track iterations systematically:

  • Hypothesis: What we thought would happen
  • Observation: What actually happened
  • Diagnosis: What level is the problem?
  • Adjustment: What we changed
  • Result: Did it work?

This creates institutional memory. Don't solve the same problem twice. Don't revert changes that were made for good reasons.

When to Stop Iterating

Iteration could continue forever. Stop when:

  • Measurable targets are consistently met
  • Playtesters report target aesthetic experience
  • Remaining issues are at acceptable tolerance
  • Further iteration has diminishing returns
  • Scope or schedule requires shipping

Perfect is the enemy of good. A coherent design that delivers 80% of intended experience ships. An infinitely iterated design that never ships delivers nothing.

Part 5: Multiplayer Considerations

The derivation chain works for multiplayer games, but designing for multiple players introduces considerations at every level. This section addresses what changes when your game involves two or more people playing together.

A note on scope: multiplayer design is deep enough to warrant its own book. This section provides a survey—enough to use the derivation chain for multiplayer games, with awareness of where the complexity lies.

When This Section Applies

Not every game with multiple players requires multiplayer design thinking. The test: do players meaningfully affect each other's experience?

In parallel solitaire, players engage with the same system but don't interact. Each player could play separately and compare scores afterward. Multiplayer is social wrapper, not structural. The single-player derivation chain applies; you're designing the solo experience that happens to occur alongside others.

In true multiplayer, players interact in ways that change outcomes. Competition, cooperation, communication, or interference. What one player does affects what other players experience. Here, multiplayer considerations are load-bearing.

Multiplayer Modifications by Level

Level 0: Context

Multiplayer affects medium and platform constraints. Local multiplayer requires shared screen or components. Online multiplayer requires networking infrastructure. Async multiplayer requires state persistence across time.

Player count becomes a context constraint. Two-player games have different design space than party games than MMOs.

Level 1: Aesthetic Profile

Multiplayer enables aesthetics that single-player cannot:

Fellowship requires other people. The experience of teamwork, shared triumph, collective struggle—these need real humans, not AI substitutes.

Competition against humans differs from AI competition. Human opponents adapt, surprise, and carry social stakes.

Social context becomes load-bearing. Who are these players to each other? Friends at a table, strangers in matchmaking, long-term guildmates? The relationship affects what dynamics are possible.

Level 2: Required Dynamics

Multiplayer introduces dynamics that don't exist in single-player:

  • Communication dynamics: Players conveying information, intentions, coordination
  • Coordination dynamics: Players acting together toward shared goals
  • Competition dynamics: Players opposing each other
  • Social deduction dynamics: Hidden information, deception, trust

Multiplayer also introduces anti-dynamics:

  • Toxicity: Hostile communication damaging experience
  • Quarterbacking: One player dominating group decisions
  • Kingmaking: Eliminated players determining winners
  • Collusion: Cooperation breaking competitive assumptions

Spatial dynamics in multiplayer often involve shared space, territory control, or spatial coordination. "Where are my allies?" becomes a question alongside "where am I?"

Level 3: Temporal Architecture

Multiplayer significantly complicates temporal design.

Turn structure: Simultaneous play eliminates downtime but limits reaction. Sequential play enables response but creates waiting.

Downtime: When it's not your turn, what do you do? Extended downtime kills engagement. This is the most common multiplayer design failure.

Session coordination: Getting multiple people together is harder than playing solo. This constrains viable session lengths.

Game length scaling: More players often means longer games. Design must account for this.

Level 4: Balance Paradigm

Consistency paradigm requires fairness between players. Symmetric games give everyone equal tools. Asymmetric games must achieve balance across different capabilities.

Framework paradigm can use other players for adjudication—voting, negotiation, social pressure.

Adaptive paradigm becomes complicated. Adapting for one player may disadvantage others.

Level 5: Mechanical Specification

Multiplayer requires mechanics that don't exist in single-player:

  • Communication mechanics: How players share information
  • Role differentiation: Why each player matters
  • Catch-up mechanics: How losing players stay engaged
  • Anti-quarterbacking measures: Preventing dominant players

Level 6: Presentation Architecture

Multiplayer presentation must serve multiple players:

  • Shared vs. private information: What everyone sees vs. what's hidden per player
  • Communication channels: Voice, text, pings, visible actions
  • Social feedback: How player actions affect others' experience
  • Spectator considerations: If applicable, how non-players observe

Level 7: Parameters

  • Turn length limits prevent excessive downtime
  • Player count scaling adjusts resources and pacing
  • Catch-up tuning determines comeback potential
  • Snowball prevention limits how early advantages compound

Competitive vs. Cooperative

Competitive priorities:

  • Fairness (perceived balance)
  • Meaningful skill expression
  • Catch-up/snowball tuning
  • Kingmaking prevention
  • Matchmaking support

Cooperative priorities:

  • Distributed purpose (every player has unique value)
  • Quarterbacking prevention
  • Shared stakes
  • Difficulty scaling for different groups
  • Communication design

Mixed modes (teams, traitors, shifting alliances) combine both sets of concerns.

The Downtime Problem

When players wait with nothing meaningful to do, engagement collapses.

Causes:

  • Sequential turns with slow players
  • Elimination removing players from play
  • Resolution phases involving only some players

Solutions:

  • Simultaneous play
  • Fast turns
  • Engagement during others' turns
  • Reducing player count
  • Using downtime for social interaction

Limitations

This section surveys multiplayer considerations well enough to apply the derivation chain. It doesn't deeply solve the problems it raises. For multiplayer designs, expect to spend significant effort on problems this section only names. Playtest multiplayer dynamics heavily—humans interacting create emergence no designer fully anticipates.

Part 6: Worked Examples

Theory becomes concrete through application. This section walks through complete derivations for two games: a casual mobile roguelike and a TTRPG one-shot. They differ in medium, paradigm, content approach, and target audience, yet both follow the same chain.

Worked Example 1: Casual Mobile Roguelike

Starting point: Aesthetic-first with constraints. A roguelike-inspired mobile game for casual players. Light humor, pixel art. Something for commutes.

Immediate tension: Roguelikes are typically midcore/hardcore. Casual roguelike is deliberate subversion requiring careful derivation.

Level 0: Context

ElementChoiceImplication
MediumVideo game (mobile)Touch input, portrait orientation, interruptions
PlatformiOS/AndroidSessions 5-15 minutes, processing limited
ScopeSmall team, 6-12 monthsLimited content production capacity
SettingLight fantasy/humorSilly enemies, funny deaths, not grim
ArchetypeRoguelike (modified)Run-based, procedural, but reduced punishment

Setting as derivation accelerator: "Light humor" immediately suggests failure should be amusing, enemies should be silly, tone should never feel punishing. This affects every subsequent level.

Level 1: Aesthetic Profile

Primary aesthetic: Challenge (earned triumph through skill development)

Secondary aesthetic: Progression (meta-unlocks for return motivation)

Player profile: Casual mobile

  • 5-15 minute sessions
  • Cognitive budget of 3±2 tracked elements
  • Low complexity tolerance
  • Expect interruption

Success criteria:

  • "I did it!" moments at least once per session
  • Noticeable improvement over first 5-10 runs
  • Voluntary return (not FOMO-driven)
  • Clean exit possible at any moment

Level 2: Required Dynamics

Core loop: Attempt → fail/succeed → learn → retry. Compressed: learning in 3-5 tries, not 50.

Peak moments: Victory over previously-lethal enemy, run completion, new personal best. Must occur every session.

Anti-dynamics: Grind, confusion, frustration, complexity overwhelm.

Humor dynamic: Death produces chuckle, not groan. Enemies are silly. Failure text is funny.

Spatial dynamics: Minimal. Positioning matters in encounters, but no complex exploration or territory. Space is functional, not primary.

System function requirements:

  • Adjudication: Clear, immediate—player knows why they died
  • Information: Simple, visible—health hearts, enemy telegraphs
  • Content: Procedural but learnable—same enemies, different arrangements
  • Persistence: Meta-progress persists, run state resets
  • Feedback: Punchy, satisfying—juice on hits, funny death animations
  • Framing: Minimal tutorial, intuitive through play

Cognitive check: 3±2 budget = health, attack capability, enemy patterns. Resource management must be minimal or automated.

Level 3: Temporal Architecture

Run (macro): 5-8 minutes. Fits casual session, allows 1-2 complete runs per play.

Floor (meso): 90-120 seconds each, 3-4 floors per run. Each floor is completable unit.

Encounter (micro): 15-30 seconds. Fast iteration, pattern recognition.

Pacing: Escalating within run. Floor 1 easy, Floors 3-4 where most deaths occur.

Stopping points: Between floors (good), between runs (excellent), mid-encounter (acceptable with pause).

Content scale:

  • 6-8 enemy types
  • 3-4 floor tilesets
  • 10-15 upgrade options
  • 4 bosses

Level 4: Balance Paradigm

Paradigm: Consistency. Pattern learning requires deterministic enemies.

Why not Adaptive: Would undermine Challenge aesthetic. Earned triumph requires consistent obstacles.

Archetype inheritance: Roguelike gives procedural generation, run-based structure. Not inheriting: punishment severity. Light humor frames death as funny, not frustrating.

Level 5: Mechanical Specification

Alternatives considered:

  • A: Auto-runner with timing challenges (simpler, less variety)
  • B: Turn-based tactical (more strategic, slower)
  • C: Real-time action with positioning (immediate, intuitive)

Chosen: C—action with positioning. Touch-drag to move, auto-attack nearest enemy. Skill expression through positioning and dodge timing, not complex inputs.

Resources:

  1. Health (Limiter): 3 HP. One hit = one damage. Hearts display.
  2. Coins (Currency): Collected during run, spent between floors. Resets each run.
  3. Stars (Meta-resource): Earned at run-end, spent on permanent unlocks.

Three resources. Within budget.

Recovery: No health recovery within floor. Possible between floors (uncommon). Tension without attrition spiral.

Upgrades: Between floors, choose 1 of 3. Stat boosts or simple abilities. No complex synergies.

Content approach: Procedural floor layouts, hand-authored enemy patterns and bosses.

Spatial structure: Simple rectangular rooms, obstacles for cover, clear sightlines. Functional space for positioning, not exploration.

Level 6: Presentation Architecture

Adjudication: Immediate, visual. Hit lands → damage number → health heart disappears. Death → funny animation → witty text. Player always knows what killed them.

Information management:

  • Always visible: Health hearts, enemy count
  • On-demand: Upgrade descriptions (tap to read)
  • Not shown: Enemy HP (irrelevant for casual), detailed stats
  • Warning: Enemy telegraph (red flash 0.5 sec before attack)

Content delivery: Enemy types introduced one per floor. By Floor 4, all common types encountered. Bosses are surprises on first encounter, learnable patterns.

State persistence:

  • Within run: Coins, upgrades, floor progress
  • Across runs: Stars, unlocks, bestiary
  • Not persisted: Run state after death (full reset)

Feedback:

  • Hit enemy: Screen shake, particle burst, satisfying thump sound
  • Take damage: Screen flash red, heart breaking animation, oof sound
  • Enemy death: Comedic poof, coin scatter, silly death sound
  • Player death: Elaborate funny animation, random quip from enemy

Cognitive framing:

  • No explicit tutorial. Floor 1 is tutorial (one easy enemy type, simple layout).
  • UI minimal: hearts top-left, coins top-right, nothing else persistent
  • Upgrades self-explanatory ("More Damage: +1 damage per hit")

Coherence check: Casual player can track 3 things (health, enemy patterns, positioning). Presentation keeps information minimal. Feedback is clear and satisfying. ✓

Level 7: Parameters

Target measurables:

  • Run completion: ~20% initially, ~60% after 10 runs
  • Average run (new): 3-4 minutes
  • Average run (learned): 5-7 minutes
  • Deaths per session: 2-4
  • Triumph moments per session: 1-3

Combat parameters:

Encounter target: 20 seconds.

Player DPS: 1 damage per attack, attack every 2 seconds = 0.5 DPS. 3 enemies × 3 HP = 9 HP ÷ 0.5 DPS = 18 seconds. ✓

Enemy damage: 1 per hit. Player survives 3 hits.

Enemy attack rate: Floor 1: every 4 seconds, 15% hit rate (forgiving). Floor 4: every 2.5 seconds, 25% hit rate (challenging).

Floor timing: 4 encounters × 20 sec + 20 sec traversal = 100 sec/floor. 4 floors = 6.6 minutes. ✓

Boss: 15 HP, 3-phase pattern, learnable in 2-3 attempts.

Meta-progression:

  • 3 stars per completion, 1 for reaching boss
  • Unlock costs: 10-50 stars
  • Time to complete all: 2-4 weeks casual play

Content Derivation

Enemy count: 16 encounters/run, 3-5 encounters to learn each type. 8 common + 3 rare + 4 bosses = 15 total.

Sprites per enemy: 9-14 frames (idle, telegraph, attack, hit, death). Bosses: 25-30 frames.

Player character: 18-27 frames. Variants via palette swap.

Environment: 4 tilesets × 40-60 pieces = 160-240 pieces.

Total art budget: 450-600 pieces (with palette swaps).

Writing:

  • Enemy descriptions: 300-500 words
  • Death messages: 20-30 unique (humor-critical)
  • Upgrade text: 75-200 words
  • Boss intros: 80-160 words
  • Total: ~1,000-2,400 words

What This Derivation Shows

Each number traces through the chain:

  • 6.6-minute runs ← casual session windows ← player profile (Level 1)
  • 3 HP ← cognitive simplicity ← casual budget (Level 1)
  • 20-second encounters ← 90-second floors ← temporal architecture (Level 3)
  • Telegraph visibility ← pattern learning ← Challenge dynamics (Level 2)
  • Funny death feedback ← humor tone ← setting (Level 0)

The derivation produced implementable specifications: art budgets, word counts, frame requirements, combat math. These are starting points for production and playtesting—derived from experience goals, not arbitrary.

Worked Example 2: TTRPG One-Shot

Starting point: Theme-first. A GM wants to explore "the evil of ends justify the means" through a one-shot.

Key differences from roguelike: Framework paradigm, narrative-focused, minimal mechanical complexity, heavy content authoring, multiplayer, human adjudication.

Level 0: Context

MediumTTRPGHuman GM, social play, voice/gesture

Setting as derivation accelerator: "Village under supernatural threat that offers bargains" immediately suggests moral transactions, community stakes, isolation, escalating desperation. The Engine (fog entity) provides transactional pressure without being "evil"—it doesn't lie or negotiate, just offers terms.

Level 1: Aesthetic Profile

Theme → Aesthetic translation: "Ends justify the means" could serve multiple aesthetics. GM intent ("explore the evil") points to:

Primary aesthetic: Tension (weight of choices, stakes that matter)

Secondary aesthetic: Narrative (story of compromise, character arcs)

Excluded: Challenge (no optimization of morality), Discovery (not a mystery)

Player profile: Midcore TTRPG players

  • 3-4 hour tolerance
  • Comfortable with narrative
  • Consent to dark themes
  • 4 players + GM

Success criteria:

  • Genuine conflict during choices
  • No obvious "right answers"
  • Players discuss afterward
  • Emotional arc completes

Level 2: Required Dynamics

Core dynamic: Moral deliberation—genuine weighing, disagreement, painful commitment.

Peak moments:

  • Commitment ("We're doing this")
  • Consequence (learning the cost)
  • Breaking point (someone can't continue)

Progression: Escalation. Each choice harder than the last.

Anti-dynamics:

  • Optimization (finding loopholes)
  • Detachment (puzzle-solving morality)
  • Player conflict becoming personal
  • Wallowing (grimness without meaning)

Theme-specific dynamics:

  • Justification (hearing yourself rationalize)
  • Complicity (no clean hands in group decisions)

Spatial dynamics: Minimal. The village is setting, but space isn't load-bearing mechanically. Characters move between locations narratively, not tactically.

System function requirements:

  • Adjudication: GM judgment within framework—mechanics for uncertainty, pure decision for morality
  • Information: Players know stakes, consequences partially hidden (revealed after choice)
  • Content: Authored dilemmas with escalating difficulty
  • Persistence: Choices accumulate, Humanity erodes, consequences compound
  • Feedback: Slow, deliberate—consequences play out over scenes
  • Framing: Character creation establishes what matters (bonds, lines)

Decision forcing: Moral choices can't stall indefinitely. Mechanisms:

  • The Dimming advances (environmental pressure)
  • NPCs die while deliberating (named deaths)
  • The Engine's offers expire

Level 3: Temporal Architecture

Session: 3-4 hours including breaks and debrief.

Acts:

  • Act 1 (45-60 min): Establish stakes, first compromise (uncomfortable but justifiable)
  • Act 2 (90-120 min): Escalating compromises, consequences visible
  • Act 3 (45-60 min): Ultimate choice, aftermath

Scenes: Three types rotating:

  • Pressure (15-25 min): Present dilemma, force choice
  • Consequence (10-15 min): Show results
  • Breath (5-10 min): Character moments, humanity

Scene count: 11-12 total, ~15-20 minutes average.

Stopping points: Between acts. Mandatory breaks for safety check-in.

Pacing pattern: Escalating pressure with valleys. Rising action to crisis to resolution.

Level 4: Balance Paradigm

Paradigm: Framework with mechanical scaffolding.

Why Framework: Moral dilemmas can't have calculable optimal solutions. If players can solve dilemmas through mechanics, Tension collapses.

Why scaffolding: Pure GM fiat feels arbitrary. Players need mechanical grounding for investment.

Mechanical triggers:

  • Capability uncertainty → Roll (2d6 + trait)
  • Moral choice → Decision (no roll)
  • Consequences → GM adjudication within established logic

The mechanics scaffold judgment, not replace it.

Level 5: Mechanical Specification

Alternatives considered:

  • A: No mechanics (pure roleplay)—risks feeling arbitrary
  • B: Heavy mechanics (detailed simulation)—wrong for Narrative/Tension
  • C: Light mechanics with moral resource—supports arc without dominating

Chosen: C—minimal resolution plus Humanity tracker.

Level 6: Presentation Architecture

Adjudication:

  • Roll results: GM narrates outcome, player adds detail
  • Moral choices: No adjudication—consequences follow from fiction
  • Humanity loss: Announced clearly, threshold effects narrated

Information management:

  • Players know: Immediate stakes, Humanity levels, established consequences
  • Hidden: Long-term consequences of choices (revealed in later scenes)
  • Shared: All players hear all narration (no private information between players)

Content delivery:

  • Dilemmas introduced through scenes, not exposition
  • NPCs personalized before being endangered
  • Consequences shown, not told

State persistence:

  • Everything persists within session
  • Humanity losses are permanent until recovered
  • Consequences accumulate and compound
  • The fiction remembers what players did

Feedback:

  • Slow, deliberate pacing
  • Silence after hard choices
  • NPCs react to player decisions
  • Environment reflects consequences (fog advances, resources dwindle)

Cognitive framing:

  • Character creation IS the tutorial (creating Bond and Line explains what matters)
  • Humanity tracker is visible throughout
  • GM provides context, not solutions
  • "What do you do?" prompts focus on choice, not optimization

Coherence check: Midcore TTRPG players can handle Humanity tracking plus simple resolution. Presentation emphasizes narrative weight over mechanical complexity. ✓

  • Act 2: 8-12 total
  • Act 3: 4-6 total

NPC parameters: 6-8 significant NPCs, 2-3 per act. Each needs:

  • Name
  • Relationship to stakes
  • Position on dilemmas
  • Want from players

Scene timing:

  • Pressure scenes: 15-25 min (don't rush decisions)
  • Consequence scenes: 10-15 min (let impact land)
  • Breath scenes: 5-10 min (humanity before next blow)

Content Derivation

The Setting: The Dimming. Remote village, supernatural fog advancing. Ancient machine-entity (The Engine) offers salvation at escalating cost.

Why this setting: Contained (pressure cooker), clear stakes (survival), transactional force (Engine has no morality), personal connections (everyone knows everyone).

The Engine: Not evil, not good. Transactional. Doesn't lie or negotiate. States terms, fulfills contracts. No concept of cruelty because no concept of kindness. Players can't externalize blame.

Dilemma progression:

  1. Warmth: Price = church bells + labor (identity vs. survival)
  2. Food: Price = one person's memories (personhood vs. community)
  3. Healing: Price = one specific life, someone's Bond (one love vs. many lives)
  4. End the Dimming: Price = all children, transformed not killed (future vs. present)

Dilemma preparation (~200-400 words each):

  • Clear stakes
  • "Evil means" option
  • Rationalization argument available
  • Hidden cost (revealed later)
  • Alternative (harder, less certain)

NPC preparation (~50-100 words each):

  • Name and role
  • Stake in each dilemma
  • How they'll pressure players
  • What happens if they die

Total GM prep: ~1,500-3,000 words plus scene structure.

Part 7: Common Patterns

The worked examples showed complete derivations. This section provides sketches—starting points for common aesthetic targets. These aren't blueprints; they're illustrations of how different inputs produce different outputs through the same chain.

Challenge (Midcore)

Aesthetic target: Earned triumph through skill development.

Typical dynamics: Pattern learning, skill expression, failure recovery, escalating difficulty.

Temporal tendency: Short-to-medium loops (allowing multiple attempts), escalating pacing, clear stopping points.

Paradigm tendency: Consistency. Pattern learning requires deterministic systems.

Mechanical direction: Skill-testing mechanics, clear success/failure states, player execution matters.

Presentation direction: Immediate feedback, clear adjudication, visible information for learning.

Parameter focus: Difficulty curves, learning rates, time-to-mastery.

Discovery (Casual-Midcore)

Aesthetic target: Wonder at uncovering the unknown.

Typical dynamics: Exploration, revelation moments, theory formation, mystery unfolding.

Temporal tendency: Episodic or expanding loops, variable pacing driven by exploration choices.

Paradigm tendency: Consistency (for puzzle-like discovery) or Framework (for narrative discovery).

Mechanical direction: Information-revealing mechanics, gated access, optional depth.

Presentation direction: Hidden information revealed over time, mystery preserved, "aha!" moments supported.

[LLM TAG: Discovery games with procedural mystery generation represent significant LLM potential—generating coherent mysteries, planting fair clues, maintaining narrative consistency without exhaustive pre-authoring.]

Parameter focus: Revelation pacing, mystery depth, exploration rewards.

Tension (Midcore-Hardcore)

Aesthetic target: Anxiety from meaningful stakes.

Typical dynamics: Resource pressure, threat awareness, close calls, consequential decisions.

Temporal tendency: Escalating with valleys, building to crisis moments.

Paradigm tendency: Consistency (for survival) or Adaptive (for horror pacing) or Framework (for moral stakes).

Mechanical direction: Risk-reward mechanics, resource scarcity, irreversible choices.

Presentation direction: Threatening feedback, visible consequences, atmosphere building.

Parameter focus: Scarcity calibration, threat intensity, close-call frequency.

Mastery (Midcore-Hardcore)

Aesthetic target: Satisfaction from deep optimization.

Typical dynamics: System learning, strategy experimentation, efficiency pursuit, build crafting.

Temporal tendency: Extended loops, complex meso structure, long-term progression.

Paradigm tendency: Consistency. Optimization requires calculable systems.

Mechanical direction: Interconnected systems, multiple viable strategies, depth rewarding investment.

Presentation direction: Dense information for those who want it, system transparency, performance metrics.

Parameter focus: Strategy viability balance, optimization ceiling, complexity depth.

Submission (Casual)

Aesthetic target: Relaxation through low-stakes engagement.

Typical dynamics: Pleasant repetition, gentle progress, minimal pressure, aesthetic pleasure.

Temporal tendency: Steady-state pacing, no urgency, flexible sessions.

Paradigm tendency: Adaptive (maintaining comfort) or Consistency (predictable, safe).

Mechanical direction: Simple, satisfying interactions, no fail states, progress always possible.

Presentation direction: Calm feedback, pleasant aesthetics, no stress indicators.

Parameter focus: Engagement sustainability, satisfaction rate, pressure minimization.

Narrative (Variable)

Aesthetic target: Emotional engagement with story.

Typical dynamics: Character investment, agency expression, consequence experience, story progression.

Temporal tendency: Story-driven pacing, act structure, emotional beats.

Paradigm tendency: Framework (TTRPGs, choice-based) or Consistency (authored branches).

[LLM TAG: Narrative games are perhaps the most obvious LLM application—dynamic dialogue, responsive NPCs, branching narratives that don't require exhaustive pre-authoring. This shifts Framework paradigm from TTRPG-exclusive to potentially available in solo digital games.]

Mechanical direction: Choice mechanics, consequence systems, character/relationship tracking.

Presentation direction: Story delivery systems, emotional pacing, character communication.

Parameter focus: Choice weight, consequence visibility, pacing calibration.

Using These Patterns

These sketches are starting points. Your design will diverge based on:

  • Specific aesthetic emphasis: "Challenge" contains multitudes; your vision matters
  • Player profile: Casual Challenge looks different from hardcore Challenge
  • Constraints: What you can build affects what you should design
  • Secondary aesthetics: Most games blend; secondaries modify patterns

The patterns illustrate the type of thinking the derivation chain requires. For each aesthetic, ask:

  • What behaviors create this experience?
  • What timing enables those behaviors?
  • What rules approach produces those behaviors?
  • What systems implement the approach?
  • How should those systems communicate?
  • What numbers calibrate the systems?

Your answers will be yours. The questions are universal.

Part 8: Framework Flexibility

The worked examples and pattern sketches demonstrate that the same framework produces different games for different inputs. This isn't a weakness—it's the point.

How Inputs Shape Outputs

What Remains Constant

Regardless of inputs:

  • Aesthetics determine dynamics
  • Dynamics determine temporal structure
  • Temporal structure determines content scale
  • Paradigm shapes mechanical approach
  • Mechanics require presentation architecture
  • Parameters derive from measurable targets

The chain order holds. The logical dependencies hold. What changes is the content at each level, not the structure of the derivation.

Using the Framework

Start where you are. Entry point doesn't matter as long as you derive what's missing.

Check coherence between levels. When something feels wrong, diagnose which level contains the problem.

Test predictions, not hopes. Make hypotheses explicit. Failed predictions teach more than vague successes.

Iterate efficiently. Start with parameter adjustments before questioning presentation. Question presentation before questioning mechanics. Question mechanics before questioning structure.

The framework doesn't design games. It structures thinking about games. The design still requires creativity, taste, and judgment. But with the framework, those qualities have somewhere to go.

Conclusion

We began with pieces: aesthetics, context, resources, cognition, time, balance. This chapter assembled them into a unified process.

Games are experiences held in systems. The system isn't the experience—players bring that—but without a well-constructed system, the experience has nowhere to live.

This chapter presented game design as a derivation chain: aesthetic goals determining dynamics, dynamics determining temporal structure, temporal structure and paradigm determining mechanical implementation, mechanics requiring presentation architecture, and presentation plus mechanics determining parameters. Each level constrains and enables the next. The chain can be entered from different points, but the logical dependencies remain constant.

The two worked examples—a casual mobile roguelike and a TTRPG one-shot—demonstrated that the same framework produces radically different games depending on inputs. A Consistency paradigm with Challenge aesthetic yields DPS calculations and frame-count budgets. A Framework paradigm with Tension aesthetic yields Humanity economies and scene structures. The framework flexes; the thinking process holds.

Four principles guided the derivation:

Games are experiential scaffolds. Every design decision traces back to the experience it enables. Mechanics exist to produce dynamics. Dynamics exist to create aesthetics. Aesthetics exist because someone, somewhere, will invest their time and attention expecting a return. Honor that investment.

Cognitive budget is real. Players have finite attention, memory, and processing capacity. Every system competes for that budget. Complexity must earn its place through richer experience. What doesn't contribute, detracts.

UI is game. How the game communicates is as designable as what the game does. The same mechanics with different presentation architecture produce different experiences. Presentation decisions are design decisions, not polish.

Derivation beats intuition—then intuition refines derivation. Starting from reasoned first principles produces better initial designs than starting from gut feeling. But playtesting reveals what reasoning missed. The best designs emerge from the cycle: derive, test, adjust, derive again.

The practical chapters that follow apply this framework to complete designs—testing whether the theory survives contact with real players and real constraints. Theory that doesn't produce playable games is worthless. But theory that structures the design process makes the difference between stumbling toward something that works and building toward something that sings.

The derivation chain is a tool. Use it to structure your thinking, diagnose your problems, and communicate your decisions. Then set it aside and watch players play. They'll tell you what you actually built.

That's where design begins again.
