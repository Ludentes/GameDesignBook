Chapter 10: Designing Hearthfire — A Complete Derivation

How This Chapter Works

The previous chapters established our derivation chain—the logical flow from aesthetic goals to specific parameters. Chapter 8 tested this by deriving Rimworld from first principles, showing how the framework explains an existing game's coherence. Chapter 9 used the framework to compare design alternatives for a board game, demonstrating how it structures decision-making.

This chapter does something different. We're going to design a complete TTRPG system in response to a specific request, showing the derivation as a conversation between designer and framework.

The Framework as Conversation Partner

The derivation chain isn't a formula that produces single answers. At each level, it surfaces the questions that matter and constrains the possibility space. The designer still makes choices—choices informed by their understanding of the specific situation, their creative instincts, and their read of what players actually need.

This is by design. The framework provides structured thinking, not automated design. It ensures each choice is made with awareness of what it enables and forecloses downstream. It catches incoherence before it becomes a problem. It translates fuzzy intentions into concrete specifications. But the designer remains the author.

We'll show this conversation explicitly. When the framework offers options, we'll show the options. When we choose, we'll show why. When the choice could reasonably go another way, we'll acknowledge that. A different designer with different instincts might make different choices at several points and arrive at a different but equally valid system.

The goal is to demonstrate how a designer actually uses this framework in practice—not as a rigid procedure but as a thinking partner that asks the right questions at the right time.

The Request

A GM comes to us with this situation:

"I have three players—professionals in their 30s-40s who were friends in college. They want to play D&D like they did back then, but they have jobs and kids now. They can only meet once a month for 2 hours. Last time they tried, they spent half the session remembering what we were doing and looking up rules. Can you help me run something that actually works with their schedule?"

When we probe deeper, we learn:

Fixed constraints:

  • 2 hours per session, once per month (time is non-negotiable)
  • 3 players with limited availability
  • Sometimes 6+ weeks between sessions if someone cancels
  • Must feel complete each session (can't have 6-week cliffhangers)

What they want:

  • The campaign experience (not just one-shots)
  • To feel like heroes
  • Characters that matter and grow
  • Fun, collaborative, not grimdark

What's failing now:

  • 30+ minutes remembering what happened
  • Rules lookups eating session time
  • Forgetting character abilities
  • Cliffhangers that resolve a month later (or never)

What success looks like:

  • Less than 10 minutes from "hello" to actively playing
  • Each session accomplishes something
  • Players reference events from multiple sessions ago
  • No one pulls out phones from boredom or confusion

This is our design brief. Let's derive a solution.

Level 0: Context

The Framework Asks: What's Fixed Before Design Begins?

Medium?

TTRPG. This is non-negotiable—the request is explicitly for tabletop play, and the underlying need (friendship maintenance through shared creative activity) requires physical co-presence. Fellowship aesthetic needs actual fellowship.

Platform constraints?

  • Physical gathering, once monthly
  • 2 hours total (we'll treat this as ~90 minutes of actual play after settling in)
  • 3 players + GM
  • No digital tooling assumed (though not prohibited)
  • 4-6 week gaps between sessions as baseline, occasionally longer

Scope constraints?

The GM needs something they can run, not something they need to design from scratch. This pushes toward existing systems or light modifications rather than ground-up creation. The GM also has limited prep time—they're a busy adult too.

Setting?

Framework asks: Does setting need to be load-bearing or can it be cosmetic?

The players want fantasy—it's what they know from D&D, it satisfies nostalgia, and it has vocabulary they already understand. But nothing in their core needs requires fantasy mechanically. The problems they're experiencing (memory, rules overhead, session incompleteness) are structural, not setting-dependent.

Our answer: Fantasy setting, cosmetic (Level 1-2 on Space Coffee scale). We're leveraging their existing genre knowledge to reduce cognitive load. We could reskin to any setting without changing the core design. This is a feature—if one player really wants space opera instead, the system should accommodate that.

The Critical Question: What Archetype?

This is where the framework forces clarity that might otherwise be missed.

Framework notes: The players say "D&D" but D&D is actually multiple archetypes awkwardly combined:

  • Tactical combat (the combat system with its action economy and grid positioning)
  • Dungeon crawl (the original exploration and resource-management structure)
  • Heroic adventure (the power fantasy and narrative arc)
  • Campaign progression (character advancement over extended play)

Which are they actually asking for?

GM's answer: "They loved combat. Or at least they remember loving it. They definitely want to feel like heroes fighting monsters. But the combats we ran last time took forever—one fight was most of a session, and then we had to stop mid-dungeon."

So they want combat. But D&D-style tactical combat takes 30-60 minutes per encounter. In a 2-hour session, that's one or two fights maximum, with no room for anything else. The archetype as D&D implements it is temporally incompatible with their constraints.

The framework helps us see a crucial distinction: They want the fantasy of tactical combat (heroes fighting monsters, using cool abilities, working together) but they cannot afford the implementation of tactical combat (grid positioning, action economy, modifier calculation, multi-round attrition).

Our archetype answer: Heroic Adventure with Fast Combat.

This means:

  • Combat exists and matters
  • Combat is light, cooperative, decisive
  • Combat resolves in minutes, not half-hours
  • The feel is heroic and exciting, not tactical and crunchy

Additionally: Episodic structure with Campaign persistence.

Each session is a complete episode. But episodes connect—characters grow, the world remembers, long-term stories develop. This is TV-season structure, not movie structure or continuous-novel structure.

Level 1: Aesthetic Profile

The Framework Asks: What Experience Are You Creating?

This is the foundation. Everything downstream derives from here.

Framework prompts: What do players seek from this game? What feelings should emerge from play? What would success feel like from inside the experience?

GM reflects: "When they talk about college D&D, they light up. They tell stories about characters who did ridiculous things—the time someone bluffed a dragon, the fighter who crit at exactly the right moment, the session where everything went wrong and they barely escaped. They don't tell stories about optimal builds or tactical positioning. They tell stories about moments."

What are those moments actually delivering?

Let's check each aesthetic from Chapter 1:

Challenge (overcoming difficulty through skill): Sort of. They want to overcome obstacles. But they're not seeking difficult optimization or mastery over complex systems. They want to feel clever, not tested.

Tension (high stakes, constant danger): They want stakes that matter, but "constant danger" doesn't match "fun and collaborative, not grimdark." They want dramatic tension, not anxiety.

Discovery (finding new things, uncovering secrets): Somewhat. Exploring a world is part of the fantasy. But it's not the core—they're not asking for mystery or investigation.

Mastery (deep system understanding): No. Explicitly not—rules complexity is a failure mode.

Fellowship (social connection): Yes, strongly. The request is fundamentally about maintaining friendship through shared experience. This is the hidden core of "D&D like we did in college."

Narrative (stories that unfold, characters that matter): Yes. "Characters matter and are growing," "world feels persistent," wanting campaign over one-shots—this is Narrative aesthetic.

Expression (creating something uniquely yours): Yes, to a degree. They want their characters, their choices mattering. But not the blank-canvas expression of Minecraft.

Progression (growing stronger over time): Yes. "Feel like their characters matter and are growing." This isn't just narrative growth—they want mechanical advancement too.

Submission (relaxation, flow state): Not exactly, but there's something here. They want play to feel easy—low friction, not work. They've got jobs for work.

Primary and Secondary Aesthetics

Primary: Fellowship + Narrative (intertwined)

The core experience is friends creating stories together. Neither element stands alone. Fellowship without Narrative is just hanging out (they could do that without a game). Narrative without Fellowship is solo writing (doesn't require monthly meetups). The combination—collaborative storytelling among friends—is the actual target aesthetic.

Secondary: Progression + Heroic Fantasy

They want characters who grow and who feel like heroes. This is the D&D flavor they're nostalgic for.

Note: "Heroic Fantasy" isn't in our standard aesthetic list. This is what Chapter 1 calls "going deeper when common aesthetics aren't enough." We need a specific term for feeling like capable heroes in a fantasy world—not Challenge (too much about difficulty), not pure Expression (too open-ended). Let's define Heroic Fantasy as: experiencing competence, importance, and wonder in a fantastical context.

Explicitly Excluded:

  • Mastery: System depth is anti-goal. Rules should disappear.
  • High Tension: Grimdark and anxiety explicitly rejected.
  • Challenge (as primary): Not here to be tested or struggle against difficulty.

The Framework Asks: Who Is the Target Player?

Framework prompts: What's their investment capacity? Time, cognitive load tolerance, session gaps, mental model persistence?

Chapter 1 gives us three profiles: Casual, Midcore, Hardcore.

But Chapter 1 also identifies the Busy Adult Problem: experienced players with casual time constraints.

These players are:

  • Gaming history: High (played D&D extensively in college)
  • System literacy: High (understand TTRPG conventions)
  • Available time: Casual (2 hours monthly)
  • Cognitive load tolerance: Moderate during play, but cannot maintain complex state between sessions
  • Session gaps: Extreme (4-6+ weeks)

This is the specific profile that breaks normal categorization. They could handle complexity within a session, but the gaps destroy any complex mental model. They need a system that:

  • Respects their intelligence and experience
  • Doesn't require homework between sessions
  • Rebuilds context fast at session start
  • Doesn't punish gaps with confusion

Our answer: Design for Competent Casual—players who can engage with moderate complexity in the moment but cannot maintain it across month-long gaps.

The Framework Asks: What Defines Success?

Framework prompts: How will you know if the design works? What's observable? What's testable?

From the original request, translated into testable criteria:

Session-level success:

  • Time from "hello" to active play: <10 minutes
  • Rules lookups during session: <5 (ideally 0)
  • Session feels complete: Observable wrap-up, players describe what happened as a unit
  • No phone-checking from disengagement

Campaign-level success:

  • Players reference past sessions unprompted
  • Characters feel continuous (players remember who they're playing)
  • Scheduling next session happens with enthusiasm, not obligation
  • Player retention across 6+ months

Aesthetic success:

  • Players laugh together (Fellowship)
  • Players tell others about "what happened in the game" as stories (Narrative)
  • Players describe their characters with affection and investment (Heroic Fantasy + Progression)

The Framework Notes a Tension

From Chapter 1: "Narrative aesthetic is inaccessible to casual players because they can't follow complex plots with long gaps."

We've set Narrative as primary aesthetic for players with extreme session gaps. The framework is warning us.

But this isn't the framework fighting us—it's the framework doing its job. It's surfacing the core design challenge: how do we deliver Narrative aesthetic to players whose temporal situation normally precludes it?

The answer isn't "pick a different aesthetic." The answer is "design temporal architecture and presentation architecture specifically to solve this problem." The framework has identified where our design innovation must occur.

We carry this tension forward as a design requirement, not a reason to compromise on goals.

Level 2: Required Dynamics

The Framework Asks: What Must Players DO to Have This Experience?

Dynamics are the bridge between aesthetics (what players feel) and mechanics (what the system does). We need to identify what observable behaviors create Fellowship + Narrative for Competent Casual players.

Framework prompts: What must players do? What must they feel at key moments? What must they learn or master? What behaviors would undermine the aesthetic?

Deriving Dynamics from Primary Aesthetics

Fellowship Dynamics

What behaviors create social connection through play?

Collaborative creation: Players build on each other's contributions. Someone says "I leap across the chasm," another says "I grab your hand as you almost slip," a third says "I cast light so you can see where to land." The story emerges from the group, not from any individual.

Shared memorable moments: Players experience highs and lows together. The dramatic escape, the perfect timing, the terrible roll at the worst moment. These become stories they tell: "Remember when..."

Table talk and banter: Space for conversation that weaves in and out of the fiction. Players riffing on situations, joking about characters, celebrating each other's moments. The game is a context for socializing, not a replacement for it.

Low-stakes disagreement: Players can have different ideas about what to do without it causing real conflict. "I think we should fight" / "I think we should negotiate" is productive tension, not argument.

What undermines Fellowship:

  • Long individual turns where others disengage
  • Spotlight hogging where one player dominates
  • Mechanics that punish cooperation or reward selfish play
  • Overly serious tone that forbids banter
  • Rules arguments that create real interpersonal conflict

Narrative Dynamics

What behaviors create meaningful stories?

Character-driven choices: Players make decisions as their characters, based on who those characters are. Not "what's optimal" but "what would Kira do?"

Consequence visibility: Choices matter and players can see that they matter. The village they saved remembers them. The enemy they spared returns.

Dramatic arc completion: Tensions introduced get resolved. Questions raised get answered. Setups pay off. Within session (episodic satisfaction) and across sessions (campaign satisfaction).

Emergent storytelling: The best moments aren't scripted. They emerge from character choices meeting situations meeting dice. Surprising even the GM.

What undermines Narrative:

  • Choices without consequences (nothing matters)
  • Randomness without meaning (things happen but don't connect)
  • GM railroad where players are audience, not authors
  • Forgotten threads (setup without payoff)
  • Character interchangeability (anyone could make any choice)

The Memory Problem as Dynamic Failure

The framework has helped us see something important. The original failures—"spent half the session remembering what we were doing"—aren't just logistical problems. They're dynamic failures.

When players can't remember what happened:

  • Narrative dies: No consequence visibility if you forgot the setup
  • Fellowship weakens: Can't share references to moments no one remembers
  • Character continuity breaks: "Wait, who am I again?"

The required dynamic isn't just "play the game." It's rapid context reconstruction—the ability to rebuild shared understanding of the fiction in minutes, not half-hours.

This is a dynamic we must design for explicitly. It doesn't emerge naturally; we must engineer it.

Deriving Dynamics from Secondary Aesthetics

Progression Dynamics

What behaviors create the feeling of growth?

Visible advancement: Characters demonstrably become more capable. Not just bigger numbers, but new options, new reach, new status.

Earned power: Advancement connects to play. "We leveled up because we saved the village" not "we leveled up because four sessions passed."

Build identity: Characters grow in directions that reflect player choices. Two fighters who started the same become different based on what their players chose.

Heroic Fantasy Dynamics

What behaviors create the feeling of being a capable hero in a wondrous world?

Competence expression: Characters succeed at things they're supposed to be good at. The warrior fights well. The wizard's magic works. Players feel effective.

Meaningful obstacles: Challenges exist but are overcome. Not easily (boring) but not impossibly (frustrating). Heroes struggle and then prevail.

Wonder and discovery: The world contains things worth seeing. Magic is magical. Monsters are monstrous. The fantastic is treated as fantastic.

Heroic framing: The context frames players as protagonists. Not antiheroes, not gritty survivors, not morally gray operators. Heroes.

Combat as Dynamic

The GM told us players want combat—or at least the feeling of combat they remember. What dynamics does combat need to serve?

Combat must serve Fellowship:

  • Everyone participates (no watching one player solo)
  • Turns are fast (engagement maintained)
  • Cooperation matters (working together feels good)

Combat must serve Narrative:

  • Stakes are clear (why this fight matters)
  • Outcomes are meaningful (consequences follow)
  • Dramatic moments emerge (crits, clutch saves, last stands)

Combat must serve Heroic Fantasy:

  • Characters feel capable (heroes fight well)
  • Action is exciting (not grinding down HP)
  • Resolution is decisive (heroes win or make heroic sacrifices)

Combat must respect time constraints:

  • Complete in 10-15 minutes maximum
  • Rules should not require lookup
  • State should be minimal (not tracking twelve conditions)

This gives us a specific dynamic requirement: Fast Dramatic Combat—combat that generates drama, serves fellowship, expresses heroism, and completes quickly. This is fundamentally different from tactical combat, which optimizes for decision depth and positional complexity.

System Function Requirements

Chapter 2 introduced system functions. Let's define what each needs to do for our aesthetics:

Adjudication: Fast, clear, dramatic. No calculation time, no ambiguity, results that feel meaningful. This eliminates complex modifier stacking and table lookups.

Information Management: Aggressive transparency with fiction-based uncertainty. Players always know what their characters can do and what's at stake. Mystery comes from the fiction, not from system opacity.

Content Generation: GM-light, fiction-forward. Content emerges from player choices, simple prompts, and collaborative establishment—not from extensive GM prep.

State Persistence: Robust memory with minimal tracking. The game must remember who characters are, what they've done, what's happening in the world—but players and GM cannot be expected to hold this in their heads across 6-week gaps. The system must externalize memory.

Feedback: Immediate, social, celebratory. Rolls happen in the open, outcomes are narrated instantly, the table reacts together.

Cognitive Framing: Fiction-first. Players think in terms of fiction ("I intimidate the guard") not mechanics ("I use my Bold approach to make an Overcome action").

Anti-Dynamics (What We Must Prevent)

Prevent: Analysis paralysis — Long decision-making kills Fellowship (others disengage) and pacing (Narrative stalls). Solution: Limited options, fast resolution, forward momentum.

Prevent: Rules lawyering — Arguments about mechanics destroy Fellowship and break immersion. Solution: GM authority clear, rules simple enough to not dispute, fiction-first resolution.

Prevent: Spotlight monopoly — One player dominating kills Fellowship for others. Solution: Scene rotation, explicit invitation to quieter players, mechanics that include everyone.

Prevent: Consequence amnesia — If players forget what happened, Narrative fails. Solution: Externalized memory, aggressive recaps, "previously on" as designed system feature.

Prevent: Character confusion — If players forget their characters, Heroic Fantasy and Narrative both fail. Solution: Simple characters, physical reminder cards, distinctive single traits.

The Framework Confirms a Design Direction

Looking at our required dynamics, a pattern emerges:

We need:

  • Fiction-first resolution (not mechanics-first)
  • Collaborative authority (not GM-only narration)
  • Fast dramatic combat (not tactical combat)
  • Externalized memory (not player memory dependence)
  • Simple character expression (not complex builds)
  • Scene-based structure (not continuous time)

This pattern points toward a specific design family: Fate-like systems rather than D&D-like systems.

The framework hasn't told us "use Fate." It's told us what dynamics we need, and those dynamics happen to align with what Fate does well. This is the derivation working—we've arrived at a system direction through systematic analysis, not assumption.

But we're not done. We need to derive temporal architecture, balance paradigm, and specific mechanics. Fate as-written may need significant modification to fully serve our specific needs.

Level 3: Temporal Architecture

The Framework Asks: When Do Those Behaviors Occur, and For How Long?

We've identified what players must do (dynamics). Now we determine when they do it—the rhythm and duration of play experiences.

Framework prompts: How long is a complete play session? What loops exist within that session? How do loops relate to each other? Where are natural stopping points? How does pacing create the intended experience?

The Brutal Math

Let's start with what we have:

Total session time: 2 hours (120 minutes)

Realistic usable time:

  • Arrival, settling, snacks: 10-15 minutes
  • Bathroom breaks, interruptions: 10 minutes
  • Wrap-up, scheduling next session: 5-10 minutes

Actual play time: ~90 minutes

This is our temporal budget. Everything must fit within 90 minutes of actual play.

Session gaps: 4-6 weeks typical, sometimes longer

This is our memory decay interval. Any mental model that can't survive 6 weeks without reinforcement cannot be relied upon.

Deriving Loop Structure from Dynamics

What Must Happen Every Session?

From our required dynamics:

  1. Rapid context reconstruction — Remember who we are, what's happening
  2. Character-driven choices — Make decisions as our characters
  3. Collaborative creation — Build story together
  4. Fast dramatic combat — At least one exciting action sequence
  5. Consequence visibility — See that our choices mattered
  6. Dramatic arc completion — Resolve something satisfying

Each session must contain ALL of these. A session that skips any of them fails the aesthetic.

The Episodic Requirement

The original request was explicit: "Must feel complete each session."

This means each session is a complete episode, not a chapter that ends mid-scene. TV structure, not novel structure.

Think of it like this:

  • Novel chapter: "The heroes entered the dark cave. To be continued..."
  • TV episode: "The heroes entered the dark cave, faced the guardian, and escaped with the artifact—but now the villain knows they're coming."

Episodes have:

  • A beginning (situation established)
  • A middle (complications, action, choices)
  • An end (resolution, new status quo)
  • A hook (thread for next time, but not a cliffhanger)

Our macro loop IS the episode. One session = one episode. No exceptions.

Loop Architecture

Macro Loop: The Episode (One Session = 90 minutes)

Structure:

  1. Previously On (context reconstruction) — 5-10 minutes
  2. The Hook (situation established) — 5-10 minutes
  3. The Action (play proper) — 60-70 minutes
  4. The Resolution (wrap-up) — 10 minutes
  5. Next Time On (hook for next session) — 5 minutes

What this accomplishes:

  • Context reconstruction is designed in, not hoped for
  • Complete arc every session (episodic satisfaction)
  • Forward momentum maintained (hooks create anticipation)
  • Clean stopping point (no cliffhangers)

Meso Loop: The Scene (10-15 minutes each)

Within the Action phase, play proceeds in scenes—discrete units of dramatic action.

Scene structure:

  • Frame: Where are we? What's at stake? Who's present?
  • Play: Characters act, situation evolves, dice roll if uncertain
  • Resolve: What changed? What's the new situation?

How many scenes per session?

With 60-70 minutes of Action phase, and 10-15 minutes per scene:

  • 4-6 scenes per session

This is tight. Every scene must matter. No filler, no wandering, no "what do you want to do now?" dead air.

Scene types for variety:

  • Action scene: Combat, chase, escape (Fast Dramatic Combat)
  • Social scene: Negotiation, persuasion, gathering information
  • Discovery scene: Exploration, investigation, revelation
  • Character scene: Relationships, internal conflict, growth moment

A good episode probably has: 1-2 action scenes, 1-2 social scenes, 1 discovery or character scene. Mix varies based on the story.

Micro Loop: The Exchange (30 seconds - 2 minutes)

Within scenes, play proceeds through rapid exchanges:

The fundamental micro loop:

  1. GM frames: "The guard spots you. What do you do?"
  2. Player declares: "I try to bluff that we're supposed to be here."
  3. Resolution: Roll if needed, narrate outcome
  4. New situation: "He looks suspicious but waves you through. You're in the courtyard."

Duration: 30 seconds to 2 minutes per exchange.

What keeps it fast:

  • No complex rules lookup
  • Player-facing rolls (GM doesn't slow things down)
  • Immediate narration of outcomes
  • Clear handoff ("What do you do?" / "What does everyone else do?")

Exchanges per scene: 5-10 exchanges per scene feels right. Enough for drama, not so many it drags.

Meta Loop: The Campaign (Across Sessions)

Sessions connect into a campaign. This is where Narrative aesthetic lives at the macro scale.

Campaign structure:

  • Arcs: 3-5 sessions each, one major storyline
  • Seasons: 2-3 arcs (6-15 sessions), one "big picture" resolved
  • Series: Multiple seasons if players want to continue

Given monthly sessions:

  • One arc = 3-5 months
  • One season = 6-15 months (potentially longer with gaps)

This is slow. A single arc takes as long as some entire campaigns. We must design for this pace:

  • Each arc must have satisfying internal structure
  • Arcs must be somewhat self-contained (players might forget earlier arcs)
  • Meta-progression must be visible even at slow pace

Pacing Pattern

Framework asks: What pacing pattern serves your aesthetics?

From Chapter 5, the options include: Escalating, Episodic, Cyclical, Steady-state, Emergent.

Our answer: Episodic pacing

Each session is a complete episode with its own arc:

  • Tension rises through the Action phase
  • Peak intensity during climactic scene (usually final action scene)
  • Resolution brings tension down
  • Hook raises just enough anticipation for next time

This is classic three-act structure compressed into 90 minutes.

Stopping Points

Framework asks: Where can players stop and feel satisfied?

Primary stopping point: End of session

This is designed in. Every session ends at a complete episode boundary. This is non-negotiable given the constraints.

What makes it work:

  • Clear completion signal (resolution scene ends, "Next time on...")
  • Progress persistence (what happened is recorded)
  • State simplification (no mid-combat stops)
  • Emotional resolution (arc complete, not cliffhanger)
  • Re-entry clarity ("Previously on" designed for next session)

Emergency stopping points: End of scene

If session must end early (emergency, time miscalculation), any scene boundary is an acceptable stop:

  • Worse than episode end (arc incomplete)
  • But manageable (scene provides natural break)
  • GM notes "we stopped mid-episode, pick up at scene X"

Never stop: Mid-scene

This is the cliffhanger problem. Mid-combat, mid-negotiation, mid-revelation—these stops mean 4-6 weeks of unresolved tension. Players forget the specifics but remember the frustration.

Design implication: Scene pacing must prevent overtime. If session is running long, GM must resolve or abbreviate scenes to hit episode end, not stop mid-scene.

The Memory Problem: Temporal Solutions

The framework surfaced this at Level 1: Narrative aesthetic for players with extreme session gaps. Now we solve it through temporal architecture.

Previously On (5-10 minutes)

This isn't optional GM nicety. It's a designed system feature.

Structure:

  1. GM recap: "Last time, you infiltrated the Baron's estate, discovered he was dealing with demons, and escaped with the evidence. Kira was wounded. Marcus made a new enemy."
  2. Player additions: "Didn't we also find that weird key?"
  3. Character check: Each player states one thing about their character—a trait, a goal, their current situation

Why this works:

  • Externalizes memory (GM has notes, players don't need to)
  • Collaborative reconstruction (players add what they remember, validates shared memory)
  • Character re-embodiment (stating character trait helps players "become" them again)

Next Time On (5 minutes)

Structure:

  1. GM teases: "Next time: the evidence goes public, but the Baron isn't going to take this lying down..."
  2. Players declare: Each player states one thing their character wants to do or find out next session
  3. Record: GM notes these for next session prep

Why this works:

  • Creates anticipation (hook survives the gap)
  • Player declarations give GM prep direction
  • Stated intentions survive in notes even if players forget

Session Record (Created during wrap-up)

This is the memory prosthetic. After each session, the group creates:

  • What happened (3-5 bullet points)
  • What changed (world state, character state)
  • What's unresolved (threads for future)
  • What each character did/felt (one line per character)

Format matters: This must be fast to create and fast to read. If it takes 20 minutes to write, it won't happen. If it takes 10 minutes to read, it won't be used.

Target: 1 page maximum, created in 5 minutes, readable in 2 minutes.

Level 4: Balance Paradigm

The Framework Asks: What Approach to Rules Creates the Required Dynamics?

Balance paradigm determines how the game maintains appropriate challenge, fairness, and engagement. The framework offers three paradigms:

Consistency Paradigm: Fixed rules, deterministic outcomes, player skill as variable. The system behaves identically regardless of player state.

Framework Paradigm: Rules provide structure and boundaries, but human judgment determines specific outcomes. A GM or group consensus handles what rules cannot specify.

Adaptive Paradigm: System adjusts to player state, maintaining target experience regardless of skill level.

Framework prompts: How is difficulty managed? How much player customization is supported? Who or what makes judgment calls? How does the system handle edge cases?

Why Framework Paradigm Is Required

Our constraints make this nearly inevitable:

Time constraint eliminates Consistency:

Consistency paradigm requires comprehensive rules—every situation has a defined resolution. This works for D&D because you can (theoretically) look up grappling rules, falling damage tables, or spell interactions.

But we have 90 minutes and "rules lookup" is an explicit failure mode. We cannot have rules comprehensive enough for Consistency paradigm while also having rules simple enough to never require lookup.

Memory constraint eliminates Consistency:

Consistency requires players to know rules. With 4-6 week gaps, players will not retain rule knowledge. Every session would require re-teaching, burning our precious time budget.

Narrative aesthetic requires Framework:

Narrative emerges from judgment calls. "Does the dragon believe this lie?" can't be fully determined by rules—it depends on fiction, history, character, plausibility. A GM must judge.

Fellowship aesthetic requires Framework:

Fellowship means reading the room. Is everyone engaged? Is someone hogging spotlight? Is this combat dragging? These require human judgment and adjustment in real-time.

Adaptive paradigm doesn't fit:

Adaptive paradigm (like Left 4 Dead's AI Director) adjusts difficulty algorithmically based on player performance. This requires either digital implementation or mechanical complexity we can't afford. It also feels manipulative when visible in tabletop—players don't want to know the GM is secretly adjusting numbers.

Our answer: Framework Paradigm with GM tools

The GM is the balance mechanism. Rules provide structure—they tell you when to roll and what outcomes mean—but the GM determines difficulty, pacing, and fictional outcomes through judgment.

The Danger of Framework Paradigm

This is powerful but dangerous. Framework paradigm without support produces:

  • GM burnout (too much improvisation load)
  • Inconsistent experiences (depends entirely on GM skill)
  • Player frustration (rulings feel arbitrary)

The solution: robust GM tools.

The GM needs scaffolding for their judgment—vocabulary, frameworks, templates, and references that make good judgment easier and more consistent.

What Framework Paradigm Requires

From Chapter 6: "Framework doesn't mean 'no mechanics.' It means mechanics serve judgment rather than replace it."

We still need:

Resolution Mechanics

When do dice roll? What do results mean?

The GM needs clear triggers for when to call for rolls and clear interpretation of results. Otherwise every action becomes negotiation about whether to roll.

Required:

  • Clear fiction-to-roll trigger ("When outcome is uncertain and stakes exist...")
  • Simple roll mechanic (one roll, fast interpretation)
  • Result categories that guide narration (not just pass/fail)
  • GM authority to skip rolls when outcome is obvious

Resource Mechanics

What creates tension and tracks attrition?

Even in Framework paradigm, resources pace the fiction. Something must make "we keep fighting" eventually untenable.

Required:

  • Simple attrition tracking (not HP for every NPC)
  • Clear "this is getting worse" signal
  • Flexible interpretation (resource as narrative truth, not just bookkeeping)

Character Mechanics

What makes characters distinct and capable?

Players need to know what their characters can do. "I'm a fighter" must mean something mechanically, not just fictionally.

Required:

  • Clear character capabilities (what you're good at)
  • Differentiation (characters feel different to play)
  • Minimal tracking (can't forget abilities over gap)
  • Growth path (Progression aesthetic requires advancement)

Scene Mechanics

How do scenes start, flow, and end?

Our temporal architecture requires discrete scenes. The GM needs tools to frame, run, and close scenes effectively.

Required:

  • Scene framing structure (what to establish)
  • Pacing controls (how to move scenes forward)
  • Closing triggers (when scene ends)
  • Transition tools (how to move between scenes)

GM as Balance Mechanism

In Consistency paradigm, the designer balances the game. A CR 5 monster is balanced for a level 5 party because the math was done in advance.

In Framework paradigm, the GM balances each session. The GM judges:

  • How hard should this obstacle be?
  • How many enemies are appropriate?
  • When should this combat end?
  • Is this challenge too easy/hard for the current situation?

This requires GM tools for:

Encounter Balancing

How does the GM know what's appropriate?

D&D's encounter building gives CR and XP budgets—math that theoretically produces balanced fights. The math is often wrong, but it gives GMs a starting point.

We need something faster and more flexible.

The challenge: Our combat is dramatic, not tactical. "Balance" doesn't mean "50% chance of PC death." It means "fight feels dangerous but heroic victory is likely."

What the GM needs to know:

  • How threatening should this feel? (Trivial / Challenging / Dangerous / Desperate)
  • How long should this take? (Quick scene / Full scene / Extended sequence)
  • What's the dramatic purpose? (Establish competence / Create tension / Climactic payoff)

Tool: Threat Dial

Rather than calculating CR, GM sets a threat dial:

Threat LevelWhat It MeansTypical Outcome
TrivialHeroes are clearly superiorVictory certain, show off competence
ChallengingReal opposition, heroes favoredVictory likely with some cost
DangerousSerious threat, could go badlyVictory possible but costly
DesperateOverwhelming oddsSurvival/escape is the victory

The dial determines how many obstacles, how hard they are to overcome, how much attrition they inflict, when the fight ends. This is Framework balance: GM judgment picks the threat level based on dramatic needs, not mathematical calculation.

Pacing Controls

How does the GM keep sessions on track?

With 90 minutes and 4-6 scenes required, GM needs tools to know when to end a scene, accelerate scenes running long, expand scenes running short, and track overall session pacing.

Tool: Scene Clock

A simple tracking tool: session has ~6 scene slots, mark scenes as they complete, visual indicator of "we need to wrap up" threshold. Not mechanical precision—awareness that enables good judgment.

Spotlight Management

How does the GM ensure Fellowship dynamic?

Framework paradigm means GM controls spotlight. Without tools, some players dominate while others fade.

Tool: Spotlight Awareness

Simple tracking of who's had focus. Each player should have a "moment" each session. Scene framing can direct spotlight. Combat cycles through all players. Quiet players get explicit invitations.

NPC and Situation Creation

How does the GM create content quickly?

Busy adult GM has ~30 minutes prep. They need tools to generate NPCs with enough personality to be memorable, situations with built-in drama, complications that emerge naturally.

Tool: Quick Creation Templates

NPC in 30 seconds: Name, Role (what they do), Want (what they're trying to achieve), Quirk (one memorable trait). No stats unless they're an obstacle.

Situation in 1 minute: Status quo (what's happening), Pressure (why it can't stay static), Stakes (what's at risk), Opportunity (what players can gain).

These are improvisational prompts, not random tables. GM picks what fits.

What This Means for Design

Framework paradigm with robust GM tools means:

Players need:

  • Clear character capabilities (what can I do?)
  • Simple resolution understanding (when do I roll? what do results mean?)
  • Trust in GM fairness (rulings feel consistent and reasonable)
  • Permission to contribute to fiction (collaborative, not GM-dictated)

Players don't need:

  • Deep rules knowledge (GM handles complexity)
  • Tactical optimization (GM balances encounters to drama, not math)
  • System mastery (fiction-first means fiction knowledge matters more)

The trade-off:

We're putting significant load on the GM. In exchange, players have very low cognitive load, sessions run with minimal rules reference, pacing stays in human hands, and fiction stays primary.

This trade-off is appropriate for our context. The GM is already doing more work than players. We're giving them tools to do that work efficiently, not eliminating the work.

Level 5: Mechanical Specification

The Framework Asks: What Specific Systems Implement the Paradigm?

We've established Framework paradigm with GM tools. Now we specify concrete mechanics—the actual systems players and GM interact with.

Framework prompts: What are the core mechanics? What resources exist and what functions do they serve? How is content generated or structured? What is the player's role and agency?

Level 5a: Mechanical Alternatives

Before committing to specific mechanics, the framework asks us to sketch alternatives.

Alternative 1: Minimal Fate (Fate Accelerated Core)

Use Fate Accelerated almost as-is: six approaches, aspects for character definition, fate points for narrative economy, four actions, stress and consequences for harm.

Pros: Proven system, fiction-first, aspects solve memory problem Cons: Fate point economy requires tracking and strategy, four actions still require learning, no built-in session structure for our temporal needs

Alternative 2: Ultra-Light (Lasers & Feelings Style)

Strip to absolute minimum: one stat per character, single resolution mechanic, no resources, pure fiction-positioning.

Pros: Zero learning curve, nothing to forget Cons: May feel too light, no progression mechanics, no combat differentiation

Alternative 3: Blades-Lite (Position and Effect)

Borrow Blades in the Dark's core: Position + Effect, GM sets before roll, simplified action list, stress for resources, clocks for progress.

Pros: Position/Effect is brilliant GM tool, dramatic resolution built-in Cons: Heist-focused needs reskinning, clock tracking adds overhead

Alternative 4: Hybrid Purpose-Built

Take best elements from each: approaches from Fate (simplified), Position/Effect from Blades (GM tool), aspects from Fate (memory solution), custom session structure, streamlined harm.

Pros: Specifically designed for our constraints, includes GM tools as first-class design Cons: Untested, requires more specification

Our choice: Hybrid Purpose-Built

We'll build a system taking from Fate (aspects, approaches, fate points) and Blades (Position/Effect) while adding our own session structure and GM tools.

Level 5b: Core Resolution Mechanic

The Basic Roll:

When a character attempts something with uncertain outcome and meaningful stakes:

  1. Player declares intent and approach

    • "I want to convince the guard to let us pass" (intent)
    • "By intimidating him with my warrior reputation" (approach)

  2. GM sets Position and Effect

    • Position: How risky? (Safe / Risky / Dangerous)
    • Effect: How much impact on success? (Limited / Standard / Great)

  3. Player rolls dice pool

    • Start with 2d6
    • +1d6 if relevant approach applies
    • +1d6 if relevant aspect applies
    • +1d6 if ally assists
    • Maximum 5d6

  4. Read highest die

    • 6: Full success. You achieve your intent.
    • 4-5: Partial success. You achieve intent, but with cost or complication.
    • 1-3: Failure. You don't achieve intent, and face consequences based on Position.

Why this system:

  • 2d6 base: Accessible dice, not specialized "gamer dice"
  • Additive pool: Easy to understand ("more dice = better")
  • Highest die: Fast to read (no addition)
  • Three-tier results: More interesting than pass/fail, but simple
  • Position/Effect: GM's judgment is transparent and nameable
  • Cap at 5d6: Prevents runaway advantages

Position and Effect (GM Tool):

PositionOn Failure...On Partial...
SafeNothing bad; just don't succeedMinor inconvenience
RiskyModerate consequenceChoose: reduced effect OR consequence
DangerousSevere consequenceBoth consequence AND reduced effect

StandardAchieve intent as stated
GreatAchieve intent plus extra benefit

GM states these BEFORE the roll. This makes judgment transparent and gives players information to negotiate approach.

Character Mechanics

Each character has:

1. Concept (one sentence)

  • "Grizzled dwarven warrior seeking redemption"
  • Guides all interpretation; reminds player who they're playing

2. Three Aspects

  • Phrases that are true about your character
  • Can be invoked for +1d6 when relevant
  • Can be compelled for complications (and fate point rewards)

3. Three Approaches (rated +1, +1, +0)

  • Bold: Direct action, courage, force, standing firm
  • Clever: Tricks, plans, knowledge, perception
  • Swift: Speed, reflexes, stealth, mobility

4. Heroic Ability (one special thing)

  • "Battle Rage: Once per session, automatically succeed on a combat roll"
  • "Ancient Lore: Once per session, ask GM one question and get a true answer"
  • Guarantees each character has a spotlight moment

5. Stress Track (five boxes)

  • Mark stress to absorb harm or push yourself
  • Clears between sessions (episodic reset)

6. Advancement

  • Mark one tick per session
  • Three ticks = one advancement
  • Options: new aspect, improve approach, new heroic ability, narrative reward

Why this structure:

  • Concept: One-sentence reminder survives 6-week gaps
  • Three aspects: Rich enough for variety, few enough to remember
  • Three approaches: Simpler than Fate's six, covers needed action types
  • Heroic Ability: Guarantees spotlight, simple tracking (once per session)
  • Stress: Simple harm system, episodic reset reduces tracking
  • Advancement: Visible progression at monthly pace (one upgrade per quarter)

Character Creation

Character creation must accomplish multiple functions: filter inappropriate characters, establish theme, create connections, generate GM content, and produce artifacts that survive memory gaps.

The Filtering Problem

A player creates a mercenary whose only motivation is money. In a heroic fantasy, this character has no reason to help people, no emotional stakes, no patience for anything but payment. This isn't a bad character—it's a bad fit.

Character creation must encode requirements such that incompatible characters cannot be created.

Character Creation Process (20-30 minutes for full party)

Step 1: Campaign Framework (5 minutes, GM-led)

GM establishes premise, tone, and hook before individual creation.

"You are heroes in the Shattered Kingdoms, where the old empire fell and petty lords fight while dark forces gather. You protect those who can't protect themselves. The tone is heroic fantasy—you're the good guys, there's real danger but also real hope. You all have connections to Millhaven, a town threatened by something in the old forest."

Step 2: Concept (2 minutes per player)

Complete: "I am a _______ who _______."

Filter check: Does this concept fit heroic fantasy where characters help people?

Step 3: Heroic Drive (1 minute per player)

Choose what drives your heroism:

  • Justice: You right wrongs and protect the innocent
  • Redemption: You atone for past failures
  • Glory: You seek to become legendary
  • Duty: You serve a cause greater than yourself
  • Hope: You believe the world can be better
  • Love: You protect those you care about

This becomes your first aspect. The constrained choice ensures heroic orientation.

Step 4: Two More Aspects (5 minutes per player)

Your Past: "I was/am a _______ from _______."

  • Examples: "Knight of the Silver Dawn," "Survivor of the Burning Village"

Your Trouble: "I can't resist _______" or "I struggle with _______"

  • Examples: "Can't Resist a Cry for Help," "The Duke's Men Are Still Looking for Me"

Filter check: Troubles should create interesting problems, not party-destroying ones.

Step 5: Approaches (1 minute)

Distribute +1, +1, +0 among Bold, Clever, Swift.

Step 6: Heroic Ability (2 minutes)

Choose from menu or create with GM approval. Combat abilities, social abilities, utility abilities available.

Step 7: Connections (5 minutes, collaborative)

  • One connection to each other player character
  • One connection to the world (place, person, or group you care about)
  • One connection to the campaign hook (why you can't walk away)

This is GM content. Every connection is something the GM can use.

Step 8: Final Check (2 minutes)

Each player states concept, drive, and why they're here. Table confirms everyone fits.

The Character Card

Everything fits on an index card:

Front:

  • Name and Concept
  • Three Aspects
  • Approaches (Bold/Clever/Swift with ratings)
  • Heroic Ability

Back:

  • Stress boxes
  • Fate points
  • Connections (party, world, hook)
  • Advancement track

The card IS the character. After six weeks, player picks up card, knows who they're playing.

Combat Mechanics (Fast Dramatic Combat)

Combat is a scene, not a subsystem.

There's no initiative. There are no rounds. Combat uses the same resolution as everything else, framed for action.

Combat scene structure:

  1. Frame the fight: Who's here? What's the environment? What's at stake?

  2. GM sets threat level: Trivial / Challenging / Dangerous / Desperate

    • Determines starting Position and enemy resilience

  3. Zoom around the table: GM asks each player "What do you do?" in turn

    • Player describes action cinematically
    • Roll if outcome uncertain
    • GM narrates result, advances situation
    • Move to next player

  4. Track progress with enemy Stress:

    • Minor enemies: 2 stress
    • Significant enemies: 4 stress
    • Major enemies: 6 stress
    • Boss enemies: 8+ stress

    Full success = 2 stress to enemy, Partial = 1 stress

  5. End when dramatically appropriate:

    • All enemies defeated
    • Enemies flee or surrender
    • Players flee or surrender
    • Situation changes

Why this works:

  • No initiative tracking
  • No grid
  • Spotlight rotates naturally
  • Progress visible
  • Ends when fiction demands
  • Fast (10-15 minutes typical)

Combat example:

GM: "Three bandits burst from the treeline, swords drawn. Challenging threat. Kira, you're closest—what do you do?"

Kira's player: "I roar and charge the nearest one, axe swinging. Bold approach?"

GM: "Definitely Bold. Risky position—they're ready for you. Standard effect. Roll it."

Kira's player: [Rolls 3d6: 2, 4, 5] "Got a 5."

GM: "Partial success. You slam into him, axe biting deep—mark 1 stress on bandits—but another one flanks you. Take 1 stress or give ground?"

Kira's player: "I take the stress. I don't retreat."

GM: "The blade scores across your ribs but you hold position. Marcus, they're focused on Kira—what are you doing?"

Resource Mechanics

Stress (harm and pushing)

  • Mark when taking harm or pushing beyond limits

Fate Points (narrative currency)

  • Start each session with 2
  • Spend to: Add +1d6, reroll all dice, or establish minor story fact
  • Earn by: Accepting compels or complications

Advancement Ticks

  • Mark one per session
  • Three ticks = one advancement
  • Options: new aspect, improve approach, new heroic ability, narrative reward

Level 6: Presentation Architecture

The Framework Asks: How Does the Game Communicate With Players?

Presentation architecture is how the game delivers information, feedback, and cognitive framing. The framework reminds us: UI is game. The same mechanics with different presentation produce different experiences.

For TTRPGs, "presentation" means physical artifacts, verbal structures, session rituals, and information visibility.

The Six System Functions as Presentation Decisions

1. Adjudication: Communicating Outcomes

Dice are player-facing and public.

  • Players roll their own dice (agency)
  • Rolls happen in the open (shared experience)
  • Everyone sees results simultaneously (collective reaction)
  • GM never rolls secretly (transparency builds trust)

Results are announced fiction-first.

Not: "You got a 4, that's a partial success."

Instead: "Your blade catches the bandit's sword arm—he drops his weapon, but his partner is already swinging at your back. That's partial: you got what you wanted, but there's a cost."

Position and Effect stated before rolling. GM always says the framing before dice hit table. Transparent judgment.

2. Information Management: What Players Know

Aggressive transparency with fictional uncertainty.

Players always know:

  • Their character's capabilities (on the card)
  • The current situation (GM states clearly)
  • The stakes (GM states before rolls)
  • What happened (GM narrates immediately)

Players may not know:

  • NPC motivations (discovered through play)
  • Hidden threats (revealed when encountered)
  • Future consequences (emerge from choices)

The distinction: System information is always clear. Fictional information can be hidden.

Session state is visible. Stress boxes tracked on cards. Enemy stress is public. Scene count visible on GM dashboard. Fate points are physical tokens.

3. Content Generation: How Content Emerges

Content emerges from multiple sources:

  • From characters: Aspects generate hooks, backstory provides NPCs
  • From GM tools: Templates create situations, prompts generate complications
  • From play: Choices create consequences, dice create surprises

Content is generated just-in-time, not pre-built. GM prepares situations, not scripts. Content emerges in play.

4. State Persistence: What the Game Remembers

Within session: Stress, fate points, fictional positioning, scene outcomes

Across sessions: Character advancement, aspects, world state, relationships, campaign threads

Resets between sessions: Stress (full heal), fate points (start with 2)

The Session Record is the persistence mechanism. Created in 5 minutes, readable in 2 minutes, carries memory across gaps.

5. Feedback: How the Game Signals

Verbal feedback is immediate. When dice roll, GM narrates outcome within seconds. Outcome includes fictional result AND consequences.

Stress marking is tactile. Physical checkboxes make harm visible.

Fate point economy is physical. Tokens move between players and GM. Visible, tangible.

6. Cognitive Framing: How Players Learn

Fiction-first language.

Players think in fiction: "I intimidate the guard" GM translates to mechanics: "That's Bold. Risky position. Roll it."

Players learn that they describe actions; the system responds.

Minimal rules, learned through play. Entire player-facing system fits on a reference card. After one session, most players don't need it.

Level 7: Parameters

The Framework Asks: What Specific Numbers Calibrate the Systems?

Parameters are the concrete values that tune mechanics to deliver intended experience. The framework reminds us: numbers derive from measurable targets, not intuition.

The Measurables Bridge

Between dynamics and parameters sits a critical step: measurables—quantifiable indicators that a dynamic is occurring.

Key Measurables

For Fellowship + Narrative:

  • All players contribute to fiction each scene
  • At least one shared reaction moment (laughter, gasps) per session
  • Players reference past sessions unprompted by session 3
  • Time from session start to first in-character action: <10 minutes

For Combat:

  • Combat scenes complete in 10-15 minutes
  • Each player acts at least twice per combat
  • At least one character reaches 3+ stress per session

For Progression:

  • Players gain advancement every 3 sessions
  • Characters at session 10 feel different from session 1

For Session Pacing:

  • 4-6 scenes per session
  • Session completes in 2-hour window
  • Episode arc completes (no mid-story stops)

Conclusion: What the Derivation Demonstrates

The Framework in Action

We began with a request that could have gone anywhere: "Help three busy professionals play D&D together." Without structure, a designer might have started hacking D&D's encumbrance rules or debating initiative systems. Instead, the framework forced a different question first: what do these players actually need?

The answer—Heroic Adventure, not Tactical Combat—reframed the entire project. We weren't fixing D&D; we were building something for a different purpose entirely. That insight emerged from Level 0, before any mechanical work began.

This is what the framework does. It doesn't generate designs automatically. It asks the right questions in the right order, and each answer constrains what comes next.

The Derivation Chain

Watch how each level built on the previous:

Level 0 established that monthly sessions with long gaps required episodic structure and external memory support. This wasn't a preference—it was a constraint.

Level 1 identified Fellowship and Narrative as primary aesthetics, but flagged a tension: Narrative typically requires engagement that casual schedules can't support. Rather than compromise, we carried this forward as a design challenge.

Level 2 translated those aesthetics into required dynamics, including "rapid context reconstruction"—a dynamic that doesn't exist in most games because most games don't need it.

Level 3 designed the temporal architecture around that constraint: Previously On, Next Time On, Session Records. The memory problem got solved through structure, not rules.

Level 4 eliminated most balance paradigms through constraint analysis. Consistency requires comprehensive rules these players can't maintain. Adaptive requires technology that doesn't fit tabletop. Framework was the only viable option—not a preference, a derivation.

Level 5 built mechanics that serve the dynamics: fiction-first resolution, Position/Effect for transparent GM judgment, stress that resets between sessions, aspects that externalize character identity onto physical cards.

Level 6 specified how everything reaches players: verbal rituals that shift modes, physical artifacts that survive gaps, GM tools that enable Framework balance without overwhelming preparation.

Level 7 derived specific numbers from measurable targets: 5 stress boxes because heroes should survive 3-5 hits; 3d6 baseline because 82% success rate feels heroic; 10-15 minute combats because that's what the time budget allows.

No level was skipped. No level was arbitrary. Each built on what came before.

What We Built

Hearthfire is a complete tabletop RPG designed for a specific audience with specific constraints:

  • Sessions that start in under 10 minutes
  • Episodes that complete in 90 minutes
  • Gaps of 4-6 weeks that don't kill the story
  • Rules that disappear during play
  • Characters that fit on index cards
  • GM prep measured in minutes, not hours

Whether Hearthfire is good remains to be tested at actual tables. But it is coherent—every piece serves the whole, and that whole serves the original request.

The Conversation Structure

This chapter presented the framework as a conversation between designer and tool. That structure reflects something true about how the framework works in practice.

The framework doesn't hand you answers. It asks questions. When you answer, it asks follow-up questions, points out tensions, and constrains your options. You're still making every decision—but you're making them in an order that builds toward coherent design rather than accumulated compromises.

A designer using this framework will have different conversations than the one shown here. Their context will differ. Their aesthetic priorities will differ. Their solutions will differ. But the structure of the conversation—the levels, the derivation chain, the way each answer shapes the next question—that structure transfers.

The Production Gap

One thing this derivation revealed: the framework produces designs, not documents.

At the end of Level 7, we had a complete, validated system specification. We did not have printable character sheets, quick reference cards, a starting adventure, or an example of play. Those production artifacts require additional work—work the framework doesn't address.

This isn't a flaw. Design and production are different activities. But designers should understand that completing a derivation is not the same as completing a game. The path from validated design to playable product requires attention to presentation, layout, teaching, and accessibility that the framework treats as downstream concerns.

What Makes This Different

Many design methodologies start with mechanics and hope aesthetics emerge. The framework inverts this: start with what players should feel, derive what dynamics create those feelings, then build mechanics that generate those dynamics.

Many design methodologies treat balance as a tuning problem—playtest and adjust until it feels right. The framework treats balance as an architectural decision made early, with specific consequences for every subsequent choice.

Many design methodologies skip from concept to implementation. The framework insists on temporal architecture—how does this game structure time?—before any mechanical work begins. This prevents a common failure mode: systems that work moment-to-moment but fail session-to-session or campaign-to-campaign.

Using This Chapter

If you're working through the framework yourself, this chapter offers:

A complete example of derivation from request to validated system. When you're unsure what a level should produce, look at what it produced here.

A model for the conversation between designer and framework. Notice how each level's output becomes the next level's input. Notice how tensions get flagged and carried forward rather than ignored or immediately resolved.

A demonstration of constraint productivity. Every limitation—monthly sessions, casual engagement, long gaps—became a design feature. Constraints aren't problems to overcome; they're parameters that shape solutions.

Evidence that the framework scales. Hearthfire addresses a complex design challenge (Fellowship + Narrative for casual players with episodic schedules) that could easily have produced incoherent compromises. The framework kept everything aligned.

The Name

We called this system Hearthfire—a name that evokes friends gathering around warmth, the comfort of reunion, light against encroaching darkness.

The name emerged late, but it captures what the derivation was always building toward: a game for people who can't play often but want those rare sessions to matter. A game where the fire is always waiting, no matter how long you've been away.

The framework asked questions. The designer answered them. A game emerged.

That's what structured design looks like.
