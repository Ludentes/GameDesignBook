The Design Framework: Phase 4 - Content Specification

Part 7: Phase 4 - Content Specification

"How Much Content Do You Need?"

You have your mechanics, resources, and parameters defined. Now you face the practical question: how much content must you create?

This isn't about perfection—it's about sufficiency. Too little content and players exhaust your game quickly. Too much and you waste resources creating content players never see. This phase helps you calculate "enough."

Content needs vary dramatically based on:

  • Aesthetics (Discovery consumes content fast, Mastery re-uses it)
  • Architecture (loops require different content than narrative arcs)
  • Medium (video games vs. TTRPGs have different content types)
  • Budget (what's realistic to create?)

This phase provides calculation methods, heuristics, and decision frameworks to determine your content requirements.

Step 4.1: Calculate Content Needs

The Base Challenge

Content is expensive to create but easy to consume. Players can exhaust hours of authored content in minutes if they're looking for novelty. But content that supports mastery or challenge can be replayed hundreds of times.

The key question: What is your content consumption rate?

This depends primarily on your aesthetic profile and scaffold architecture.

Content Consumption by Aesthetic

Discovery-Heavy Games

Consumption Pattern: Fast—novelty is the content

Why: Discovery aesthetic requires new things to discover. Once discovered, content is "spent."

Heuristic: "Discovery games need 3x more unique content than Mastery games for the same play hours"

Calculation Method:

Examples:

  • Subnautica: Hundreds of unique biomes, creatures, crafting recipes, story beats
  • Outer Wilds: Every planet has unique mechanics and secrets
  • No Man's Sky: Procedural generation creates unlimited variety (but struggled with "sameness")

Content Types for Discovery:

  • New locations/biomes
  • New mechanics/interactions
  • Hidden secrets
  • Lore/story reveals
  • Unique encounters
  • Novel combinations

Warning Signs:

  • Players report "seeing everything" too quickly
  • Repetition noticed within first 10 hours
  • Reddit posts: "Is this all there is?"

Mastery-Heavy Games

Consumption Pattern: Slow—improvement is the content

Why: Mastery aesthetic comes from getting better at same challenges. Content is replayable.

Heuristic: "Mastery games need depth over breadth—few challenges, many replays"

Calculation Method:

Examples:

  • Into the Breach: 8 island campaigns, replayed hundreds of times
  • Slay the Spire: 4 characters × 3 acts = 12 runs, but players do 100+ runs
  • Dark Souls bosses: 20-30 bosses, each fought dozens of times while learning

Content Types for Mastery:

  • Core challenges with depth
  • Difficulty variations
  • Scoring/ranking systems
  • Optimization opportunities
  • Skill expression variance

Warning Signs:

  • Content exhausted but no reason to replay
  • Skill ceiling reached too quickly
  • No interesting optimization space

Challenge-Heavy Games

Consumption Pattern: Moderate—variety prevents pattern memorization

Why: Challenge aesthetic needs enough variety that solutions don't become rote, but can re-use content if difficulty scales.

Heuristic: "Challenge games need enough variety to prevent memorization, plus difficulty progression"

Calculation Method:

Examples:

  • Wordle: 1 challenge per day, solution exists but finding it is the challenge
  • Portal: 19 test chambers, most played 1-3 times
  • Celeste: 200+ screens, organized into difficulty tiers

Content Types for Challenge:

  • Puzzles with solutions
  • Skill-based obstacles
  • Strategic scenarios
  • Difficulty-scaled variations

Warning Signs:

  • Players memorize patterns (challenge becomes execution)
  • Too hard too fast (players quit)
  • Too easy throughout (boredom)

Narrative-Heavy Games

Consumption Pattern: Linear—story consumed once (branching increases needs)

Why: Narrative aesthetic is about experiencing story beats. Unlike mechanics, story doesn't replay well without branching.

Heuristic: "Linear narratives need 1:1 content-to-playtime ratio; branching narratives need more"

Calculation Method:

Examples:

  • The Last of Us: ~15 hour game, ~15 hours of content (linear)
  • Disco Elysium: ~25 hour game, ~40 hours of written content (branching dialogue)
  • 80 Days: ~2 hour playthroughs, but 500+ cities and events for replayability

Content Types for Narrative:

  • Story beats/scenes
  • Dialogue trees
  • Character development arcs
  • Environmental storytelling
  • Branching paths

Warning Signs:

  • Second playthrough feels identical
  • Choices don't matter (false branching)
  • Padding between story beats

Fellowship-Heavy Games

Consumption Pattern: Content is context—social interaction is the real content

Why: Fellowship aesthetic comes from player interaction. Your content creates situations for interaction, but players generate the experiences.

Heuristic: "Social games need enough content to contextualize interaction, not to be the main attraction"

Calculation Method:

Examples:

  • Among Us: 4 maps, but social deduction is the content
  • D&D: Same dungeon played multiple times with different groups feels different
  • MMO raids: 10-20 raid encounters can entertain for hundreds of hours

Content Types for Fellowship:

  • Social situations/scenarios
  • Cooperative challenges
  • Competition frameworks
  • Communication tools
  • Shared goals

Warning Signs:

  • Players want to socialize but game doesn't provide context
  • Content so consuming it overshadows social
  • No tools for coordination/communication

Horror-Heavy Games

Consumption Pattern: Single-use—horror depends on not knowing what's coming

Why: Horror aesthetic requires surprise, dread of unknown. Replay undermines horror unless mechanics create emergent fear.

Heuristic: "Horror games must complete their arc in one playthrough OR have systemic horror that stays scary"

Calculation Method:

Examples:

  • Alien: Isolation: 15-hour campaign, designed for one playthrough
  • Amnesia: 8 hours, single playthrough
  • Phasmophobia: Systemic horror (AI behavior) allows replay
  • Aliens TTRPG (our example): 3-4 hour one-shot, no replay

Content Types for Horror:

  • Revelation sequences
  • Environmental horror
  • Scripted scares (limited replay)
  • Systemic threat behavior (replayable)
  • Consequence systems

Warning Signs:

  • Second playthrough not scary
  • Horror becomes routine
  • Players learn to "game" the fear

Step 4.2: Procedural vs. Authored Decision

When to Use Procedural Generation

Procedural Generation Strengths:

  • Creates large content volumes efficiently
  • Enables unlimited variety
  • Reduces storage/download size
  • Allows dynamic difficulty adjustment
  • Cheaper to produce at scale

Procedural Generation Weaknesses:

  • Often feels "samey" despite variety
  • Requires complex systems to feel hand-crafted
  • Difficult to balance difficulty
  • Can't tell authored stories well
  • Emergent bugs hard to predict

Use Procedural When:

  • High content volume needed (100+ elements)
  • Content follows systemic rules
  • Budget/timeline limited
  • Aesthetic tolerates variation (Discovery, Challenge, Mastery)
  • Replayability is priority

Examples of Good Procedural:

  • Slay the Spire: Card encounters procedurally arranged
  • Spelunky: Level layouts generated but hand-crafted feeling
  • Minecraft: Terrain generation allows infinite worlds
  • Hades: Room combinations from template pools

When to Use Authored Content

Authored Content Strengths:

  • Every piece can be perfect
  • Enables precise narrative beats
  • Supports unique/memorable moments
  • Easier to balance exactly
  • Can break systemic rules for effect

Authored Content Weaknesses:

  • Expensive to create (time and money)
  • Limited volume possible
  • Requires storage space
  • Fixed content can be exhausted
  • Replay value limited

Use Authored When:

  • Narrative is primary aesthetic
  • Quality critical over quantity
  • Lower volume needed (<100 elements)
  • Aesthetic requires precision (Narrative, Horror)
  • Budget allows handcrafting

Examples of Good Authored:

  • The Last of Us: Every story beat handcrafted
  • Portal: Every test chamber designed precisely
  • Disco Elysium: Every conversation written specifically
  • Inside: Every sequence authored for specific experience

The Hybrid Approach (Most Common)

Reality: Most games use both.

Common Patterns:

Pattern 1: Authored Templates, Procedural Variations

  • Hand-craft 20 puzzle templates
  • Procedurally vary start conditions, difficulty
  • Result: Feels authored but has variety
  • Example: Daily puzzle games, Tetris variants

Pattern 2: Procedural Layout, Authored Content

  • Procedurally generate dungeon layouts
  • Hand-craft all enemies, items, encounters
  • Result: Feels fresh but quality consistent
  • Example: Binding of Isaac, Enter the Gungeon

Pattern 3: Authored Critical Path, Procedural Side Content

  • Hand-craft main story/missions
  • Procedurally generate side quests, collectibles
  • Result: Story beats perfect, bulk content acceptable
  • Example: Assassin's Creed, many open-world games

Pattern 4: Procedural with Authored "Showpieces"

  • Mostly procedural generation
  • Hand-craft boss fights, special encounters
  • Result: Variety with memorable highlights
  • Example: Risk of Rain 2, Dead Cells

Procedural Generation Pitfalls

Pitfall 1: "Sameness Despite Variety"

  • Problem: 1000 statistically different dungeons that feel identical
  • Solution: Procedural constraints that create distinct archetypes
  • Example: Spelunky themes (jungle, ice, temple) with different mechanics

Pitfall 2: "Impossible or Trivial Generations"

  • Problem: RNG creates unwinnable or boring scenarios
  • Solution: Validation passes, playtest procedural seeds extensively
  • Example: Into the Breach guarantees solvability

Pitfall 3: "No Memorable Moments"

  • Problem: Nothing stands out when everything is generated
  • Solution: Hybrid approach with authored peaks
  • Example: Hades' boss fights are always authored and placed

Pitfall 4: "Difficulty Spikes"

  • Problem: Random generation creates unfair difficulty jumps
  • Solution: Difficulty budgets, escalation rules
  • Example: Slay the Spire's act structure controls escalation

Implementation Notes

For Procedural:

  • Build robust testing tools (need to test thousands of generations)
  • Plan for edge cases (RNG will find them)
  • Include designer controls (seed specific generations, adjust weightings)
  • Budget more time than expected (procedural systems complex)

For Authored:

  • Create content pipelines (tools, templates, workflows)
  • Plan for localization (if applicable)
  • Budget storage/download costs
  • Accept volume limitations

For Hybrid:

  • Clearly define boundary (what's authored, what's procedural)
  • Ensure seamless integration (transitions shouldn't feel jarring)
  • Test both systems independently and together

Step 4.3: Information Architecture

What Players Need to Know

Information is content. Teaching players what they need to know, when they need to know it, is as important as creating the content itself.

The Information Problem:

  • Tell too much up front → cognitive overload, boredom
  • Tell too little → confusion, frustration
  • Tell at wrong time → information forgotten when needed

The Goal: Players know what they need, when they need it, without overwhelming them or leaving them lost.

Tutorial Design

Philosophy: Tutorials should be invisible. Best case: players learn by playing, never realize they're being taught.

Tutorial Content Questions:

What must players know immediately? (First 60 seconds)

  • Core verb (what button makes things happen)
  • Primary goal (what am I trying to do)
  • Failure condition (how do I know if I'm doing it wrong)

What can be learned through play? (First 10 minutes)

  • Secondary mechanics
  • Resource management basics
  • Success strategies

What should be discovered? (After comfort established)

  • Advanced techniques
  • Optimization strategies
  • Hidden systems

What should never be explained?

  • Things intuitive from context
  • Discovery content (ruins the surprise)
  • Optional systems (only explain if player engages)

Tutorial Length by Cognitive Load:

Low Cognitive Load (Casual):

  • Tutorial: 2-5 minutes maximum
  • Complexity adds gradually over first hour
  • Heavy use of visual cues, minimal text

Moderate Cognitive Load (Midcore):

  • Tutorial: 5-15 minutes
  • Can introduce multiple systems if clear
  • Mix of guided and experimental learning

High Cognitive Load (Hardcore):

  • Tutorial: 10-30 minutes (or optional)
  • Can explain complex systems upfront
  • May include reference materials
  • Players expect and tolerate learning curve

Tutorial Integration Strategies:

Integrated Tutorial (Best):

  • Tutorial IS the first level/session
  • Real gameplay from the start, but simplified
  • Teaches through doing, not telling
  • Example: Portal's first 10 chambers, Mega Man X's opening stage

Separated Tutorial (Acceptable):

  • Distinct tutorial mode/level
  • Clear transition to "real game"
  • Can be skipped on replay
  • Example: Most fighting games, strategy games

No Tutorial (Risky but Valid):

  • Players learn entirely through experimentation
  • Requires extremely intuitive design
  • Works for very simple games or specific audiences
  • Example: Tetris, many early arcade games

Teaching Methods:

Method 1: Contextual Prompts

  • Show information when relevant
  • Dismiss when understood
  • Don't block gameplay
  • Example: "Press X to jump" appears near first gap

Method 2: Forced Success

  • Make failure impossible during teaching moment
  • Guide player to correct action
  • Remove training wheels after demonstrated
  • Example: First enemy has 1 HP, teaches combat

Method 3: Safe Experimentation

  • Create no-stakes environment for testing
  • Encourage trying things out
  • Explain results of actions
  • Example: Shooting range, practice mode

Method 4: Demonstrate, Then Challenge

  • Show the mechanic in action (NPC does it, cutscene)
  • Ask player to replicate
  • Introduce challenge using that mechanic
  • Example: NPC companion teaches by example

Onboarding Sequence

Beyond Tutorial: First Week (or equivalent)

Tutorials teach basics. Onboarding reveals depth gradually.

Onboarding Goals:

  • Player reaches competence (not mastery)
  • All core systems introduced
  • First meaningful success achieved
  • Player decides to continue

Onboarding Timeline by Player Profile:

Casual (Multiple Short Sessions):

  • Session 1: Core loop only
  • Session 2: First additional system
  • Session 3: Second additional system
  • Session 4-5: Full feature set revealed
  • Session 6+: Comfortable with everything

Midcore (Fewer, Longer Sessions):

  • Session 1 (Hour 1): Core loop + first system
  • Session 1 (Hour 2): Second and third systems
  • Session 2: Full feature complexity
  • Session 3+: Mastering interactions

Hardcore (Long Sessions, High Tolerance):

  • Session 1 (Hour 1-2): Most/all systems introduced
  • Session 1 (Hour 3-4): Practice and experimentation
  • Session 2+: Optimization and mastery

Complexity Ramping:

Start with minimal complexity, add layers:

Don't add everything at once:

Feature Gating:

Progressive Unlock:

  • Lock advanced features initially
  • Unlock as player demonstrates understanding
  • Each unlock feels like progression (Discovery aesthetic)
  • Example: New weapons unlock as you master previous ones

Context-Triggered Explanations:

  • Don't explain until player encounters
  • "Just-in-time" information delivery
  • Reduces upfront cognitive load
  • Example: Explain crafting when player picks up first ingredient

Optional Depth:

  • Core systems mandatory
  • Advanced systems optional
  • Game playable without mastering everything
  • Example: Can finish game without learning advanced combos

Feedback Systems

Feedback is information about game state. Players need feedback at multiple time scales.

Immediate Feedback (Micro Loop)

  • Action results shown instantly (<100ms)
  • Visual/audio confirmation
  • State changes visible
  • Examples: Hit effects, card played animation, health bar change

Short-Term Feedback (Meso Loop)

  • Progress toward session goal
  • Resource accumulation/depletion
  • Victory/defeat conditions approaching
  • Examples: Boss health bar, mission progress percentage, time remaining

Long-Term Feedback (Macro Loop)

  • Overall progression
  • Unlocks approaching
  • Mastery improvement
  • Examples: Level up progress, achievement progress, skill rating trends

Feedback Design Principles:

Clarity: Is it obvious what the feedback means?

  • Bad: Vague visual effect (what did that do?)
  • Good: Clear numeric change (+10 HP, -5 MP)

Immediacy: Does feedback appear when needed?

  • Bad: Damage numbers appear 2 seconds after hit
  • Good: Damage numbers appear instantly

Appropriate Detail: Right amount of information for player type?

  • Casual: Simple (health bar)
  • Midcore: Moderate (health numbers)
  • Hardcore: Detailed (damage types, resistances, DPS meter)

Consistency: Does same feedback always mean same thing?

  • Bad: Red flash means damage sometimes, low health other times
  • Good: Red flash always damage, red screen edge always low health

Non-Intrusive: Does feedback help without blocking gameplay?

  • Bad: Modal popup interrupts action
  • Good: Peripheral information visible but not blocking

Information Overload Prevention

Signs of Too Much Information:

  • Players ignore important information
  • Tutorial completion rate low
  • Support requests about "how do I...?"
  • Players don't use available systems

Solutions:

Progressive Disclosure:

  • Show only what's needed now
  • Reveal complexity gradually
  • Gate advanced features

Reduce Necessary Information:

  • Cut unnecessary systems
  • Simplify mechanics
  • Make more things intuitive

Better Presentation:

  • Icons over text
  • Visual language
  • Contextual help

Allow Ignorance:

  • Game playable without knowing everything
  • Advanced info optional
  • No punishment for ignoring optional systems

Step 4.4: Context Integration (Build/Suggest/Omit)

When Does Setting Enter?

If you started Theme-First: Setting was chosen early, now ensure coherence.

If you started other ways: Setting can be chosen now (after mechanics solid) or game can remain abstract.

The Question: Does your game need thematic dressing?

Abstract is Valid:

  • Tetris, Chess, Go, Wordle
  • Mechanics are the content
  • Theme would add cognitive load without benefit
  • Player focus is pure gameplay

Theme Adds Value When:

  • Helps players understand mechanics (theme makes rules intuitive)
  • Supports aesthetic goals (horror theme for horror aesthetic)
  • Creates market appeal (players attracted to theme)
  • Provides content structure (theme suggests content)

The Build/Suggest/Omit Framework

Reference: Context Chapter (Part 5: Build/Suggest/Omit)

This framework helps ensure mechanics and setting reinforce each other rather than fight.

Build: Essential Thematic Elements

  • What MUST be present for setting to work?
  • What makes the setting recognizable?
  • What supports the mechanics directly?

Suggest: Supporting Atmospheric Elements

  • What adds flavor without adding complexity?
  • What reinforces the mood/tone?
  • What fills in the world peripherally?

Omit: Contradictory or Distracting Elements

  • What would players expect but fights mechanics?
  • What would add cognitive load without value?
  • What contradicts aesthetic goals?

Applying Build/Suggest/Omit

Example 1: Spatial Puzzle Game (from our Busy Parents scenario)

If Abstract (mechanics-first):

  • Build: Clear grid, distinct tiles, goal pattern visibility
  • Suggest: Pleasant colors, satisfying sounds
  • Omit: All narrative, character, backstory

If Themed (garden planting):

  • Build: Tiles as plants, grid as garden plot, goal as desired arrangement
  • Suggest: Seasons changing, growth animations, nature sounds
  • Omit: Dialogue, story, characters (keep focus on puzzle)

Trade-off: Theme adds understanding (plants in garden intuitive) but adds cognitive load (memorizing which tile is which plant).

Decision: For casual players with low cognitive budget, probably stay abstract. For slightly higher budget, light theme acceptable.

Example 2: Colonial Horror TTRPG (from our Aliens scenario)

Setting: Sci-fi Colonial Horror (theme-first)

Build:

  • Colonial power dynamic (players are authorities)
  • Resource extraction (reason for conflict)
  • Native population (source of horror)
  • Information control (company withholds truth)
  • Irreversible consequences (horror from permanence)

Suggest:

  • Corporate bureaucracy (forms, reports)
  • Communication barriers (language/culture)
  • Economic pressure (quotas, investors)
  • Frontier mythology ("pioneers" rhetoric)
  • Environmental details (dust, stars, isolation)

Omit:

  • Combat focus (would make violence exciting, not horrifying)
  • Power fantasy elements (upgrades, getting stronger)
  • Easy moral solutions ("be nice" doesn't work)
  • Humor/levity (until epilogue debrief)
  • Redemption mechanics (can't undo complicity)

Result: Mechanics and setting mutually reinforce horror aesthetic. Setting isn't decoration—it's essential to the experience.

Mechanics and Setting Coherence

Coherent: Mechanics and setting make sense together

  • Example: Deck-building in a game about building a criminal empire (Griftlands)
  • Card play = making plans, combinations = complex schemes
  • Theme makes mechanics intuitive

Incoherent ("Space Coffee"): Mechanics and setting contradict

  • Example: Farming simulation where you can't grow coffee because you're in space
  • Setting suggests coffee should exist, mechanics arbitrarily remove it
  • Players confused by contradiction

Testing Coherence:

  • Can players intuit mechanics from setting?
  • Does setting explain why mechanics work this way?
  • Are there contradictions that confuse players?
  • Does theme add clarity or cognitive load?

Theme Affects Content

Content Creation Changes with Setting:

Abstract Game:

  • Content is mechanical variations
  • No need for visual uniqueness
  • Can reuse assets freely
  • Creation time: Faster

Themed Game:

  • Content needs thematic justification
  • Visual/narrative uniqueness expected
  • Asset variety required
  • Creation time: Slower

Example: Enemy Varieties

Abstract (Tetris-like):

  • 7 piece shapes (all mechanical)
  • No need for unique appearances
  • Pieces can repeat

Themed (Fantasy RPG):

  • 30 enemy types expected
  • Each needs unique appearance
  • Each needs thematic justification
  • Repeated enemies feel cheap

Budget Implications:

  • Abstract: Mechanics-only budget
  • Themed: Mechanics + Art + Writing + Audio budget

Iterating Mechanics ↔ Setting

Theme and mechanics are interdependent. Expect to iterate:

Iteration Pattern 1: Mechanics → Theme Adjustment

  • Mechanics suggest theme: "This plays like heist planning" → crime theme
  • Mechanics constrain theme: "Turn-based combat" → can't be real-time shooter theme
  • Mechanics create opportunities: "Resource conversion" → alchemy/crafting theme

Iteration Pattern 2: Theme → Mechanics Adjustment

  • Theme suggests mechanics: "Space exploration" → discovery mechanics
  • Theme constrains mechanics: "Horror" → avoid power fantasy mechanics
  • Theme creates expectations: "Ninja" → should have stealth, mobility

How to Iterate:

  1. Start with one (mechanics or theme)
  2. Apply the other
  3. Check coherence: Do they reinforce or fight?
  4. Adjust weaker element (usually easier to adjust theme than mechanics)
  5. Repeat until coherent

When to Pivot:

  • If mechanics keep breaking theme, consider different theme
  • If theme keeps constraining mechanics, consider abstract approach
  • If iteration isn't converging, one element is wrong

Decision Point: Phase 4 Complete

Before proceeding to Phase 5 (Validation), verify you have:

Content Specification:

  • Calculated content quantity needed (formulas applied)
  • Decided procedural vs. authored (or hybrid approach)
  • Content volume is realistic for budget/timeline
  • Content serves aesthetic goals (not padding)

Information Architecture:

  • Tutorial scope defined (what's taught when)
  • Onboarding sequence planned (complexity ramping)
  • Feedback systems designed (immediate, short-term, long-term)
  • Information load appropriate for player profile

Context Integration:

  • Setting chosen (or abstract decision made)
  • Build/Suggest/Omit applied (if themed)
  • Mechanics and setting coherent (no "space coffee")
  • Theme adds value or is omitted entirely

Practical Requirements:

  • You can list what needs to be created
  • You know how much is "enough"
  • Creation is feasible with resources
  • Content plan supports intended play hours

Output from Phase 4: Complete content specification including quantity, type, authorship method, information delivery plan, and thematic integration (if applicable).

Common Phase 4 Problems

Problem: "I calculated I need 500 hours of content but have 3 months"

  • Solution: Reduce scope (lower target hours) or increase procedural generation
  • Or: Pivot to aesthetic that requires less content (Mastery vs. Discovery)

Problem: "My theme requires systems that blow the cognitive budget"

  • Solution: Simplify theme or go abstract
  • Or: Accept higher cognitive load and adjust player profile target

Problem: "Players keep getting lost in my tutorial"

  • Solution: Tutorial is too long or too complex
  • Reduce tutorial scope, teach through play instead

Problem: "Content feels repetitive despite variety"

  • Solution: Variety isn't meaningful (statistical vs. experiential difference)
  • Create fewer types with more depth instead of many shallow types

Problem: "Procedural generation creates broken scenarios"

  • Solution: Need validation passes, constraints, or hand-crafted templates
  • Or: Reduce procedural scope, hand-craft more

Problem: "Themed content creation taking 3x longer than expected"

  • Solution: Theme is more expensive than anticipated
  • Simplify theme or reduce content volume

You're now ready to proceed to Phase 5: Validation & Iteration.

Phase 5 will help you:

  • Audit coherence across all design elements
  • Predict likely dynamics before implementation
  • Design effective playtests
  • Identify what level needs iteration (parameters, mechanics, architecture, or aesthetic)

Turn to Phase 5 when ready to validate your complete design.
