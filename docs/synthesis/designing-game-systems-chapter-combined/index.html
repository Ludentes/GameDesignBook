Designing Game Systems

Part 1: Introduction

Every chapter in this book has examined a piece of game design: aesthetics and what players seek from games, context and how it shapes mechanical needs, resources and how they create meaning through scarcity, cognitive load and the limits of player attention, temporal architecture and the rhythm of play, balance and the calibration of experience. Each piece matters. But pieces alone don't make a game.

This chapter is about assembly. Not in the mechanical sense of snapping components together, but in the architectural sense: understanding how each element constrains and enables the others, how decisions at one level ripple through every subsequent choice, and how the whole must serve a unified experiential purpose.

The core insight is simple: game design follows a derivation chain. Each level of design constrains the next. You cannot determine parameters without first establishing mechanics. You cannot select mechanics without understanding what temporal structure they must support. You cannot design temporal structure without knowing what player behaviors you need. And you cannot identify required behaviors without clarity on what experience you're creating.

This isn't a claim about how designers should work—creativity strikes from any direction. It's a claim about how constraints flow. Wherever you start, you'll eventually need to answer questions in a specific logical order, because each answer depends on answers that come before it.

The Experiential Scaffold

Chapter 1 introduced the metaphor of games as experiential scaffolds: purpose-built structures that enable specific experiences. We don't build systems for their own sake. We build scaffolding that supports players in reaching emotional and experiential states they couldn't reliably reach alone.

This metaphor should guide everything that follows. At every level of the derivation chain, the question isn't "is this system elegant?" or "is this mechanic interesting?" but "does this scaffolding support the experience we're building toward?" A beautiful scaffold that doesn't reach the intended destination has failed, regardless of its craftsmanship.

Investment and Payoff

Chapter 4 established that players invest cognitive resources—attention, memory, decision-making effort—and receive aesthetic payoff in return. This transaction underlies all design decisions.

The derivation chain isn't just about logical dependency; it's about ensuring that every element earns its cognitive cost. A mechanic that fits within the complexity budget but doesn't contribute to aesthetic delivery is still a failure. Conversely, a mechanic that exceeds typical budget guidelines might be justified if its aesthetic contribution is proportionally large.

Throughout this chapter, we'll frame constraints not just as limits ("can we fit this?") but as transactions ("is this worth it?"). Casual players aren't simply "lower capacity"—they're seeking efficient transactions where modest investment yields satisfying payoff. Hardcore players aren't simply "higher capacity"—they're seeking substantial payoffs that justify substantial investment.

The Chain

The derivation chain has seven levels:

Level 0: Context — What's fixed before design begins? Medium, external constraints, and where you're starting from.

Level 1: Aesthetic Profile — What experience are you creating, and for whom? Primary and secondary aesthetics, player investment capacity, success criteria.

Level 2: Required Dynamics — What must players do to have that experience? The observable behaviors that create aesthetic payoff.

Level 3: Temporal Architecture — When do those behaviors occur, and for how long? Loop scales, durations, pacing patterns, stopping points.

Level 4: Balance Paradigm — What approach to rules creates the required dynamics within that temporal structure? Consistency, Framework, or Adaptive paradigm.

Level 5: Mechanical Specification — What specific systems implement the paradigm and create the dynamics? Core mechanics, resources, content approach.

Level 6: Parameters — What specific numbers calibrate the mechanics to deliver the right intensity of experience?

Level 7: Validation — Does the complete system actually produce the intended experience? Configuration coherence, playtesting, iteration.

Each level answers a specific question. Each level's answer constrains what's possible at subsequent levels. Skip a level, and you'll find yourself making arbitrary decisions that may undermine the whole.

How to Use This Chapter

This chapter is a reference, not a narrative to read once and absorb. During active design work, you'll return to specific sections as you encounter specific problems.

If you're starting a new design: Begin with Part 2 (Entry Points) to locate yourself, then work through Part 3 (The Derivation Chain) level by level.

If your design isn't working: Jump to Part 5 (Validation and Iteration) to diagnose which level contains the problem.

If you want to see the chain in action: Part 6 (Worked Examples) walks through complete derivations for two very different games.

The chapter assumes familiarity with previous chapters. Rather than repeat their content, it references them at the appropriate moments. You may find yourself flipping between this chapter and others—that's intended. This chapter provides the map; other chapters provide the detailed terrain.

A Note on Creativity

A framework this structured might seem to constrain creativity. It doesn't—or rather, it constrains creativity in productive ways.

Creative insight can strike anywhere: a mechanic that feels right, a theme that demands exploration, a constraint that forces innovation. The derivation chain doesn't tell you where ideas must come from. It tells you how to integrate ideas once you have them.

"I have a cool mechanic" becomes "what dynamics does this create, and what aesthetic do those dynamics serve?" The framework helps you understand your own inspiration, find its natural home in a larger system, and identify what else must be true for it to work.

The alternative—building without understanding how pieces connect—leads to systems where individual elements are interesting but the whole is incoherent. Configuration coherence (the test introduced in Chapter 1) requires that every element serve the same experiential purpose. The derivation chain is how you achieve that coherence systematically rather than accidentally.

Part 2: Entry Points

Designers rarely start with a blank slate. Usually something sparks the project: a feeling to capture, a constraint to work within, a theme to explore, or a mechanic that seems promising. These entry points differ, but they all converge on the same derivation chain.

Aesthetic-First Entry

"I want players to feel the triumph of overcoming genuine challenge."

Starting with aesthetic goals is the cleanest path through the chain. You know the destination; derivation reveals the route.

From aesthetic targets, derive:

  • What dynamics create this feeling? (Level 2)
  • What temporal structure supports these dynamics? (Level 3)
  • What paradigm serves this experience? (Level 4)
  • What mechanics enable these dynamics within this structure? (Level 5)
  • What parameters tune the experience correctly? (Level 6)

Convergence point: Level 1 (Aesthetic Profile) is already complete. Proceed directly down the chain.

Constraint-First Entry

"I have six months, a solo developer, and a mobile platform."

Constraints narrow the possibility space. They don't determine aesthetics, but they eliminate options.

Mobile platform eliminates certain input schemes. Six months eliminates certain content scales. Solo development eliminates certain technical ambitions. What remains is the viable space.

From constraints, derive:

  • What aesthetics are achievable within these limits? (Level 1)
  • What temporal structures fit the platform and scope? (Level 3)
  • What content scale is producible? (Level 5)

Convergence point: Constraints populate Level 0 (Context), then inform Level 1. Once aesthetic targets are established within constraints, proceed normally.

Theme-First Entry

"I want to make a game about the costs of moral compromise."

Theme suggests aesthetics but doesn't determine them. A game "about" moral compromise could emphasize Challenge (optimize outcomes despite costs), Tension (feel the weight of choices), Narrative (experience a story of compromise), or Discovery (learn what you're capable of).

The design question isn't "how do I make a game about this theme?" but "what experience do I want players to have while engaging with this theme?"

From theme, derive:

  • What aesthetic serves this theme? (Level 1)
  • What dynamics make the theme felt rather than merely present? (Level 2)

Convergence point: Theme informs Level 1, but the designer must still select specific aesthetic targets. Once selected, proceed normally.

Mechanic-First Entry

"I have this cool card-drafting mechanism I want to build around."

Starting with a mechanic means working backward to aesthetics and forward to parameters simultaneously.

Backward: What experiences does this mechanic naturally support? Card drafting creates anticipation, planning, and competition for scarce options. What aesthetic does that serve? Challenge? Mastery? Tension?

Forward: Given the aesthetic this mechanic serves, what temporal structure supports it? What other mechanics complement it? What parameters tune it correctly?

Convergence point: The mechanic enters at Level 5. Derive Levels 1-4 backward (what aesthetic and structure does this mechanic serve?), then proceed forward through Level 6.

Part 3: The Derivation Chain

Level 0: Context

Before designing, establish what you're designing within.

Questions answered:

  • What medium? (Video game, tabletop, TTRPG, hybrid)
  • What platform constraints? (Mobile, console, physical components)
  • What scope constraints? (Team size, timeline, budget)
  • What business constraints? (Monetization requirements, audience expectations)
  • What content constraints? (Rating, cultural considerations)

Outputs:

  • Fixed constraints that cannot be designed around
  • Flexible constraints that could be challenged if necessary
  • Opportunities specific to this context

Context doesn't determine design, but it bounds it. A mobile game has different constraints than a tabletop game. A solo developer has different scope limits than a studio. Establish these before proceeding.

Level 1: Aesthetic Profile

The aesthetic profile defines what experience you're building toward.

Questions answered:

  • What primary aesthetic? (The core experience)
  • What secondary aesthetics? (Supporting experiences)
  • What excluded aesthetics? (Experiences that would conflict)
  • Who is the target player? (Casual, midcore, hardcore—and what that means for your design)
  • What defines success? (How will you know if the design works?)

Inputs required:

  • Context (Level 0)
  • Designer intention (what experience matters?)

Outputs:

  • Primary aesthetic with clear definition
  • Secondary aesthetics with priority order
  • Player profile with cognitive implications (see Chapter 4)
  • Success criteria that can be tested

Constraints imposed on subsequent levels:

  • Dynamics must create the specified aesthetics
  • Temporal structure must fit player profile
  • Complexity must fit cognitive budget

The cognitive budget begins here. A casual player profile means ~3 simultaneously tracked elements, short sessions, low complexity tolerance. A hardcore profile means ~7+ tracked elements, extended sessions, high complexity tolerance. This budget constrains every subsequent decision.

Level 2: Required Dynamics

Dynamics are the observable behaviors and experiences that emerge from play. They're the bridge between aesthetics (what players feel) and mechanics (what the system does).

Questions answered:

  • What must players do to experience the target aesthetic?
  • What must players feel at key moments?
  • What must players learn or master?
  • What behaviors would undermine the aesthetic?

Inputs required:

  • Aesthetic profile (Level 1)
  • Understanding of how aesthetics emerge from behavior

Deriving dynamics from aesthetics:

For Challenge aesthetic: Players must face genuine obstacles, develop skills, and experience earned triumph. Anti-dynamics include grinding (success through repetition, not skill) and confusion (failure without learning).

For Tension aesthetic: Players must face meaningful stakes with uncertain outcomes. Anti-dynamics include predictability (outcomes feel predetermined) and triviality (stakes don't matter).

For Narrative aesthetic: Players must experience story progression with agency. Anti-dynamics include railroading (choices don't matter) and incoherence (story doesn't track).

For Discovery aesthetic: Players must uncover new information through exploration. Anti-dynamics include exhaustion (nothing new to find) and arbitrariness (discoveries feel random, not earned).

Outputs:

  • Core loop behaviors (what players do repeatedly)
  • Peak moment dynamics (what creates memorable highlights)
  • Progression dynamics (how experience develops over time)
  • Anti-dynamics (what must be prevented)

Constraints imposed:

  • Temporal structure must support required dynamics
  • Mechanics must enable required behaviors
  • Parameters must tune for target feelings

Decision forcing: For dynamics that require player decisions, consider how the design ensures decisions actually happen. Indefinite stalling undermines most aesthetics. Solutions include time pressure, escalating consequences, resource depletion, or mechanical forcing functions.

Level 3: Temporal Architecture

Temporal architecture defines the rhythm and duration of play experiences.

Questions answered:

  • How long is a complete play session?
  • What loops exist within that session? (Micro, meso, macro)
  • How do loops relate to each other?
  • Where are natural stopping points?
  • How does pacing create the intended experience?

Inputs required:

  • Required dynamics (Level 2)
  • Player profile temporal constraints (from Level 1)
  • Medium constraints (from Level 0)

Deriving temporal structure from dynamics:

If the core dynamic requires "attempt, fail, learn, retry," loops must be short enough for multiple iterations per session. A learning dynamic with 30-minute loops means only 2-4 attempts in a casual session—probably too few for satisfying skill development.

If peak moments require buildup, the structure needs escalation patterns with appropriate duration. Climax without buildup falls flat; buildup without climax frustrates.

If the player profile is casual mobile, sessions are 5-15 minutes with potential interruption. Loops must complete within this window. Stopping points must allow clean exit without significant loss.

Outputs:

  • Session duration targets
  • Loop structure with specific durations
  • Pacing pattern (escalating, steady-state, episodic, etc.)
  • Stopping point locations
  • Content scale requirements (derived from loops × duration)

Content scale derivation:

Temporal architecture determines content volume. Calculate:

  • Loops per session × sessions before content exhaustion = minimum unique content pieces
  • Loop duration × consumption rate = content per loop

Example: A 7-minute run with 4 floors, 4 encounters per floor, 3-5 enemy types per encounter. Player should see variety, but also recognize enemies for pattern learning. 6-8 enemy types provides ~40 encounters before full rotation, allowing learning without exhaustion.

Constraints imposed:

  • Mechanics must fit within loop durations
  • Parameters must tune pacing correctly
  • Content must fill temporal structure without premature exhaustion

Level 4: Balance Paradigm

The balance paradigm determines how the game maintains appropriate challenge, fairness, and engagement.

Questions answered:

  • How is difficulty managed?
  • How much player customization is supported?
  • Who or what makes judgment calls?
  • How does the system handle edge cases?

Inputs required:

  • Temporal architecture (Level 3)
  • Target aesthetic (Level 1)
  • Medium (Level 0)

The three paradigms:

Consistency Paradigm: Fixed rules, deterministic outcomes, player skill as variable. Appropriate when mastery and pattern learning are core dynamics. The system behaves identically regardless of player state—improvement comes from player adaptation.

Framework Paradigm: Rules provide structure and boundaries, but human judgment determines specific outcomes. Appropriate when narrative flexibility, social dynamics, or moral complexity are core dynamics. A GM, group consensus, or interpretive process handles what rules cannot specify.

Adaptive Paradigm: System adjusts to player state, maintaining target experience regardless of skill level. Appropriate when consistent emotional arc matters more than skill measurement. The system gets easier when you struggle, harder when you dominate.

Framework paradigm requires mechanical scaffolding:

Framework doesn't mean "no mechanics." It means mechanics serve judgment rather than replace it.

A Framework paradigm TTRPG still needs:

  • Resolution mechanics for uncertainty (when dice roll vs. GM decision)
  • Resource mechanics for pacing (tracking attrition, stakes, progression)
  • Character mechanics for differentiation (what makes each player's role distinct)
  • Clear triggers defining when mechanics apply vs. when judgment applies

The mechanics scaffold the GM's judgment rather than replacing it. "Roll to see if you can reach the door in time" uses mechanics. "Do you sacrifice the prisoner?" does not—that's pure decision.

[LLM TAG: Framework paradigm has traditionally required human GMs for real-time judgment. LLMs potentially enable Framework-style adaptive adjudication in solo digital games—responding to edge cases, maintaining narrative coherence, and making judgment calls that pure rule systems cannot.]

Archetype implications:

Choosing an archetype (roguelike, euro-game, one-shot, etc.) commits you to certain paradigm expectations. Roguelikes assume Consistency—pattern learning requires predictable enemies. TTRPGs typically assume Framework—GMs adjudicate edge cases. Match paradigm to archetype expectations, or deliberately subvert them with clear understanding of what you're changing.

Outputs:

  • Selected paradigm with rationale
  • Edge case handling approach
  • Player expectation management strategy
  • For Framework: specific triggers for mechanics vs. judgment

Constraints imposed:

  • Mechanics must implement chosen paradigm
  • Parameters must support paradigm (Consistency needs tight tuning; Adaptive needs state detection)

Level 5: Mechanical Specification

Mechanics are the concrete rules and systems players interact with.

Questions answered:

  • What are the core mechanics?
  • What resources exist and what functions do they serve?
  • How is content generated or structured?
  • What is the cognitive load distribution?

Inputs required:

  • Balance paradigm (Level 4)
  • Temporal architecture (Level 3)
  • Required dynamics (Level 2)
  • Cognitive budget (from Level 1)

Deriving mechanics from dynamics:

Each required dynamic suggests mechanical implementations. Challenge dynamics need skill-testing mechanics. Tension dynamics need risk-reward mechanics. Narrative dynamics need choice-consequence mechanics. Discovery dynamics need information-revealing mechanics.

The cognitive budget constrains complexity. A casual game with 3-element budget can't support a 6-resource economy. Either reduce resources or automate some management.

Resource specification:

Resources shape possibility space (see Chapter 3). Specify:

  • What resources exist
  • What function each serves (Limiter, Currency, Tracker, Meta-resource)
  • Recovery patterns (fast/slow, predictable/variable)
  • How resources interact

Threshold-based state trackers: Some resources track internal state rather than external game state. These often have threshold effects rather than simple depletion—different mechanical effects at different levels. A "Humanity" resource might impose -1 to rolls at low levels and trigger a breaking point scene at zero. A "Heat" resource might increase enemy awareness at thresholds. These pacing resources shape emotional arc alongside mechanical arc.

Content specification:

Content type (procedural, authored, hybrid) must serve dynamics and fit production constraints.

  • Procedural: Enables variety and replayability; requires robust generation systems; risks incoherence
  • Authored: Enables precision and narrative control; requires production capacity; risks exhaustion
  • Hybrid: Procedural structure with authored key moments; balances variety with quality

Specify content categories needed:

  • Enemies/obstacles/challenges (how many, what variety)
  • Environments/settings/locations (how many, what variety)
  • Items/upgrades/options (how many, what variety)
  • Narrative content if applicable (word count, branch structure)
  • Audio/visual assets (scope estimate)

Safety mechanics (for applicable aesthetics):

Games targeting Tension, Horror, or emotionally challenging Narrative may require safety mechanics as design elements, not add-ons:

  • Consent frameworks (what players agree to before play)
  • Opt-out mechanisms (X-card, lines and veils)
  • Debrief structures (processing after intense content)
  • Break points in temporal structure (breathing room)

These affect temporal architecture (breaks must exist), content (alternatives for intense material), and sometimes mechanics (ways to reduce intensity).

Outputs:

  • Complete mechanical specification
  • Resource system design
  • Content requirements with categories and quantities
  • Cognitive load distribution across systems
  • Safety mechanics if applicable

Constraints imposed:

  • Parameters must tune these specific mechanics
  • Content must fill these specific categories

Level 6: Parameters

Parameters are the specific numbers that tune the mechanical systems.

Questions answered:

  • What are the specific values for each variable?
  • How do these values produce intended experience?
  • What measurables validate the parameters?

Inputs required:

  • Mechanical specification (Level 5)
  • Target dynamics with specific intensities (Level 2)
  • Temporal targets (Level 3)

Deriving parameters from measurables:

Start with target experience, express as measurable, derive parameter from measurable.

Example chain:

  • Target: "Encounters feel tense but winnable" (experience)
  • Measurable: "Player wins 70% of encounters, feels at risk in 80%" (testable)
  • Parameter: Enemy damage = player HP ÷ 4, so player survives ~4 hits (derived)

Work backward from experience to number, not forward from arbitrary number to hoped-for experience.

Content parameters:

Content volume derives from temporal architecture:

  • Enemy types: enough for variety across expected plays before exhaustion
  • Asset counts: sprites, sounds, words based on content type requirements
  • Upgrade/item counts: enough for meaningful choice without overwhelming

Parameter relationships:

Parameters interact. Changing one often requires adjusting others. Document relationships:

  • If enemy HP increases, encounter duration increases, loop timing changes
  • If resource recovery slows, pressure increases, cognitive load increases
  • If content volume decreases, repetition increases, engagement may decrease

Outputs:

  • Complete parameter specification
  • Rationale connecting each parameter to experience target
  • Identified parameter relationships and dependencies

Part 4: Multiplayer Considerations

The derivation chain works for multiplayer games, but multiplayer adds considerations at every level. This section covers what changes when designing for two or more players.

When This Section Applies

Not every game with multiple players requires multiplayer design thinking. The test: do players meaningfully affect each other's experience?

In parallel solitaire, players engage with the same system but don't interact meaningfully. The single-player derivation chain applies; multiplayer is social wrapper, not structural.

In true multiplayer, player interaction changes outcomes. Competition, cooperation, communication, or interference matter. Here, multiplayer considerations are load-bearing.

Multiplayer Modifications by Level

Level 1: Aesthetic Profile

Fellowship requires multiple players. Competition against humans differs from AI competition—human opponents adapt and carry social stakes. Social context becomes load-bearing: friends at a table play differently than strangers in matchmaking. Player count constrains design throughout the chain.

Level 2: Required Dynamics

Multiplayer introduces communication dynamics (conveying information between players), coordination dynamics (acting together), and competition dynamics (opposing others). Social anti-dynamics emerge: toxicity, quarterbacking (one player dominating decisions), kingmaking (eliminated players determining winners), and collusion.

Level 3: Temporal Architecture

Turn structure matters: simultaneous play eliminates downtime but limits reaction; sequential play enables response but creates waiting. Session coordination is harder with more people. Game length often scales with player count. The downtime problem—what players do when it's not their turn—is the most common multiplayer design failure.

Level 4: Balance Paradigm

Consistency paradigm requires fairness between players. Framework paradigm can use other players for adjudication (voting, negotiation). Adaptive paradigm becomes complicated—adapting for one player may disadvantage others.

Level 5: Mechanical Specification

Multiplayer requires communication mechanics, role differentiation (why each player matters), catch-up mechanics (keeping losing players engaged), and anti-quarterbacking measures (preventing dominant players from reducing others to executors).

Level 6: Parameters

Turn length limits prevent excessive downtime. Player count scaling adjusts resources and pacing. Catch-up tuning determines how aggressively trailing players can recover.

Competitive vs. Cooperative

Competitive priorities: Fairness (perceived balance), meaningful skill expression, catch-up/snowball tuning, kingmaking prevention, matchmaking support.

Cooperative priorities: Distributed purpose (every player has unique value), quarterbacking prevention (no single optimal decision-maker), shared stakes, difficulty scaling for different groups, communication design.

Mixed modes (teams, traitors, shifting alliances) combine both sets of concerns.

The Downtime Problem

When players wait with nothing meaningful to do, engagement collapses.

Causes: Sequential turns with slow players, elimination mechanics, resolution phases involving only some players.

Solutions: Simultaneous play, fast turns, engagement during others' turns (meaningful observation, responsive options), reducing player count, or deliberately using downtime for social interaction.

Part 5: Validation and Iteration

A derived design is a hypothesis. Level 7 tests it.

Coherence Audit

Before testing with players, audit the design for internal coherence.

The configuration coherence test: For each level, verify that outputs serve the levels above and constrain the levels below appropriately.

  • Do mechanics actually enable required dynamics?
  • Does temporal structure support the target aesthetic?
  • Do parameters produce the intended experience (on paper)?
  • Are there contradictions between levels?

Common coherence failures:

  • Mechanics that don't support stated dynamics
  • Temporal structure mismatched to player profile
  • Parameters that mathematically can't produce target experience
  • Cognitive load exceeding stated budget

Prediction

Make hypotheses explicit before testing. "Players will feel tension during resource decisions" is testable. "The game will be fun" is not.

Strong predictions specify:

  • What will happen (behavior or response)
  • Under what conditions
  • How you'll observe it

Write predictions down. Vague predictions are unfalsifiable and teach nothing.

Testing

Testing validates whether the design produces intended experience.

Test by chain level:

LevelWhat to TestMethods
AestheticDoes target emotion emerge?Post-session interviews, observation
DynamicsDo intended behaviors occur?Behavioral observation, think-aloud
TemporalDoes pacing feel right?Timed observation, session logs
ParadigmDoes balance approach work?Outcome tracking, fairness perception
MechanicsDo systems function?Exploit testing, edge cases
ParametersAre numbers tuned?Measurable tracking, A/B testing

Testing formats:

  • Solo testing: Designer plays own game. Limited for aesthetic validation but useful for mechanical function.
  • Internal testing: Team or friends. Stress-tests mechanics, limited for target audience validation.
  • External testing: Target audience. Actual validation of experience. Limited iteration speed.
  • Blind testing: No designer present. Validates self-teaching and standalone function.

Iteration

When testing reveals problems, diagnose before adjusting.

Symptom-level mapping:

SymptomLikely Level
"Not fun" (vague)Aesthetic mismatch or unclear
Wrong emotion emergesDynamics not producing aesthetic
Pacing feels offTemporal architecture
Feels unfair or too easy/hardParadigm or parameters
Confusing or overwhelmingMechanics exceeding cognitive budget
Specific numbers feel wrongParameters

Iteration cost:

Different levels have different change costs:

  • Parameters: Hours to days. Safe to adjust frequently.
  • Mechanics: Days to weeks. Moderate risk of cascade effects.
  • Temporal/Paradigm: Weeks to months. Significant restructuring.
  • Aesthetic/Dynamics: Major pivot. Reconsider project scope.

Start with parameter adjustments. If parameters can't fix the problem, move up the chain.

The Six Functions as Diagnostic Lens

When dynamics don't emerge as intended, check which system function is failing (see Chapter 2):

  • Adjudication unclear → Players don't know what succeeded or failed
  • Information management broken → Players lack needed information or drown in excess
  • Content generation insufficient → Repetition, exhaustion, predictability
  • State persistence confusing → Players lose track of game state
  • Feedback missing → Actions don't feel connected to outcomes
  • Cognitive framing wrong → Players don't understand what they're doing or why

Part 6: Worked Examples

Two complete examples demonstrate the derivation chain across different paradigms and mediums.

Worked Example 1: Casual Mobile Roguelike

Starting point: Aesthetic-first with constraints. A roguelike-inspired mobile game for casual players. Light humor, pixel art. Something for commutes.

Immediate tension: Roguelikes are typically midcore/hardcore. Casual roguelike is deliberate subversion requiring careful derivation.

Level 1: Aesthetic Profile

Primary aesthetic: Challenge (earned triumph through skill development) Secondary aesthetic: Progression (meta-unlocks for return motivation)

Player profile: Casual mobile

  • 5-15 minute sessions
  • Cognitive budget of 3±2 tracked elements
  • Low complexity tolerance
  • Expect interruption

Tone: Light humor means failure is amusing, not punishing. Death is punchline, not tragedy.

Success criteria:

  • "I did it!" moments at least once per session
  • Noticeable improvement over first 5-10 runs
  • Voluntary return (not FOMO-driven)
  • Clean exit possible at any moment

Level 2: Required Dynamics

Core loop: Attempt → fail/succeed → learn → retry. But compressed: learning must happen in 3-5 tries, not 50.

Peak moments: Victory over previously-lethal enemy, run completion, new personal best. Must occur every session.

Anti-dynamics: Grind (repetition without skill), confusion (unclear deaths), frustration (failure without learning), complexity overwhelm.

Humor dynamic: Death produces chuckle, not groan. Enemies are silly. Failure text is funny.

Cognitive check: 3±2 budget = health, attack capability, enemy patterns. Resource management must be minimal or automated.

Level 3: Temporal Architecture

Run (macro): 5-8 minutes. Fits casual session, allows 1-2 complete runs per play.

Floor (meso): 90-120 seconds each, 3-4 floors per run. Each floor is completable unit with progress persistence.

Encounter (micro): 15-30 seconds. Fast iteration, long enough for pattern recognition. 3-5 encounters per floor plus mini-boss.

Pacing: Escalating within run. Floor 1 easy (confidence), Floor 2 introduces challenge, Floors 3-4 where most deaths occur.

Stopping points: Between floors (good), between runs (excellent), mid-encounter (acceptable with pause).

Content scale:

  • 6-8 enemy types (learnable in casual timeframe)
  • 3-4 floor tilesets
  • 10-15 upgrade options
  • 4 bosses (one per tileset)

Level 4: Balance Paradigm

Paradigm: Consistency. Pattern learning requires deterministic enemies. Red flash always means jump.

Why not Adaptive: Would undermine Challenge aesthetic. Earned triumph requires consistent obstacles.

Archetype inheritance: Roguelike gives procedural generation, run-based structure, build variety. Not inheriting: permadeath punishment severity (light humor framing softens failure).

Level 5: Mechanical Specification

Core mechanic: Auto-attack combat with positioning. Player moves character, character auto-attacks nearest enemy. Skill expression through positioning and dodge timing.

Why auto-attack: Touch precision limited. Positioning is intuitive; combos are not.

Resources:

  1. Health (Limiter): 3 HP. One hit = one damage. Hearts display.
  2. Coins (Currency): Collected during run, spent between floors. Resets each run.
  3. Stars (Meta-resource): Earned at run-end, spent on permanent unlocks.

Three resources. Within budget.

Recovery: No health recovery within floor. Possible between floors (uncommon). Creates tension without attrition spiral.

Upgrades: Between floors, choose 1 of 3. Stat boosts or simple abilities. No complex synergies.

Content approach: Procedural floor layouts, hand-authored enemy patterns and bosses.

Level 6: Parameters

Target measurables:

  • Run completion: ~20% initially, ~60% after 10 runs
  • Average run (new player): 3-4 minutes (die Floor 2-3)
  • Average run (learned): 5-7 minutes (reach boss)
  • Deaths per session: 2-4
  • Triumph moments per session: 1-3

Combat parameters:

Target encounter: 20 seconds.

Enemies per encounter: 2-4.

Player DPS: 1 damage per attack, attack every 2 seconds = 0.5 DPS. 3 enemies × 3 HP = 9 HP ÷ 0.5 DPS = 18 seconds. ✓

Enemy damage: Player has 3 HP, should survive 2-3 floors initially. ~1 hit per 4-6 encounters. Floor 1 enemies: attack every 4 seconds, 15% hit rate. Floor 3-4: attack every 2.5 seconds, 25% hit rate.

Floor timing: 4 encounters × 20 seconds + 20 seconds traversal = 100 seconds per floor. 4 floors = 6.6 minutes per run. ✓

Boss: 15 HP, 3-phase pattern, learnable over 2-3 attempts.

Meta-progression:

  • 3 stars per completion, 1 for reaching boss
  • Unlock costs: 10-50 stars
  • Total unlocks: 15-20
  • Time to complete: 2-4 weeks casual play

Content Derivation

Enemy count: 16 encounters per run, 3-5 encounters to learn each type, want variety without overwhelming pattern-learning. 8 common enemies, 3 rare, 4 bosses = 10-12 total.

Sprites per enemy:

  • Idle: 1-2 frames
  • Telegraph: 2-3 frames (critical for pattern learning)
  • Attack: 2-3 frames
  • Hit: 1-2 frames
  • Death: 3-4 frames (humor opportunity)
  • Total: 9-14 frames per enemy

Bosses: 25-30 frames each (3 attack patterns, phase transitions, dramatic death)

Player character: 18-27 frames. Variants via palette swap.

Environment: 4 tilesets × 40-60 pieces = 160-240 pieces.

UI: ~70-90 sprites.

Effects: ~25-35 frames.

Total art budget: 450-600 pieces (with palette swaps).

Writing:

  • Enemy descriptions: 300-500 words
  • Death messages: 20-30 unique, 100-300 words (humor-critical)
  • Upgrade text: 75-200 words
  • Boss intros: 80-160 words
  • Tutorial/tips: 300-750 words
  • Meta flavor: 100-450 words
  • Total: ~1,000-2,400 words

What This Example Shows

Each number traces back through the chain:

  • 6.6-minute runs ← casual session windows ← player profile (Level 1)
  • 3 HP ← cognitive simplicity ← casual budget (Level 1)
  • 20-second encounters ← 90-second floors ← temporal architecture (Level 3)
  • Enemy attack rates ← pattern learning requirements ← Challenge dynamics (Level 2)
  • 10-12 enemy types ← variety ÷ mastery time ← temporal architecture (Level 3)
  • 9-14 frames per enemy ← telegraph requirements ← pattern learning dynamics (Level 2)
  • 20-30 death messages ← expected deaths × repetition tolerance ← Challenge aesthetic (Level 1)

Worked Example 2: TTRPG One-Shot

Starting point: Theme-first. A GM wants to explore "the evil of ends justify the means" through a one-shot.

Key difference from roguelike: Framework paradigm, narrative-focused, minimal mechanical complexity, heavy content authoring.

Level 1: Aesthetic Profile

Theme → Aesthetic translation: "Ends justify the means" could serve Challenge (optimize despite costs), Narrative (story of compromise), Tension (weight of choices), or Discovery (learn what you're capable of).

GM intent ("explore the evil") → Tension primary, Narrative secondary. Not optimization. Felt experience.

Player profile: Midcore TTRPG (3-4 hour tolerance, narrative comfort, consent to dark themes).

Success criteria:

  • Genuine conflict during choices
  • No obvious "right answers"
  • Players discuss afterward
  • Emotional arc completes

Level 2: Required Dynamics

Core dynamic: Moral deliberation—genuine weighing, disagreement, painful commitment.

Peak moments: Commitment ("We're doing this"), consequence (learning the cost), breaking point (someone can't continue).

Progression: Escalation. Each choice harder than the last.

Anti-dynamics: Optimization (loopholes), detachment (puzzle-solving), player conflict (in-character becoming personal), wallowing (grimness without meaning).

Theme-specific: Justification (hearing yourself rationalize), complicity (no clean hands in group decisions).

Decision forcing: For moral choices, indefinite stalling undermines Tension. Escalating consequences, named deaths, and time pressure force decisions.

Level 3: Temporal Architecture

Session: 3-4 hours including breaks and debrief.

Acts:

  • Act 1 (45-60 min): Establish stakes, first compromise (uncomfortable but justifiable)
  • Act 2 (90-120 min): Escalating compromises, consequences visible (harmful, harder to justify)
  • Act 3 (45-60 min): Ultimate choice, aftermath (near-unforgivable)

Scenes: Three types rotating for pacing:

  • Pressure (15-25 min): Present dilemma, force choice
  • Consequence (10-15 min): Show results
  • Breath (5-10 min): Character moments, humanity

Scene count: 11-12 total ≈ 15-20 minutes average.

Stopping points: Between acts (breaks for safety check-in).

Level 4: Balance Paradigm

Paradigm: Framework with mechanical scaffolding.

Why Framework: Moral dilemmas can't have calculable optimal solutions. If players can solve dilemmas through mechanics, Tension collapses.

Why scaffolding: Pure GM fiat feels arbitrary. Players need mechanical grounding for investment.

Mechanical triggers:

  • Capability uncertainty (can you reach the door in time?) → Roll
  • Moral choice (do you sacrifice the prisoner?) → Decision, no roll
  • Consequences → Follow from fiction, GM adjudication within established logic

Content Derivation

Setting: The Dimming. Remote village under supernatural fog. Ancient machine-entity (The Engine) offers salvation at escalating cost.

Why this setting: Contained (pressure cooker), clear stakes (survival), escalating threat (fog advancing), transactional force (Engine has no morality), personal connections (everyone knows everyone).

The Engine: Not evil, not good. Transactional. Doesn't lie or negotiate. States terms, fulfills contracts. No concept of cruelty because no concept of kindness. Players can't externalize blame.

Dilemma progression:

  1. Warmth: Price = church bells + labor (identity vs. survival)
  2. Food: Price = one person's memories (personhood vs. community)
  3. Healing: Price = one specific life, a Bond (one love vs. many lives)
  4. End the Dimming: Price = all children, transformed not killed (future vs. world)

Each dilemma needs:

  • Clear stakes
  • "Evil means" option
  • Rationalization argument
  • Hidden cost (revealed later)
  • Alternative (harder, less certain)

Dilemma prep: ~200-400 words each × 4 = 800-1,600 words.

NPC prep: 6-8 NPCs × 50-100 words = 300-800 words.

Scene structure: 11 scenes with specific purposes, Humanity implications stated.

Part 7: Common Patterns

Before concluding, six aesthetic sketches showing how typical goals flow through the derivation chain. These are compressed—starting points, not complete designs.

Challenge (Midcore)

Aesthetic goal: Players experience earned triumph through developing skill and overcoming genuine obstacles.

Dynamics derived:

  • Core loop: Attempt challenge, fail or succeed, learn from outcome, retry with knowledge
  • Peak moments: First victory over previously-impossible challenge; clutch successes
  • Progression: Player skill increases; challenges scale or new challenges unlock
  • Anti-dynamics: Grinding (success without skill), confusion (failure without learning), frustration (repeated failure without progress)

Temporal architecture:

  • Micro loops: Individual attempts (seconds to minutes depending on challenge type)
  • Meso loops: Challenge clusters or runs (10-30 minutes)—long enough for skill development, short enough for multiple sessions
  • Macro loops: Skill plateaus and breakthroughs across sessions
  • Pacing: Escalating difficulty with occasional valleys for consolidation
  • Stopping points: After challenge completion; between runs. Challenge games need "one more try" pull balanced against clean exit points.

Paradigm: Consistency. Skill development requires predictable systems with learnable patterns.

Mechanical direction:

  • Skill-testing core mechanics (timing, precision, decision-making under pressure)
  • Clear success/failure feedback
  • Difficulty progression (either designed levels or player-driven challenge selection)
  • Recovery limited enough to create stakes but generous enough to enable learning
  • Pattern-based obstacles with tells and counterplay

Parameter focus:

  • Time-to-kill / time-to-death ratios (per Chapter 6)
  • Attempt frequency (how quickly can players retry?)
  • Difficulty curve (how fast does challenge escalate?)
  • Feedback clarity (how quickly do players understand what went wrong?)

Discovery (Casual to Midcore)

Aesthetic goal: Players experience the pleasure of uncovering new information, locations, mechanics, or narrative through exploration.

Dynamics derived:

  • Core loop: Explore space (physical, mechanical, or narrative), encounter unknown, reveal information, integrate into understanding
  • Peak moments: Major revelations—plot twists, hidden areas, secret mechanics
  • Progression: Map fills in; understanding deepens; mysteries resolve
  • Anti-dynamics: Exhaustion (nothing new to find), arbitrariness (discoveries feel random rather than earned), confusion (can't integrate discoveries into coherent picture)

Temporal architecture:

  • Micro loops: Individual discoveries (seconds to minutes)—frequent enough to maintain engagement
  • Meso loops: Exploration sessions or narrative chapters (15-45 minutes)—complete enough to feel progress
  • Macro loops: Mystery arcs or world completion (hours to campaign)
  • Pacing: Episodic with revelation beats. Can be self-paced (player-driven exploration) or guided (narrative reveals).
  • Stopping points: After discoveries; at natural chapter breaks. Discovery games can often be paused anywhere since exploration is self-motivated.

Paradigm: Framework (for mystery/investigation where GM adjusts to player theories) or Consistency (for exploration of fixed content).

Mechanical direction:

  • Exploration mechanics (movement, investigation, interaction)
  • Information revelation systems (clues, environmental storytelling, NPC dialogue)
  • Progress tracking (maps, journals, achievement systems)
  • Gating mechanics (keys, abilities, narrative triggers that unlock new areas)
  • Minimal punishment for exploration (don't discourage poking at things)

Parameter focus:

  • Revelation density (how often do meaningful discoveries occur?)
  • Content volume (enough to sustain discovery across intended play time)
  • Clue clarity (discoverable but not obvious)
  • Exploration friction (low enough to encourage, high enough to feel like traversal matters)

[LLM TAG: Discovery games with procedural mystery generation could use LLM for consistent NPC knowledge states, ensuring clues remain coherent as players investigate in any order]

Tension (Midcore to Hardcore)

Aesthetic goal: Players experience sustained pressure and uncertainty. Stakes are real. Outcomes hang in balance. Relief when danger passes.

Dynamics derived:

  • Core loop: Assess threat, commit resources, experience uncertainty, resolve (succeed or face consequences)
  • Peak moments: Close calls—nearly failed but didn't. Also: release after sustained pressure.
  • Progression: Threats escalate; player capability may also increase but never enough to feel safe
  • Anti-dynamics: Safety (no real threat), hopelessness (threat is overwhelming), predictability (outcomes never in doubt)

Temporal architecture:

  • Micro loops: Threat response (seconds)—immediate decisions under pressure
  • Meso loops: Encounter or sequence (15-45 minutes)—long enough for tension to build, contains multiple close-call opportunities
  • Macro loops: Session or arc—sustained tension with eventual release
  • Pacing: Escalating within meso, with release between meso loops. Cannot sustain maximum tension indefinitely; needs valleys.
  • Stopping points: After release moments, not at peak tension. (Leaving players at peak tension is aversive, not motivating.)

Paradigm: Often Adaptive (maintaining tension requires adjusting to player state) or Consistency with careful calibration.

Mechanical direction:

  • Resource scarcity (something to lose)
  • Threat/consequence systems (failure has cost)
  • Information uncertainty (don't know if you'll make it)
  • Close-call mechanics (success by narrow margins)
  • Recovery limited enough that losses accumulate but not so limited that death spiral occurs

Parameter focus:

  • Resource pressure rate (30-50% scarcity per Chapter 3 guidelines)
  • Close-call frequency (2-3 per 10 minutes for high tension)
  • Consequence severity (matters but recoverable)
  • Difficulty calibration to hover at player's capability edge

Mastery (Midcore to Hardcore)

Aesthetic goal: Players experience deep understanding and skillful optimization of complex systems. The game rewards expertise.

Dynamics derived:

  • Core loop: Experiment with systems, observe outcomes, refine approach, achieve efficiency
  • Peak moments: Optimization breakthrough—when a new strategy clicks, when mastery is demonstrated
  • Progression: Player's understanding deepens; they see interactions invisible to novices
  • Anti-dynamics: Shallow systems (nothing to master), solved games (optimal strategy is known), execution gates (understanding doesn't translate to success)

Temporal architecture:

  • Micro loops: Individual optimization attempts (variable—as long as the decision space requires)
  • Meso loops: Strategy testing (30-90 minutes)—long enough to see strategy effects play out
  • Macro loops: Multiple runs or extended sessions—mastery develops across repeated engagement
  • Meta loops: Cross-session skill development; returning with new knowledge
  • Pacing: Can be steady-state (continuous optimization) or episodic (complete runs, then optimize for next)
  • Stopping points: After runs complete; between optimization cycles. Less frequent than casual games.

Paradigm: Consistency. Optimization requires predictable systems with learnable interactions.

Mechanical direction:

  • Systems with non-obvious interactions (depth to discover)
  • Multiple viable strategies (optimization space, not single optimal path)
  • Clear feedback on efficiency (know when you're improving)
  • Sufficient complexity to sustain expertise development
  • Execution requirements that match player investment profile

Parameter focus:

  • System depth (5+ viable builds within 20% power of each other, per Chapter 6)
  • Interaction legibility (can players perceive what's happening?)
  • Skill ceiling (high enough to sustain interest)
  • Run length (long enough to test strategies, short enough for iteration)

Submission (Casual)

Aesthetic goal: Players experience flow, relaxation, or escape. Low stress, high engagement. The game is a comfortable place to be.

Dynamics derived:

  • Core loop: Engage in pleasant, low-stakes activity; experience flow state
  • Peak moments: Flow state itself (sustained engagement without anxiety)
  • Progression: Optional; accumulation can feel satisfying but shouldn't create pressure
  • Anti-dynamics: Stress, failure punishment, forced attention, time pressure, complex decisions

Temporal architecture:

  • Micro loops: Simple, rhythmic actions (seconds)—low cognitive load
  • Meso loops: Short sessions or natural activity arcs (5-15 minutes)—completable in available time
  • Macro loops: Optional; meta-progression if present should be gentle
  • Pacing: Steady-state. No escalation required; consistent pleasant engagement.
  • Stopping points: Very frequent. Players can leave at almost any moment without loss.

Paradigm: Adaptive preferred (maintain pleasant state regardless of player variation) or Consistency with low stakes.

Mechanical direction:

  • Simple, satisfying core action
  • Minimal resource pressure (abundance over scarcity)
  • No or low failure states
  • Automated complexity (system handles bookkeeping)
  • Gentle progression without urgency
  • Pleasant aesthetics (visual, audio) contribute as much as mechanics

Parameter focus:

  • Flow sustainability (can players maintain engagement without attention spikes?)
  • Session flexibility (any session length works)
  • Accessibility (low barrier to entry and return)
  • Gentle difficulty (if any)

Narrative (Variable Investment)

Aesthetic goal: Players experience meaningful story—emotional engagement with characters, investment in outcomes, sense of authorship or witness to events.

Dynamics derived:

  • Core loop: Engage with story content, make meaningful choices (or witness meaningful events), experience consequences
  • Peak moments: Emotional story beats—revelation, triumph, tragedy, catharsis
  • Progression: Story advances; relationships develop; character arcs complete
  • Anti-dynamics: Disconnection from characters, meaningless choices, incoherent plot, broken immersion

Temporal architecture:

  • Meso loops: Story scenes or chapters (variable by medium; 15-60 minutes)
  • Macro loops: Act structure or session arcs
  • Pacing: Narrative pacing (typically escalating to climax, then resolution); follows story needs more than mechanical consistency
  • Stopping points: Between story beats; never mid-scene if avoidable. Story provides natural break points.

Paradigm: Framework for TTRPGs (human judgment enables narrative flow). For video games, often Consistency with narrative layered on top (story happens through consistent mechanical play).

[LLM TAG: Narrative games are perhaps the most obvious LLM application—dynamic dialogue, responsive NPCs, branching narratives that don't require exhaustive pre-authoring. This shifts Framework paradigm from TTRPG-exclusive to potentially available in solo digital games.]

Mechanical direction:

  • Choice mechanics with meaningful consequences
  • Character/relationship systems (if relationships matter)
  • Narrative pacing controls (preventing story from stalling)
  • Minimal ludonarrative dissonance (mechanics and story agree)
  • Dialogue/interaction systems appropriate to medium

Parameter focus:

  • Pacing calibration (scenes of appropriate length)
  • Choice weight (consequences that matter)
  • Relationship development rate
  • Content volume for intended story length

Using These Patterns

These sketches are starting points, not blueprints. Your design will diverge based on:

  • Specific aesthetic emphasis: "Challenge" contains multitudes; your specific vision matters.
  • Player profile: A casual Challenge game looks very different from a hardcore one.
  • Constraints: What you can build affects what you should design.
  • Secondary aesthetics: Most games blend aesthetics; secondaries modify the patterns.

The patterns illustrate the type of thinking the derivation chain requires. For each aesthetic, ask:

  • What behaviors create this experience?
  • What timing enables those behaviors?
  • What rules approach produces those behaviors reliably?
  • What systems implement the approach?
  • What numbers calibrate the systems?

Your answers will be yours. The questions are universal.

Part 8: Framework Flexibility

The derivation chain produces different games for different inputs. This isn't a weakness—it's the point. The framework is a thinking tool, not a template.

How Inputs Shape Outputs

If You Start With...The Chain Emphasizes...
Challenge aestheticSkill mechanics, Consistency paradigm, tight parameters
Narrative aestheticChoice mechanics, Framework paradigm, authored content
Casual profileShort loops, minimal complexity, forgiving parameters
Hardcore profileExtended loops, deep systems, demanding parameters
Solo digitalProcedural content, Consistency or Adaptive paradigm
Tabletop multiplayerSocial mechanics, downtime management, Framework paradigm

What Remains Constant

Regardless of inputs:

  • Aesthetics still determine dynamics
  • Dynamics still determine temporal structure
  • Temporal structure still determines content scale
  • Paradigm still shapes mechanical approach
  • Parameters still derive from measurable targets

The chain order holds. The logical dependencies hold. What changes is the content at each level, not the structure of the derivation.

Using the Framework

Start where you are. Entry point doesn't matter as long as you derive what's missing.

Check coherence between levels. When something feels wrong, diagnose which level contains the problem.

Test predictions, not hopes. Make hypotheses explicit. Failed predictions teach more than vague successes.

Iterate efficiently. Start with parameter adjustments before questioning mechanics. Question mechanics before questioning structure. Question structure before questioning aesthetics.

The framework doesn't design games. It structures thinking about games. The design still requires creativity, taste, and judgment. But with the framework, those qualities have somewhere to go.

Conclusion

Games are experiences held in systems. The system isn't the experience—players bring that—but without a well-constructed system, the experience has nowhere to live.

This chapter presented game design as a derivation chain: aesthetic goals determining dynamics, dynamics determining temporal structure, temporal structure determining mechanical implementation, mechanics determining parameters. Each level constrains and enables the next. The chain can be entered from different points, but the logical dependencies remain constant.

The two worked examples—a casual mobile roguelike and a TTRPG one-shot about moral compromise—demonstrated that the same framework produces radically different games depending on inputs. A Consistency paradigm with Challenge aesthetic yields DPS calculations and frame-count budgets. A Framework paradigm with Tension aesthetic yields Humanity economies and scene structures. The framework flexes; the thinking process holds.

Three principles guided the derivation:

Games are experiential scaffolds. Every design decision should trace back to the experience it enables. Mechanics exist to produce dynamics. Dynamics exist to create aesthetics. Aesthetics exist because someone, somewhere, will invest their time and attention expecting a return. Honor that investment.

Cognitive budget is real. Players have finite attention, memory, and processing capacity. Every system competes for that budget. Complexity must earn its place through richer experience. What doesn't contribute, detracts.

Derivation beats intuition—then intuition refines derivation. Starting from reasoned first principles produces better initial designs than starting from gut feeling. But playtesting reveals what reasoning missed. The best designs emerge from the cycle: derive, test, adjust, derive again.

The practical chapters that follow will apply this framework to complete designs—testing whether the theory survives contact with real players and real constraints. Theory that doesn't produce playable games is worthless. But theory that structures the design process makes the difference between stumbling toward something that works and building toward something that sings.

The derivation chain is a tool. Use it to structure your thinking, diagnose your problems, and communicate your decisions. Then set it aside and watch players play. They'll tell you what you actually built.

That's where design begins again.
