Resource Management in Game Systems

Document 4: Scarcity and Abundance

The Core Dynamic of Resource Management

Resource management exists because of scarcity. Abundant resources require no management. Strategic choices emerge from constraint.

This document explores:

  • How scarcity creates meaningful decisions
  • What happens when resources are abundant
  • Finding the right scarcity level for your aesthetic
  • Dynamic scarcity (changing over time)

High-Level Principle: Scarcity Creates Strategic Filtering

✅ Validated across all analyzed games

Principle: When resources are scarce, players must prioritize. This prioritization IS the strategic gameplay. Remove scarcity, remove decisions. Create too much scarcity, remove options.

The continuum:

  • Abundant: No choices needed (everything possible)
  • Moderate scarcity: Interesting choices (must prioritize)
  • Severe scarcity: Few options (constrained)
  • None: Blocked entirely (no agency)

Implication: Design scarcity level to match desired decision complexity.

Understanding Scarcity

What Makes a Resource Scarce?

Four factors create scarcity:

  1. Limited supply (can't get more easily)
  2. Multiple demands (many uses for same resource)
  3. Opportunity cost (choosing one prevents another)
  4. Time pressure (can't wait forever)

All four together: Maximum scarcity Any one alone: Moderate scarcity None: Abundance (no scarcity)

Example: Army Units in HoMM3

Limited supply: Weekly recruitment caps Multiple demands: Need units for attack, defense, exploration Opportunity cost: Units used in one battle unavailable for another Time pressure: Opponent is also building army

Result: High scarcity creates high-stakes decisions

Example: Energy in Slay the Spire

Limited supply: 3 energy per turn (fixed) Multiple demands: Multiple cards to play Opportunity cost: Play this card, can't play that one Time pressure: Must decide this turn

Result: Tactical optimization puzzle

Example: Gold in Late-Game RPG

Limited supply: ❌ (earning is easy) Multiple demands: ❌ (can afford everything) Opportunity cost: ❌ (no meaningful trade-offs) Time pressure: ❌ (no urgency)

Result: Gold becomes meaningless (false scarcity)

Note: Even "scarce" resources become abundant if acquisition outpaces spending. Late-game wealth is common problem.

The Scarcity Spectrum

Level 1: Abundance (No Scarcity)

Characteristics:

  • Resources freely available
  • No need to choose between options
  • Can do everything

Strategic impact: None (no decisions)

When to use:

  • Resources you DON'T want players thinking about
  • Things that should be automatic
  • Removing barriers to intended gameplay

Examples:

  • Unlimited movement in exploration-focused game (movement not the challenge)
  • Infinite mana in power fantasy game (want ability spam)
  • Modern WoW healing (keeps players in combat, not resting)

Aesthetic alignment:

  • ✓ Submission (no stress from scarcity)
  • ✓ Fantasy (if scarcity would break immersion)
  • ✗ Challenge (no optimization possible)

Level 2: Gentle Scarcity

Characteristics:

  • Resources sufficient for core needs
  • Must choose between bonus/optional goals
  • Rarely run out completely

Strategic impact: Mild (comfort zone with occasional choices)

When to use:

  • Want accessibility without trivialization
  • Core gameplay should flow, extras are choices
  • Forgiving experience

Examples:

  • Catan resources (production is regular, but must choose which building)
  • D&D 5e spell slots (enough for normal encounters, must conserve for hard days)
  • Many puzzle games (moves sufficient if playing reasonably)

Aesthetic alignment:

  • ✓ Fellowship (simple enough for socializing)
  • ✓ Discovery (can explore without constant resource stress)
  • ~ Challenge (light optimization)

Level 3: Moderate Scarcity

Characteristics:

  • Resources sufficient for EITHER this OR that, not both
  • Constant meaningful choices
  • Running out is possible but avoidable with skill

Strategic impact: High (the sweet spot for many games)

When to use:

  • Challenge/Mastery aesthetic
  • Want optimization without overwhelming
  • Meaningful trade-offs

Examples:

  • Slay the Spire energy (can play some cards, must choose which)
  • Blades downtime actions (can recover OR advance, not both)
  • Catan trades (must negotiate to get what you need)

Aesthetic alignment:

  • ✓ Challenge (meaningful optimization)
  • ✓ Mastery (rewards efficiency)
  • ✓ Tension (pressure without overwhelming)

This is the "default" scarcity level for most strategy games.

Level 4: Severe Scarcity

Characteristics:

  • Resources barely sufficient
  • Can afford only most essential
  • Running out is common threat

Strategic impact: Intense (every choice critical)

When to use:

  • High-tension gameplay
  • Survival aesthetics
  • Punishing strategic depth

Examples:

  • HoMM3 army units (permanent loss, must be very careful)
  • Old-school D&D HP (healing rare, must retreat often)
  • Slay the Spire HP on high difficulties (every hit hurts)

Aesthetic alignment:

  • ✓ Tension (constant pressure)
  • ✓ Challenge (unforgiving optimization)
  • ✗ Submission (too stressful)
  • ✗ Fellowship (if individual, too much pressure for social play)

Level 5: Critical Scarcity (Blockage)

Characteristics:

  • Resources insufficient for progress
  • Running out blocks gameplay
  • Must grind or restart

Strategic impact: Negative (frustration, not challenge)

When to use:

  • Never for critical path
  • Only for optional challenges

Examples (all anti-patterns):

  • Required item used up, can't progress (bad adventure game design)
  • Investigation system where failed check blocks mystery (bad mystery design)
  • Boss requires specific consumable you might not have (bad difficulty spike)

Aesthetic alignment:

  • ✗ ALL aesthetics (blockage is not gameplay)

Anti-Pattern: Critical Path Blockage Never let resource depletion block core progress. Players should fail challenges, not be blocked from attempting them.

Finding Your Scarcity Sweet Spot

Design Process

Step 1: Define your aesthetic goal

  • What experience do you want players to have?
  • Which aesthetic(s) are you serving?

Step 2: Determine desired decision frequency

  • Constant choices? (high scarcity)
  • Occasional choices? (moderate scarcity)
  • Rare choices? (gentle scarcity)
  • No choices? (abundance)

Step 3: Set scarcity level

  • Based on Steps 1 and 2
  • Start conservative (less scarce), tune up

Step 4: Test failure modes

  • What happens if players run out?
  • Is it interesting problem or dead end?
  • Can players recover or must restart?

Step 5: Iterate

  • Too easy? Increase scarcity
  • Too frustrating? Decrease scarcity
  • Wrong type of challenge? Rethink resource

Example: Tuning Investigation Points (Gumshoe)

Aesthetic goal: Mystery investigation (interpretation challenge, not resource management)

Desired decision frequency: Occasional (when to investigate deeply)

Scarcity level: Gentle

  • Enough points for reasonable investigation
  • Must choose where to spend for depth
  • Never blocks core clues

Failure mode: Running out means less detail, not stuck

Result: Scarcity creates depth choices without blocking progress

Example: Tuning Army Units (HoMM3)

Aesthetic goal: Strategic warfare (high-stakes battles)

Desired decision frequency: Constant (every battle is decision)

Scarcity level: Severe

  • Weekly recruitment (limited supply)
  • Permanent loss (no recovery)
  • Multiple fronts (multiple demands)

Failure mode: Running out means must retreat, rebuild (interesting problem)

Result: Scarcity creates meaningful stakes for every engagement

Multiple Demands: Creating Opportunity Cost

Scarcity alone isn't enough. Resource must have multiple valuable uses.

Single Use Resource (No Opportunity Cost)

Example: Currency that only buys one thing

  • Have gold, can only buy swords
  • No decision: "Do I have enough?" not "What should I buy?"
  • No strategic depth

Multiple Use Resource (Opportunity Cost)

Example: Gold buys multiple item types

  • Have 100 gold
  • Can buy: Sword (100g) OR Armor (80g) + Potions (20g)
  • Decision: Offense or defense?
  • Strategic depth

The principle: More valuable uses = more interesting scarcity

Example: Energy in Slay the Spire

Multiple demands on 3 energy:

  • Play attack cards (deal damage)
  • Play defense cards (block damage)
  • Play skill cards (special effects)
  • Each turn: Different optimal combination

What creates depth:

  • Can't do everything
  • Must prioritize based on situation
  • No single "best" answer

Example: Downtime Actions in Blades

Multiple demands on 2-4 actions:

  • Recover stress (survival)
  • Heal harm (recovery)
  • Work on project (advancement)
  • Train ability (growth)
  • Indulge vice (stress relief + risk)

What creates depth:

  • Can't do all
  • Different characters have different priorities
  • Reveals player values

Dynamic Scarcity: Changing Over Time

Static scarcity: Same throughout game Dynamic scarcity: Changes based on game state

Types of Dynamic Scarcity

1. Progression-Based (Abundance Increases)

Pattern: Start scarce, become abundant

Examples:

  • RPG gold (scarce early, abundant late)
  • Character abilities (few early, many late)
  • Building resources in strategy games

What it creates:

  • Early game: Tough choices (scarcity)
  • Late game: Power fantasy (abundance)
  • Sense of progression

Problem: Late game can become trivial if too abundant

Fix: Scale demands with supply (bigger challenges need more resources)

2. Attrition-Based (Scarcity Increases)

Pattern: Start abundant, become scarce

Examples:

  • HP in dungeon crawl (depletes over time)
  • Limited healing in survival game
  • Dwindling supplies

What it creates:

  • Early game: Comfortable (can afford mistakes)
  • Late game: Tense (every resource precious)
  • Mounting pressure

Problem: Can become frustrating if too punishing

Fix: Provide replenishment opportunities, or make scarcity the point (survival aesthetic)

3. Cyclical (Alternating)

Pattern: Periods of scarcity and abundance alternate

Examples:

  • Catan production (feast or famine based on dice)
  • Quest rewards in RPGs (scarce during quest, abundant after)
  • Seasonal resources

What it creates:

  • Varied pacing
  • Planning around cycles
  • Adaptation to changing conditions

Good for: Long-form games needing pacing variation

4. Crisis-Based (Sudden Scarcity)

Pattern: Normal abundance disrupted by events

Examples:

  • Catan robber (suddenly blocks production)
  • Resource denial in competitive games
  • Boss phases that limit resources

What it creates:

  • Adaptation challenge
  • Disruption of routine
  • Heightened tension

Good for: Creating memorable moments, forcing strategic shifts

Designing Dynamic Scarcity

Question: "Should scarcity change over time?"

Consider:

  • Progression aesthetic: Yes (start scarce → abundant feels like growth)
  • Tension aesthetic: Yes (start abundant → scarce creates mounting pressure)
  • Challenge aesthetic: Maybe (if want difficulty curve)
  • Fellowship aesthetic: Maybe not (consistency enables social focus)

If yes, consider:

  • Direction (more or less scarce?)
  • Rate of change (gradual or sudden?)
  • Player control (can they influence?)
  • Recovery mechanisms (can reverse?)

Scarcity and Player Psychology

The Hoarding Problem

Observation: Players hoard consumables "for emergencies" that never come.

Why it happens:

  • Loss aversion (spent resource can't be recovered)
  • Uncertainty (don't know what's coming)
  • Optimization mindset (want perfect use)

Examples:

  • Potions in Skyrim (saved until game is over)
  • Elixirs in Final Fantasy (never used)
  • Powerful consumables everywhere

What creates hoarding:

  • One-time use (pressure to use "correctly")
  • Limited availability (can't get more)
  • Unclear future needs (might need later)
  • Harsh difficulty (can't afford waste)

Solutions to hoarding:

Solution 1: Use-it-or-lose-it pressure

  • Example: Catan hand limit (can't hoard safely)
  • Forces spending before accumulation
  • Creates flow instead of hoarding

Solution 2: Renewable resources

  • Example: Blades stress (recovers between scores)
  • Safe to spend (will get more)
  • Encourages use over hoarding

Solution 3: Degradation over time

  • Example: Food spoils, potions expire
  • Must use before lost anyway
  • Incentivizes consumption

Solution 4: Abundant enough to experiment

  • Example: Modern games with generous consumables
  • Players feel safe trying things
  • Removes anxiety around spending

✅ Validated: Hoarding correlates with harsh scarcity + one-time use

The FOMO Problem

FOMO: Fear Of Missing Out

When resources create FOMO:

  • Time-limited availability (must act now)
  • Unknown optimal choice (might pick wrong)
  • Irreversible decisions (can't change later)
  • Hidden information (don't know all options)

Example - WoW Daily Quests:

  • Must complete today or miss rewards
  • Falls behind who did complete
  • Creates obligation, not excitement

Example - Skill Trees:

  • Must commit points
  • Don't know if choice is good
  • Can't respec (or costs heavily)
  • Paralysis from fear of wrong choice

Solutions to FOMO:

Solution 1: Transparency

  • Show what options exist
  • Explain consequences clearly
  • Let players make informed choices

Solution 2: Reversibility

  • Allow respec/rewind
  • Reduce commitment weight
  • Enable experimentation

Solution 3: Multiple paths work

  • No "trap" choices
  • All options viable
  • Removes optimization pressure

Solution 4: Remove time pressure

  • No daily/weekly lockouts for critical progress
  • Let players engage at their pace

⚠️ Hypothesis: FOMO from scarcity can drive engagement (games-as-service) but often feels manipulative

The Wealth Problem

Observation: Late-game wealth trivializes strategic choices.

Why it happens:

  • Income accumulates faster than spending
  • Costs don't scale with wealth
  • No meaningful money sinks

Example - RPG gold:

  • Early: 100g is meaningful, must choose purchases
  • Late: 100,000g, buy everything without thought
  • Gold becomes meaningless

Solutions:

Solution 1: Scaling costs

  • Late-game items cost proportionally more
  • Maintains relative scarcity

Solution 2: Money sinks

  • Ongoing expenses (upkeep, taxes)
  • Prestige purchases (cosmetics)
  • Competitive expenses (outbid others)

Solution 3: Conversion to other resources

  • Gold → buy time (hire help)
  • Gold → buy power (permanent upgrades)
  • Creates continued utility

Solution 4: Accept it

  • Late game power fantasy INCLUDES wealth
  • Let players feel rich
  • Not everything needs to be challenging

✅ Validated: Wealth accumulation is common challenge in long-form games with economic systems

Scarcity in Competitive vs Cooperative Games

Competitive Scarcity (Zero-Sum)

Characteristic: Your resource gain is opponent's loss (or vice versa)

Examples:

  • Catan: Limited board space, robber steals from others
  • Territory control: Control this mine OR opponent does, not both
  • Auction games: Outbid others for limited goods

What it creates:

  • Direct competition
  • Denial strategies (prevent opponent access)
  • Tension from conflict

Aesthetic alignment:

  • ✓ Challenge (optimize against opposition)
  • ✓ Tension (conflict)
  • ~ Fellowship (competitive socializing, not cooperative)

Cooperative Scarcity (Shared Challenge)

Characteristic: All players face same scarcity, must work together

Examples:

  • Pandemic: Limited actions to stop diseases
  • Blades crew resources: Shared coin, shared problems
  • Co-op survival: Limited supplies for whole team

What it creates:

  • Collaborative problem-solving
  • Shared triumph/defeat
  • Social bonding through challenge

Aesthetic alignment:

  • ✓ Fellowship (cooperation)
  • ✓ Challenge (overcome together)
  • ✓ Narrative (shared story)

Mixed Scarcity (Cooperative with Competition)

Characteristic: Cooperate against challenge, compete for rewards

Examples:

  • WoW raiding: Work together to kill boss, compete for loot drops
  • Board games with shared threat + individual victory

What it creates:

  • Complex social dynamics
  • Tension between cooperation and self-interest
  • Can breed toxicity if poorly designed

Aesthetic alignment:

  • ~ Mixed (depends on balance)
  • ⚠️ Risk of conflict

Warning: Mixed scarcity can create toxic dynamics if cooperation is essential but rewards are competitive. Players resent helping others who get better rewards.

Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: False Scarcity

Anti-Pattern: Theoretical Limits That Never Bind Resource appears scarce in design but is abundant in practice.

Example: Mana bar that never runs empty

  • Nominally limited resource
  • Actually always sufficient
  • Players learn to ignore it

Fix: Either make scarcity real or remove the resource

Mistake 2: Runaway Abundance

Anti-Pattern: Accumulation Without Sinks Resources accumulate faster than spent, becomes meaningless.

Example: Late-game RPG wealth

  • Gold piles up
  • Nothing worth buying
  • Strategic depth evaporates

Fix: Scale costs, add sinks, or accept power fantasy

Mistake 3: Critical Path Blockage

Anti-Pattern: Can't Progress Due to Resource Depletion Spent resources thinking they were optional, now can't proceed.

Example: Used key item earlier, needed for final boss

  • Player stuck
  • Must restart or grind
  • Feels like punishment for playing

Fix: Never gate critical path with consumable resources

Mistake 4: Whiplash Scarcity

Anti-Pattern: Scarcity Level Changes Too Dramatically Resource goes from abundant to scarce (or vice versa) too quickly.

Example: Game is generous with resources, then sudden spike of difficulty

  • Players weren't conserving (no need to before)
  • Suddenly can't progress (unprepared)
  • Feels unfair (rules changed mid-game)

Fix: Gradual scarcity changes, or telegraph changes clearly

Summary: Designing Scarcity

The scarcity spectrum:

  1. Abundance → No decisions
  2. Gentle → Occasional choices
  3. Moderate → Constant choices (sweet spot for many games)
  4. Severe → Every choice critical
  5. Critical → Blockage (avoid for critical path)

Scarcity created by:

  • Limited supply
  • Multiple demands
  • Opportunity cost
  • Time pressure

Choose scarcity level based on:

  • Aesthetic goals
  • Desired decision frequency
  • Acceptable failure modes

Dynamic scarcity patterns:

  • Progression (scarce → abundant)
  • Attrition (abundant → scarce)
  • Cyclical (alternating)
  • Crisis (sudden changes)

Avoid:

  • False scarcity (theoretical only)
  • Critical path blockage (can't progress)
  • Runaway abundance (becomes meaningless)
  • Whiplash changes (too sudden)

The fundamental test: "Does this scarcity level create the strategic experience I want?"
