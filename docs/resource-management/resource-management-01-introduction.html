Core Philosophy: Resources as Experiential Tools

The Wrong Question

Traditional game design often asks: "What resources should our game have?"

This leads to answers like:

  • "We need HP, mana, and gold" (copying other RPGs)
  • "Let's have energy that regenerates over time" (mobile game convention)
  • "Players should manage stamina, hunger, and thirst" (survival game tropes)

These aren't necessarily wrong, but they're starting from mechanics rather than purpose.

The Right Question

"What experience do we want players to have, and what resources would create that experience?"

This reframes resource management from:

  • Copying conventions → Designing for purpose
  • "What's normal?" → "What serves our aesthetic?"
  • Mechanics-first → Experience-first

Example: Mystery Investigation Games

Wrong approach: "Detective games need investigation resources, so let's add Perception skill checks to find clues"

Right approach:

  1. What experience? "Players solve mysteries through interpretation, not lucky dice rolls"
  2. What does that require? "Clues must be findable (not gatable), challenge is what you DO with information"
  3. What resources serve this? "Investigation points for OPTIONAL depth clues, core clues automatic"

The resource design (investigation points) emerges from aesthetic goal (interpretation challenge), not from genre convention (skill checks).

Resources vs. Constraints: A Critical Distinction

Before we can discuss resource management, we must clarify what resources ARE and ARE NOT.

Resources: In-Game Mechanics

Resources are in-game entities that:

  • Players manage and make decisions about
  • Can be tracked by the game system
  • Have explicit mechanics for acquisition and spending
  • Create strategic choices through scarcity

Examples:

  • Health Points (survival resource)
  • Gold (economic resource)
  • Spell slots (ability resource)
  • Investigation points (information resource)
  • Stress (buffer resource)
  • Army units (strategic resource)

Key characteristic: Resources are MANAGED BY the system and players make choices ABOUT them.

Constraints: Design Boundaries

Constraints are external factors that:

  • Limit what systems can practically do
  • Affect players but aren't managed in-game
  • Shape design space but aren't mechanics themselves
  • Must be accommodated, not "spent"

Examples:

  • Player attention/cognitive load
  • Session length (real time available)
  • Group coordination difficulty
  • Emotional safety boundaries
  • Player skill/experience level

Key characteristic: Constraints are ON the system, not IN the system.

Why This Distinction Matters

Problem: Designers often conflate these, leading to confusion.

Example confusion: "Player attention is a resource we must manage"

  • ❌ Wrong framing: Attention can't be "spent" or "recovered" like HP
  • ✅ Right framing: Attention is a constraint that limits complexity budget

Example confusion: "Time is a resource in real-time games"

  • ❌ Wrong framing if meaning real-world time (that's a constraint)
  • ✅ Right framing if meaning in-game time pressure (that's a mechanic)

The test:

  • Can players make strategic choices about allocating it? → Resource
  • Does it limit what's feasible in game design? → Constraint

Where Cognitive Load Fits

Cognitive load is a CONSTRAINT, not a resource.

Why this matters:

  • You can't "spend brainspace" and have less for later
  • You can't "trade attention" with another player
  • Complexity doesn't deplete and recover like HP

However, cognitive load deeply affects resource design:

  • Complex resource tracking increases cognitive load
  • Simple resources free up attention for other gameplay
  • Design question: "Is tracking this resource worth the cognitive overhead?"

Cross-reference: See separate document "Cognitive Load and System Complexity" for detailed analysis of how player attention shapes design space.

Design Implication

When designing resources, ask:

  1. Is this thing players will make strategic choices about? (If yes → resource)
  2. Or is this a limit on what players can handle? (If yes → constraint)

Don't call everything a resource just because it's scarce. Reserve the term for in-game mechanics that players actively manage.

Aesthetic Framework: What Experiences Do Resources Create?

Starting from MDA

We use the MDA (Mechanics-Dynamics-Aesthetics) framework as our foundation for understanding how resources create experiences.

MDA's insight: Games are designed backwards from player experience:

  • Aesthetics: The emotional/experiential goals (what players feel)
  • Dynamics: The behavior that emerges from mechanics in play
  • Mechanics: The rules and systems (including resources)

For resource design: Start with aesthetic goal → determine what dynamics you need → design resource mechanics to create those dynamics.

MDA's Eight Aesthetics

From Hunicke, LeBlanc, and Zubek (2004):

  1. Sensation: Sensory pleasure (beautiful, visceral, immersive)
  2. Fantasy: Make-believe, imagining yourself in another world
  3. Narrative: Story and drama
  4. Challenge: Obstacle course, overcoming difficulty
  5. Fellowship: Social framework, playing with others
  6. Discovery: Exploration, finding new things
  7. Expression: Self-discovery, creativity
  8. Submission: Pastime, relaxation, "turning brain off"

Limitations of MDA's List

MDA's eight aesthetics are valuable but incomplete for our purposes. Through analyzing resource systems across multiple games, we've identified additional aesthetic goals not fully captured by MDA:

Additional aesthetic goals:

Mastery: Getting deeply skilled at understanding and optimizing systems

  • Related to Challenge but distinct: Not just overcoming difficulty, but understanding deeply
  • Example: Chess players studying openings, Slay the Spire players calculating draw pile odds
  • Requires: Systems with sufficient depth to repay study

Tension: Adrenaline, time pressure, high-stakes decisions

  • Related to Sensation but distinct: Not sensory pleasure but psychological pressure
  • Example: StarCraft real-time decision-making, XCOM Ironman permadeath
  • Requires: Consequences that matter, pressure to decide quickly

Progression: Growth, becoming more powerful over time

  • Related to Expression but distinct: Not about creativity but advancement
  • Example: Leveling up, unlocking abilities, empire building
  • Requires: Accumulation mechanics, power curve

Collection/Completion: Accumulation, completionism, "gotta catch 'em all"

  • Related to Discovery but distinct: Not about finding new things but possessing all things
  • Example: Pokemon collection, achievement hunting, card collecting
  • Requires: Finite sets to complete, tracking what's missing

Our Expanded Framework

For this document series, we use:

  • MDA's eight aesthetics as foundation (established, well-understood)
  • Plus four additional aesthetic goals identified through game analysis
  • With acknowledgment that this list is still not exhaustive

Why expand MDA?

  • Our game analyses revealed aesthetic goals not well-described by MDA's eight
  • Resource design specifically benefits from distinguishing these goals
  • Better to be precise than force patterns into ill-fitting categories

When to use which:

  • MDA's original eight cover most cases
  • Additional four clarify specific resource design patterns
  • Both are tools, not dogma

How Resources Map to Aesthetics

Different resource designs serve different aesthetic goals:

Challenge aesthetic needs:

  • Scarcity that forces difficult choices
  • Consequences for poor resource management
  • Optimization opportunities
  • Example: HoMM3 permanent army loss

Fellowship aesthetic needs:

  • Simple resources (free up brainspace for socializing)
  • Shared resources or trading mechanics
  • Visible game state (enable discussion)
  • Example: Catan's five resources + trading

Narrative aesthetic needs:

  • Resources that tell story (stress/harm accumulation)
  • Asymmetric recovery (different time scales)
  • Consequences that become character history
  • Example: Blades in the Dark trauma

Mastery aesthetic needs:

  • Depth that rewards study
  • Optimization space
  • Systems that scale with understanding
  • Example: Slay the Spire deck composition

Tension aesthetic needs:

  • High stakes (permanent or slow-recovering losses)
  • Time pressure or urgency
  • Risk/reward decisions
  • Example: Slay the Spire HP as permanent resource

Submission aesthetic needs:

  • Simple resource management (minimal cognitive load)
  • Forgiving (abundant resources, fast recovery)
  • Low stakes (mistakes don't cascade)
  • Example: Modern WoW (fast HP recovery, minimal downtime)

Design Principle: Aesthetic Alignment

Principle: Every resource design choice should serve your aesthetic goals. Resources that don't serve the intended experience are at best unnecessary complexity, at worst actively harmful.

Examples of misalignment:

❌ Complex resource tracking in Fellowship game

  • Want: Social interaction and negotiation
  • Have: 10 resource types requiring calculation
  • Result: Players do math instead of talking
  • Fix: Simplify (Catan's 5 resources vs 18XX's complexity)

❌ Abundant/fast-recovering resources in Challenge game

  • Want: Difficult optimization decisions
  • Have: Resources refresh constantly, always abundant
  • Result: No meaningful scarcity, no hard choices
  • Fix: Slow recovery or permanent loss (HoMM3 army units)

❌ Highly punishing resources in Submission game

  • Want: Relaxing, low-stress pastime
  • Have: Permanent consequences, harsh penalties
  • Result: Anxiety, not relaxation
  • Fix: Forgiving recovery, low stakes (mobile puzzle games)

✅ Stress/Harm separation in Narrative game (Blades in the Dark)

  • Want: Character arc of veteran getting worn down
  • Have: Fast-recovering buffer (stress) + slow-recovering consequences (harm)
  • Result: Mechanics create "grizzled criminal" story through play
  • Works because: Resource design directly serves narrative aesthetic

Using This Framework

For each resource in your design, ask:

  1. What aesthetic goal does this serve? (Which experience are you creating?)
  2. How does this resource's scarcity/recovery/spending create that aesthetic?
  3. Could a simpler design serve the same aesthetic? (Complexity budget check)
  4. Does this resource conflict with other aesthetic goals? (Trade-off awareness)

If you can't clearly answer question 1, the resource might not belong in your game.

Why These Boundaries?

Cognitive load is related but distinct:

  • Complex resources increase cognitive load
  • But cognitive load principles apply beyond just resources
  • Deserves separate, focused treatment
  • We note connections but don't duplicate coverage

Balance is downstream from resource design:

  • First: Decide what resources serve your aesthetic
  • Then: Tune numbers to achieve desired experience
  • Balance is important but separate concern

Implementation is context-dependent:

  • Tabletop vs. video game have different considerations
  • Technical details vary by platform
  • Principles are universal, implementation is specific
