Resource Management in Game Systems

Part 7: Psychological Effects of Resource Design

Overview

Resource systems don't just create mechanical challenges - they create psychological states that shape player experience. How players feel about having, accumulating, and spending resources is as important as the mechanical systems themselves.

This section examines three key psychological phenomena that emerge from resource design:

  1. Possession - What does "having" create experientially?
  2. Greed - Why do players accumulate beyond need?
  3. FOMO (Fear of Missing Out) - How does resource design create anxiety?

These aren't problems to solve universally - they're tools that serve different aesthetic goals. Understanding them helps designers create intended experiences rather than accidental ones.

1. Possession: The Experience of Having

What Possession Creates Psychologically

"Having" resources creates multiple distinct experiential states:

1.1 Security/Safety

Experience: "I have potions, therefore I can survive mistakes"

What it creates:

  • Psychological buffer against failure
  • Confidence to take risks
  • Comfort in preparation

Examples:

  • Slay the Spire potions: Full potion slots = "I can handle the next fight"
  • HoMM3 gold reserves: Stockpiled gold = "I can recover from setback"
  • Blades in the Dark stress capacity: Low stress = "I can push myself if needed"

Serves aesthetics:

  • Challenge (need buffer to attempt difficult content)
  • Discovery (safe to explore unknown)

Design consideration: Too much security eliminates tension. Too little creates paralysis.

1.2 Identity/Expression

Experience: "I have this unique item, this is who I am"

What it creates:

  • Self-definition through possessions
  • Collection as personal narrative
  • Gear as character identity

Examples:

  • WoW rare mounts: "I'm the player who has the Ashes of Al'ar"
  • Slay the Spire relics: "This is my poison deck build"
  • Blades trauma marks: "My character has 'Cold' trauma from the ghost heist"

Serves aesthetics:

  • Expression (possessions reflect choices)
  • Fantasy (items = who you are in fiction)
  • Progression (collection shows journey)

Design consideration: Make possessions distinctive and memorable, not just +1 stat increases.

1.3 Potential/Options

Experience: "I have these tools, I can handle situations"

What it creates:

  • Preparedness fantasy
  • Strategic flexibility
  • Problem-solving capacity

Examples:

  • Blades in the Dark Load: "I'm carrying heavy load, I prepared for anything"
  • Slay the Spire diverse deck: "I have answers to different threats"
  • Catan varied resources: "I can build whatever I need"

Serves aesthetics:

  • Challenge (options enable strategy)
  • Mastery (recognizing when to use what)
  • Fantasy (competent protagonist)

Design consideration: Too many options → analysis paralysis. Too few → lack of agency.

1.4 Status/Achievement

Experience: "I earned this rare item, I'm accomplished"

What it creates:

  • Visible wealth as social signal
  • Achievement markers
  • Prestige through possession

Examples:

  • WoW legendary weapons: Social recognition of accomplishment
  • HoMM3 powerful artifacts: "I conquered the map and got this"
  • Blades crew upgrades: "We're now a Tier 3 crew"

Serves aesthetics:

  • Challenge (proof of overcoming difficulty)
  • Progression (visible advancement)
  • Fellowship (status within community)

Design consideration: Status through possession can create unhealthy social dynamics (gatekeeping, elitism).

1.5 Power Fantasy

Experience: "I have everything, I'm unstoppable"

What it creates:

  • Apotheosis through accumulation
  • Overwhelming capability
  • "God mode" satisfaction

Examples:

  • Late-game Slay the Spire: Synergistic deck + relics = unstoppable
  • HoMM3 maxed hero: Level 30+ hero with best artifacts
  • WoW fully geared character: Maximum item level

Serves aesthetics:

  • Challenge (earned through difficulty)
  • Progression (culmination of growth)
  • Submission (relaxing power trip)

Design consideration: Can trivialize challenge if not balanced. Timing matters - often best at end of content.

Design Principle: Match Possession Experience to Aesthetic

High-level principle: "Different aesthetics require different 'having' experiences - design what possession means based on intended player experience"

Guidelines:

For Challenge aesthetic:

  • Possession = preparedness, strategic options
  • Example: Slay the Spire relics enable different strategies

For Expression aesthetic:

  • Possession = identity, uniqueness
  • Example: Character builds show player creativity

For Fellowship aesthetic:

  • Possession = shared resources, group identity
  • Example: Blades crew upgrades belong to everyone

For Progression aesthetic:

  • Possession = achievement markers, visible growth
  • Example: WoW gear shows character advancement

For Power Fantasy aesthetic:

  • Possession = overwhelming capability
  • Example: Late-game god builds

Note: Same resource can create different possession experiences based on context

  • Gold in HoMM3 = security (can recover from losses)
  • Gold in Catan = potential (can buy what I need)
  • Gold in WoW = status (can afford expensive items)

2. Greed: Accumulation Beyond Need

What Is Greed in Game Design?

Definition: The desire to accumulate resources beyond immediate need, often to the point of never using them.

Manifestations:

  • Hoarding consumables ("save potions for boss, never use them")
  • Completionism ("must collect everything")
  • Optimal play over experiential play ("this is the best build")
  • Analysis paralysis ("can't spend until I know perfect use")

Why Does Greed Emerge?

2.1 Loss Aversion

Psychological principle: Loss hurts more than equivalent gain feels good

In games:

  • "If I use this potion now and don't need it later, I'll regret it"
  • Fear of making "wrong" choice
  • Hoarding as insurance against uncertainty

Example: Slay the Spire potions

  • Common pattern: Never use potions, lose with full slots
  • Why: "What if I need them more later?"
  • Result: Resource goes unused entire run

Design implications:

  • Players need explicit permission/encouragement to spend
  • Irreversible spending creates anxiety
  • Uncertainty about future needs triggers hoarding

2.2 Sunk Cost Fallacy

Psychological principle: Past investment influences present decisions

In games:

  • "I've been building toward this strategy, can't change now"
  • "I already spent resources on X, must commit"
  • Continuing suboptimal path due to prior investment

Example: Slay the Spire deck building

  • Took poison cards early
  • Keep taking poison even when not optimal
  • "Already committed to poison strategy"

Design implications:

  • Allow strategy pivots (card removal in Slay the Spire)
  • Make sunk costs visible so players recognize trap
  • Or: Embrace commitment (no respec = meaningful choices)

2.3 Optimization Mindset

Psychological principle: Treating game as puzzle with "correct" solution

In games:

  • Finding "best" build and refusing alternatives
  • Optimizing away experiential variation
  • "Solving" the game rather than playing it

Example: WoW class specs

  • Simulations determine optimal builds
  • Players copy best builds rather than experimenting
  • Reduces Expression aesthetic to Challenge aesthetic

Design implications:

  • Multiple viable strategies (prevent single dominant strategy)
  • Opaque optimization (can't perfectly calculate optimal)
  • Rewards for experimentation (Slay the Spire encourages weird builds)

2.4 Collection as Goal

Psychological principle: Accumulation itself is satisfying

In games:

  • Pokémon "gotta catch 'em all"
  • Completing collections (achievements, mounts, items)
  • Acquisition more satisfying than use

Example: WoW mount collecting

  • Players farm rare mounts (0.01% drop rates)
  • Mount itself is goal, not using it
  • Collection number is achievement

Design implications:

  • This can be INTENDED (collection as aesthetic)
  • But conflicts if resources are meant to be spent
  • Clarify: Is collecting the point or the means?

When Is Greed Good vs. Bad?

Greed isn't inherently a problem - it depends on aesthetic goals.

Greed Serves the Aesthetic

When accumulation IS the point:

Power Fantasy aesthetic:

  • Goal: Become overwhelmingly powerful
  • Accumulation = success
  • Example: Late-game Slay the Spire - hoarding relics makes you godlike

Collection aesthetic:

  • Goal: Complete the collection
  • Accumulation = achievement
  • Example: Pokémon, WoW mount collecting

Progression aesthetic:

  • Goal: Visible growth over time
  • Accumulation = character development
  • Example: HoMM3 hero leveling, artifact collection

Design principle: If aesthetic is accumulation-focused, enable and reward hoarding.

Greed Harms the Aesthetic

When spending creates the experience:

Challenge aesthetic (strategic resource management):

  • Goal: Make difficult choices under scarcity
  • Hoarding = avoiding choices
  • Example: Never using Slay the Spire potions removes strategic decisions

Narrative aesthetic:

  • Goal: Drama, consequences, flow
  • Hoarding = stopping to optimize, breaking fiction
  • Example: Stopping mid-story to farm resources

Fellowship aesthetic:

  • Goal: Social interaction
  • Hoarding = preventing trade/cooperation
  • Example: Catan if players could safely hoard (hand limit prevents this)

Design principle: If aesthetic requires spending, create mechanisms that encourage/force it.

Design Techniques: Managing Greed

Technique 1: Use-It-Or-Lose-It Pressure

Mechanism: Resources expire, reset, or become vulnerable

Examples:

  • Catan hand limit: 8+ cards on 7-roll = lose half
  • Blades stress/trauma: Can't hoard stress forever (trauma at 4)
  • WoW weekly caps: Resources reset weekly, can't stockpile infinitely

What it creates:

  • Forced circulation of resources
  • "Spend before it's wasted" motivation
  • Removes hoarding as dominant strategy

Serves: Challenge, Fellowship (keeps game flowing)

Technique 2: Increasing Marginal Cost

Mechanism: Hoarding becomes increasingly expensive or difficult

Examples:

  • Inventory limits: Can't carry infinite items (space cost)
  • Storage costs: Gold required to maintain stockpiles
  • Opportunity costs: Hoarding prevents using slots for better things

What it creates:

  • Natural limit on accumulation
  • Makes spending relatively attractive
  • Still allows some hoarding (for those who value it)

Serves: Challenge (creates optimization puzzle)

Technique 3: Reward Spending

Mechanism: Using resources creates opportunities hoarding doesn't

Examples:

  • Slay the Spire potions: Often find new potions, empty slots enable acquisition
  • Blades flashbacks: Spend stress retroactively, encourages using capacity
  • HoMM3 artifacts: Equipped artifacts help win battles → get more resources

What it creates:

  • Positive feedback loop (spending → gains)
  • Overcoming loss aversion (spending pays off)
  • Experimentation encouraged

Serves: Challenge, Discovery (trying things is rewarded)

Technique 4: Opaque Future Needs

Mechanism: Can't predict exactly what you'll need later

Examples:

  • Slay the Spire encounters: Don't know what's coming, can't optimize perfectly
  • Catan dice rolls: Can't predict production, can't plan perfectly
  • Blades consequences: GM determines problems, can't prepare for everything

What it creates:

  • Prevents perfect hoarding strategy
  • Forces adaptability over optimization
  • Makes "good enough now" viable

Serves: Challenge (adapting to uncertainty)

Anti-Pattern: Consumables That Are Never Optimal to Use

The problem: If consumables are:

  • Rare enough to be precious
  • Powerful enough to save "for when I really need them"
  • But never clearly THE moment to use them

Result: Players hoard entire game and never use them

Example: Skyrim consumable overload

  • Hundreds of potions accumulated
  • "Might need them later"
  • Beat game with full inventory

Solution options:

  1. Make consumables abundant (okay to use freely)
  2. Create clear "use now" moments (boss fights)
  3. Force expiration (use it or lose it)
  4. Make non-consumable alternatives available (abilities that don't deplete)

Design Principle: Greed and Aesthetic Alignment

High-level principle: "Greed emerges when accumulation is rewarded more than spending - design resource flow to match whether accumulation or spending should dominate"

Guidelines:

Want accumulation to dominate?

  • Make collection the goal (Pokémon model)
  • Reward stockpiling (bigger reserves = safer)
  • No forced spending (players can hoard)

Want spending to dominate?

  • Create use-it-or-lose-it pressure (expiration, caps)
  • Reward spending (positive feedback loops)
  • Make hoarding costly (inventory limits, vulnerability)

Want balance?

  • Some resources accumulate (permanent progression)
  • Some resources circulate (tactical choices)
  • Different resources serve different purposes

3. FOMO: Fear of Missing Out

What Is FOMO in Game Design?

Definition: Anxiety created by the possibility of missing opportunities, content, or optimal choices.

Manifestations:

  • "What if I go left and miss something important on the right?"
  • "What if I spec wrong and my character is broken?"
  • "What if I don't log in daily and fall behind?"
  • "What if I use this resource suboptimally?"

How Resource Design Creates FOMO

3.1 Irreversible Scarcity + Uncertainty

The formula: FOMO emerges when:

  • Resources are permanently lost if not acquired
  • Future needs are uncertain
  • No way to go back and get missed resources

Example: Slay the Spire route choices

  • Choose path A (elite fight) → miss path B (shop)
  • Can't go back
  • Don't know which was better until after

But this is GOOD FOMO:

  • Creates meaningful choices
  • Replayability (try different paths next run)
  • No persistence → failure is cheap

Why it works:

  • Consequences within run only (next run is fresh start)
  • Uncertainty is part of challenge
  • Multiple paths are viable

Serves aesthetic: Challenge, Discovery

3.2 Time-Limited Resources

Mechanism: Content available only during specific windows

Example: WoW daily/weekly lockouts

  • Daily quests reset at midnight
  • Weekly raid lockouts Tuesday mornings
  • Seasonal events (2-week windows)

Creates FOMO:

  • "If I don't log in today, I lose today's progress"
  • "If I miss raid night, I don't get loot this week"
  • "If I miss seasonal event, I can't get limited items"

This is PROBLEMATIC FOMO:

  • Creates obligation (must play on schedule)
  • Punishes life circumstances (vacation, illness)
  • Game feels like job

Serves aesthetic: Progression (but at cost of player wellbeing)

Note: Time-gating is often commercial, not aesthetic

  • Goal: Increase daily active users (DAU) for metrics
  • Not about creating better experience
  • Creates engagement through anxiety

3.3 Optimal Build Anxiety

Mechanism: Fear of making suboptimal character choices

Example: RPG skill trees

  • Limited skill points
  • Can't respec (or expensive to do so)
  • "Wrong" choices make character weaker

Creates FOMO:

  • Analysis paralysis (afraid to commit)
  • Following guides instead of experimenting
  • Reduces Expression to optimization

Example: Slay the Spire cards

  • Each card choice matters
  • Taking "bad" card weakens deck
  • Must carefully curate

But handled well:

  • Can skip cards (not forced to take)
  • Can remove cards later (costs gold but possible)
  • Most builds are viable (not one "right" answer)

Serves aesthetic: Challenge (meaningful choices) Harms aesthetic: Expression (if players afraid to experiment)

3.4 Competitive FOMO

Mechanism: Falling behind other players

Example: WoW progression races

  • World-first raid clears
  • Gear score comparisons
  • Arena ratings

Creates FOMO:

  • "I need to keep up with my guild"
  • "Others are getting ahead"
  • "I'm falling behind the meta"

Can be healthy:

  • Competitive aesthetic (racing others is the point)
  • Community identity (progression together)
  • Clear milestones

Can be toxic:

  • Burnout from constant progression pressure
  • Gatekeeping (too low gear score = excluded)
  • Arms race (never enough)

Serves aesthetic: Challenge, Fellowship (if managed well)

When Is FOMO Good vs. Bad?

FOMO That Serves Experience

Creates meaningful choices:

  • Slay the Spire paths: Choose one route, miss others
  • Permanent consequences create weight
  • Replayability from trying different options
  • Failure is cheap (runs are short, can try again)

Creates urgency/tension:

  • Timed decisions add pressure
  • "Must choose now" increases stakes
  • Can serve Tension aesthetic

Creates discovery:

  • "What if I try this path?"
  • Exploration encouraged by unknown outcomes
  • Serves Discovery aesthetic

Design pattern:

  • Consequences are serious within bounded context
  • Easy to try again (new run, new game, replayability)
  • Multiple paths are viable
  • Uncertainty is part of challenge

FOMO That Harms Experience

Creates obligation:

  • Daily/weekly requirements
  • "Must log in or fall behind"
  • Game becomes job

Creates anxiety:

  • Fear of making wrong choice
  • Paralysis instead of play
  • Optimization over experimentation

Creates exclusion:

  • Missing limited-time content
  • Can't catch up to others
  • Gatekeeping based on timing

Design pattern:

  • Permanent external consequences (can't try again)
  • Real-time pressure (must play on schedule)
  • Competitive comparison to others
  • Single "correct" path

Design Techniques: Managing FOMO

Technique 1: Bounded Consequences

Mechanism: Consequences exist but are contained

Example: Slay the Spire

  • Choices matter within run
  • But each run is fresh start
  • Missing something in run 1 doesn't affect run 2

What it creates:

  • Meaningful decisions (consequences exist)
  • Low anxiety (can try again easily)
  • Experimentation encouraged

Serves: Challenge, Discovery

Technique 2: Multiple Viable Paths

Mechanism: No single "correct" choice

Example: Catan building strategies

  • Road strategy vs. city strategy vs. development cards
  • All can win
  • Choice based on position and preference

What it creates:

  • Reduces optimal build anxiety
  • Enables Expression (play your way)
  • Replayability (try different approaches)

Serves: Challenge, Expression

Technique 3: Respec/Undo Options

Mechanism: Choices can be revised

Example: Modern game skill resets

  • Can change spec for cost (gold, item)
  • Experimentation has exit ramp
  • Still has cost (not totally free)

What it creates:

  • Lower anxiety about "wrong" choices
  • Encourages trying things
  • But cost prevents constant changing

Serves: Expression, Discovery

Trade-off: Reduces weight of choices

Technique 4: Transparency About Consequences

Mechanism: Show what you're choosing between

Example: Slay the Spire map

  • Can see upcoming paths
  • Know what you're choosing between
  • Still uncertainty (what's in shops, what enemies)

What it creates:

  • Informed decisions
  • Reduces "blind" FOMO
  • Still has meaningful choice

Serves: Challenge (strategy with information)

Technique 5: No Forced Timing

Mechanism: Players progress at their own pace

Example: Single-player games

  • No daily quests
  • No weekly lockouts
  • No seasonal events
  • Play when you want

What it creates:

  • Zero obligation FOMO
  • Respects player time
  • Still has in-game choices

Serves: Most aesthetics (removes external pressure)

Trade-off: Loses scheduled social coordination

Anti-Pattern: Artificial Scarcity for Engagement

The problem: Creating FOMO intentionally to drive engagement metrics

Manifestations:

  • Daily login rewards
  • Limited-time events with exclusive rewards
  • "Battle pass" systems with time pressure
  • Weekly lockouts that reset progress

Why it's problematic:

  • Engagement through anxiety, not enjoyment
  • Manipulative (exploits psychology)
  • Creates obligation (game as job)
  • Prioritizes metrics over experience

When it might be justified:

  • Scheduled social events (raid nights) - creates coordination
  • Seasonal content (keeps game fresh) - if not exclusive rewards
  • Commercial necessity (free-to-play sustainability)

Better alternatives:

  • Make events recurring (miss this time, comes back)
  • Remove exclusive rewards (cosmetics only)
  • Don't reset progress (accumulate at own pace)
  • Focus on enjoyment, not retention metrics

Design Principle: FOMO and Aesthetic Alignment

High-level principle: "FOMO emerges from irreversible scarcity combined with uncertainty - design whether missing opportunities creates productive tension or unproductive anxiety"

Guidelines:

Productive FOMO (serves experience):

  • Meaningful choices with consequences
  • Within bounded context (run, game, match)
  • Multiple viable paths
  • Easy to try different options later
  • Uncertainty is part of challenge

Unproductive FOMO (harms experience):

  • Obligation through time-gating
  • Real-time pressure to log in
  • Single optimal path (fear of suboptimal)
  • Exclusive content that can't be obtained later
  • Competitive pressure to keep up

Aesthetic alignment:

Challenge aesthetic:

  • ✓ Productive FOMO (meaningful choices)
  • ✗ Time-limited pressure (unrelated to skill)

Discovery aesthetic:

  • ✓ Multiple paths to explore
  • ✗ Punishing "wrong" exploration

Expression aesthetic:

  • ✓ Multiple viable builds
  • ✗ One optimal spec

Fellowship aesthetic:

  • ✓ Scheduled events (social coordination)
  • ✗ Exclusion for missing events

Progression aesthetic:

  • ⚠️ Time-gating can work (pacing)
  • ✗ But creates obligation if excessive

4. Connecting Psychological Effects to Resource Design

The Feedback Loop

Resource design → Psychological effects → Player behavior → Aesthetic experience

Example: Slay the Spire potions

  1. Design: Limited potion slots (3), powerful effects, found regularly
  2. Psychology: Loss aversion → hoarding ("save for boss")
  3. Behavior: Never use potions, lose with full slots
  4. Experience: Aesthetic undermined (strategic resource management goal not achieved)

Better design: Make potions abundant, less powerful

  • Psychology: Less precious → willing to use
  • Behavior: Use potions tactically
  • Experience: Strategic resource management achieved

Design Checklist: Psychological Effects

For each resource in your system, ask:

Possession Questions

  • What does having this resource feel like?
  • Is that feeling aligned with intended aesthetic?
  • Does accumulation create desired experience?

Example: HoMM3 armies

  • Possession feels like: Security, power, preparedness
  • Aligned with: Challenge aesthetic (battles require strong army)
  • Accumulation: Weekly rhythm of building forces
  • ✓ Well-aligned

Greed Questions

  • Will players want to hoard this resource?
  • Is hoarding aligned with intended aesthetic?
  • If not, what mechanisms discourage it?

Example: Catan resources

  • Players want to hoard: Yes (security, options)
  • Aligned with aesthetic: No (want trading, not stockpiling)
  • Mechanism: Hand limit + robber (forces spending)
  • ✓ Well-managed

FOMO Questions

  • Does missing this resource create anxiety?
  • Is that anxiety productive (meaningful choice) or unproductive (obligation)?
  • Can players recover from "missing" this?

Example: WoW weekly raid lockouts

  • Missing creates: Yes (fall behind on gear)
  • Type of anxiety: Unproductive (obligation to raid on schedule)
  • Can recover: Partially (next week, but others progressed)
  • ⚠️ Questionable alignment (commercial goal > aesthetic)

Integration with Aesthetic Goals

For each aesthetic, consider psychological profile:

Challenge Aesthetic

  • Possession: Preparedness, strategic options (good)
  • Greed: Hoarding can undermine decisions (manage carefully)
  • FOMO: Meaningful choices with consequences (good if bounded)

Fellowship Aesthetic

  • Possession: Shared resources, group identity (good)
  • Greed: Individual hoarding undermines sharing (prevent)
  • FOMO: Scheduled events for coordination (good), exclusion for missing (bad)

Narrative Aesthetic

  • Possession: Character identity, story history (good)
  • Greed: Stopping to optimize breaks fiction (minimize)
  • FOMO: Irreversible story choices (good), time pressure (bad)

Expression Aesthetic

  • Possession: Unique builds, personal style (good)
  • Greed: Collection can be the point (depends)
  • FOMO: Fear of "wrong" build undermines expression (prevent)

Progression Aesthetic

  • Possession: Achievement markers, visible growth (good)
  • Greed: Accumulation IS the aesthetic (enable)
  • FOMO: Falling behind others (manage carefully)

5. Summary and Key Takeaways

Core Insights

1. Psychological effects are not bugs - they're features (or should be)

  • Design creates psychology
  • Psychology shapes behavior
  • Behavior creates experience
  • Align them intentionally

2. Same psychology serves different aesthetics differently

  • Greed good for: Progression, Collection aesthetics
  • Greed bad for: Challenge (if prevents spending), Fellowship (if prevents sharing)
  • Designer must decide: Is this effect desired?

3. Commercial goals and aesthetic goals can conflict

  • Time-gated content creates engagement metrics
  • But also creates obligation/anxiety
  • Designer must choose: Metrics or experience?

Design Principles Summary

On Possession:

  • Match "having" experience to aesthetic goal
  • Security serves Challenge, identity serves Expression, status serves Progression

On Greed:

  • Greed emerges when accumulation > spending in reward
  • Encourage spending through: use-it-or-lose-it, reward loops, opacity
  • Or embrace accumulation if that's the aesthetic

On FOMO:

  • Productive FOMO: Meaningful choices, bounded consequences, multiple paths
  • Unproductive FOMO: Time pressure, obligation, optimization anxiety
  • Design for productive only

Practical Application

When designing resource systems:

  1. Identify intended aesthetic (what experience do we want?)
  2. Map psychological effects (what will this design create?)
  3. Check alignment (does psychology serve aesthetic?)
  4. Adjust mechanisms (if misaligned, change design)
  5. Playtest specifically for psychology (do players hoard when they should spend? Feel anxious when they should feel excited?)
