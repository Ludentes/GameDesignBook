Resource Management in Game Systems

Document 6: Spending and Agency

The Player's Perspective

Previous documents covered what resources are, when to use them, and how they replenish. This document focuses on the player's experience: How do players interact with resources? What choices do they make? What creates satisfying agency?

Core insight: Resource management is meaningless without player agency. The quality of spending decisions determines whether resources create engaging gameplay or tedious bookkeeping.

High-Level Principle: Agency Creates Engagement

✅ Validated across all analyzed games

Principle: Players must have meaningful choices about resource acquisition and spending for resource management to be engaging. Remove agency (automatic spending, obvious choices, no alternatives) and resources become busywork.

The spectrum:

  • High agency: Multiple viable options, unclear optimal choice, player expression
  • Moderate agency: Several options, some better than others, optimization possible
  • Low agency: Few options, obvious best choice, mechanical execution
  • No agency: Automatic or forced spending, no player decision

Implication: Resources without agency should be reconsidered. Either add meaningful choices or remove the resource.

Types of Resource Spending

Spending Type 1: Active Choice (Direct Player Control)

Definition: Player explicitly decides when and how to spend resource.

Examples:

  • Playing cards in Slay the Spire (choose which cards)
  • Spending gold on purchases (choose what to buy)
  • Using abilities in D&D (choose when to cast spell)
  • Blades stress resistance (choose whether to resist)

Characteristics

Decision point: Clear and conscious Player control: Complete Satisfaction: High (when choice matters)

What Creates Good Active Choices

Multiple viable options:

  • Not one obviously best answer
  • Different options for different situations
  • Player preference matters

Clear trade-offs:

  • Understand consequences
  • Can predict outcomes (roughly)
  • Risk/reward calculable

Timing matters:

  • When to spend is meaningful
  • Early vs. late spending creates different outcomes
  • Saving for later is viable strategy

Example - Slay the Spire Card Play:

  • Have 5 cards, 3 energy
  • Can play cards in different orders
  • Each order creates different outcome
  • Good because: Multiple viable sequences, timing matters, clear trade-offs

Example - Blades Stress Resistance:

  • GM presents consequence
  • Choose: Accept harm OR spend stress to resist
  • Must weigh: Stress now vs. injury later
  • Good because: Meaningful choice, player expression, consequences clear

When Active Choice Works Best

✓ Good for:

  • Challenge/Mastery aesthetic (optimization)
  • Strategic depth
  • Player expression
  • Tactical gameplay

✗ Poor for:

  • Fast-paced action (too much decision-making slows flow)
  • Submission aesthetic (choices create stress)
  • When optimal choice is obvious (becomes mechanical)

Spending Type 2: Passive Drain (Involuntary Loss)

Definition: Resource depletes automatically or through external forces, player doesn't choose to spend.

Examples:

  • HP lost when hit (damage happens TO you)
  • Time passing (real-time strategy, survival games)
  • Upkeep costs (automatic deductions)
  • Entropy/decay systems

Characteristics

Decision point: Indirect (prevent loss, not control spending) Player control: Defensive (minimize loss, not choose use) Satisfaction: From prevention, not spending

What Makes Passive Drain Work

Predictable rate:

  • Know how fast depleting
  • Can plan around drain
  • Not arbitrary or random

Player can influence:

  • Avoid damage (defensive play)
  • Slow drain (efficiency)
  • Counteract with recovery

Creates pressure:

  • Urgency from depletion
  • Must act before runs out
  • Tension over time

Example - HP in Combat:

  • Takes damage from enemies
  • Didn't choose to spend HP
  • But: Can avoid damage through positioning, defense
  • Creates: Tactical challenge in damage prevention

Example - Hunger in Survival Games:

  • Depletes automatically over time
  • Didn't choose to get hungry
  • But: Must manage food supply, plan meals
  • Creates: Resource pressure, survival tension

When Passive Drain Works Best

✓ Good for:

  • Tension aesthetic (mounting pressure)
  • Survival gameplay
  • Time pressure
  • Defense/prevention challenges

✗ Poor for:

  • Player expression (not choosing use)
  • Strategic depth (no spending optimization)
  • When feels arbitrary or unavoidable

Spending Type 3: Automatic Optimization (System-Managed)

Definition: System spends resources optimally for player, no conscious choice needed.

Examples:

  • Auto-battle in mobile games
  • Passive abilities (always on, no activation)
  • Automatic resource allocation in some strategy games
  • Experience points (automatically level up)

Characteristics

Decision point: None (system handles it) Player control: Minimal to none Satisfaction: From outcomes, not process

When Automatic Works

For non-core gameplay:

  • Grinding/repetitive tasks
  • Bookkeeping that isn't interesting
  • Tedious micromanagement

When optimal choice is obvious:

  • Only one viable option
  • Choice would be mechanical, not strategic
  • Automation removes busywork

For accessibility:

  • Let players opt into automation
  • Simplifies complex systems
  • Reduces cognitive load

Example - XP Auto-Leveling:

  • Gain XP automatically from actions
  • Level up when reach threshold (no spending decision)
  • Works because: Spending XP isn't interesting (always want to level up)
  • Alternative: Manual leveling with build choices (if customization matters)

⚠️ Warning: Automation removes agency. Only automate when choice isn't meaningful or when offering as accessibility option.

When Automatic Doesn't Work

✗ Core gameplay: If spending resource IS the game, can't automate ✗ Player expression: If build/style depends on choices, need manual control ✗ Challenge aesthetic: If optimization is the challenge, automation removes it

Anti-Pattern: Over-Automation Automating choices that should be player expression or strategic decisions.

Example: Auto-spec character builds in RPG Problem: Removes player identity, one of the reasons to play Fix: Either make choices more meaningful or accept automation is appropriate

Spending Type 4: Opportunity Cost (Implicit Spending)

Definition: "Spending" resource by choosing not to get it, or by using it one way that prevents another use.

Examples:

  • Slay the Spire skipping card reward (choose not to "spend" deck slot)
  • Taking one path in branching game (can't take other path)
  • Blades downtime actions (do this OR that, not both)
  • Specialization in skill trees (invest here, not there)

Characteristics

Decision point: What to pursue/acquire Player control: Indirect (shape what you have) Satisfaction: From long-term build/path

What Makes Opportunity Cost Work

Multiple valuable options:

  • All paths are useful
  • No single "best" choice
  • Different builds/playstyles viable

Long-term consequences:

  • Choice affects future options
  • Creates commitment
  • Specialization emerges

Meaningful trade-offs:

  • Understand what you're giving up
  • Can evaluate relative value
  • Risk/reward visible

Example - Slay the Spire Card Skipping:

  • Offered card after combat
  • Can take or skip
  • Opportunity cost: Taking weak card dilutes deck
  • Choice: "Does this improve my deck?" not just "Is this good?"
  • Works because: Quality over quantity, curation is skill

Example - Blades Downtime Actions:

  • Can recover stress OR heal harm OR advance project
  • Can't do all with limited actions
  • Opportunity cost: Each action chosen prevents others
  • Works because: Reveals player priorities, character-defining

When Opportunity Cost Works Best

✓ Good for:

  • Mastery aesthetic (optimization through curation)
  • Player expression (choices reveal identity)
  • Long-term planning
  • Replayability (different paths each time)

✗ Poor for:

  • Short-term gameplay (consequences not felt)
  • When all options equivalent (no real choice)
  • Submission aesthetic (creates decision stress)

The Spending Decision Framework

Good resource spending creates this decision structure:

Step 1: Awareness

Player knows:

  • How much resource they have
  • What spending options exist
  • Rough consequences of each option

Design requirement: Clear information

  • Visible resource levels
  • Understandable options
  • Predictable outcomes (at least generally)

Step 2: Evaluation

Player considers:

  • Immediate benefit vs. future need
  • Risk vs. reward
  • Alternatives and opportunity costs

Design requirement: Meaningful trade-offs

  • Multiple viable options
  • Different situations favor different choices
  • No single dominant strategy

Step 3: Commitment

Player decides:

  • Spend now or save
  • This option or that option
  • Accept consequences

Design requirement: Irreversibility (at least short-term)

  • Can't undo immediately
  • Choice matters
  • Creates weight

Step 4: Feedback

Player experiences:

  • Consequences of choice
  • Success or failure
  • Learning for next time

Design requirement: Clear causality

  • Understand why outcome occurred
  • Can improve decision-making
  • Not random or arbitrary

When This Framework Breaks Down

Missing Awareness:

  • Hidden costs or consequences
  • Unclear options
  • Result: Frustration, can't make informed choice

Missing Evaluation:

  • Only one viable option
  • Obvious best choice
  • Result: Mechanical execution, no strategy

Missing Commitment:

  • Can immediately undo choice
  • No consequences
  • Result: Choice doesn't matter

Missing Feedback:

  • Can't tell if choice was good
  • Random outcomes
  • Result: Can't learn or improve

Spending Patterns and What They Create

Pattern 1: Conservative Hoarding

Behavior: Save resources for "when really needed"

Causes:

  • Harsh scarcity (resource very limited)
  • Uncertain future (don't know what's coming)
  • High consequence (mistakes costly)
  • One-time use (can't get more)

Example: Never using elixirs in Final Fantasy

  • Rare items
  • Don't know what future bosses require
  • Fear of wasting

Design implications:

  • If want spending: Reduce uncertainty, enable recovery
  • If want hoarding: Make it viable strategy (survival aesthetic)

Fix for unwanted hoarding:

  • Renewable resources (reduces fear)
  • Use-it-or-lose-it pressure (Catan hand limit)
  • Clear telegraphing (know what's coming)
  • Abundant enough to experiment

Pattern 2: Aggressive Spending

Behavior: Spend resources freely, don't conserve

Causes:

  • Fast recovery (will get more soon)
  • Clear immediate benefit (spending is obviously good)
  • Low consequence for running out (just recover)
  • Optimization mindset (efficiency > conservation)

Example: Modern WoW cooldown usage

  • Abilities recover quickly
  • Want to use as often as possible
  • Holding them wastes potential uses

Design implications:

  • If want aggressive play: Fast recovery, clear benefits
  • If want conservation: Slow recovery, uncertain future needs

Pattern 3: Optimization

Behavior: Calculate optimal use, maximize efficiency

Causes:

  • Clear optimal strategies exist
  • Perfect information available
  • Mathematical relationships transparent
  • Mastery aesthetic appeals to player

Example: Slay the Spire energy optimization

  • 3 energy per turn
  • Can calculate exact damage/block
  • Find optimal card sequence

Design implications:

  • If want optimization: Provide information, clear math
  • If want intuition: Hide information, complex interactions

Pattern 4: Satisficing

Behavior: "Good enough" rather than optimal, prioritize convenience

Causes:

  • Optimization too complex/tedious
  • Good enough succeeds anyway
  • Cognitive load too high for optimization
  • Not Mastery aesthetic

Example: Casual players in strategy games

  • Don't calculate exact optimal
  • Use heuristics ("this seems fine")
  • Succeed without perfection

Design implications:

  • Support both: Optimization possible but not required
  • Multiple difficulty levels
  • Accessibility through satisficing, depth through optimization

Spending and Agency Patterns

High Agency: Build Your Own Strategy

Pattern: Players construct unique approaches through spending choices

Example - Slay the Spire Deck Building:

  • Every card choice shapes deck
  • Different builds viable
  • Player expression through curation

Creates:

  • Replayability (different builds each time)
  • Player identity (this is MY deck)
  • Mastery depth (learn what works)

✓ Best for: Mastery, Expression, Challenge aesthetics

Moderate Agency: Choose Your Path

Pattern: Branching paths, must choose between good options

Example - RPG Skill Trees:

  • Can't max everything
  • Must specialize
  • Different paths for different playstyles

Creates:

  • Strategic commitment
  • Trade-offs
  • Build diversity

✓ Best for: Challenge, Expression aesthetics

Low Agency: Obvious Optimal Choice

Pattern: One choice clearly best, others sub-optimal

Example: Required upgrades in many games

  • Must buy to progress
  • No real alternative
  • Just gate-keeping

Creates:

  • Mechanical execution
  • Illusion of choice
  • Busywork

✗ Poor for: Most aesthetics (not engaging) ⚠️ Consider: Remove resource entirely if choices aren't meaningful

No Agency: Automatic or Forced

Pattern: Player doesn't choose, system handles

Example: Auto-level systems, forced story beats

Creates:

  • Streamlined experience
  • Removes tedium
  • But also removes player expression

✓ Good for: Non-core systems, accessibility ✗ Poor for: Core gameplay, player expression

Spending Complexity vs. Depth

Complexity: How Many Options/Calculations

High complexity:

  • Many spending options
  • Complex interactions
  • Difficult to calculate optimal
  • Example: D&D 3.5e feat selection

Low complexity:

  • Few spending options
  • Simple interactions
  • Easy to evaluate
  • Example: Binary choice games

Depth: How Meaningful Choices Are

High depth:

  • Multiple viable strategies
  • No dominant option
  • Context determines best choice
  • Example: Slay the Spire card play

Low depth:

  • One clearly best option
  • Choice is mechanical
  • Context doesn't matter much
  • Example: Obvious upgrades

The Sweet Spot: High Depth, Moderate Complexity

Goal: Meaningful choices without overwhelming

Characteristics:

  • Enough options to create variety
  • Not so many that analysis paralysis
  • Clear trade-offs
  • Context-dependent optimization

Example - Blades Stress Resistance:

  • Binary choice (resist or don't)
  • But meaningful (immediate cost vs. lasting consequence)
  • Context-dependent (depends on stress level, consequence severity)
  • Low complexity, high depth

Example - Slay the Spire Energy:

  • Moderate complexity (5-10 cards to consider)
  • High depth (sequence matters, situation varies)
  • Clear information (can calculate)
  • Moderate complexity, high depth

Design Principle: Prefer depth over complexity. Simple systems with meaningful choices beat complex systems with obvious choices.

Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Illusion of Choice

Anti-Pattern: Fake Decisions Multiple spending options but one obviously best.

Example: Upgrade system where one option is 2x better than others

  • Appears to be choice
  • Actually no decision
  • Just gating

Fix: Balance options or remove pretense of choice

Mistake 2: Analysis Paralysis

Anti-Pattern: Too Many Options, Unclear Consequences So many spending options that choosing is overwhelming.

Example: 100 skills in skill tree, no clear specializations

  • Can't evaluate all options
  • Unclear what's good
  • Paralyzing, not engaging

Fix: Reduce options, provide guidance, or make reversible

Mistake 3: Unclear Causality

Anti-Pattern: Can't Tell if Choice Was Good Spend resource but can't evaluate outcome.

Example: Stat investment with hidden calculations

  • Don't know what "+3 Magic" does
  • Can't optimize
  • Feels arbitrary

Fix: Show effects clearly, enable learning

Mistake 4: Irrelevant Choices

Anti-Pattern: Spending Decisions That Don't Matter Choices exist but don't affect outcomes significantly.

Example: Cosmetic-only "choices" presented as strategic

  • Game treats as meaningful
  • Actually doesn't matter
  • Wastes player attention

Fix: Either make choices matter or acknowledge they're cosmetic

Summary: Designing Spending and Agency

Spending types:

  1. Active choice: Player directly controls (high engagement)
  2. Passive drain: Involuntary loss (defensive challenge)
  3. Automatic: System-managed (reduces busywork)
  4. Opportunity cost: Implicit through acquisition choices (long-term strategy)

Good spending decisions require:

  • Awareness: Know options and consequences
  • Evaluation: Meaningful trade-offs
  • Commitment: Choices matter
  • Feedback: Can learn from outcomes

Agency creates engagement:

  • High agency: Build your own strategy
  • Moderate agency: Choose your path
  • Low agency: Obvious optimal choice (consider removing)
  • No agency: Automatic (only for non-core systems)

Prefer depth over complexity:

  • Meaningful choices with few options
  • Better than meaningless choices with many options
  • Context-dependent optimization

Avoid:

  • Illusion of choice (obvious optimal)
  • Analysis paralysis (too many options)
  • Unclear causality (can't evaluate)
  • Irrelevant choices (don't affect outcomes)

The fundamental test: "Do spending decisions create interesting strategic choices that serve my aesthetic?"
