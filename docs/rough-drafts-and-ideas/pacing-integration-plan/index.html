1. Opening Reframe (Add at beginning - 150 words)

Add this paragraph:

Pacing is not a single mechanism—it's the temporal experience of play that emerges from how your other components interact. When do resources deplete? How often do decisions occur? When do consequences arrive? How do tension and release alternate? These questions determine whether your game feels like a steady march, a series of explosive moments, or a gradual build to climax. Why: Establishes pacing as emergent and experiential, not just mechanical.

2. Types of Pacing Patterns (New section - 300 words)

Add after existing mechanism descriptions:

Attrition Pacing

  • Pressure builds gradually across multiple encounters/sessions
  • Resources deplete slowly, recovery is limited
  • Example: Old-school D&D (daily spell slots, slow healing)
  • Serves: Tension, strategic planning, resource management aesthetics
  • Feel: "We're being ground down, every decision matters"

Episodic Pacing

  • Each encounter/scene is self-contained
  • Resources reset frequently, fresh start each time
  • Example: Modern D&D short rests, Fate scenes
  • Serves: Challenge (per-encounter), narrative flexibility
  • Feel: "Each scene is a new opportunity, failure doesn't compound"

Wave Pacing

  • Alternating intensity: build-up → climax → recovery → repeat
  • Multiple cycles per session
  • Example: Blades in the Dark scores (prep → execution → consequences → downtime)
  • Serves: Engagement curve, emotional dynamics
  • Feel: "Tension rises and falls naturally, I can breathe between peaks"

Continuous Pacing

  • Steady state without clear breaks
  • Real-time pressure or seamless scene flow
  • Example: Real-time strategy games, some narrative games
  • Serves: Immersion, flow state, sustained tension
  • Feel: "I'm in the zone" or "I can't put this down"

Punctuated Pacing

  • Long quiet periods interrupted by sudden intense moments
  • Most time is low-pressure, but stakes appear unexpectedly
  • Example: Horror games, some exploration-focused games
  • Serves: Discovery, narrative surprise, tension through anticipation
  • Feel: "Calm exploration then sudden terror" or "Waiting for the other shoe to drop"

Why: Gives designers vocabulary for discussing and designing pacing intentionally.

3. Aesthetic → Pacing Mapping (New section - 200 words)

Add after pacing patterns:

Different aesthetics require different pacing patterns:

Challenge Aesthetic:

  • Tactical challenge → Wave pacing (individual encounters with clear resolution)
  • Strategic challenge → Attrition pacing (decisions compound across time)
  • Mastery challenge → Continuous or episodic (depends on skill type)

Fellowship Aesthetic:

  • Episodic or punctuated (leaves space for social interaction)
  • Avoid continuous real-time (competes with conversation)
  • Recovery periods essential (time to talk about what happened)

Narrative Aesthetic:

  • Wave pacing matching story structure (rising action, climax, resolution)
  • Scene-based episodic (natural narrative beats)
  • Punctuated for dramatic timing

Discovery Aesthetic:

  • Punctuated or continuous (exploration at player pace)
  • Avoid forced attrition (discourages experimentation)
  • Variable rhythm (different areas different pace)

Expression Aesthetic:

  • Player-controlled pacing (they set rhythm)
  • Episodic (can try different builds/approaches)
  • Avoid pressure (removes experimentation space)

Why: Connects pacing to your existing aesthetic framework, shows it serves experience goals.

4. How Pacing Emerges (New section - 250 words)

Add after aesthetic mapping:

Pacing emerges from the interaction of your other system components:

From Resources:

  • Recovery rates determine whether pressure builds or resets
  • Fast recovery → Episodic pacing (fresh start each encounter)
  • Slow recovery → Attrition pacing (pressure compounds)
  • Mixed recovery rates → Layered pacing (different rhythms for different resources)
  • See Resource Management chapter for detailed exploration of recovery as pacing mechanism

From Cognitive Load:

  • Sustained high load → Exhaustion pacing (needs breaks)
  • Waves of high/low load → Natural rhythm pacing
  • Real-time pressure → Continuous pacing (no pause to think)
  • Turn-based structure → Episodic pacing (clear decision points)
  • See Cognitive Load chapter for how mental effort patterns create experiential pacing

From Decision Frequency:

  • Many decisions per minute → Fast pacing (StarCraft, action games)
  • Few decisions per session → Slow pacing (strategic, narrative)
  • Variable decision density → Punctuated pacing

From Consequence Timing:

  • Immediate consequences → Fast feedback, episodic feel
  • Delayed consequences → Building tension, attrition feel
  • Mixed timing → Complex pacing (some immediate, some delayed)

From Configuration:

  • How mechanics, parameters, context, and pacing combine
  • Same mechanics + different pacing = completely different feel
  • Example: Weather Factory (Cultist Simulator vs. Book of Hours—same mechanics, opposite pacing through parameter changes)

Why: Shows pacing as emergent property, connects to other foundational concepts, sets up later chapters.

5. Measuring and Adjusting Pacing (New section - 150 words)

Add before closing:

Unlike mechanics or resources, pacing must be measured experientially:

Observable Indicators:

  • Session time distribution (% in different activities)
  • Player energy patterns (engaged, exhausted, bored, excited)
  • Natural break points (when players pause/discuss)
  • Emotional arc (tension building, release, satisfaction)

Common Pacing Problems:

  • Too fast: Players feel rushed, can't make meaningful decisions
  • Too slow: Players feel bored, sessions drag
  • No rhythm: Constant same-level intensity creates fatigue
  • Mismatched: Pacing conflicts with intended aesthetic

Adjustment Strategies:

  • Change resource recovery rates (faster = more episodic)
  • Add/remove rest periods (waves need recovery)
  • Adjust decision frequency (fewer decisions = slower pace)
  • Modify consequence timing (immediate = faster feel)

For specific measurement techniques and target values, see Principles to Numbers chapter.

Why: Provides practical guidance, previews later content, acknowledges pacing needs different measurement approach.

1. Add Explicit Pacing Connection to Recovery Section (150 words)

Location: In existing recovery discussion (likely Part 2 or Part 3)

Add this section:

Recovery Rates ARE Pacing Mechanisms

When you set recovery rates, you're designing pacing:

Fast Recovery (within scene/encounter):

  • Creates episodic pacing—each encounter feels fresh
  • Players can use resources freely without long-term consequences
  • Supports: Challenge (per-encounter), narrative flexibility
  • Example: Modern D&D short rests restore some resources
  • Feel: "I can go all-out this fight"

Slow Recovery (across multiple sessions):

  • Creates attrition pacing—pressure compounds over time
  • Players must conserve, plan ahead, make tough tradeoffs
  • Supports: Strategic challenge, tension, survival
  • Example: Old-school D&D daily spell slots, limited healing
  • Feel: "Every spell slot matters for the next three sessions"

Mixed Recovery (different rates for different resources):

  • Creates layered pacing—multiple rhythms simultaneously
  • Some resources for immediate tactical decisions, others for strategic planning
  • Supports: Complex challenge, narrative with mechanical stakes
  • Example: Blades in the Dark (stress recovers between scores, harm recovers slowly)
  • Feel: "I can push myself now, but I'm building toward trauma"

No Recovery (permanent loss):

  • Creates high-stakes pacing—every loss matters forever
  • Maximum tension, but only works if losses are recoverable indirectly
  • Supports: Tension, strategic resource preservation
  • Example: Heroes of Might and Magic III (armies lost in battle don't regenerate)
  • Feel: "Did I win efficiently enough?"

This is why recovery rate is one of your most important design decisions—it determines the temporal experience of your entire game.

Why: Makes explicit that recovery rates create pacing, provides concrete examples.

3. Expand Examples to Show Pacing Impact (100 words)

Location: In game analysis sections (Blades, HoMM3, etc.)

For Blades in the Dark example, add:

The genius of Blades' two-resource system is that stress and harm create different pacing layers. Stress recovers quickly (between scores), creating episodic pacing for tactical risk-taking—you can push yourself freely knowing you'll recover. Harm recovers slowly (costs downtime actions and coin), creating attrition pacing for strategic decision-making—accumulating harm across multiple scores creates mounting pressure. This dual-layer pacing serves both Challenge (tactical stress management per score) and Narrative (long-term trauma accumulation) aesthetics simultaneously.

For HoMM3 example, add:

Permanent army losses create unforgiving attrition pacing. Unlike most games where resources regenerate, every battle's casualties are forever. This makes each tactical decision carry strategic weight—"Did I win efficiently?" matters because losses compound across the campaign. The weekly production rhythm provides the only recovery mechanism, creating a higher-level pacing layer: the rhythm of expansion (build up armies) and expenditure (spend them in battles). This dual-timescale pacing serves strategic Challenge aesthetic perfectly.

Why: Shows pacing analysis of existing games, demonstrates how recovery creates experiential differences.

1. Add "Cognitive Load Patterns Create Pacing" Section (200 words)

Location: After discussing load types or in implications section

Add new section:

Cognitive Load Patterns Create Experiential Pacing

The temporal pattern of cognitive load determines experiential pacing:

Sustained High Load:

  • Creates exhaustion pacing—no mental recovery
  • Real-time strategy games, action games during combat
  • Serves: Challenge (reactive), tension through pressure
  • Problem: Unsustainable for long sessions without breaks
  • Design implication: Must include recovery periods or accept short sessions

Wave Pattern (High → Low → High):

  • Creates rhythm pacing—natural tension/release cycle
  • Turn-based tactics (planning = high, execution = lower), story games (intense scenes + calm scenes)
  • Serves: Most aesthetics (natural engagement curve)
  • Benefit: Sustainable for longer sessions, emotionally satisfying

Steady Low Load:

  • Creates relaxed pacing—comfortable sustained engagement
  • Casual games, social games, cozy games
  • Serves: Fellowship, expression, narrative (social focus)
  • Benefit: Can maintain indefinitely, supports conversation

Punctuated Load (Low → Sudden High → Low):

  • Creates surprise pacing—calm interrupted by intensity
  • Horror games, some exploration games, dramatic story beats
  • Serves: Tension through anticipation, discovery, narrative surprise
  • Challenge: Timing is critical—too frequent becomes rhythm, too rare becomes boring

Real-time vs. Turn-based is a Pacing Decision:

Real-time forces continuous high load:

  • No time to fully analyze options
  • Creates pressure pacing, urgency, reactive play
  • Example: StarCraft, They Are Billions (competitive mode)

Turn-based allows variable load:

  • Time to think deeply between actions
  • Enables wave pacing, rest between decisions
  • Example: Civilization, XCOM, traditional RPGs

This is why time structure is one of your first design decisions—it fundamentally constrains your pacing options.

Why: Shows cognitive load as pacing mechanism, connects to existing load discussion.

2. Add Pacing to Recovery Discussion (100 words)

Location: Where recovery from cognitive load is discussed

Add:

Recovery Periods Are Essential Pacing Elements

Just as resources need recovery mechanics, cognitive load needs recovery periods:

Mechanical Recovery:

  • Downtime scenes with minimal decisions
  • Automated/simplified sections
  • Social time with low mechanical load
  • Provides: Mental rest, sustainable engagement

Emotional Recovery:

  • Low-stakes scenes after high-stakes
  • Victory celebration moments
  • Comic relief after tension
  • Provides: Emotional processing, catharsis

Without recovery periods:

  • Sustained high load → exhaustion → disengagement
  • Constant tension → numbness (tension becomes baseline)
  • No rhythm → monotonous feel

Pacing requires intentional recovery design, not just intensity design.

Why: Connects recovery to pacing explicitly, shows it's not optional.

1. Expand Temporal Dynamics Measurables Section (300 words)

Current proposal includes:

  • Cycle length

  • Engagement continuity

Expand with explicit pacing connection:

Temporal Dynamics: Measuring Pacing

These measurables capture the pacing properties of your system. When you set target values for these, you're designing pacing intentionally.

Cycle Length (Decision → Resolution time):

  • Measures: How long from action to outcome?
  • Pacing impact: Short cycles = fast pacing, long cycles = slow pacing
  • Targets by aesthetic:
    • Challenge (Reactive): Seconds (twitch games, real-time)
    • Challenge (Tactical): Minutes (turn-based tactics, combat)
    • Narrative: Scene-based (10-30 minutes per story beat)
    • Discovery: Variable (player sets pace)
    • Fellowship: Long (don't rush social time)

Rhythm Variability (Consistency of cycles):

  • Measures: Do cycles take similar time or vary?
  • Pacing impact:
    • Steady rhythm = predictable, comfortable (or monotonous)
    • Variable rhythm = dynamic, engaging (or chaotic)
  • Targets by aesthetic:
    • Challenge: Moderate variability (some consistency, some surprise)
    • Narrative: High variability (pacing serves drama)
    • Fellowship: Low variability (predictable so social flows)

Intensity Gradient (How tension changes):

  • Measures: Pattern of high/low intensity over session
  • Pacing patterns:
    • Flat: Steady intensity (good for flow state, bad for engagement arc)
    • Waves: Build → climax → release → repeat (good for most aesthetics)
    • Ratcheting: Gradually increasing pressure (good for tension, exhausting)
    • Punctuated: Calm → sudden spike → calm (good for surprise, horror)
  • Targets: Match your intended pacing pattern from Game Systems chapter

Recovery Frequency (How often are low-intensity periods):

  • Measures: % of session time in "rest" vs. "action"
  • Pacing impact: Determines sustainability
  • Targets by aesthetic:
    • Challenge (Intense): 20-30% recovery (short breaks between challenges)
    • Narrative: 40-50% recovery (time to process, discuss)
    • Fellowship: 50%+ recovery (social time = the point)
    • Discovery: Variable (player controlled)

Session Arc Structure:

  • Measures: How does single session flow?
  • Common structures:
    • Single arc: Hook → build → climax → resolution
    • Multiple arcs: 2-3 complete cycles per session
    • Episodic: Several discrete scenes/encounters
    • Open: No predetermined structure, emergent
  • Targets: Match engagement curve from Game Systems

These measurables are how you make pacing concrete and measurable. "Wave pacing" becomes "3-5 cycles per session, each 15-20 minutes, with 5-minute recovery periods between."

Why: Makes pacing measurable and targetable, bridges concept to implementation.

2. Add Pacing Example to Aesthetic Templates (200 words per aesthetic)

Location: In the aesthetic-specific template sections

For Challenge (Tactical) Aesthetic, add pacing section:

Pacing Design for Challenge (Tactical):

Intended Pacing Pattern: Wave pacing with clear cycles

Measurable Targets:

  • Cycle length: 15-20 minutes per tactical encounter
  • Cycles per session: 3-4 encounters
  • Intensity gradient: Moderate baseline → high during encounters → low recovery
  • Recovery frequency: 25-30% of session (5-10 minutes between encounters)
  • Session arc: Multiple waves (each encounter is complete arc)

How to Achieve These Targets:

Cycle length (15-20 min per encounter):

  • Time-to-kill: 5-7 rounds at 2-3 minutes per round
  • Therefore: Damage:HP ratio ~20% (enemy survives ~5 rounds)
  • Therefore: If player damage = 15, enemy HP = 75

Recovery periods (5-10 min between encounters):

  • Include mechanical downtime (healing, looting, planning)
  • Not just "move to next room"—give mental break
  • Resource recovery here supports episodic feel per encounter

Intensity gradient (waves):

  • Encounters have internal build: start → middle → climax → resolution
  • Each encounter feels complete
  • Recovery between is genuine low intensity (no pressure)

Validation During Playtest:

  • Time actual encounters (are they 15-20 min?)
  • Observe player energy (do they seem engaged during, relaxed after?)
  • Count cycles per session (hitting 3-4 target?)
  • Ask: "Did pacing feel right?" (too rushed, too slow, good rhythm?)

If Pacing Wrong:

  • Too fast: Reduce damage:HP ratio (longer encounters) OR add more recovery time
  • Too slow: Increase damage or reduce enemy HP OR cut recovery time
  • No rhythm: Add explicit recovery mechanics (short rests, safe zones)
  • Exhausting: More recovery time OR reduce cognitive load during encounters

Why: Shows complete process from pacing goal → measurable targets → actual numbers → validation.

Location: In playtesting/validation section

Measuring Pacing (Observational + Timer):

Time-based measurements:

  • Start timer at session beginning
  • Note timestamps for:
    • Each encounter/scene start and end
    • Natural break points (when players pause, discuss)
    • Intensity changes (combat starts, tension rises, relief moments)
  • Calculate:
    • Average cycle length
    • % time in high vs. low intensity
    • Recovery period frequency and duration

Observational measurements:

  • Player energy patterns:
    • Leaning forward (engaged/intense) vs. leaning back (relaxed)
    • Quick speech (excited) vs. slow/thoughtful (processing)
    • Laughter, tension, relief visible in body language
  • Note when energy changes occur
  • Track: Does energy pattern match intended pacing?

Post-session questions:

  • "Did pacing feel right?" (rushed, slow, good rhythm)
  • "Were there moments you needed a break but didn't get one?"
  • "Were there sections that dragged?"
  • "How was the rhythm—steady, waves, unpredictable?"

Compare to targets:

  • Actual cycle length vs. target cycle length
  • Actual recovery % vs. target recovery %
  • Observed intensity pattern vs. intended pacing pattern

If mismatched, adjust ONE variable and retest.

Why: Provides concrete playtest protocol for pacing, shows it's measurable not just subjective.

2. Example: Scenario 1 (Busy Adults Campaign)

Add this section:

Pacing Design for Busy Adults Campaign

Aesthetic Goal: Fellowship (social bonding) + Narrative (shared storytelling)

Intended Pacing Pattern: Episodic with slow attrition overlay

Why This Pacing Serves the Aesthetic:

  • Episodic: Each monthly session must feel complete (can't depend on remembering last session)
  • Slow attrition: Campaign-level story progression, but forgiving pacing
  • Fellowship needs: Low mechanical intensity, time for social interaction
  • Monthly cadence constraint: Can't use continuous or tight attrition (too much time between sessions)

Measurable Pacing Targets:

  • Cycle length: Scene-based (20-30 minutes per major scene)
  • Cycles per session: 2-3 complete scenes (not rushed)
  • Intensity gradient: Gentle waves (mostly social, occasional dramatic peaks)
  • Recovery frequency: 50%+ (social time = primary content)
  • Session arc: Complete story beat each session (episodic, not cliffhangers)

System Design to Achieve Pacing:

  • Resource recovery: Fast (reset between sessions—no tracking across months)
  • Decision frequency: Low mechanical (0.5-1 per minute), moderate narrative (what do we do?)
  • Cognitive load pattern: Low sustained (minimize mechanics, maximize fiction)
  • Consequence timing: Immediate story consequences, but reset mechanical state

Concrete Design Choices:

Session structure:

  • Opening: "Last time recap" (5 min) + social catch-up (10 min)
  • Scene 1: Low-intensity establishing (20 min)
  • Scene 2: Higher-intensity dramatic moment (30 min)
  • Scene 3: Resolution + setup for next time (20 min)
  • Closing: Social wrap-up (10 min)
  • Total: ~90 minutes (fits busy adult schedule)

Resource design:

  • No resources that accumulate across sessions (reset each time)
  • Exception: Narrative resources (relationships, reputation) tracked by LLM
  • Within-session resources: Simple, minimal tracking (3 stress boxes, not 9)

Recovery mechanics:

  • Assume full recovery between monthly sessions
  • Within-session recovery: Frequent "breather" scenes
  • No death spirals (avoid compounding negative consequences)

Why These Choices: Monthly cadence means you CANNOT use:

  • Tight attrition pacing (players forget resource states)
  • Continuous narrative (lose thread between sessions)
  • Complex mechanics (rules forgotten between sessions)

You MUST use:

  • Episodic completeness (each session satisfying)
  • Simple state (what can be remembered or externalized)
  • Social pacing (time for life catch-up is feature not bug)

Playtesting Validation: [If implemented, add:]

  • Time each scene (hitting 20-30 min target?)
  • Measure social time (actually 50%+ of session?)
  • Ask: "Did session feel complete?" (not rushed, not dragging, satisfying ending)
  • Check: "Did we spend right amount of time on mechanics vs. story vs. socializing?"

Why: Shows pacing design process applied to specific constraints, demonstrates how monthly cadence forces episodic pacing.

3. Example: Scenario 3 (Mystery Investigation)

Add this section:

Pacing Design for Mystery Investigation

Aesthetic Goal: Challenge (solving puzzle) + Narrative (dramatic investigation)

Intended Pacing Pattern: Progressive revelation waves (investigation → clue → complication cycle)

Why This Pacing Serves the Aesthetic:

  • Challenge: Each investigation cycle has decision (where to investigate?), action (gather clues), resolution (interpret findings)
  • Narrative: Mystery needs building tension, not constant intensity
  • Progressive revelation: Information accumulates, picture becomes clearer
  • Each clue discovery = small payoff, solution = big payoff

Measurable Pacing Targets:

  • Cycle length: Investigation scene = 15-20 minutes
  • Cycles per session: 4-6 investigation scenes
  • Intensity gradient: Ratcheting upward (tension increases as solution nears)
  • Recovery frequency: 20-30% (brief between investigations, longer at act breaks)
  • Session arc: Act structure (setup → investigation → revelation → climax)

System Design to Achieve Pacing:

  • Resource recovery: Investigation points reset per scene (episodic feel per investigation)
  • NPC patience/cooperation: Slow depletion (attrition across session)
  • Decision frequency: Moderate (1-2 per scene: where to go, what to ask)
  • Cognitive load: Building (more clues = more synthesis required)
  • Consequence timing: Delayed (villain reacts to investigation, pressure builds)

Concrete Design Choices:

Investigation cycle pacing:

  1. Scene selection (2-3 min): Players choose where to investigate
  2. Scene play (10-15 min): Discover clues, gather information
  3. Interpretation (3-5 min): Players discuss what they learned
  4. Track villain reaction: Timer ticks down (time pressure)
  5. Repeat cycle

Resource pacing:

  • Core clues: Automatic (no resource cost, ensures progress)
  • Depth clues: Cost investigation points (creates meaningful choices)
  • Investigation points: Refresh per scene (keeps decision space open)
  • NPC cooperation: Depletes over session (creates time pressure)
  • Villain action track: Advances after X scenes (ratchets tension)

Intensity curve across session:

  • Act I (Scenes 1-2): Setup, low pressure, gathering basic clues
  • Act II (Scenes 3-4): Complications, moderate pressure, pattern emerging
  • Act III (Scenes 5-6): Crisis, high pressure, solution within reach
  • Climax: Confrontation or revelation

Why These Choices:

Progressive revelation pacing requires:

  • Early scenes: Easy clues, build foundation (low cognitive load)
  • Middle scenes: Harder clues, conflicting information (moderate load)
  • Late scenes: Synthesis required, time pressure (high load)
  • This ratcheting load creates satisfying build toward solution

Per-scene resource refresh prevents:

  • Investigation stalling (running out of points mid-session)
  • Optimal-play pressure (saving points for "important" scenes)
  • Paralysis (fear of spending resources wrong)

Villain reaction timer creates:

  • Time pressure without rush (X scenes available, use them wisely)
  • Tension gradient (timer visible, ticking down)
  • Meaningful pacing choices ("Do we investigate one more scene or confront now?")

Playtesting Validation:

  • Time investigation scenes (hitting 15-20 min each?)
  • Count scenes per act (hitting 2/2/2 structure?)
  • Observe cognitive load (building appropriately?)
  • Measure time pressure (players feel urgency in Act III?)
  • Ask: "Did mystery feel like it built to satisfying conclusion?"
  • Check: "Was pacing too rushed (couldn't think) or too slow (lost interest)?"

If Pacing Wrong:

  • Too fast: Add more investigation points (more depth possible per scene) OR reduce villain timer speed
  • Too slow: Reduce scenes required OR add complications (force decisions)
  • No tension build: Make villain timer more visible/threatening OR add more complications in later acts
  • Cognitive overload: Give more explicit structure (act breaks, recap tools) OR reduce clue density

Why: Shows pacing design for building tension and progressive revelation, demonstrates how resources create pacing structure.

Optional: Appendix - "Pacing Patterns Quick Reference"

Purpose: Provide single-page overview of pacing concepts for quick reference

Length: 3-4 pages

Content Structure:

Page 1: Pacing Pattern Definitions

Attrition Pacing

  • What: Pressure builds gradually across multiple encounters/sessions
  • Created by: Slow resource recovery, accumulating consequences
  • Serves: Strategic challenge, tension, survival aesthetics
  • Examples: Old-school D&D, survival games, XCOM campaign layer
  • Feel: "Being ground down, every decision matters"

Episodic Pacing

  • What: Each encounter/scene is self-contained, fresh start
  • Created by: Fast resource recovery, consequence reset between scenes
  • Serves: Challenge (per-encounter), narrative flexibility, Fellowship
  • Examples: Modern D&D, Fate, most video game levels
  • Feel: "Each scene is new opportunity, failure doesn't compound"

Wave Pacing

  • What: Alternating intensity cycles (build → climax → recovery → repeat)
  • Created by: Structured scene types, intentional recovery periods
  • Serves: Engagement curve, emotional dynamics, most aesthetics
  • Examples: Blades in the Dark scores, story game scene structure
  • Feel: "Natural rhythm, tension rises and falls"

Continuous Pacing

  • What: Steady state without clear breaks or resets
  • Created by: Real-time pressure, seamless scene flow
  • Serves: Immersion, flow state, sustained tension
  • Examples: Real-time strategy, some narrative games
  • Feel: "In the zone" or "Can't put this down"

Punctuated Pacing

  • What: Long calm periods interrupted by sudden intense moments
  • Created by: Mostly player-controlled exploration, occasional triggered events
  • Serves: Discovery, narrative surprise, horror
  • Examples: Horror games, open-world exploration
  • Feel: "Calm then sudden terror" or "Waiting for the other shoe to drop"

Page 2: What Creates Pacing

Pacing emerges from interaction of:

Resource Recovery Rates:

  • Fast recovery → Episodic pacing
  • Slow recovery → Attrition pacing
  • Mixed rates → Layered pacing (multiple rhythms)
  • No recovery → High-stakes pacing
  • See: Resource Management chapter

Cognitive Load Patterns:

  • Sustained high → Exhaustion pacing
  • Waves (high/low) → Rhythm pacing
  • Steady low → Relaxed pacing
  • Punctuated (low with spikes) → Surprise pacing
  • See: Cognitive Load chapter

Decision Frequency:

  • Many per minute → Fast pacing
  • Few per session → Slow pacing
  • Variable density → Punctuated pacing
  • See: Principles to Numbers, Decision Density measurable

Consequence Timing:

  • Immediate consequences → Fast feedback, episodic feel
  • Delayed consequences → Building tension, attrition feel
  • Mixed timing → Complex pacing

Session Structure:

  • Single arc → Unified pacing
  • Multiple arcs → Wave pacing
  • Episodic scenes → Scene-based pacing
  • Open/emergent → Player-controlled pacing

Page 4: Measuring and Adjusting Pacing

Key Measurables (see Principles to Numbers for details):

  • Cycle length (minutes per resolution)
  • Cycles per session
  • Intensity gradient pattern
  • Recovery frequency (% of session)
  • Session arc structure

Observable Indicators:

  • Session time distribution
  • Player energy patterns (engaged, exhausted, bored)
  • Natural break points
  • Emotional arc

Common Problems and Fixes:

ProblemSymptomsLikely CauseSolutions
Too fastRushed, can't make decisionsCycles too short OR no recoveryLengthen cycles OR add recovery periods
Too slowBored, draggingCycles too long OR low intensityShorten cycles OR increase stakes
No rhythmMonotonous, fatigueConstant same intensityAdd wave pattern, include recovery
ExhaustingPlayers tired, disengagedSustained high load, no breaksAdd recovery periods, reduce cognitive load
Mismatched aestheticSystem works but feels wrongPacing conflicts with intended feelCheck aesthetic → pacing mapping above
