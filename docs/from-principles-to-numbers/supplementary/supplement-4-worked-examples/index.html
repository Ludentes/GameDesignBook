Balance Supplement 4: Extended Worked Examples

Document Purpose

This supplement provides complete, step-by-step balance design examples for different paradigms and game types. Each example shows the entire process from aesthetic selection through parameter derivation and validation.

Use these examples to:

  • See the complete framework in action
  • Understand decision-making at each step
  • Learn from worked problems similar to yours
  • Validate your own approach

Example 1: Village Mystery Investigation (Framework Balance)

Game Type: Single-session TTRPG mystery Aesthetic: Discovery (players uncover secrets, solve mystery) Paradigm: Framework Balance (GM adjudicates using Position/Effect system) Session Length: 3-4 hours Players: 3-5 investigators

Step 1: Identify Required Dynamics

Discovery aesthetic requires:

  • Information gathering (players actively seek clues)
  • Theory formation (players connect dots, form hypotheses)
  • Revelation moments ("Aha!" experiences when understanding clicks)
  • Systematic investigation (methodical exploration)

Why Framework Balance serves these dynamics:

  • Unique situations require GM flexibility
  • Players can try creative investigation approaches
  • Framework ensures consistent adjudication
  • Success/failure both provide information

Step 2: Design the Framework

Position/Effect System (adapted from Blades in the Dark):

Position Types:

  • Controlled: Safe investigation, low risk of complication
  • Risky: Possible complications (alerting suspects, drawing attention)
  • Desperate: Serious danger (confrontation, violence, exposure)

Effect Types:

  • Limited: Partial information (one clue, surface detail)
  • Standard: Good information (core clue, full scene understanding)
  • Great: Exceptional information (multiple clues, hidden connections)

Resolution:

  • Roll 2d6 + relevant stat
  • 6+: Full success
  • 4-5: Partial success or success with complication
  • 1-3: Failure with consequence

Step 3: Define Key Measurables

For Discovery + Framework Balance:

1. Framework Consistency

  • Target: Similar investigations get similar position/effect (>75% consistency)
  • Why: Players need to predict outcomes to strategize

2. Information Flow Rate

  • Target: Major revelation every 45-60 minutes
  • Target: Minor discovery every 10-15 minutes
  • Why: Maintains discovery feeling, prevents stalling

3. Clue Distribution

  • Target: Core clues guaranteed (100% discoverable)
  • Target: Supporting clues available (60-80% discoverable with effort)
  • Target: Bonus clues for creativity (30-50% discoverable)
  • Why: Progress guaranteed, thoroughness rewarded

4. Theory Formation Frequency

  • Target: Players form theories 3-5 times per session
  • Target: Red herrings: 1-2 per session (realism without frustration)
  • Why: Engagement through active deduction

5. Creative Approach Success Rate

  • Target: Novel approaches succeed 60-70% of time
  • Why: Rewards creativity, encourages engagement

Step 4: Design the Mystery

Core Mystery: Who murdered the village blacksmith?

Suspects:

  1. The Merchant (motive: debt)
  2. The Miller's Wife (motive: affair)
  3. The Priest (motive: secret heresy)
  4. The Blacksmith's Apprentice (motive: inheritance)

Actual Killer: The Miller's Wife (framed the Apprentice)

Clue Structure:

Core Clues (100% discoverable - automatic or very easy):

  1. Blacksmith died from poisoned wine (scene examination)
  2. Wine bottle from Merchant's shop (found at scene)
  3. Apprentice stands to inherit (village gossip)
  4. Blacksmith was arguing with someone night before (witnesses)
  5. Miller acting nervous and avoiding questions (observable behavior)

Supporting Clues (60-80% discoverable - require investigation): 6. Merchant's inventory shows poison purchase, but buyer name obscured 7. Letters between Blacksmith and Miller's Wife (hidden in forge) 8. Priest saw Miller's Wife near blacksmith's house that night 9. Apprentice has alibi (was at tavern, multiple witnesses) 10. Miller's Wife has key to forge (from affair)

Bonus Clues (30-50% discoverable - require creative approaches): 11. Miller's Wife knows which wine Blacksmith prefers (subtle detail) 12. Poison matches type available at Miller's home (chemistry knowledge) 13. Miller's Wife practiced Blacksmith's handwriting (hidden papers) 14. Merchant's records show poison sold weeks ago (forensic timeline)

Red Herrings: 15. Priest's heretical texts (suspicious but unrelated) 16. Merchant's debt ledger (shows motive but he didn't do it)

Step 5: Framework Application Examples

Scene 1: Examining the Crime Scene

Player Action: "I want to examine the blacksmith's body for cause of death"

GM Assessment:

  • Position: Controlled (no danger in examining body)
  • Effect: Standard (straightforward examination reveals core clue)
  • Framework: "This is a safe examination of evidence, standard effect"

Player rolls 2d6+1 (investigation skill) = 7

  • Success!
  • GM: "The blacksmith's lips are blue-tinged, and there's a bitter almond smell—classic signs of poison. The wine bottle next to him is from the Merchant's shop."
  • Core Clues #1 and #2 delivered

Scene 2: Questioning the Merchant

Player Action: "I want to look at the Merchant's sales records to see who bought poison"

GM Assessment:

  • Position: Risky (Merchant is wary, might hide evidence)
  • Effect: Standard (records would show purchase if successful)
  • Framework: "The Merchant is defensive about accusations. You'll need to convince or trick him."

Player negotiates: "What if I say I'm investigating insurance fraud, not murder?" GM reassesses:

  • Position: Controlled (clever cover story reduces suspicion)
  • Effect: Limited (he'll show records but buyer name is obscured)
  • Framework: "Smart approach. He's more cooperative, but the records aren't complete."

Player rolls 2d6+2 (persuasion) = 8

  • Success!
  • GM: "The Merchant, relieved it's 'just insurance,' shows you the records. Someone bought poison three weeks ago, but the name is smudged. You can make out '...ife' at the end."
  • Supporting Clue #6 delivered, pointing toward Miller's Wife

Scene 3: Creative Investigation

Player Action: "Can I use my chemistry knowledge to identify what kind of poison this is and where it might come from?"

GM Assessment:

  • Position: Controlled (no danger in chemical analysis)
  • Effect: Great (creative application of skill, deserves bonus)
  • Framework: "Excellent use of expertise. This could reveal a lot."

Player rolls 2d6+1 (knowledge) = 9

  • Success!
  • GM: "This is arsenic, commonly used for rat poison. The Miller keeps stores of it for vermin control, and this particular mixture matches the type he uses."
  • Bonus Clue #12 delivered, strong evidence toward solution

Step 6: Pacing the Investigation

Hour 1 (0:00-1:00): Scene Setting

  • Introduction: Village, suspects, crime scene
  • Core Clues delivered: #1, #2 (cause of death, wine bottle)
  • First theory formation: "Merchant sold poisoned wine?"

Hour 2 (1:00-2:00): Initial Investigation

  • Question suspects, gather alibis
  • Supporting Clues: #8, #9 (Priest saw Miller's Wife, Apprentice has alibi)
  • Theory revision: "Maybe it's not the Apprentice?"

Hour 3 (2:00-3:00): Deep Investigation

  • Hidden evidence found
  • Supporting Clues: #7, #10 (letters reveal affair, Wife has forge key)
  • Major revelation: "Miller's Wife had motive AND access!"

Hour 4 (3:00-4:00): Resolution

  • Confrontation or final evidence gathering
  • Bonus Clues if creative: #11, #12, #13, #14
  • Solution: Miller's Wife killed Blacksmith, tried to frame Apprentice
  • Denouement: Justice, consequences, satisfaction

Step 7: Measurable Validation

Framework Consistency Check:

Similar Investigation Actions:

Action 1: "I search the Blacksmith's forge"

  • Position: Controlled (no danger)
  • Effect: Standard (find hidden letters)

Action 2: "I search the Miller's house"

  • Position: Risky (Miller's Wife might catch you)
  • Effect: Standard (find evidence if successful)

Consistency: Both are searches, but context differs (forge is abandoned, house is occupied). Position varies appropriately, Effect stays standard. ✓

Action 3: "I break into the Merchant's shop at night"

  • Position: Desperate (illegal, might get caught)
  • Effect: Great (access to everything)

Consistency: Higher risk = higher reward. Consistent with framework. ✓

Information Flow Validation:

Actual Session:

  • 0:15 - Core Clues #1, #2 (crime scene) ✓
  • 0:30 - Core Clue #3 (inheritance gossip) ✓
  • 0:50 - Supporting Clue #8 (Priest testimony) - Major revelation! ✓
  • 1:15 - Supporting Clue #9 (Apprentice alibi) ✓
  • 1:45 - Supporting Clue #7 (affair letters) - Major revelation! ✓
  • 2:20 - Supporting Clue #10 (forge key) ✓
  • 2:50 - Bonus Clue #12 (poison match) - Major revelation! ✓

Target: Major revelation every 45-60 minutes Actual: 0:50, 1:45, 2:50 - roughly every hour ✓

Target: Minor discovery every 10-15 minutes Actual: Clues every 15-30 minutes ✓ (slightly slower but acceptable)

Clue Distribution Validation:

Core Clues (target 100%): All 5 discovered ✓ Supporting Clues (target 60-80%): 5 of 6 discovered = 83% ✓ Bonus Clues (target 30-50%): 2 of 4 discovered = 50% ✓

Creative Approach Success:

  • 4 novel approaches attempted
  • 3 succeeded, 1 failed (75% success rate)
  • Target was 60-70%, actual 75% ✓ (slightly high but acceptable)

Step 8: Player Feedback

Post-Session Survey (1-5 scale):

"Did outcomes feel fair?"

  • Average: 4.6/5 ✓
  • Comments: "Even when we failed rolls, we got useful information"

"Could you predict consequences of actions?"

  • Average: 4.2/5 ✓
  • Comments: "Once we understood Position/Effect, it made sense"

"Did you feel you had agency?"

  • Average: 4.8/5 ✓
  • Comments: "Loved that creative approaches worked"

"Was the mystery satisfying to solve?"

  • Average: 4.5/5 ✓
  • Comments: "Felt like real detective work"

Step 9: Iteration Notes

What worked:

  • Framework provided consistent adjudication
  • Players quickly learned to predict position/effect
  • Negotiation created engagement (players proposed creative approaches)
  • Clue distribution ensured progress while rewarding thoroughness

What to adjust for next time:

  • Minor discoveries came slightly slower than target (15-30 min actual vs 10-15 min target)
    • Fix: Add more easily-discoverable ambient clues
  • One player struggled with framework initially
    • Fix: Provide written framework reference sheet

Changes for next mystery:

  • ✓ Keep framework as-is (works well)
  • ✓ Add 2-3 more minor clues for pacing
  • ✓ Create framework cheat sheet for players

Example 2: Boss Rush Campaign (Consistency Balance)

Game Type: Action video game, 10 boss fights Aesthetic: Challenge (players overcome difficulty through pattern mastery) Paradigm: Consistency Balance (fixed patterns, skill-based victory) Campaign Length: 8-12 hours Target Audience: Midcore (willing to die 8-15 times per boss)

Step 1: Identify Required Dynamics

Challenge aesthetic requires:

  • Pattern learning (observe → understand → execute)
  • Skill expression (execution quality matters)
  • Failure recovery (death → learn → retry)
  • Mastery demonstration (clear novice-to-expert progression)

Why Consistency Balance serves these dynamics:

  • Fixed patterns enable learning through repetition
  • Consistent rules reward skill development
  • Fast retry enables iteration
  • Performance improvement measurable

Step 2: Define Core Measurables

For Challenge + Consistency Balance:

1. Time-to-Kill (TTK) Consistency

  • Target: 2-4 minutes per successful attempt
  • Variance: ±15% maximum across attempts
  • Why: Learnable if consistent, frustrating if random

2. Attempts to First Victory

  • Target: 8-15 attempts for midcore audience
  • Range: 5-10 (skilled), 10-20 (casual), 20-30 (hard mode)
  • Why: Sufficient struggle for satisfaction

3. Learning Curve Slope

  • Target: TTK improvement of 20-30% from attempt 1 to 10
  • Target: Error rate reduction of 40-60%
  • Why: Clear progression indicates learning is working

4. Failure Recovery Time

  • Target: Death to retry in <5 seconds
  • Target: Checkpoint to boss in <10 seconds
  • Why: Fast iteration enables learning without frustration

5. Pattern Readability

  • Target: Players predict attacks correctly >70% by attempt 5
  • Target: Can explain death cause >85% of time
  • Why: Can't learn unclear patterns

6. Skill Ceiling

  • Target: Expert clears 2-3× faster than first-time success
  • Target: No-hit runs possible (mastery demonstration)
  • Why: Mastery aesthetic requires optimization space

Step 3: Design Boss 1 (Tutorial Boss)

Purpose: Teach core patterns, build confidence

Boss: The Stone Guardian

Attack Patterns:

Pattern A: Overhead Slam (Primary attack, 40% usage)

  • Telegraph: 1.5 seconds (boss raises hammer high)
  • Damage: 20 HP (player has 100 HP, so 5 hits to death)
  • Recovery: 2 seconds (vulnerable window)
  • Counter: Dodge left/right, attack during recovery

Pattern B: Horizontal Sweep (Secondary attack, 30% usage)

  • Telegraph: 1.2 seconds (boss winds up sideways)
  • Damage: 15 HP
  • Recovery: 1.5 seconds
  • Counter: Jump or roll backward

Pattern C: Ground Pound (AOE attack, 20% usage)

  • Telegraph: 2.0 seconds (boss kneels, fists to ground)
  • Damage: 25 HP (hits entire arena)
  • Recovery: 3 seconds (major opening)
  • Counter: Jump during shockwave, attack aggressively during recovery

Pattern D: Charge (Rare attack, 10% usage)

  • Telegraph: 1.0 seconds (boss lowers head)
  • Damage: 30 HP
  • Recovery: 4 seconds (crashes into wall)
  • Counter: Dodge sideways at last second, huge punish window

Attack Frequency:

  • 4-second cycle (2s attack + 2s neutral)
  • 60 attacks per 4-minute fight

Pattern Sequencing:

  • Patterns chosen by weighted probability (40/30/20/10)
  • No true randomness: uses seeded pseudo-random for consistency
  • Same seed = same fight (speedrun friendly)

  • Attack speed: 1 attack per second
  • Movement: Normal run speed, dodge roll with i-frames

Example 3: Co-op Survival Mode (Adaptive Balance)

Game Type: 4-player co-op video game, endless survival waves Aesthetic: Tension (sustained pressure without overwhelming) Paradigm: Adaptive Balance (system adjusts to maintain target experience) Session Length: 20-40 minute runs Target Audience: Mixed skill (casual to midcore, playing together)

Step 1: Identify Required Dynamics

Tension aesthetic requires:

  • Resource pressure (scarcity creates decisions)
  • Close calls (near-death moments create adrenaline)
  • Threat awareness (constant vigilance required)
  • Recovery opportunities (pressure relief prevents burnout)

Why Adaptive Balance serves these dynamics:

  • Adjusts for skill variance (keeps all players engaged)
  • Maintains target resource levels automatically
  • Creates tension peaks and valleys dynamically
  • Invisible adaptation prevents "pity" feeling

Step 2: Define Core Measurables

For Tension + Adaptive Balance:

1. Resource Levels (Primary)

  • Target: Team health averages 35-55%
  • Target: Ammunition 30-50% capacity
  • Target: Healing items 1-2 per player
  • Why: Scarcity creates pressure without desperation

2. Close Call Frequency

  • Target: Individual <20% health 2-4 times per 10 minutes
  • Target: Team near-wipe (1 player alive) 0-1 per session (rare, memorable)
  • Why: Tension peaks create emotional engagement

3. Threat Density

  • Target: Combat encounter every 2-3 minutes
  • Target: Safe exploration 60-90 seconds between encounters
  • Why: Pressure maintained, not constant

4. Death Consequence

  • Target: Individual death = 30-60s respawn timer
  • Target: Team wipe = run ends (stakes matter)
  • Why: Deaths serious but recoverable

5. Adaptation Invisibility

  • Target: <20% of players notice explicit difficulty adjustment
  • Why: Should feel natural, not patronizing

6. Skill-Adjusted Experience

  • Target: All skill levels report similar challenge intensity (±1 on 5-point scale)
  • Why: Mixed teams stay together, no one bored/frustrated

Step 3: Design Adaptation System

What the System Tracks (every 30 seconds):

Team Performance Metrics:

  • Average health % across living players
  • Average ammunition % across living players
  • Healing items in inventory (count)
  • Deaths in last 5 minutes (count)
  • Damage taken rate (HP lost per minute)
  • Damage dealt rate (enemy HP killed per minute)
  • Current wave number (progression)

Adaptive Levers (what system can adjust):

Lever 1: Enemy Spawn Rate

  • Baseline: 8 enemies per minute
  • Range: 4-12 enemies per minute (±50% from baseline)
  • Adjustment speed: ±1 enemy per 2 minutes

Lever 2: Enemy Health

  • Baseline: 100 HP per enemy
  • Range: 80-120 HP (±20% from baseline)
  • Adjustment speed: ±5 HP per wave

Lever 3: Enemy Damage

  • Baseline: 15 HP per hit
  • Range: 12-18 HP (±20% from baseline)
  • Adjustment speed: ±1 HP per wave

Lever 4: Item Drop Rate

  • Baseline: 30% health drop, 20% ammo drop per enemy
  • Range: 20-60% health, 15-40% ammo
  • Adjustment speed: ±5% per wave

Lever 5: Special Enemy Frequency

  • Baseline: 1 special per 3 waves
  • Range: 1 per 2 waves (harder) to 1 per 5 waves (easier)
  • Adjustment speed: One special per adjustment

Step 8: Adaptation Issues Identified

Problem 1: Mixed Skill Teams Create Variance

  • System tracks team average
  • But casual player dying repeatedly while hardcore untouched
  • Team average health looks okay, hiding individual struggles

Solution: Individual Performance Tracking

Problem 2: Adaptation Too Reactive

  • Waits until multiple deaths before adjusting
  • Casual player frustrated before system helps

Solution: Predictive Adjustment

Problem 3: Hardcore Player Bored

  • System makes things easier for team
  • Hardcore player not challenged

Solution: Individual Difficulty
