Part 2: Balance Paradigms

Balance isn't one thing. There are fundamentally different approaches to creating balanced play experiences, each serving different aesthetics and requiring different methods.

We call these balance paradigms—the underlying philosophy of how balance works in your game.

The three working paradigms:

  1. Framework-Based Balance: Explicit decision frameworks create consistent adjudication
  2. Consistency-Based Balance: Static, repeatable challenges enable pattern learning
  3. Adaptive Balance: Dynamic systems adjust to player performance

Plus: Hybrid approaches combining paradigms for different subsystems.

Understanding paradigms helps you:

  • Choose the right approach for your aesthetic
  • Communicate design intent clearly
  • Know what to build and how to validate it
  • Avoid mixing incompatible approaches

Let's examine each paradigm in detail.

Framework-Based Balance

What It Is

Framework-based balance uses explicit decision frameworks that create consistent adjudication of unpredictable situations.

Instead of pre-determining outcomes or calculating probabilities for every possible action, you define a framework for evaluating actions in the moment. The GM or system uses this framework to determine stakes, risks, and outcomes consistently.

Core mechanism: Players propose actions → Framework evaluates situation → Stakes negotiated/determined → Resolution occurs

Key example: Blades in the Dark's Position and Effect system

  • Position (risk level): Controlled, Risky, or Desperate
  • Effect (potential outcome): Limited, Standard, or Great
  • GM uses framework to assign Position and Effect for any action
  • Players can see stakes before committing
  • Same framework applies to all situations

When to Use Framework Paradigm

Best for these aesthetics:

  • ★★★ Narrative: Players need agency over story direction, transparent stakes enable meaningful choices
  • ★★★ Discovery: Framework handles novel situations players invent
  • ★★ Fellowship: Shared understanding of framework enables group decision-making
  • ★ Expression: Framework accommodates creative player approaches

Design context requirements:

  • Human GM or sophisticated AI (someone/something can apply framework)
  • Players comfortable with negotiation/discussion
  • Moderate session length (time for framework application)
  • Not time-pressured (real-time combat rarely uses framework)

When NOT to use:

  • Pure Challenge aesthetic (pattern mastery requires consistency, not negotiation)
  • Time-critical gameplay (no time for stake negotiation)
  • Solo digital games without AI capable of framework application
  • Players who prefer deterministic outcomes

How Framework Balance Works

The framework provides:

1. Risk Assessment Criteria How dangerous is this action given the situation?

Example (Blades in the Dark Position):

  • Controlled: You're in a good position, danger limited
  • Risky: Standard danger level, things could go wrong
  • Desperate: You're in serious danger, major consequences likely

What determines Position: Enemy awareness, your preparation, your resources, the fiction

2. Outcome Potential Criteria How much can you accomplish with this action?

Example (Blades in the Dark Effect):

  • Limited: Partial progress toward goal
  • Standard: Achieve your immediate goal
  • Great: Achieve goal plus extra benefit

What determines Effect: Your approach, relevant abilities, situation factors, scale mismatch

3. Consequence Structure What happens when things go wrong?

Example (Blades in the Dark):

  • Reduced effect (accomplish less)
  • Complication (new problem arises)
  • Lost opportunity (can't try this approach again)
  • Harm (damage to character)
  • Worse position (next action more dangerous)

4. Resolution Mechanism How do you determine outcome within framework?

Example (Blades in the Dark):

  • Roll dice pool, count successes
  • 6: Success at stated Effect
  • 4-5: Success at reduced Effect OR success with consequence
  • 1-3: Failure, suffer consequence at stated Position

Setting Parameters in Framework Systems

You're not setting exact values—you're defining framework tiers and ranges.

Step 1: Define your framework dimensions

  • Risk levels (typically 3-5 tiers)
  • Outcome levels (typically 3-5 tiers)
  • Consequence types (typically 4-8 types)

Step 2: Set ranges for each tier

Example: Harm consequences in Blades

  • Controlled Position: 0-1 harm
  • Risky Position: 1-2 harm
  • Desperate Position: 2-4 harm

Example: Progress toward goal

  • Limited Effect: 1-2 segments on 6-segment clock
  • Standard Effect: 2-3 segments
  • Great Effect: 3-5 segments

Step 3: Create framework application guidance

Not exact formulas, but principles:

  • "Position determined by: How prepared are you? How alert is opposition? How exposed are you?"
  • "Effect determined by: How suited is your approach? Do you have relevant abilities? Is there scale mismatch?"

Step 4: Test framework consistency

Can GM/players apply framework to same situation consistently?

  • Multiple GMs presented same scenario should give similar Position/Effect
  • Same GM should give consistent rulings for similar situations
  • Players should understand framework well enough to predict stakes

Number-Setting Example: Investigation Framework

Goal: Players investigating mystery, need framework for information gathering

Framework dimensions:

  • Risk: How dangerous is this investigation approach?

    • Low: Safe, no alert raised, no time pressure
    • Medium: Some danger, might alert opposition or consume time
    • High: Dangerous, likely to alert opposition or create complications

  • Information Gain: How much can you learn?

    • Minor: Hint or small clue (1-2 preparation points)
    • Major: Significant clue or tactical advantage (3-4 preparation points)
    • Critical: Key revelation or major advantage (5+ preparation points)

Resolution: 2d6 + relevant modifier

  • 10+: Gain information at stated level, no downside
  • 7-9: Gain information at stated level BUT pay cost (time, exposure, complication)
  • 6-: Don't gain information, suffer consequence based on Risk level

Parameters derived:

  • Not "this specific action succeeds/fails"
  • But "this class of actions has these stakes"
  • GM applies framework in moment based on fiction

Advantages of Framework Paradigm

Handles unpredictability: Players will try things you didn't anticipate. Framework evaluates new situations consistently without pre-authoring every possibility.

Player agency: Transparent stakes ("This is Risky position, Standard effect") let players make informed choices. They can adjust approach to change stakes.

Narrative flexibility: Same mechanics generate different fictional outcomes. "I convince the guard" might be Risky or Controlled depending on your preparation, creating different stories.

Scales with player creativity: More creative approaches might improve Position or Effect within framework. Rewards lateral thinking.

Challenges of Framework Paradigm

Requires judgment: GM/system must evaluate situations on the fly. Less formulaic than Consistency paradigm.

Learning curve: Players need to internalize framework to predict stakes. Initial sessions slower as players learn.

Potential inconsistency: Different GMs might apply framework differently. Requires calibration and examples.

Not suitable for pure Challenge: Framework negotiation conflicts with Challenge aesthetic's desire for consistent patterns to master.

Framework Paradigm Checklist

Use Framework paradigm if:

  • ✓ Your aesthetic prioritizes narrative agency or discovery
  • ✓ You have human GM or sophisticated AI
  • ✓ Players can negotiate stakes before committing
  • ✓ Situations too varied to pre-author all outcomes
  • ✓ You can provide framework training/examples

Don't use Framework paradigm if:

  • ✗ Pure Challenge aesthetic (needs consistency)
  • ✗ Real-time pressure (no time for negotiation)
  • ✗ Automated system without AI (can't evaluate situations)
  • ✗ Players prefer deterministic outcomes

Consistency-Based Balance

What It Is

Consistency-based balance uses static, repeatable challenges that enable pattern learning and skill mastery.

The same inputs always produce the same outputs. Enemies have fixed stats, behave predictably, and occupy consistent positions. Players learn patterns through experience and get better at executing optimal responses.

Core mechanism: Challenge presented → Player responds → Outcome determined by execution → Same challenge next time behaves identically

Key example: Dark Souls

  • Goblin always has 8 HP, always does 2 damage, always attacks with same pattern
  • Boss always occupies same arena, uses same moveset, has same HP/damage
  • Player learns through repetition: dodge timing, attack windows, positioning
  • Victory comes from player skill improvement, not character power increase

When to Use Consistency Paradigm

Best for these aesthetics:

  • ★★★ Challenge: Pattern mastery is the core appeal, requires consistency
  • ★★★ Mastery: Deep system understanding requires stable patterns to study
  • ★★ Tension: High stakes feel fair when patterns are knowable
  • ★ Narrative: Can serve authored story beats with fixed dramatic moments

Design context requirements:

  • Can pre-author content (know what challenges exist)
  • Players willing to retry and learn patterns
  • Moderate to long play time (enough time to encounter patterns multiple times)
  • Digital or tabletop with good prep

When NOT to use:

  • Discovery aesthetic (predictability kills surprise)
  • Submission aesthetic (pattern learning is work, not relaxation)
  • Single-session one-shot (no time to learn patterns through repetition)
  • When you want dynamic pacing (consistency means fixed difficulty)

How Consistency Balance Works

The system provides:

1. Fixed Challenge Parameters Every challenge has exact, unchanging stats.

Example (tactical combat):

  • Goblin: 8 HP, AC 14, +4 to hit, 1d6+2 damage (5 avg)
  • Orc: 15 HP, AC 13, +5 to hit, 1d8+3 damage (7.5 avg)
  • Dragon: 200 HP, AC 18, +10 to hit, 2d10+6 damage (17 avg)

These never change. Same goblin in area 1 = same stats as goblin in area 2.

2. Predictable Behavior Patterns Enemies/challenges behave according to consistent rules.

Example (Dark Souls boss):

  • Attack pattern: Slash → Slash → Pause → Overhead smash → Pause
  • Pattern always same at this HP range
  • Telegraphs always same (wind-up animation)
  • Dodge windows always same timing
  • Vulnerabilities always same (exposed during overhead recovery)

3. Deterministic Outcomes Same player action in same situation = same result (or known probability).

Example:

  • Attack with +6 modifier against AC 14 = 65% hit chance every time
  • Rolling a 15 = hit, rolling a 7 = miss, same every encounter
  • Boss charge attack hits for 20 damage whether it's try #1 or try #100

4. Learnable Mastery Curve Players improve by:

  • Recognizing patterns faster
  • Executing optimal responses more reliably
  • Understanding underlying systems better
  • Minimizing mistakes through practice

Setting Parameters in Consistency Systems

You're setting exact, fixed values that enable pattern learning.

Step 1: Define power baseline What's your reference point?

Example (D&D-style):

  • "Level 3 player": ~20 HP, +5 to hit, ~9 damage per hit
  • Design all challenges relative to this baseline

Step 2: Calculate expected engagements

How long should this challenge take?

Example (boss fight):

  • Target: 20-25 player turns (4 players × 5-6 rounds each)
  • Player damage per round (4 players × 9 damage × 65% hit) = ~23 DPR
  • Boss HP = 23 DPR × 23 rounds = 529 HP → round to 540 HP

Step 3: Set threat level

How dangerous should this challenge be?

Example (same boss):

  • Want: Boss can incapacitate player in 3-4 hits (high threat but not instant death)
  • Player HP: 20
  • Boss damage per attack: 20 ÷ 3.5 = ~6 damage
  • Set: Boss deals 2d6+1 damage (8 average, 3-13 range)

Step 4: Design patterns

What behaviors can players learn?

Example (same boss):

  • Pattern 1: Slash → Slash → Overhead (predictable rhythm)
  • Pattern 2: Roar telegraph → Charge attack (avoidable if you dodge on roar)
  • Pattern 3: At 50% HP, gains additional attack (learnable threshold)
  • Pattern 4: Regenerates if in moonlight (environmental interaction)

Step 5: Test repeatability

Does the pattern work every time?

  • Same boss, same player approach = same outcome?
  • Can players learn and execute optimal strategy?
  • Does mastery curve exist (first attempt hard, tenth attempt manageable)?

Number-Setting Example: Tactical Combat Encounter

Goal: Challenge encounter for 4 "level 3" players

Player baseline:

  • 20 HP each (80 HP total)
  • +5 to hit
  • 9 damage per hit
  • ~65% hit rate against AC 14

Enemy design:

  • Count: 6 enemies (action economy balance—more enemies than players)
  • HP per enemy: 15 HP (die in 2 player hits on average)
  • AC: 13 (70% player hit rate—slightly easier than baseline)
  • To-hit: +4 (need 11+ to hit player, 50% hit rate)
  • Damage: 1d6+1 (5 average)

Expected outcome:

  • Players kill 2 enemies per round (8 attacks × 70% × 9 damage ÷ 15 HP ≈ 3.36 enemies)
  • Combat lasts 2-3 rounds (6 enemies ÷ 2.5 per round)
  • Players take 15 damage per round (6 enemies × 50% × 5 damage)
  • Total player damage taken: 30-45 (out of 80 HP total—survivable but threatening)

Pattern to learn:

  • Focus fire (kill enemies quickly to reduce incoming damage)
  • Positioning (use terrain to limit enemy actions)
  • Action economy (control/disable enemies more efficient than trading damage)

Validation:

  • Run encounter multiple times
  • Does it consistently take 2-4 rounds?
  • Do players consistently survive if playing well?
  • Do players lose if playing poorly (not focusing fire)?

Advantages of Consistency Paradigm

Skill expression: Player improvement directly visible. "I beat this boss that killed me 20 times" feels earned.

Fair challenge: Same inputs = same outputs means no "the game cheated" feeling. Failures feel like player error.

Deep mastery: Consistent patterns reward study. Speedrunners can optimize to seconds because patterns are knowable.

Predictable pacing: Designer knows how long encounters take, can structure experience deliberately.

Challenges of Consistency Paradigm

Limited variety: Same encounter feels same each time. Replay value depends on player enjoying pattern mastery, not novelty.

Requires content creation: Can't dynamically generate challenges. Must author each encounter deliberately.

Can't adapt to player skill: Struggling player faces same difficulty as skilled player. No help if someone's stuck.

Discovery conflicts: Predictable patterns conflict with surprise. Once learned, mystery gone.

Consistency Paradigm Checklist

Use Consistency paradigm if:

  • ✓ Challenge or Mastery aesthetic primary
  • ✓ Players willing to retry and learn patterns
  • ✓ You can pre-author content
  • ✓ Time budget allows pattern learning (not one-shot)
  • ✓ Player skill progression is core appeal

Don't use Consistency paradigm if:

  • ✗ Discovery aesthetic (patterns kill surprise)
  • ✗ Single-session with no retry opportunity
  • ✗ Need to accommodate wide skill ranges dynamically
  • ✗ Want each playthrough to feel different

Adaptive Balance

What It Is

Adaptive balance uses dynamic systems that adjust to player performance, maintaining target experience through responsive difficulty.

Instead of fixed challenges or negotiated stakes, the system monitors player state and adjusts parameters in real-time to create desired pacing, support struggling players, or challenge successful players.

Core mechanism: System monitors player performance → Compares to target experience → Adjusts parameters (spawns, items, difficulty) → Maintains desired state

Key example: Left 4 Dead 2's AI Director

  • Monitors player health, ammo, progress rate, deaths
  • Adjusts zombie spawn rate, special infected frequency, item placement
  • Creates dramatic pacing: build-up → peak intensity → relief → repeat
  • Same map plays differently each time based on team performance

When to Use Adaptive Paradigm

Best for these aesthetics:

  • ★★★ Fellowship: Balance skill gaps, keep all players engaged
  • ★★★ Discovery: Create variety, maintain novelty across playthroughs
  • ★★ Tension: Maintain dramatic pacing through adaptive intensity
  • ★ Progression: Adjust challenge to growing player power
  • ★ Challenge: ONLY if bounded carefully (unbounded adaptation kills mastery)

Design context requirements:

  • Computer-managed system (real-time monitoring/adjustment)
  • OR experienced GM willing to track state and adapt
  • Enough playtime for adaptation to matter (not 5-minute game)
  • Player acceptance of non-deterministic difficulty

When NOT to use:

  • Pure Challenge aesthetic (adaptation prevents pattern mastery)
  • Competitive games (fairness requires consistent challenge)
  • Players who want "I beat the intended challenge" achievement
  • Limited technical capability (complex state tracking)

How Adaptive Balance Works

The system provides:

1. State Monitoring What does system track about players?

Example (L4D2 AI Director):

  • Individual player: HP, ammo, location, recent damage taken
  • Team aggregate: Average HP, items available, progress rate
  • Recent history: Deaths in last 5 minutes, time since last horde
  • Intensity level: Current stress on team (0-100 scale)

3. Bounds and Constraints Adaptation isn't unlimited—define ranges.

Example (L4D2):

  • Spawn rate range: 1-10 zombies per 30 seconds (not 0, not 100)
  • Item spawn range: 20-80% (not guaranteed, not impossible)
  • Intensity cycles: Must include relief phases (can't sustain peak)

4. Pacing Structure How does adaptation create experience flow?

Example (L4D2 Director phases):

  • Build-Up: Gradually increase spawn rate, build tension
  • Peak: Maximum intensity, overwhelming numbers
  • Relax: Reduce/stop spawns, allow recovery (30-45 seconds)
  • Repeat cycle with varying intensity and duration

Setting Parameters in Adaptive Systems

You're defining bounds, not fixed values, plus adaptation rules.

Step 1: Define baseline challenge What's the "default" difficulty?

Example (cooperative horde game):

  • Baseline spawn rate: 5 zombies per 30 seconds
  • Baseline item spawn: 40% chance at item points
  • Baseline special infected: Every 30 seconds

Step 2: Set adaptation ranges How much can difficulty vary?

Example:

  • Spawn rate range: 1-10 zombies per 30 seconds (10x variation)
  • Item spawn range: 20-80% (4x variation)
  • Special infected timing: 20-60 seconds (3x variation)

Step 4: Set pacing parameters How do intensity cycles work?

Example:

  • Build-up phase: 60-90 seconds (ramp up to peak)
  • Peak phase: 15-30 seconds (maximum intensity)
  • Relax phase: 30-45 seconds (minimal spawns)
  • Total cycle: ~2-3 minutes

Step 5: Test adaptation subtlety Can players notice adaptation? Should they?

Example questions:

  • Does difficulty adjustment feel manipulative? ("The game is helping me, so my victory doesn't count")
  • Can players exploit adaptation? (Intentionally trigger "help" states)
  • Does adaptation maintain engagement? (Neither too easy nor too hard)

Number-Setting Example: Roguelike Encounter Scaling

Goal: Adapt encounter difficulty to player's current run strength

State tracking:

  • Player power: HP, damage output, special abilities acquired
  • Run progress: Which floor (1-10), time taken so far
  • Recent performance: Damage taken last 3 encounters, deaths

Baseline encounter (Floor 5):

  • 4 enemies, 20 HP each, 6 damage per hit
  • Expected: Player takes 15-20 damage, kills enemies in 8-10 attacks

Adaptation rules:

Bounds:

  • Enemy count range: 2-6 (never 1, never 10+)
  • HP multiplier range: 0.7x-1.5x (within ±50%)
  • Must provide winning chance (not impossible scaling)

Validation:

  • Do runs feel appropriately challenging regardless of power level?
  • Can skilled players still feel powerful (don't over-adapt)?
  • Do struggling players get enough help without it feeling patronizing?

Advantages of Adaptive Paradigm

Accommodates skill variance: Different skill levels get appropriate challenge. Struggling player gets help, expert gets challenge.

Maintains engagement: Prevents both "too easy" boredom and "too hard" frustration through active adjustment.

Replay variety: Same content feels different based on performance, creating variety without new content.

Dramatic pacing: Can create movie-like intensity curves (build tension → climax → relief) dynamically.

Challenges of Adaptive Paradigm

Can feel manipulative: Players who notice adaptation may feel "the game is patronizing me" or "my success doesn't count."

Prevents pure mastery: Challenge seekers want to overcome fixed obstacle, not shifting target. Adaptation conflicts with mastery satisfaction.

Technical complexity: Real-time state tracking and parameter adjustment requires development resources.

Hard to tune: Finding right adaptation bounds and triggers requires extensive playtesting.

Exploitable: Savvy players can game adaptation (intentionally trigger "help" states).

Adaptive Paradigm Checklist

Use Adaptive paradigm if:

  • ✓ Fellowship or Discovery aesthetic primary
  • ✓ Wide skill range in player population
  • ✓ Technical capability for state tracking
  • ✓ Dramatic pacing more important than consistent challenge
  • ✓ Replay variety valued

Don't use Adaptive paradigm if:

  • ✗ Pure Challenge aesthetic (mastery requires consistency)
  • ✗ Competitive fairness critical
  • ✗ Limited development resources
  • ✗ Players expect "I beat the designer's intended challenge"
  • ✗ Short play sessions (not enough time to adapt)

Hybrid Approaches

Most games don't use one paradigm exclusively. You can combine paradigms for different subsystems.

Common Hybrid Patterns

1. Consistency + Framework

  • Core mechanics: Consistent (HP, damage, movement rules)
  • Situational resolution: Framework (stakes negotiation)
  • Example: Blades in the Dark has consistent Position/Effect framework, but specific outcomes negotiated
  • Why it works: Framework handles unpredictable player creativity, consistency provides mechanical foundation

2. Consistency + Bounded Adaptive

  • Core challenge: Consistent (boss patterns, enemy stats)
  • Support systems: Adaptive (co-op help, optional advantages)
  • Example: Dark Souls allows summoning help (adaptive difficulty), but boss patterns stay consistent
  • Why it works: Preserves mastery loop while accommodating different skill levels

3. Framework + Adaptive

  • Conflict resolution: Framework (consistent negotiation)
  • Pacing/spawning: Adaptive (intensity management)
  • Example: Could combine Blades-style framework with L4D2-style dynamic encounter generation
  • Why it works: Framework handles player choices, adaptation creates variety

4. Different Paradigms for Different Phases

  • Exploration: Framework (handle unpredictable player actions)
  • Combat: Consistency (pattern learning)
  • Example: Our Village Mystery + Boss Fight scenario from Part 1
  • Why it works: Each phase uses paradigm suited to its aesthetic goals

When to Use Hybrid

Good reasons:

  • Different subsystems serve different aesthetics (exploration = discovery, combat = challenge)
  • Want advantages of multiple paradigms without downsides
  • Core system needs consistency but edges benefit from flexibility
  • Accommodate wide player skill range while preserving mastery

Bad reasons:

  • "More paradigms = more better" (complexity without purpose)
  • Trying to please everyone (dilutes core experience)
  • Can't decide which paradigm, so use all (incoherent design)

Hybrid Design Principles

1. Clarity of boundaries Players should know which paradigm applies when.

Example:

  • "Investigation phase uses framework (negotiate stakes)"
  • "Boss fight uses consistency (fixed patterns to learn)"
  • Clear transition between phases

2. Paradigms don't conflict Combining paradigms that fight each other creates confusion.

Bad combination:

  • Adaptive difficulty during pattern-learning boss fight
  • Boss patterns change based on player performance
  • Result: Can't learn patterns, adaptation destroys Challenge aesthetic

Good combination:

  • Consistent boss patterns (learnable)
  • Adaptive loot drops after boss (help if struggling)
  • Result: Mastery preserved, but support available

3. Complexity budget respected Each paradigm adds cognitive load. Don't overload players.

Example:

  • Framework (learn negotiation rules) + Consistency (learn patterns) = manageable
  • Framework + Consistency + Adaptive (also tracking adaptation states) = potentially overwhelming
  • Consider whether benefits justify complexity

Choosing Your Paradigm

Decision Framework

Step 1: Assess Design Context What's technically feasible for you?

  • Computer-managed → Framework, Consistency, or Adaptive possible
  • Human GM → Framework or Consistency feasible, Adaptive harder
  • Pure tabletop without GM → Consistency most feasible

Step 2: Identify Primary Aesthetic What experience are you creating?

  • Challenge/Mastery → Consistency paradigm
  • Narrative/Discovery → Framework paradigm
  • Fellowship (wide skill range) → Adaptive paradigm
  • Multiple aesthetics → Consider hybrid

Step 3: Check Aesthetic-Paradigm Fit

Use this quick reference:

Legend:

  • ★★★ = Ideal fit
  • ★★ = Good fit
  • ★ = Compatible
  • ○ = Workable but not ideal
  • △ = Risky, requires care
  • ✗ = Conflicts

Step 4: Consider Practical Factors

  • Time budget: Do players have time to learn patterns (Consistency) or negotiate stakes (Framework)?
  • Development resources: Can you implement state tracking (Adaptive)?
  • Player expectations: What paradigm will players expect given your genre/platform?

Step 5: Choose and Commit Pick paradigm(s) that:

  • Are feasible given your context
  • Serve your primary aesthetic(s)
  • Match your resources and expertise

Then build everything else around that choice.

Example: Village Mystery + Boss Fight

Context: Tabletop, one GM, 4 players, single 5-hour session

Aesthetics:

  • Investigation: Narrative (player-driven story) + Discovery (uncover secrets)
  • Boss fight: Challenge (tactical victory through preparation)

Paradigm choices:

  • Investigation: Framework (handle unpredictable player approaches, transparent stakes)
  • Boss fight: Consistency (enable tactical planning, pattern learning through investigation)
  • Integration: Investigation reveals boss patterns that would normally require repetition to learn

Why this works:

  • Framework handles open-ended investigation (can't pre-author all possibilities)
  • Consistency enables Challenge aesthetic in boss fight (patterns to master)
  • Hybrid doesn't conflict (clear phase boundary, investigation informs consistency)

What we build:

  • Investigation: Risk/Information framework, 2d6 resolution
  • Boss fight: Fixed stats, consistent patterns, d20+AC resolution
  • Integration: Tactical Unlocks + Preparation Pool bridge the two
