Chapter 5: Balance - From Principles to Numbers

You've chosen your aesthetic. You've selected your archetype. You've designed your resource economy and managed your cognitive load budget. Now you face the practical question every designer eventually hits:

"How much HP should the boss have? Does my fireball do 3 damage or 30? What makes an encounter challenging but not impossible?"

This is the gap that breaks many designs. You understand what experience you want to create (Challenge aesthetic, tactical combat archetype). You even know how you want to create it (consistency-based pattern learning). But you're staring at a spreadsheet asking: "What actual numbers do I put here?"

The previous chapters told you WHAT and WHY. This chapter tells you HOW—not through magic formulas, but through systematic decision-making that connects your high-level goals to specific parameters.

What Is Balance?

Balance is not mathematical perfection. It's not about making all options equally powerful or ensuring 50/50 win rates. Balance is creating the experience your aesthetic requires.

Dark Souls (2011) is "balanced" when boss fights feel brutally difficult but learnable—when players die repeatedly but feel they're making progress through pattern recognition. If every boss died in three hits, the game would be "unbalanced" even though combat math might be internally consistent.

Animal Crossing: New Horizons (2020) is "balanced" when activities feel pleasantly achievable—when players can make meaningful progress in 20-minute sessions without stress. If rare fish required 40 hours of grinding, the game would be "unbalanced" even though probability math might be mathematically sound.

Same word, completely different requirements. Balance serves your aesthetic, not abstract mathematical ideals.

The Tools Landscape

Professional game balance uses many approaches:

Playtesting - Observing real players, gathering feedback, iterating on problems Telemetry - Collecting gameplay data at scale (win rates, completion times, player paths) Simulation - Running thousands of virtual games to find dominant strategies Expert Panels - High-skill players finding edge cases and optimal strategies
Mathematical Modeling - Damage calculators, economy spreadsheets, probability analysis

Games like League of Legends employ entire teams dedicated to balance: data analysts processing millions of matches, professional player councils testing patches, designers running controlled experiments, live iteration every two weeks.

Our scope in this chapter: Initial design balance for new games or discrete systems. We're teaching systematic approaches that work for:

  • New tabletop RPGs or board games
  • Small to medium video games (roguelikes, tactics games, small RPGs)
  • Expansions or modules for existing games
  • Single-session to short-campaign experiences

Beyond our scope: Live service balance (years of iteration with teams), massive complexity (D&D-scale multi-year development), competitive esports balance (professional optimization), ongoing content generation.

What this means: We provide systematic thinking tools and starting points. If you're designing the next League of Legends, you'll need everything here PLUS teams, telemetry, and specialized tools. But you still need to understand these foundations first.

Confidence Level: HIGH - These principles apply universally, but implementation complexity scales with scope.

The Balance Problem: Three Missing Bridges

The fundamental challenge of game balance is bridging three gaps between fuzzy concepts and concrete parameters:

GAP 1: From Experience to Behavior

  • Fuzzy: "I want Challenge aesthetic"
  • Missing: What player behaviors create that feeling?

GAP 2: From Behavior to Approach

  • Fuzzy: "Players should learn patterns"
  • Missing: What design approach creates that behavior?

GAP 3: From Approach to Numbers

  • Fuzzy: "Use consistency-based design"
  • Missing: What specific parameters achieve that?

Most designers understand Gap 3 is hard. But Gaps 1 and 2 are where designs actually fail.

Why Formulas Don't Exist

Different games with identical aesthetics need completely different numbers:

Both games want Challenge aesthetic through pattern-learning boss fights:

Hollow Knight (2017):

  • Boss: 400-1000 HP depending on stage
  • Player damage: 5-20 per hit depending on upgrades
  • Fight duration: 2-5 minutes
  • Deaths expected: 5-20 attempts

Furi (2016):

  • Boss: Multiple phases with 8-12 health bars each
  • Player damage: 1 bar per combo string
  • Fight duration: 8-15 minutes
  • Deaths expected: 10-30 attempts

Same aesthetic, completely different numbers. Why? Because they have different:

  • Action speeds (Hollow Knight is methodical, Furi is intense)
  • Mechanical complexity (Hollow Knight has simpler movesets, Furi has bullet hell patterns)
  • Player capabilities (different power curves, different skill requirements)
  • Session expectations (Hollow Knight is exploration-focused, Furi is boss-rush-focused)

The numbers emerge from constraints, not formulas.

The Missing Framework: MDA and Dynamics

To bridge these gaps, we need to understand how experiences actually work.

The MDA framework (Hunicke, LeBlanc, Zubek, 2004) describes games as three layers:

MECHANICS → DYNAMICS → AESTHETICS

Mechanics: The rules, verbs, resolution systems you design Dynamics: The runtime behaviors that emerge when players engage with mechanics
Aesthetics: The emotional experiences players have from those behaviors

Examples:

  • Bluffing (dynamic) emerges from hidden information (mechanics) → creates Fellowship (aesthetic)
  • Time pressure (dynamic) emerges from turn timers (mechanics) → creates Tension (aesthetic)
  • Pattern learning (dynamic) emerges from consistent rules (mechanics) → creates Challenge (aesthetic)

The key insight: Players don't experience your mechanics directly. They experience the behaviors (dynamics) that emerge from engaging with your mechanics. Those behaviors create emotional responses (aesthetics).

The Designer's Dilemma

Players experience games backward:

But designers build forward:

This creates the problem: You know what aesthetic you want, but you build mechanics. How do you know which mechanics to build?

MDA's answer: Work backward. Start with desired aesthetic, identify required dynamics, then choose mechanics that create those dynamics.

This is what this chapter teaches.

The Balance Framework: Five Levels

Your balance emerges from decisions at five levels, each constraining the next:

LEVEL 1: AESTHETIC GOALS What experience are you creating? (from Chapter 1)

LEVEL 2: REQUIRED DYNAMICS What player behaviors create that experience?

LEVEL 3: BALANCE PARADIGM What design approach creates those behaviors?

LEVEL 4: MEASURABLE TARGETS How do you know the behaviors are occurring?

LEVEL 5: PARAMETER VALUES What specific numbers achieve those targets?

This chapter walks through each level systematically. By the end, you'll understand how "I want Challenge aesthetic" becomes "Boss has 540 HP" without any mysterious gaps.

Let's start with Level 2: understanding dynamics.

Level 2: From Aesthetics to Dynamics

When you say "I want Challenge aesthetic," what are you really saying?

Surface answer: "I want players to feel satisfied from overcoming difficulty."

Real answer: "I want specific player behaviors to occur that create that feeling."

What behaviors?

  • Players observe attack patterns and form mental models
  • Players test hypotheses about how to succeed
  • Players demonstrate mastery through superior execution
  • Players show clear improvement from attempt 1 to attempt 10+

These are dynamics—the emergent behaviors that create aesthetic experiences.

If these behaviors don't occur, you won't achieve Challenge aesthetic. No amount of parameter tuning will fix it, because the problem isn't the numbers—it's that the required behaviors aren't emerging.

Dynamics Are Not Mechanics

Mechanics are what you design:

  • Boss has 500 HP
  • Boss telegraphs attacks for 1 second
  • Player does 90 damage per hit
  • Player respawns instantly on death

Dynamics are what emerges from players engaging with those mechanics:

  • Players learn to recognize telegraph → dodge timing
  • Players retry immediately after death
  • Players improve gradually across attempts
  • Players develop optimal strategies

You design mechanics. Dynamics emerge. But you can predict and design FOR dynamics.

The Seven Core Aesthetic-Dynamic Pairs

Let's map major aesthetics to their required dynamics:

CHALLENGE AESTHETIC

Required dynamics:

  • Pattern learning: Players observe, hypothesize, test, refine understanding
  • Skill expression: Expert execution demonstrably better than novice
  • Failure recovery: Death followed by immediate retry with gained knowledge
  • Mastery progression: Clear improvement curve from struggling to succeeding

If these dynamics don't occur, Challenge aesthetic fails.

Example failure: Boss has random attack patterns. Players can't learn patterns (no pattern learning dynamic). Result: frustration, not challenge.

TENSION AESTHETIC

Required dynamics:

  • Resource pressure: Players forced into hard choices by scarcity
  • Threat awareness: Constant vigilance required, can't fully relax
  • Close calls: Near-death moments creating adrenaline spikes
  • Risk evaluation: Continuous danger-vs-reward calculations

If these dynamics don't occur, Tension aesthetic fails.

Example failure: Resources abundant. No scarcity (no resource pressure dynamic). Result: relaxation, not tension.

DISCOVERY AESTHETIC

Required dynamics:

  • Information gathering: Players actively seek clues and evidence
  • Theory formation: Players connect dots and form hypotheses
  • Revelation moments: "Aha!" experiences when understanding clicks
  • Systematic exploration: Players investigate methodically

If these dynamics don't occur, Discovery aesthetic fails.

Example failure: All information immediately visible. Nothing to discover (no information gathering dynamic). Result: comprehension, not discovery.

MASTERY AESTHETIC

Required dynamics:

  • System optimization: Players experiment to find synergies and efficiency
  • Strategic depth: Multiple viable approaches with meaningful trade-offs
  • Build experimentation: Players theory-craft and test combinations
  • Expert differentiation: Deep knowledge provides significant advantage

If these dynamics don't occur, Mastery aesthetic fails.

Example failure: One obviously optimal strategy. No choices (no system optimization dynamic). Result: execution, not mastery.

NARRATIVE AESTHETIC

Required dynamics:

  • Character engagement: Players emotionally invest in character fates
  • Consequence tracking: Players remember and anticipate story outcomes
  • Dramatic tension: Players experience story beats emotionally
  • Agency expression: Players feel their choices matter to story

If these dynamics don't occur, Narrative aesthetic fails.

Example failure: Choices don't affect outcomes. No agency (no agency expression dynamic). Result: observation, not narrative participation.

SUBMISSION AESTHETIC

Required dynamics:

  • Flow state: Players enter "zone" where actions become automatic
  • Rhythmic engagement: Steady, predictable pacing and timing
  • Stress reduction: Low pressure, minimal consequences for mistakes
  • Meditative execution: Satisfying repetitive actions

If these dynamics don't occur, Submission aesthetic fails.

Example failure: High stakes and punishment. Stress increases (opposite of stress reduction dynamic). Result: tension, not submission.

FELLOWSHIP AESTHETIC

Required dynamics:

  • Cooperation patterns: Players coordinate actions meaningfully
  • Role specialization: Each player contributes unique capabilities
  • Shared stakes: Outcomes affect the group, not just individuals
  • Communication loops: Players discuss, plan, coordinate regularly

If these dynamics don't occur, Fellowship aesthetic fails.

Example failure: Players can succeed alone easily. No cooperation (no cooperation dynamic). Result: parallel play, not fellowship.

The Critical Principle

Every aesthetic requires specific dynamics to emerge. No dynamics = no aesthetic, regardless of parameters.

This is why "I want Challenge aesthetic" → "Boss has 500 HP" is a logical gap. You haven't identified what dynamics Challenge requires or whether your mechanics create those dynamics.

The complete chain:

  1. Choose aesthetic: "Challenge"
  2. Identify required dynamics: "Pattern learning, skill expression"
  3. Choose mechanics that create those dynamics: [this is paradigm choice]
  4. Verify dynamics are occurring: [this is measurables]
  5. Tune parameters to optimize: [this is parameter tuning]

Level 3: Balance Paradigms - How to Create Dynamics

Now you know what dynamics you need. But how do you create them?

This is where balance paradigms come in. A balance paradigm is a fundamental approach to game balance that creates specific dynamics through its core mechanisms.

Three major paradigms:

  1. Consistency Balance - Fixed rules create learnable patterns
  2. Framework Balance - Flexible adjudication creates narrative flow
  3. Adaptive Balance - Dynamic adjustment creates maintained experience

Each paradigm creates different dynamics, serves different aesthetics, and requires different design approaches.

Consistency Balance: Creating Pattern Learning

Core mechanism: Rules produce identical outcomes for identical situations

Dynamics created:

  • Pattern learning (same input → same output = learnable)
  • Skill expression (execution quality matters)
  • Mastery curves (stable optimization targets)
  • Strategic depth (if rules are complex enough)

Aesthetics served: Challenge, Mastery (primarily)

Example: Dark Souls

Consistent mechanics:

  • Boss attacks in fixed patterns triggered by positioning/health
  • Player actions have fixed framedata (dodge i-frames always same)
  • Damage calculation deterministic (no randomness)
  • Enemy behavior entirely predictable

Dynamics that emerge:

  • Players learn attack patterns through repetition
  • Players master timing through practice
  • Players optimize strategies through experimentation
  • Expert players demonstrably superior to novices

Why it works for Challenge: Challenge aesthetic requires pattern learning dynamic. Consistency paradigm creates pattern learning through repeatability. Perfect match.

Example: Into the Breach

Consistent mechanics:

  • Enemy attacks telegraphed one turn in advance
  • Damage always exact (no randomness)
  • Board state fully deterministic
  • Optimal solutions often exist

Dynamics that emerge:

  • Players analyze board state like chess puzzle
  • Players learn enemy behavior patterns quickly
  • Players optimize move sequences
  • Expert players find perfect solutions

Why it works for Mastery: Mastery aesthetic requires system optimization dynamic. Consistency paradigm creates optimization through stable rule interactions.

Design requirements for Consistency:

  • Repeatability: Same situations recur frequently
  • Clarity: Players can perceive rules and outcomes
  • Fast iteration: Death/retry loops must be quick (<10 seconds)
  • Fair feedback: Players understand why they failed
  • Skill-based: Execution and decision quality matter more than chance

When Consistency paradigm fails:

  • If rules too complex to learn (cognitive overload)
  • If situations never repeat (can't learn patterns)
  • If randomness too high (undermines pattern learning)
  • If feedback unclear (can't identify what to learn)

Framework Balance: Creating Narrative Flow

Core mechanism: GM adjudicates situations using position/effect framework rather than fixed rules

Dynamics created:

  • Social negotiation (players propose, GM evaluates)
  • Creative problem-solving (flexible approaches valid)
  • Dramatic pacing (GM controls tension/difficulty)
  • Agency expression (player creativity matters)

Aesthetics served: Narrative, Discovery, Fellowship (primarily)

Example: Blades in the Dark

Framework mechanics:

  • No fixed difficulty numbers for tasks
  • GM evaluates position (controlled/risky/desperate)
  • GM evaluates effect (limited/standard/great)
  • Players negotiate approach and consequences

Dynamics that emerge:

  • Players propose creative solutions
  • GM adjudicates based on fiction and dramatic appropriateness
  • Success/failure creates story, not just mechanical outcomes
  • Social contract maintains balance

Why it works for Narrative: Narrative aesthetic requires agency expression and dramatic pacing dynamics. Framework paradigm creates these through flexible GM adjudication.

Example: Fate Core

Framework mechanics:

  • Four outcomes (fail/succeed-at-cost/succeed/succeed-with-style)
  • Aspects invoked narratively
  • Compels drive dramatic complications
  • Numeric values exist but fiction guides application

Dynamics that emerge:

  • Players engage with fictional positioning
  • Complications create drama (compels)
  • Success/failure both drive story forward
  • Creative approaches rewarded

Design requirements for Framework:

  • GM skill: Requires competent, engaged GM
  • Social contract: Players must trust GM fairness
  • Fiction-first: Narrative coherence more important than mechanical precision
  • Flexibility: Systems support GM adaptation, not rigid rules
  • Consequence design: Failure creates interesting situations, not just "you lose"

When Framework paradigm fails:

  • Without skilled GM (needs someone to adjudicate)
  • If players want mechanical mastery (optimization requires stable rules)
  • If challenge precision required (can't guarantee difficulty curve)
  • If scales beyond GM cognitive capacity (too many moving parts)

Adaptive Balance: Creating Maintained Experience

Core mechanism: System adjusts difficulty/pacing in response to player performance

Dynamics created:

  • Flow state (difficulty matches capability)
  • Sustained pressure (threat level maintained)
  • Tension maintenance (close calls without breaking)
  • Stress reduction (if adapted to comfort level)

Aesthetics served: Tension, Submission (primarily)

Example: Left 4 Dead "AI Director"

Adaptive mechanics:

  • Tracks player resources (health, ammo)
  • Tracks player stress (recent damage, intensity)
  • Spawns threats based on current state
  • Creates crescendo moments then relief

Dynamics that emerge:

  • Pressure maintained without overwhelming
  • Close calls frequent (low resources common)
  • Flow state (always challenging, rarely impossible)
  • Team cooperation required (strain forces coordination)

Why it works for Tension: Tension aesthetic requires sustained pressure and close calls dynamics. Adaptive paradigm creates these by adjusting threat to player state.

Example: Resident Evil 4

Adaptive mechanics:

  • Enemy aggression scales with player skill
  • Ammunition scarcity adjusts to usage patterns
  • Damage scales based on player performance
  • (Controversial - some players feel manipulated)

Dynamics that emerge:

  • Resource pressure maintained regardless of skill
  • Difficulty feels "just right" for many players
  • Expert players can't trivialize encounters
  • Tension maintained across playthroughs

Design requirements for Adaptive:

  • Performance tracking: System must measure player state accurately
  • Invisible adjustment: Players shouldn't notice manipulation
  • Appropriate metrics: Track what actually indicates experience quality
  • Constraint bounds: Adaptation has reasonable min/max limits
  • Responsiveness: Adjusts quickly enough to matter

When Adaptive paradigm fails:

  • If adaptation too obvious (feels manipulative)
  • If tracking wrong metrics (optimizes wrong thing)
  • If undermines learning (patterns don't stay learned)
  • If removes skill expression (expert = novice outcomes)

Paradigm Selection: Matching Dynamics to Aesthetics

The decision process:

Step 1: Identify required dynamics from aesthetic (from previous section's mapping)

Step 2: Evaluate which paradigm creates those dynamics

For Challenge aesthetic:

  • Requires: Pattern learning, skill expression
  • Paradigm: Consistency (creates learnable patterns through repeatability)

For Tension aesthetic:

  • Requires: Resource pressure, sustained threat
  • Paradigm: Adaptive (maintains pressure through adjustment) OR Consistency (if paired with well-tuned resource economy)

For Narrative aesthetic:

  • Requires: Agency expression, dramatic pacing
  • Paradigm: Framework (enables flexible dramatic adjudication)

For Mastery aesthetic:

  • Requires: System optimization, strategic depth
  • Paradigm: Consistency (stable rules enable deep optimization)

For Discovery aesthetic:

  • Requires: Information gathering, revelation
  • Paradigm: Framework (enables flexible investigation) OR Consistency (if paired with hidden information mechanics)

For Submission aesthetic:

  • Requires: Flow state, stress reduction
  • Paradigm: Adaptive (maintains comfortable flow) OR Consistency (if paired with simple, forgiving mechanics)

For Fellowship aesthetic:

  • Requires: Cooperation, role specialization
  • Paradigm: Framework (enables social negotiation) OR Consistency (if paired with interdependent mechanics)

Can You Mix Paradigms?

Yes, but carefully. Different subsystems can use different paradigms:

Hades (2020):

  • Combat: Consistency (attack patterns learnable, skill-based)
  • Narrative: Adaptive (relationship progress adjusts to player engagement)
  • Build variety: Consistency (boon interactions stable, optimizable)

Path of Exile:

  • Combat: Consistency (damage calculations precise, optimizable)
  • Difficulty: Consistency (monster stats fixed per map tier)
  • Build complexity: Consistency (enabling deep optimization)
  • (Nearly pure Consistency paradigm for Mastery aesthetic)

Monster of the Week (tabletop RPG):

  • Investigation: Framework (GM adjudicates clue discovery)
  • Combat: Light Consistency (some fixed mechanics for resolution speed)
  • Drama: Framework (GM paces and adjusts for story beats)

The principle: Each subsystem should primarily use one paradigm. Mixing within same subsystem creates cognitive dissonance ("Can I learn this pattern or will it change?").

Summary: Paradigms Create Dynamics

Key insights:

  1. Different aesthetics require different dynamics
  2. Different paradigms create different dynamics
  3. Choose paradigm that creates your required dynamics
  4. Wrong paradigm prevents correct dynamics, breaking aesthetic

Example of mismatch:

Goal: Challenge aesthetic (needs pattern learning) Wrong choice: Adaptive paradigm (adjusts when player learns) Problem: Adaptation undermines pattern learning dynamic Result: Players frustrated—difficulty moves when they get better

Correct choice: Consistency paradigm (stable patterns) Result: Pattern learning emerges naturally, Challenge aesthetic achieved

Next section: Level 4, measuring whether dynamics are actually occurring.

Level 4: Measurables - Quantifying Dynamics

You've chosen your paradigm. Now you need to verify the required dynamics are actually emerging.

This is where measurables come in.

A measurable is an observable property that indicates whether intended dynamics are occurring.

Understanding Measurables

Measurables are not arbitrary metrics. They're evidence of dynamics.

Dynamic: Pattern learning (behavior you want) Measurable: "Time-to-kill decreases 20% from attempt 1 to attempt 10" (evidence it's happening)

Dynamic: Resource pressure (behavior you want) Measurable: "Player health averages 30-50%" (evidence it's happening)

Dynamic: Information gathering (behavior you want) Measurable: "Players discover 60-80% of available clues" (evidence it's happening)

The relationship:

Three Types of Measurables

Type 1: Quantitative (direct numbers)

Observable numeric properties:

  • Time-to-kill: 180 seconds
  • Deaths before victory: 8 attempts
  • Resource levels: 40% average
  • Close calls per hour: 3 near-death moments

Type 2: Qualitative (observable patterns)

Patterns you observe in playtesting:

  • Pattern recognition: "Players predict attacks by attempt 5"
  • Fairness perception: "80% report 'hard but fair'"
  • Emotional response: "Players celebrate victories"
  • Strategy diversity: "5+ viable approaches observed"

Type 3: Behavioral (player actions)

What players actually do:

  • Skill expression: "Expert players 3x faster than novice"
  • Risk-taking: "Players make risky choices 40% of time"
  • Cooperation: "Players coordinate strategy every 5 minutes"
  • System engagement: "All core mechanics used regularly"

Deriving Measurables From Dynamics

The process:

Step 1: Identify required dynamic Example: Pattern learning (for Challenge aesthetic)

Step 2: Ask "How would I observe this dynamic occurring?"

  • Players improve across attempts (learning happening)
  • Players predict attack patterns (comprehension developing)
  • Players explain failures correctly (understanding present)
  • Expert players significantly better (skill ceiling exists)

Step 3: Convert observations to quantifiable targets

  • "Improve across attempts" → "TTK decreases 20% from attempt 1-10"
  • "Predict patterns" → "Prediction accuracy >70% by attempt 5"
  • "Explain failures" → ">80% can describe why they died"
  • "Expert better" → "Expert TTK 50% faster than novice"

Step 4: Prioritize measurables (3-5 primary) For pattern learning dynamic:

  1. TTK improvement (most direct evidence)
  2. Prediction accuracy (comprehension metric)
  3. Expert-novice gap (skill ceiling metric)

Step 5: Set concrete targets with reasonable ranges

  • TTK improvement: 20% (±5%) improvement over 10 attempts
  • Prediction accuracy: 70% (±10%) by attempt 5
  • Expert gap: 50% (±15%) faster than novice

Measurables for Each Dynamic Type

Let's map common dynamics to their key measurables:

PATTERN LEARNING DYNAMIC (Challenge aesthetic)

Measurables that indicate it's working:

  • Performance improvement: TTK decreases across attempts
  • Prediction accuracy: Players anticipate attacks correctly
  • Error reduction: Mistakes decrease steadily
  • Skill expression: Expert demonstrably better than novice
  • Retry willingness: Players voluntarily retry after failure

Target example:

  • First attempt TTK: 5 minutes (fumbling, learning)
  • Tenth attempt TTK: 3 minutes (patterns learned)
  • Expert TTK: 2 minutes (optimal execution)
  • Prediction accuracy by attempt 5: 70%+

RESOURCE PRESSURE DYNAMIC (Tension aesthetic)

Measurables that indicate it's working:

  • Scarcity maintenance: Resources stay 30-50% (not full, not empty)
  • Close call frequency: Near-death moments 2-3 per 10 minutes
  • Decision frequency: Resource trade-offs 5+ times per session
  • Threat density: Combat encounters every 2-3 minutes
  • Recovery timing: Breathing room 30-60 seconds

Target example:

  • Average health: 40% (±10%)
  • Average ammo: 35% (±10%)
  • Close calls (<20% health): 2-3 per 10 minutes
  • Resource decisions: 5-8 per session

SYSTEM OPTIMIZATION DYNAMIC (Mastery aesthetic)

Measurables that indicate it's working:

  • Strategy diversity: 5+ viable builds with <20% power gap
  • Optimization space: Expert strategies 30% more efficient
  • Experimentation rate: Players test new builds regularly
  • Theory-crafting: Players discuss optimization externally
  • Skill ceiling: Continuous improvement possible 20+ hours

Target example:

  • Viable builds: 5-10 within 20% power
  • Expert efficiency: 30% better than casual optimal
  • Meta diversity: No single strategy >40% playrate
  • Learning curve: Performance still improving at 50 hours

INFORMATION GATHERING DYNAMIC (Discovery aesthetic)

Measurables that indicate it's working:

  • Discovery rate: 60-80% of clues found (thoroughness)
  • Theory formation: Hypothesis creation 3-5 times per investigation
  • Revelation frequency: "Aha!" moments reported by >70% of players
  • Systematic behavior: Evidence of methodical searching
  • Clue interconnection: Players connect 4+ clues correctly

Target example:

  • Clue discovery: 60-80% found
  • False theories: 2-3 per investigation (meaningful uncertainty)
  • Correct revelation: By attempt 3-4
  • Connection insights: 3-4 "aha!" moments per mystery

SOCIAL NEGOTIATION DYNAMIC (Narrative aesthetic, Framework paradigm)

Measurables that indicate it's working:

  • Negotiation frequency: Position/effect discussion every 5-10 minutes
  • Creative proposals: Novel approaches suggested 3+ times per session
  • Framework consistency: Similar situations → similar rulings (>80%)
  • Agency perception: Players feel choices matter (>80% survey)
  • Dramatic satisfaction: Story beats land emotionally (>70% survey)

Target example:

  • Player-GM negotiation: 4-6 per session
  • Creative attempts: 3-5 per session
  • Framework application consistency: >80%
  • Player-reported agency: >80%

FLOW STATE DYNAMIC (Submission aesthetic, Adaptive paradigm)

Measurables that indicate it's working:

  • Difficulty matching: Challenge feels "just right" (>70% survey)
  • Uninterrupted play: Sessions extend beyond target (60+ min actual vs 45 min planned)
  • Stress reduction: Heart rate/anxiety decreases during play
  • Automatic execution: Players report "zone" state (>60%)
  • Replay willingness: >80% want to continue immediately

Target example:

  • Perceived difficulty: "Just right" >70%
  • Session length: Actual 60+ min when planned 45 min
  • Stress indicators: Decrease after 5 minutes
  • Flow state reports: >60%

How Paradigm Changes Measurables

Same aesthetic, different paradigm → different measurables:

Challenge aesthetic achieved through Consistency paradigm:

  • Measure: TTK consistency (±10% variance)
  • Measure: Pattern stability (same approach works reliably)
  • Measure: Learning curve (steady improvement)

Challenge aesthetic achieved through Framework paradigm:

  • Measure: Framework consistency (similar situations → similar rulings)
  • Measure: Creative success rate (novel approaches work ~60%)
  • Measure: Agency perception (players feel challenged but fair)

Both create Challenge, but you measure different properties.

Common Measurable Mistakes

Mistake 1: Measuring parameters, not dynamics

Bad: "Fireball does 30 damage"

  • This is a parameter value, not evidence of dynamic
  • Tells you nothing about whether dynamics are occurring

Good: "Fireball kills basic enemies in 2 hits"

  • This is a measurable (hits-to-kill relationship)
  • Indicates power balance working or not

Mistake 2: Measurables disconnected from dynamics

Bad: Measuring "average session length" for Challenge aesthetic

  • Session length doesn't validate pattern learning dynamic
  • Might be interesting data, but not primary concern

Good: Measuring "TTK improvement across attempts"

  • Directly validates pattern learning occurring
  • Primary evidence of required dynamic

Mistake 3: Over-precision

Bad: "TTK should be exactly 186.3 seconds"

  • False precision (impossible to achieve exactly)
  • Wastes time on meaningless tuning

Good: "TTK should be 2-3 minutes"

  • Achievable range
  • Meaningful differences (minutes matter, seconds don't)

Mistake 4: Too many measurables

Bad: Tracking 20 different metrics simultaneously

  • Can't optimize all of them
  • Cognitive overload, lose focus

Good: 3-5 primary measurables per major dynamic

  • Focused optimization
  • Clear priorities

Mistake 5: Unmeasurable "measurables"

Bad: "Game should feel impactful"

  • Can't observe objectively
  • Can't quantify meaningfully
  • "Feel" is aesthetic, not measurable

Good: "Players celebrate in >70% of victories"

  • Observable behavior
  • Quantifiable percentage
  • Indicates satisfaction indirectly

Practical Example: Complete Dynamic-to-Measurable Chain

Aesthetic: Challenge Required Dynamic: Pattern learning Paradigm: Consistency Balance

Measurable derivation:

What does pattern learning look like?

  • Players retry same encounter repeatedly
  • Performance improves across attempts
  • Players predict enemy behavior
  • Experts demonstrate mastery
  • Players can explain success/failure

Convert to measurables:

  1. TTK improvement: First attempt 5 min, tenth attempt 3 min (40% improvement)
  2. Prediction accuracy: 70% correct anticipation by attempt 5
  3. Expert gap: Expert players 50% faster than tenth-attempt novice
  4. Failure comprehension: >80% can explain why they died
  5. Retry engagement: >70% voluntarily retry after death

Prioritize top 3:

  1. TTK improvement (most direct evidence)
  2. Prediction accuracy (comprehension metric)
  3. Expert gap (skill ceiling metric)

Set targets:

  • TTK: 5 min → 3 min over 10 attempts (40% improvement)
  • Prediction: 70% accuracy by attempt 5
  • Expert: 50% faster than trained novice

These targets now guide parameter setting (next section).

Summary: Measurables Bridge Dynamics to Numbers

Key insights:

  1. Measurables are evidence that dynamics are occurring
  2. Different dynamics require different measurables
  3. Same dynamic measured differently in different paradigms
  4. 3-5 primary measurables per major dynamic
  5. Targets should be ranges, not false precision

The complete chain so far:

Level 5: From Measurables to Parameters - Worked Examples

Now you have measurable targets. But you still face the original question: "What actual numbers do I put in my game?"

This section shows complete worked examples of deriving parameters from measurables for each paradigm.

The Derivation Process

The general approach:

  1. Start with measurable target (from previous section)
  2. Work backward to find constraints
  3. Calculate parameters that satisfy constraints
  4. Test and iterate on the math

This is not guessing. This is constraint-solving.

Example 1: Consistency Balance for Challenge Aesthetic

Context: Boss fight in action game, Challenge aesthetic

From previous levels:

  • Aesthetic: Challenge
  • Dynamics: Pattern learning, skill expression
  • Paradigm: Consistency Balance
  • Measurables:
    • TTK improves from 5 minutes (attempt 1) to 3 minutes (attempt 10)
    • Expert TTK: 2 minutes
    • Pattern prediction accuracy: 70% by attempt 5

Deriving parameters:

Step 1: Establish time constraints

Target TTK: 3 minutes for trained player

  • This means fight must be completable in 180 seconds
  • Implies certain action density and damage requirements

Step 2: Determine player action frequency

Question: "How often can players meaningfully act?"

Depends on genre:

  • Fast action game: Actions every 1-2 seconds
  • Methodical action game: Actions every 3-5 seconds
  • Tactical game: Actions every 5-10 seconds

Let's say: Action-focused boss fight, player can attack every 3 seconds (dodge window → attack opening → recovery)

Over 180-second fight: 180 ÷ 3 = 60 possible attack opportunities

But not every opportunity converts to hit (some used for dodging, repositioning):

  • Novice hits maybe 20% of opportunities: 12 hits
  • Trained hits maybe 50% of opportunities: 30 hits
  • Expert hits maybe 80% of opportunities: 48 hits

Step 3: Calculate damage per hit

If trained player needs 30 hits to win 180-second fight:

Boss HP ÷ 30 hits = Damage per hit

Let's work backward from target time-to-kill:

  • Trained player: 180 seconds, 30 hits
  • Novice player: 300 seconds, 12 hits (same boss HP)
  • Expert player: 120 seconds, 48 hits (same boss HP)

Key insight: Same boss HP, different execution efficiency creates TTK variance.

If boss has 900 HP:

  • Damage per hit needs to be: 900 ÷ 30 = 30 damage
  • Novice: 12 hits × 30 damage = 360 damage dealt (not enough, dies multiple times)
  • Trained: 30 hits × 30 damage = 900 damage (victory at 180 sec)
  • Expert: 48 hits × 30 damage = 1440 damage (overkill, actually finishes at 120 sec)

This doesn't work. Expert finishes too fast if player damage is fixed.

Better approach: Scale HP with target hit count

If trained player hits 30 times over 180 seconds:

  • Boss HP = 30 hits × player damage
  • If player weapon does 90 damage: Boss HP = 2700
  • Trained: 30 hits × 90 = 2700 (victory)
  • Expert: Can hit ~48 times in 180 sec but boss dies at 30 hits
  • So expert time: 2700 ÷ 90 = 30 hits needed ÷ (48 hits/180 sec) = 113 seconds

Check against targets:

  • Trained TTK: 180 seconds ✓ (target was 3 min)
  • Expert TTK: 113 seconds ✓ (target was ~2 min, close enough)
  • Novice: Would take 12 hits × 90 = 1080 damage in first attempt, dying multiple times (expected)

Step 4: Verify pattern learning measurable

Target: TTK improves from 300 seconds (attempt 1) to 180 seconds (attempt 10)

Improvement driven by: Hit success rate increases (learning attack patterns)

  • Attempt 1: 12 hits over 300 seconds = 20% hit rate
  • Attempt 10: 30 hits over 180 seconds = 50% hit rate

Is 20% → 50% hit rate improvement realistic for pattern learning?

Yes, if:

  • Attack patterns are learnable (telegraphed clearly)
  • Players have time to observe and react
  • Failure teaches (player understands what went wrong)

Step 5: Add telegraph timing

For pattern learning to work, attacks must be:

  • Visible: Players can see telegraph
  • Reactable: Players have time to respond
  • Consistent: Same telegraph → same attack

Typical human reaction time: 200-300ms Need buffer for decision-making: 500-800ms

Telegraph timing: 1.0 seconds

  • Long enough for pattern recognition
  • Short enough to require attention
  • Consistent across attacks

Step 6: Add failure recovery timing

For pattern learning to work through iteration, retry must be fast:

Target: Death → retry in <10 seconds

Implementation:

  • Instant respawn checkpoint (no loading)
  • Boss immediately accessible (no travel)
  • Health/resources reset instantly

Final parameters:

These aren't random numbers. Each derives from measurable constraints.

Example 2: Framework Balance for Narrative Aesthetic

Context: Investigation scene in tabletop RPG, Narrative aesthetic

From previous levels:

  • Aesthetic: Narrative
  • Dynamics: Agency expression, creative problem-solving, dramatic pacing
  • Paradigm: Framework Balance
  • Measurables:
    • Player-GM negotiation: 4-6 times per session
    • Creative proposals: 3-5 per session
    • Framework consistency: >80%
    • Players report feeling agency: >80%

Deriving parameters:

Note: Framework paradigm has fewer numeric parameters, more structural decisions.

Step 1: Define position/effect framework

Position (how dangerous):

  • Controlled: Safe, low risk
  • Risky: Moderate danger, consequences possible
  • Desperate: High danger, serious consequences likely

Effect (how effective):

  • Limited: Partial progress
  • Standard: Expected progress
  • Great: Exceptional progress

Step 2: Map narrative situations to framework

Example: Gathering information from hostile informant

Controlled approach examples:

  • Bribe with gold (they want money, low risk)
  • Trade information (mutually beneficial)
  • Position: Controlled

Risky approach examples:

  • Intimidate (might anger them, might work)
  • Trickery (might see through it, might succeed)
  • Position: Risky

Desperate approach examples:

  • Threaten violence (likely to escalate badly)
  • Break into their home (serious legal consequences)
  • Position: Desperate

Step 3: Define success/failure outcomes for each position

Controlled position:

  • Success: Get information, no complications
  • Partial success: Get information, minor complication (costs extra gold)
  • Failure: No information, minor setback (they're suspicious now)

Risky position:

  • Success: Get information, no complications
  • Partial success: Get information, moderate complication (they spread rumors about you)
  • Failure: No information, moderate consequence (they become hostile)

Desperate position:

  • Success: Get information, but at cost
  • Partial success: Partial information, serious complication (guards arrive)
  • Failure: No information, serious consequence (arrested, or informant killed)

Step 4: Set effect levels

Limited effect examples:

  • Amateur attempting expert task
  • Wrong tools for the job
  • Opposed by significant obstacles
  • Outcome: Partial progress, will need follow-up

Standard effect examples:

  • Appropriate approach with proper tools
  • Reasonable difficulty for capability
  • Outcome: Expected progress, meaningful advancement

Great effect examples:

  • Expert using optimal approach
  • Critical success (player rolled very well)
  • Exceptional circumstances align
  • Outcome: Exceptional progress, bonus benefits

Step 5: Define consistency guidelines

To hit >80% framework consistency target:

Create position/effect reference examples for common situations:

Step 6: Set session structure targets

To hit 4-6 negotiation moments per session:

Session structure:

  • 3-4 major scenes per 3-hour session
  • Each scene has 1-2 significant player choices requiring position/effect negotiation
  • Total: 3-6 negotiation moments (within target)

To hit 3-5 creative proposals per session:

Encourage through:

  • Open-ended scene framing ("What's your approach?")
  • Reward creativity (bonus effect for clever ideas)
  • Avoid prescriptive solutions (no "right" way)

Step 7: Define dramatic pacing guidelines

To maintain narrative aesthetic:

Pacing structure:

  • Opener (30 min): Controlled position scenes (establish situation)
  • Rising action (60 min): Escalate to risky positions (build tension)
  • Climax (45 min): Desperate position (high stakes)
  • Resolution (45 min): Controlled positions return (denouement)

Final parameters (structural):

These aren't strict rules—they're structural guidelines that create the required dynamics.

Example 3: Adaptive Balance for Tension Aesthetic

Context: Survival horror game, Tension aesthetic

From previous levels:

  • Aesthetic: Tension
  • Dynamics: Resource pressure, sustained threat, close calls
  • Paradigm: Adaptive Balance
  • Measurables:
    • Average health: 40% (±10%)
    • Average ammo: 35% (±10%)
    • Close calls (<20% health): 2-3 per 10 minutes
    • Threat density: Encounter every 2-3 minutes

Deriving parameters:

Step 1: Define what you're tracking

Player state metrics:

  • Current health (% of maximum)
  • Current ammunition (% of carry capacity)
  • Time since last combat
  • Time in "danger zone" (<20% health)

Step 2: Define target ranges for each metric

Health targets:

  • Minimum: 20% (too low = frustration, player quits)
  • Target: 40% (sustained pressure)
  • Maximum: 70% (too high = no tension)

Ammo targets:

  • Minimum: 15% (too low = can't engage threats)
  • Target: 35% (forces conservation)
  • Maximum: 60% (too high = spam optimal weapon)

Encounter timing targets:

  • Minimum spacing: 90 seconds (need breathing room)
  • Target spacing: 150 seconds (2.5 minutes)
  • Maximum spacing: 240 seconds (too safe, tension drops)

Step 3: Define adaptation rules

If health >70% (too safe):

  • Increase encounter difficulty (more enemies or tougher enemies)
  • Reduce healing item spawns
  • Increase time to next encounter reduction (encounters come faster)

If health <20% (too dangerous):

  • Decrease encounter difficulty (fewer enemies)
  • Increase healing item spawns
  • Increase time to next encounter (breathing room)

If ammo >60% (too abundant):

  • Increase encounter difficulty (forces ammo expenditure)
  • Reduce ammo drops
  • Spawn more durable enemies (require more shots)

If ammo <15% (too scarce):

  • Decrease encounter difficulty (conserve remaining ammo)
  • Increase ammo drops
  • Spawn weaker enemies (fewer shots required)

Step 4: Calculate specific parameter adjustments

Base parameters:

Adaptation scaling:

When health >70%:

  • Enemy HP: Scale up to 120 (20% increase) → requires 5 shots instead of 4
  • Encounter size: Add 1 enemy → now 4 enemies instead of 3
  • Health spawn rate: Reduce to every 7 minutes (was 5 minutes)
  • Effect: Forces more resource expenditure, brings health down

When health <20%:

  • Enemy HP: Scale down to 80 (20% decrease) → requires 3-4 shots instead of 4
  • Encounter size: Remove 1 enemy → now 2 enemies instead of 3
  • Health spawn rate: Increase to every 3 minutes (was 5 minutes)
  • Effect: Reduces resource expenditure, allows recovery

When ammo >60%:

  • Enemy HP: Scale up to 125 (25% increase) → requires 5 shots instead of 4
  • Ammo drops: Reduce by 30% → now 14 rounds instead of 20
  • Effect: Depletes excess ammunition

When ammo <15%:

  • Enemy HP: Scale down to 75 (25% decrease) → requires 3 shots instead of 4
  • Ammo drops: Increase by 50% → now 30 rounds instead of 20
  • Effect: Allows ammunition recovery

Step 5: Define close call generation

Target: 2-3 close calls per 10 minutes

Close call = player health drops below 20%

Mechanism:

Calculate player's expected health after upcoming encounter:

  • Current health: 50%
  • Upcoming encounter: 3 enemies × 10 damage each = 30 expected damage
  • Expected health after: 50% - 30% = 20% (close call)

Adjustment:

If no close call expected soon, increase encounter difficulty:

  • Add damage (more enemies or harder enemies)
  • Reduce recent healing availability

If too many close calls recently, decrease difficulty:

  • Reduce damage (fewer enemies)
  • Increase healing availability

Step 6: Add bounds to prevent extremes

Adaptation limits:

Enemy HP: Cannot go below 50 or above 150 (±50% of baseline 100)

  • Prevents trivialization or impossibility

Encounter size: Cannot go below 1 or above 5

  • Prevents empty world or overwhelming spam

Resource spawn rate: Cannot go below 2 minutes or above 10 minutes

  • Prevents feast-or-famine extremes

Step 7: Set measurement intervals

System checks player state every:

  • 30 seconds (continuous monitoring)

System adjusts parameters every:

  • After each combat encounter
  • When spawning resources
  • When calculating next encounter timing

Prevents too-frequent adjustment (feels manipulative) or too-rare adjustment (ineffective).

Final parameters:

These numbers define an adaptive system that maintains tension through dynamic adjustment.

Summary: Parameters Derive From Measurables

Key insights from examples:

1. Parameters aren't guessed—they're calculated from constraints

  • Consistency example: Boss HP derived from TTK × hit rate × damage
  • Framework example: Position/effect structure derived from negotiation frequency
  • Adaptive example: Adaptation ranges derived from target resource levels

2. Different paradigms have different "parameter types"

  • Consistency: Numeric values (HP, damage, timing)
  • Framework: Structural guidelines (position tiers, effect levels, consistency rules)
  • Adaptive: Base values + adaptation rules + bounds

3. Every parameter should trace back to a measurable

  • Boss HP 2700 → enables 180-second TTK target
  • Telegraph 1.0 seconds → enables pattern learning
  • Health 40% target → maintains resource pressure
  • Position/effect 3×3 → enables consistent framework application

4. Parameters have ranges, not exact values

  • Consistency: Telegraph 1.0 seconds (±0.2 acceptable)
  • Adaptive: Health 40% (±10% range)
  • Framework: 4-6 negotiations per session (not exactly 5)

No gaps. Each level derives from the previous.

Scaling and Evolution: When Simple Parameters Break

The examples above assume relatively simple systems. But what happens as your game grows?

Three complexity thresholds where simple parameter approaches break:

Threshold 1: Multiple Interacting Systems (10+ variables)

When: You have 5+ mechanics that interact (damage, armor, resistances, buffs, debuffs, positioning, cooldowns, resources, etc.)

Problem: Parameters don't exist in isolation—changing one affects others

Example: Path of Exile

Simple parameter approach:

  • Set monster HP
  • Set player damage
  • TTK = HP ÷ damage

Doesn't work because:

  • Player damage varies by 1000x based on build
  • Monster resistances vary
  • Damage types interact with resistances
  • Buffs multiply damage
  • Positioning affects hit rates
  • Cooldowns affect sustained DPS vs. burst

Solution approach:

1. Model the system:

  • Build spreadsheet with all interactions
  • Calculate damage ranges (min/average/max)
  • Identify dominant factors
  • Test edge cases

2. Set baselines:

  • Define "baseline" player build (median power)
  • Balance for baseline first
  • Then test extremes

3. Define acceptable variance:

  • Not all builds need identical power
  • But gap shouldn't exceed 3-5x between viable builds
  • Identify outliers and nerf/buff

4. Use simulation:

  • Run thousands of virtual encounters
  • Measure actual TTK distribution
  • Identify broken combinations

This is beyond initial design scope—requires dedicated tools and iteration.

Threshold 2: Long-Term Progression (20+ hours)

When: Players accumulate power over 20+ hour campaigns

Problem: What's balanced at hour 1 is broken at hour 20

Example: Traditional RPGs

Early game (hour 1):

  • Player HP: 30
  • Player damage: 5
  • Enemy HP: 20
  • Enemy damage: 3
  • TTK: 4 hits = 12 seconds
  • Player survives 10 hits

Late game (hour 50):

  • Player HP: 3000 (100x increase)
  • Player damage: 500 (100x increase)
  • Enemy HP: 2000 (100x increase)
  • Enemy damage: 300 (100x increase)
  • TTK: 4 hits = 12 seconds (same!)
  • Player survives 10 hits (same!)

If you scale everything proportionally, nothing changes (boring).

But if you don't scale proportionally, balance breaks:

Bad scaling:

  • Player HP: ×100
  • Player damage: ×200 (double the HP scaling)
  • Enemy HP: ×100
  • Enemy damage: ×100

Result:

  • TTK: 2 hits = 6 seconds (too fast, trivial)
  • Player survives 10 hits (unchanged)

Solution approach:

1. Define power curve:

  • How should power feel at each stage?
  • Early: Struggle (intentional difficulty)
  • Mid: Capable (earned power)
  • Late: Powerful (reward investment)

2. Scale asymmetrically:

  • Don't scale all parameters equally
  • Scale damage faster than HP (fights stay similar duration)
  • Add new mechanics at later tiers (complexity, not just numbers)

3. Add progression dimensions:

  • Not just bigger numbers
  • New abilities (change dynamics)
  • New build options (strategic depth)
  • New enemy types (require adapted strategies)

4. Test at multiple progression points:

  • Balance hour 1
  • Balance hour 10
  • Balance hour 20
  • Balance hour 50
  • Smooth transitions between

This requires long-term vision and extensive testing.

Threshold 3: Emergent Optimization (100+ hours of player mastery)

When: Expert players find strategies you didn't anticipate

Problem: Optimal strategies might trivialize your careful balance

Example: Into the Breach

Designer intention:

  • Tactical puzzle game
  • Multiple approaches viable
  • Rewards clever positioning

Expert player discovery:

  • "Vek AI manipulation" strategies
  • Predict enemy targeting, manipulate into blocking each other
  • "Perfect" solutions to most boards
  • Game becomes deterministic puzzle with one answer

Is this broken balance?

Depends on aesthetic:

  • If aesthetic is Challenge (overcome difficulty), optimization undermines it
  • If aesthetic is Mastery (deep system understanding), optimization achieves it

Solution approaches:

If you want to preserve multiple approaches:

1. Add randomness:

  • Variable enemy AI
  • Procedural board generation
  • Prevents single optimal solution

2. Patch dominant strategies:

  • Nerf exploits
  • Buff underused approaches
  • Maintain strategic diversity

3. Add new content:

  • New enemies that counter meta strategies
  • New maps that require different approaches
  • Force adaptation

If you embrace optimization:

1. Design for depth:

  • Make optimization the goal
  • Celebrate player mastery
  • Add harder challenges for optimizers

2. Provide challenge tiers:

  • Normal mode for casual players
  • Hard modes for optimizers
  • Separate balance for each

3. Accept it:

  • Not all games need perfect balance forever
  • Mastery is valid endstate
  • Let players "solve" the game

This is ongoing balance work—requires community engagement and live iteration.

When to Scale Up Your Approach

Stick with simple parameters if:

  • Single-session or short campaign (<10 hours)
  • Limited interacting systems (<5 major mechanics)
  • Casual or narrative focus (balance less critical)
  • Resources limited (small team, short timeline)

Scale up to complex approaches when:

  • Long-term progression (20+ hours)
  • Complex interconnected systems (10+ variables)
  • Competitive or mastery focus (balance critical)
  • Resources available (dedicated balance designer, telemetry, time for iteration)

Most designs should start simple. Only add complexity when simple approaches demonstrably fail.

Validation and Iteration: How to Test Balance

You've set parameters. Now you need to verify they actually create the dynamics and achieve the aesthetics you intended.

What to Playtest

Don't playtest randomly. Test specific measurables.

Bad playtest:

  • "Play the game and tell me what you think"
  • Vague, unfocused
  • Players give opinions, not data

Good playtest:

  • "Play the boss fight 10 times. I'm measuring how your TTK changes across attempts."
  • Specific, measurable
  • Gets data on pattern learning dynamic

Testing by Paradigm

Different paradigms require different testing approaches:

Consistency Balance testing:

What to measure:

  • TTK across multiple attempts (pattern learning occurring?)
  • Hit success rate over time (improvement happening?)
  • Player-reported pattern comprehension (can they explain attacks?)
  • Expert vs. novice performance gap (skill ceiling exists?)

Testing protocol:

  1. First playtest: 10 attempts on same encounter
  2. Measure TTK each attempt
  3. Ask player to predict attacks (accuracy measurement)
  4. Calculate improvement curve
  5. Compare to measurable targets

Red flags:

  • No TTK improvement (patterns not learnable)
  • Random variance >±20% in TTK (too much randomness)
  • Player can't explain patterns (feedback unclear)
  • Expert = novice performance (no skill expression)

Framework Balance testing:

What to measure:

  • Negotiation frequency (enough player agency moments?)
  • Framework consistency (similar situations → similar rulings?)
  • Creative proposal rate (players trying new things?)
  • Player-reported agency (feel their choices matter?)

Testing protocol:

  1. Run full session (3-4 hours)
  2. Count position/effect negotiations
  3. Document edge case rulings
  4. Survey players on agency and satisfaction
  5. Check for consistent framework application

Red flags:

  • <3 negotiations per session (too rigid, not framework)
  • Inconsistent rulings (players confused by arbitrariness)
  • No creative proposals (players defaulting to safe approaches)
  • Players report railroading (GM overriding reasonable attempts)

Adaptive Balance testing:

What to measure:

  • Resource level distribution (maintaining target ranges?)
  • Close call frequency (appropriate pressure?)
  • Adaptation visibility (players noticing manipulation?)
  • Satisfaction with difficulty (feels "just right"?)

Testing protocol:

  1. Long session (60+ minutes)
  2. Log resource levels every 30 seconds
  3. Count close call moments
  4. Survey on difficulty perception
  5. Check if adaptation detectable

Red flags:

  • Resources at extremes (0% or 100%, adaptation failing)
  • No close calls or constant close calls (pressure wrong)
  • Players notice adaptation ("the game is going easy on me")
  • Difficulty feels arbitrary (adaptation too aggressive)

Observable Balance Problems

Balance problems show specific symptoms:

Problem: Challenge becomes frustration

Symptoms:

  • Players quit rather than retry
  • Anger/frustration rather than determination
  • TTK not improving (can't learn)
  • Players report "unfair" or "cheap"

Diagnosis:

  • Patterns too complex to learn
  • Feedback too unclear
  • Recovery too punishing (long walk back)
  • Too much randomness (undermines learning)

Fix:

  • Simplify patterns (fewer attack types)
  • Clarify telegraphs (longer warnings, clearer visuals)
  • Reduce recovery time (faster respawn)
  • Remove or reduce randomness

Problem: Tension becomes stress

Symptoms:

  • Players anxious rather than engaged
  • Resources too scarce (can't make progress)
  • Close calls become failures (breaking player)
  • Players avoid encounters (not engaging)

Diagnosis:

  • Resource pressure too high
  • No recovery opportunities
  • Consequences too severe
  • Threat density too high

Fix:

  • Increase resource availability
  • Add safe moments (breathing room)
  • Reduce failure consequences
  • Space out encounters

Problem: Mastery becomes solved

Symptoms:

  • One dominant strategy (no diversity)
  • Expert trivializes content
  • No discovery of new approaches
  • Meta stagnates

Diagnosis:

  • Power imbalance (one approach too strong)
  • Lack of viable alternatives
  • System too shallow (optimization space exhausted)

Fix:

  • Nerf dominant strategy
  • Buff underused approaches
  • Add new strategic options
  • Increase system complexity

Problem: Narrative becomes mechanical

Symptoms:

  • Players optimize rather than roleplay
  • Choices feel tactical, not dramatic
  • Story outcomes feel arbitrary
  • Players disengaged from fiction

Diagnosis:

  • Framework too rigid (becoming mechanical)
  • Consequences don't map to fiction
  • Lack of dramatic stakes
  • GM following rules instead of story

Fix:

  • Looser mechanical framework
  • Emphasize fictional positioning
  • Make consequences narrative, not just mechanical
  • Train GM on framework flexibility

Problem: Dynamics not emerging

Symptoms:

  • Measurables way off target
  • Players not behaving as expected
  • Aesthetic not achieved despite parameters

Diagnosis:

  • Wrong paradigm for required dynamics
  • Mechanics don't support intended behaviors
  • Environmental factors preventing dynamics

Fix:

  • Re-examine paradigm choice (might need different approach)
  • Add mechanics that create required behaviors
  • Remove barriers to intended dynamics

Iteration Protocol

1. Identify the problem

  • Which measurable is off target?
  • By how much?
  • Which dynamic isn't emerging?

2. Diagnose root cause

  • Is it parameters (wrong numbers)?
  • Is it mechanics (missing/wrong rules)?
  • Is it paradigm (wrong fundamental approach)?

3. Form hypothesis

  • "If I change X, measurable Y should improve"
  • Specific, testable

4. Make minimal change

  • Adjust one thing at a time
  • Small changes first (10-20%)
  • Large changes if clearly broken

5. Test hypothesis

  • Playtest with same protocol
  • Measure same measurables
  • Verify improvement

6. Iterate

  • If improved but not fixed: Iterate same direction
  • If made worse: Revert and try different approach
  • If fixed: Move to next problem

Common Iteration Mistakes

Mistake 1: Changing too many things at once

Bad:

  • Boss had 1000 HP, did 10 damage
  • Playtested: Too easy
  • Changed to: 5000 HP, 50 damage, added new attack, changed telegraph timing
  • Playtested again: Now too hard
  • Don't know which change caused problem

Good:

  • Boss had 1000 HP, did 10 damage
  • Playtested: Too easy (TTK 30 seconds, target 180)
  • Changed to: 6000 HP (only change)
  • Playtested: TTK 180 seconds ✓
  • Know HP was the issue

Mistake 2: Chasing perfect numbers

Bad:

  • Target TTK: 180 seconds
  • Attempt 1: 175 seconds (close enough!)
  • But iterate again: 178 seconds
  • Iterate again: 179 seconds
  • Iterate again: 180 seconds
  • Wasted time on meaningless precision

Good:

  • Target TTK: 180 seconds (±10% acceptable)
  • Attempt 1: 175 seconds
  • Within acceptable range, move on

Mistake 3: Ignoring dynamics, tuning parameters

Bad:

  • Goal: Challenge aesthetic
  • Problem: Players not learning patterns
  • Solution: Reduce boss HP (make easier)
  • Doesn't fix learning problem, just makes fight shorter

Good:

  • Goal: Challenge aesthetic
  • Problem: Players not learning patterns
  • Diagnosis: Telegraphs too short (can't see patterns)
  • Solution: Increase telegraph timing to 1.5 seconds
  • Fixes actual dynamic problem

Mistake 4: Listening to all feedback equally

Bad:

  • Casual player: "Too hard!"
  • Expert player: "Too easy!"
  • Designer: "I'll make it medium difficulty"
  • Satisfies no one, wrong target audience

Good:

  • Define target audience
  • Weight feedback from target audience higher
  • Expert feedback matters for Mastery aesthetic
  • Casual feedback matters for Submission aesthetic
  • Serve your intended audience

When Balance Is "Done"

Balance is never perfectly done, but you reach "good enough" when:

Measurables in target ranges:

  • Primary measurables within ±15% of targets
  • Secondary measurables not breaking experience

Dynamics emerging reliably:

  • Pattern learning happening (if Consistency)
  • Agency felt (if Framework)
  • Pressure maintained (if Adaptive)

Aesthetics achieved:

  • Players report intended emotional responses

  • 70% of target audience satisfied

  • Observable behaviors match intended dynamics

Iteration hitting diminishing returns:

  • Changes <5% improvement
  • Players not noticing differences
  • Time better spent elsewhere

At this point: Ship it. Further tuning can happen post-launch if needed.

Closing Thoughts

Game balance is not magic. It's not guessing. It's not random parameter tweaking until something works.

Game balance is systematic constraint satisfaction:

  • Your aesthetic requires specific dynamics
  • Your dynamics require specific paradigms
  • Your paradigms require specific measurables
  • Your measurables require specific parameters

The framework in this chapter gives you:

  • Clear decision-making process at each level
  • Traceable reasoning from experience to numbers
  • Validation methodology to test your choices
  • Iteration protocol to fix problems

You still need playtesting. No framework eliminates the need for real players engaging with your game.

But this framework makes playtesting effective:

  • You know what to test (measurables, not vibes)
  • You know what problems mean (which dynamic failed)
  • You know how to fix them (systematic adjustment)
