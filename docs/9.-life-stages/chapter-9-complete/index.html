Chapter 9: Deriving an Inequality Game — A Comparative Design Study

What This Chapter Is For

The previous chapters gave you frameworks: aesthetics, context, resources, cognitive load, temporal architecture, balance. Chapter 8 demonstrated the complete derivation chain by working backward from Rimworld — showing how an existing game's design could be understood as a series of derived decisions.

This chapter does something different. Instead of analyzing an existing game, we'll design a new one from scratch — and more importantly, we'll show how the framework helps a designer choose between competing ideas.

Every designer faces the same problem: too many ideas, not enough clarity about which to pursue. The instinct is to combine everything, to pile on mechanics until the game collapses under its own weight. The framework's value isn't just helping you develop an idea — it's helping you choose which idea to develop, and which to set aside.

We'll work through a complete example: designing a board game that teaches economic inequality. Along the way, we'll encounter multiple viable approaches, use the derivation chain to evaluate them, make explicit tradeoffs, and arrive at a complete, playable design.

The game we produce will be real — printable components, complete rules, ready to play. But the chapter's purpose isn't the game itself. It's demonstrating the process of systematic design choice.

Part 1: The Designer's Problem

The Brief

Someone approaches you with this goal:

"I want to create a board game that teaches economic inequality to teenagers and young adults. Not theoretically — I want players to feel it, to understand it viscerally. I want them to experience what it means when initial advantages compound, when fair rules produce unfair outcomes, when the system is rigged before you make your first choice."

Immediately, ideas multiply.

The Initial Ideas

Idea 1: "Isn't this just Monopoly?"

Monopoly — or rather, The Landlord's Game from which it descended — was literally designed to teach this lesson. Elizabeth Magie created it in 1903 to demonstrate how property ownership concentrates wealth. The game exists. It's culturally ubiquitous. Why not just use it?

Idea 2: "A simple compounding game"

What if we stripped everything to the core mechanism? A game where early advantages directly increase future income. Roll dice, get resources, buy things that give you more resources. Early luck snowballs into late dominance. Simple, clean, focused.

Idea 3: "Life stages"

What if we made the real-world parallel explicit? Each round is a decade of life. Your starting position is your birth circumstances — some players are born wealthy, others poor. Same rules, different starting points. Watch inequality emerge across a lifetime.

Idea 4: "The deep simulation"

What if we went further? Not just showing that inequality compounds, but why systems create it. Players could experience the incentives that lead a ruling class to fund universal literacy but gate higher education. Lobbying mechanics. Policy votes. Social capital versus financial capital. The whole interconnected system.

This fourth idea — let's call it The Long Game — is the most ambitious. It promises to teach not just that inequality exists but why it persists, why those with power rationally act to maintain it. It's intellectually exciting.

It's also probably a trap.

The Instinct to Combine

The natural impulse is synthesis: "What if we combined the life stages framing with the political mechanics and added property like Monopoly and..."

This impulse is almost always wrong.

Each mechanic adds cognitive load. Each system requires learning, tracking, deciding. A game that tries to teach five lessons often teaches none — players are so busy managing complexity that they never feel the underlying patterns.

More importantly, mechanics can work against each other. If players are focused on political maneuvering, they may not notice the compounding economic dynamics. If they're managing property, they may attribute outcomes to territorial strategy rather than structural advantage.

The framework's purpose: Instead of combining until something breaks, we derive what the game needs from its experiential goals. Ideas that don't serve those goals get cut — no matter how clever they seem.

The Alternative: Trial and Error

Without a framework, game design often looks like this:

  1. Pick a promising idea
  2. Build it
  3. Playtest
  4. Something doesn't work
  5. Add a mechanic to fix it
  6. Playtest
  7. Something else doesn't work
  8. Add another mechanic
  9. Eventually abandon or ship a bloated, unfocused design

This can produce good games. Iteration is genuinely valuable. But it's inefficient — often requiring dozens of cycles before finding the design, if ever. Many designers give up. Others ship incoherent games where individual mechanics are interesting but the whole is muddy.

The framework's promise: Fewer iterations to a coherent design. Not zero — playtesting remains essential — but we arrive at playtesting with a design that's structurally sound, needing parameter tuning rather than architectural rework.

Let's demonstrate.

Part 2: The Real Situation

Before we begin the derivation, we need to understand how this game will actually be used. Context isn't just about medium and platform — it's about the social reality of play.

How This Game Reaches Players

A board game about teaching inequality isn't purchased at a store and played repeatedly by enthusiasts. The actual use case:

Someone wants to create an experience for others.

A teacher wants their students to understand inequality. A discussion group organizer wants to spark conversation. A parent wants to reach their teenager. Someone who read about this game — or played it themselves — wants to share that experience with others.

This person becomes the facilitator. They've read the rules. They understand the purpose. They bring the game to a group.

The group knows roughly what they're getting into.

This isn't a mystery. Nobody sits down to a game called "Life Stages" about economic inequality without knowing the general territory. The genre is known. The specific mechanism isn't.

This is like watching a horror movie. You know something scary will happen. You don't know exactly what or when. The knowledge of genre doesn't spoil the experience — it sets appropriate expectations.

The game is played once.

This is a one-shot experience. The lesson lands or it doesn't. There's no "it gets better after a few plays." The design must work the first time, for players who will never play again.

The facilitator knows more than the players.

Asymmetric knowledge is built into the situation. The facilitator has read ahead. They know how inequality will emerge. The players discover it.

This isn't a flaw — it's the design space. The facilitator isn't a game master improvising content. They're someone who set up the experience and guides it to conclusion.

What This Means for Design

One-shot impact: Every element must serve the core lesson. No mechanics that "pay off after multiple plays." No depth that requires experience to appreciate.

Minimal facilitator burden: The facilitator shouldn't need expertise to run the game. Read the rules that morning, explain them clearly, guide the outro. That's it. No improvisation required, no deep knowledge of inequality economics, no ability to handle emotional crises.

Known genre, unknown specifics: We can assume players arrive with "this is about inequality" but design for the discovery of how inequality emerges. The mechanism is the surprise, not the topic.

Customization options: Different facilitators have different contexts. A classroom has different constraints than a game night. We can provide options — clearly labeled, with guidance — and let facilitators choose.

This social reality shapes everything that follows.

Part 3: Level 0 — Establishing Context

Medium: Board Game

This is fixed by the brief. Board games have specific affordances compared to video games (Chapter 8's Rimworld) or TTRPGs:

Affordances:

  • Physical state creates visceral feedback. Your small pile of tokens versus their large pile — visible, tangible, undeniable.
  • Simultaneous visibility. Everyone sees the inequality on the table at once. No hidden screens.
  • Social presence. You watch faces when realization dawns. The wealthy player's comfort. The struggling player's frustration.
  • No technology barriers. Anyone can play with household items.

Constraints:

  • Human-executable rules only. No hidden calculations, no complex simulations.
  • Turn-based or simultaneous phases. No real-time pressure.
  • State must be physically trackable. Everything represented by components.
  • Consistency or Framework paradigm only. No Adaptive paradigm — the game cannot secretly adjust difficulty based on player state.

Platform: Print-and-Play

The goal is maximum accessibility: "anyone can create this at home." This constrains component design significantly:

  • Standard components: Printable cards, household tokens (coins, beans, paper clips), standard dice players already own
  • No elaborate boards requiring professional printing
  • No custom dice, miniatures, or specialty components
  • Rules learnable without video tutorials
  • Reasonable preparation time (under 30 minutes to print and cut)

This constraint helps the design. Elaborate components often mask complexity. Simple components demand elegant mechanics. If the game can't work with index cards and pennies, it's probably too complex.

Player Count: 3-6 Players

We want maximum reach. Analysis of viable range:

2 players: Fundamentally different dynamics. Inequality requires a spectrum, not a binary. Two-player creates direct opposition rather than systemic observation. Excluded.

3 players: Minimum viable. Shows distribution — someone at top, someone at bottom, someone watching from the middle. The middle player's experience may be most instructive.

4 players: Sweet spot. Clear stratification into quartiles. Maps naturally to real-world class discussion. Not so many that turns drag.

5-6 players: Viable with adjustments. More data points for inequality distribution. But longer play time, more components, more facilitator management.

7+ players: Downtime problems emerge. Tracking others' positions becomes cognitively expensive. Excluded for core design.

Design target: 4 players as default, scalable to 3-6.

Session Length: Derived from Audience

Target audiences: Teenagers (13-17) and young adults (18-25)

These audiences have different constraints:

FactorTeenagersYoung Adults
Sustained attention45-90 minutes2-3 hours
Cognitive capacityMidcoreMidcore to Hardcore
Patience for learningLowModerate

The tension: Serving both audiences suggests different designs. A teenager-optimized game might bore adults. An adult-optimized game might overwhelm teenagers.

Resolution: Design for the stricter constraint (teenagers) with optional extension for adults who want more.

Target: 60-75 minute core experience. Clean stopping possible at 60 minutes. Extended play options to 90+ minutes for groups who want more depth.

Archetype: Economic Engine

All four initial ideas share a structural pattern: resource accumulation with compounding effects. This is the economic engine archetype — a game where you acquire things that help you acquire more things.

Monopoly adds spatial movement (the board), but analysis reveals this is primarily randomization delivery. Dice determine where you land; you interact with whatever's there. The spatial relationships between properties rarely matter strategically. You can't choose to "go to Boardwalk" — you arrive by chance.

Decision: Unless spatial mechanics carry specific thematic weight (not just randomization), we exclude them. This significantly reduces components, rules complexity, and play time.

Alternative noted: A "life path" spatial structure — where position represents career track or social mobility — might carry meaning. We'll revisit if derivation calls for it. For now, we proceed with pure economic engine.

Part 4: Level 1 — Aesthetic Profile

Primary Aesthetic: Discovery (Revelatory Variant)

The brief is explicit: players should "feel" inequality, not just observe it intellectually. This points to Discovery aesthetic — but a specific variant.

Standard Discovery: Finding something new. A secret room, a plot twist, a hidden mechanic. "I didn't know this existed!"

Revelatory Discovery: Understanding something known but unfelt. "I knew this intellectually, but now I understand it." The insight comes not from new information but from experiential comprehension of existing information.

Consider the song "Common People" by Pulp. The narrator describes a wealthy woman who wants to experience poverty as aesthetic tourism — "live like common people." The song's insight is that she can never truly understand because she can always "call her dad" and escape. She knows about poverty. She doesn't feel it.

Our game should make that kind of distanced observation impossible. Players should end up unable to treat inequality as abstraction.

Design implications:

  • Minimal thematic framing at start. Don't announce "this is about inequality" — let the mechanics reveal it.
  • Inequality should emerge from apparently fair rules. The discovery is "the rules were the same but the outcomes weren't."
  • The "aha" moment should be felt, not explained. Revelation through play, not instruction.

Secondary Aesthetic: Tension

Visceral understanding requires Tension. Players falling behind should feel the walls closing in. The gap should create anxiety, not mere observation.

But carefully calibrated: Pure Tension without resolution creates suffering, not learning. Players need cognitive space to understand what happened, not just endure it. The Tension must resolve into Discovery.

The arc: mounting Tension through mid-game → crystallization in late game → resolution in the outro. Players feel trapped, then understand why, then articulate it.

Explicitly Excluded: Challenge (Skill-Expression Variant)

This is counterintuitive but essential. Most games want player skill to matter — better decisions should produce better outcomes. Good game design usually creates space for skilled players to excel.

For our teaching goal, this is actively harmful.

If disadvantaged players could catch up through superior strategy, the lesson becomes: "I lost because I played badly." The implication is that better choices would have produced better outcomes. This is exactly the "meritocracy" myth we're trying to puncture.

Design requirement: Optimal play from disadvantaged positions should reliably lose to suboptimal play from advantaged positions.

Players can make decisions, and those decisions do matter at the margins. But the margins aren't big enough. You can play perfectly from behind and still lose. You can play sloppily from ahead and still win. The structure dominates individual choices.

This is the lesson.

Player Profile: Midcore

Both target audiences (teenagers and young adults) fall into midcore cognitive capacity:

  • 5±2 simultaneously tracked elements
  • Moderate decision complexity acceptable
  • Tolerance for rules learning if payoff is clear
  • Need clear state visibility

Cognitive budget: Maximum 5-7 trackable elements per player. Moderate strategic depth. Rules explainable in under 10 minutes.

This budget constrains everything downstream. Every mechanic, every resource, every system competes for these limited slots.

Success Criteria

How do we know the design works? We need observable indicators, not just "players liked it."

Observable during play:

  • Gap between players becomes visually obvious by mid-game (Rounds 3-4)
  • Trailing players express frustration at systemic barriers (not at luck, not at other players)
  • Leading players recognize their advantages weren't "earned" through superior play
  • Players stop trying to catch up and start observing the pattern

Observable in outro:

  • Post-game conversation naturally turns to real-world parallels without facilitator prompting
  • Players articulate "the rules were fair but the outcomes weren't"
  • Wealthy players express discomfort at their unearned success
  • Struggling players express understanding (not just frustration) at their constrained position

Measurable:

  • Players who fall behind cannot catch up even with optimal play (test via simulation)
  • Rules learned in under 10 minutes (test via naive players)
  • Game completes in 60-75 minutes (test via timed sessions)
  • Works with 3-6 players (test each count)

Part 5: Level 2 — Required Dynamics

With aesthetic goals established, we derive what player behaviors must emerge.

For Discovery (Revelatory):

Gradual revelation: Inequality must build over time, not start obvious. Early rounds should feel fair — "we're all playing the same game." Late rounds should feel systemically rigged — "the game was never fair."

Pattern recognition: Players should perceive the compounding mechanism. Not "random stuff happened" but "oh, this advantage feeds that advantage which feeds the next." The system should be learnable, even as it's inescapable.

Moment of crystallization: There should be a point — probably Round 4 of 6 — where trailing players realize they cannot catch up. Not suspect, not fear — know. This is the moment the lesson lands.

Anti-dynamics to prevent:

  • Front-loaded inequality: If the gap is obvious from Round 1, there's no discovery. The lesson is too easy, arrived at without experiential weight.
  • Luck-masking: If players attribute outcomes to dice rolls, they miss the systemic pattern. "I rolled badly" is a different lesson than "the system disadvantages people like me."
  • Blame on decisions: If players think "I should have bought X in Round 2," they're learning the wrong lesson. Individual choices shouldn't appear decisive.

For Tension:

Resource pressure on trailing players: Disadvantaged positions should feel constrained. Fewer options, harder choices, watching opportunities pass because you can't afford them.

Visible gap: The difference between players must be physically obvious. Different pile sizes on the table. Different capabilities clearly displayed. No hiding from the inequality.

Narrowing options: As disadvantage compounds, agency should feel like it's shrinking. Early rounds: "I have choices." Late rounds: "I have no good choices."

Anti-dynamics to prevent:

  • Hopelessness too early: If players mentally check out in Round 2, they miss the full arc. The grind must be long enough to feel systemic, not just unlucky.
  • Kingmaking: Trailing players deciding the winner (through spite or alliance) shifts focus from system to interpersonal dynamics.
  • Grinding: Going through motions when outcome is determined. If the last two rounds feel pointless, the pacing is wrong.

The Core Dynamic: Compounding Advantage

All our candidate ideas require this: early advantages must compound into late dominance. What mechanisms create compounding?

Mechanism 1: Multiplicative returns More resources → more resource generation. "Money makes money." Example: Tools that add dice to future rolls.

Mechanism 2: Threshold access Options only available above certain wealth levels. Gates that only the wealthy can pass. Example: Can't buy education until you have 8 Wealth.

Mechanism 3: Rule asymmetry Rules that technically apply equally but functionally benefit the wealthy. Example: Flat fee of 3 Wealth hurts poor players proportionally more than rich ones.

Mechanism 4: Positional advantage Better position creates better opportunities. The rich have access the poor don't. Example: Investment opportunities only offered to high-wealth players.

Mechanism 5: Social/political capital Connections that convert to material advantage. Power beyond mere wealth. Example: Lobbying to change rules in your favor.

Design question: How many mechanisms do we need?

Each mechanism adds cognitive load and design complexity. A game with one mechanism teaches one clear lesson. A game with five mechanisms might teach five muddled lessons — or overwhelm players before any lesson lands.

The framework forces this tradeoff to be explicit. We don't ask "is this mechanic interesting?" We ask "does this mechanic serve our aesthetic goal, and is it worth its cognitive cost?"

Part 6: Evaluating Candidate Approaches

Now we can systematically evaluate our initial ideas against Levels 0-2 requirements.

Candidate: Monopoly

The appeal: It already exists. It was designed for this purpose. Everyone knows how to play.

Fatal problems:

  1. Session length: Monopoly routinely runs 2-4 hours. You can't just "play fewer rounds" — the game requires time for property accumulation to produce returns. Shortening changes fundamental dynamics.

  2. Luck dominance: Roll-and-move means dice determine your position. Players reasonably attribute outcomes to luck ("I kept landing on your hotels"). The systemic compounding is obscured.

  3. Player elimination: Bankrupt players are removed. They miss the rest of the game — and the lesson. They leave frustrated rather than enlightened.

  4. Negotiation complexity: Property trading introduces social dynamics that obscure systemic patterns. "I lost because nobody would trade with me" is a different lesson than "I lost because the system compounds initial advantages."

Verdict: Monopoly's problems are structural, not parametric. We can't fix them by adjusting numbers. We'd need to replace core mechanics — at which point we're designing a new game anyway.

Lesson for designers: Familiarity isn't fitness. Starting from a broken foundation creates more work than starting fresh.

Candidate: Simple Compounding Game (The Commons)

The concept: Strip everything to the core mechanism. Dice generate resources. Resources buy assets. Assets generate more resources. Early advantages compound into late dominance. No board, no properties, no negotiation — just the engine.

A game called "The Commons" exists in this space: players roll for Labor, buy Tools (more dice) and Land (fixed bonus), compete for Luxury points. Round 1 luck compounds across 5 rounds. Simple, clean, teachable.

Candidate: Life Stages

The concept: Same core mechanics as the simple compounding game, but reframed. Rounds become decades of life. Starting position is explicitly "birth circumstances" — dealt cards that say "Wealthy Family" or "Working Class." The real-world parallel is unavoidable.

What changes from simple compounding:

  1. Thematic frame: "Years of life" instead of abstract "rounds." Each round is a decade — Childhood, Young Adult, Adult, Middle Age, Late Career, Legacy.

  2. Starting conditions explicit: Players don't roll for initial position. They draw Birth Circumstance cards that set starting resources. The inequality is named: "You were born wealthy. You were born poor."

  3. Event texture: Life Events (medical emergencies, education opportunities, economic downturns) replace abstract resource fluctuations. Players see why the same event affects rich and poor differently.

Key improvement over simple compounding:

The Birth Circumstance cards transform how players interpret outcomes. In simple compounding, "I rolled low in Round 1" feels like bad luck. In Life Stages, "I was dealt Working Class" feels like birth circumstances. Same mathematics, different attribution.

This is a Level 6 (Presentation Architecture) change, not a Level 5 (Mechanics) change. The underlying engine is identical. The meaning shifts.

Verdict: Strongest candidate so far. Clear improvement in teaching efficacy with minimal additional complexity.

Candidate: The Long Game (Deep Structural Simulation)

The vision: Teach not just that inequality compounds, but why systems create and maintain it. Players experience the incentives that lead to inequality-preserving policies.

Why does a ruling class fund universal literacy but make higher education expensive? Because literate workers are more productive (benefits everyone, including elites), but highly educated workers compete for elite positions (threatens those currently in power). The rational strategy is "educated enough to be useful, not educated enough to challenge."

Why does lobbying emerge? Because when you have vastly more resources than others, influencing rule-makers becomes more efficient than playing within rules.

What this would require:

  1. Role asymmetry or progression: Some players must occupy "elite" positions to experience elite incentives. Either start asymmetric or allow class mobility.

  2. Multiple parallel systems: Economic capital, social capital, education, political influence — all interacting.

  3. Rule modification mechanics: Voting, lobbying, policy changes that players can influence.

  4. Problem/solution structure: Life challenges with both "normal solution" and "pay-to-skip" options.

  4. Abandon this path

The deeper problem: Which lesson is primary?

The Long Game tries to teach multiple interconnected lessons:

  • Advantages compound
  • Money becomes universal problem-solver
  • Social capital becomes redundant to wealth
  • Wealthy can change rules in their favor
  • Education is strategically gatekept

Each lesson requires mechanics to support it. If we teach all of them, each is diluted. If we focus on one, why build complexity for the others?

It might be worth building. But not here, not now, not for this audience.

Part 7: The Decision

The derivation reveals that our four candidates aren't equally viable — they serve different purposes under different constraints.

The Choice

Life Stages is the clear winner for our stated goals:

  1. Serves the primary audience: Both teenagers and young adults can engage with midcore complexity.

  2. Fits session constraints: 60-75 minutes is achievable.

  3. Maximizes teaching efficacy: Birth Circumstance cards remove luck-attribution. Thematic frame makes parallels unavoidable.

  4. Manageable complexity: Core economic engine plus events fits cognitive budget.

  5. Printable: Cards, tokens, dice — all accessible.

What we're choosing against:

  • Monopoly: Broken foundation, can't be fixed
  • Simple Compounding: Works but Life Stages is strictly better for teaching
  • The Long Game: Different game for different context — worth noting, not worth building here

What This Demonstrates

The framework didn't tell us which game is "best." It clarified what we were choosing between.

Life Stages vs. The Long Game isn't good vs. bad. It's:

  • Breadth vs. depth
  • Accessibility vs. sophistication
  • Clear single lesson vs. complex interconnected lessons
  • Teenagers included vs. adults only

A designer could pursue either. The framework's value is making the tradeoff explicit before investing months of development in the wrong direction.

We now continue the derivation chain for Life Stages.

Part 8: Level 3 — Temporal Architecture

Session Target

Total session: 60-75 minutes

  • Setup: 5 minutes
  • Play: 45-55 minutes
  • Outro: 10-15 minutes

Round Structure

Each round represents a decade of life. Six rounds total:

  1. Childhood (Ages 0-18): Starting conditions established
  2. Young Adult (Ages 18-28): First independent choices
  3. Adult (Ages 28-38): Career establishment
  4. Middle Age (Ages 38-48): Peak earning or falling behind
  5. Late Career (Ages 48-58): Trajectories locked in
  6. Legacy (Ages 58-68): Final positions, what you leave behind

Why six rounds:

  • Enough to show compounding (5 rounds of returns after starting position)
  • Natural mapping to life stages
  • Short enough to complete in session
  • Long enough to feel like a journey

Round Duration

Target: 8-10 minutes per round

PhaseDurationPurpose
Income1 minGenerate resources from assets
Events3-4 minLife circumstances occur
Spending3-4 minPurchase assets or luxuries
Transition1 minAdvance round, brief narration

6 rounds × 9 minutes = 54 minutes of play ✓

Pacing Pattern: Escalating

From Chapter 5: Escalating pacing has intensity increase over time.

This matches our aesthetic requirements:

  • Early rounds feel open, exploratory
  • Middle rounds show gap widening
  • Late rounds feel constrained (for losers) or comfortable (for winners)
  • Tension builds to crystallization around Round 4

The Crystallization Point

Round 4 (Middle Age) is the inflection point.

By Round 4:

  • Wealthy players have 3+ rounds of compounded returns
  • Working class players are visibly behind
  • The gap is obvious to everyone at the table
  • Trailing players realize: "I cannot catch up"

This is where the lesson lands. The remaining rounds confirm what players already know.

Part 9: Level 4 — Balance Paradigm

Paradigm Selection: Consistency

From Chapter 6: Consistency paradigm means rules work the same way every time. Players can learn and predict outcomes.

Why Consistency for Life Stages:

The teaching goal requires that inequality feel systemic, not arbitrary. If outcomes seem random, players attribute results to luck rather than structure. Consistent rules make the pattern visible.

Players should be able to trace the causation: "They started with 3 assets, which generated extra income, which let them buy more assets, which..." The mechanism must be learnable.

The Unusual Balance Requirement

Most games want skill expression — better play produces better outcomes. Life Stages explicitly inverts this.

Target: Optimal play from disadvantaged positions reliably loses to suboptimal play from advantaged positions.

This isn't imbalance — it's precisely calibrated balance to produce the intended lesson.

Measurables:

  1. Win rate by birth circumstance:

    • Wealthy Family: 45-55% (should usually win, not always)
    • Upper Middle: 25-35% (competitive but disadvantaged)
    • Lower Middle: 10-20% (rarely wins)
    • Working Class: 0-5% (almost never wins)

  2. Catch-up rate: Players who fall behind by Round 3 should catch up less than 10% of the time, even with optimal play.

  3. Perceived fairness: Post-game, players should report "rules were fair" while also recognizing "outcomes were unfair."

Why Not Perfectly Deterministic?

If Wealthy Family won 100% of the time, the game feels pointless. Some variance maintains engagement and allows for the occasional upset that proves illuminating.

"Usually the wealthy win" teaches better than "always the wealthy win" because it matches reality and maintains dramatic possibility.

Part 10: Level 5 — Mechanical Specification

Core Loop

Each round follows this structure:

Phase 1: Income All players simultaneously generate Wealth:

  • Base income: 3 Wealth
  • Asset bonus: +1 Wealth per Asset owned

Example: Player with 4 Assets generates 3 + 4 = 7 Wealth

Phase 2: Life Events Draw and resolve one Event card for the table (affects everyone) or per player (individual events). Events create texture and demonstrate how same circumstances affect different wealth levels differently.

Phase 3: Spending In turn order (rotating start player), each player may purchase:

  • Assets (produce future income)
  • Education (efficient asset acquisition)
  • Luxuries (victory points)
  • Emergency Fund (protection from events)

Phase 4: Transition Advance to next round. Optional: Facilitator narrates the transition ("Ten years pass. Families grow. Circumstances change.")

Resource System

Following Chapter 3 guidance, we identify required resource functions:

Currency (creates choices): Wealth

  • The primary spendable resource
  • Generated by income, spent on purchases
  • Does not carry over between rounds (use it or lose it)

Accumulator (compounds over time): Assets

  • Generate Wealth each round
  • Permanent once purchased
  • The compounding mechanism

Tracker (shows progress): Victory Points

  • Purchased as Luxuries
  • Win condition
  • Do not generate further returns (spending on victory vs. spending on growth is the key tradeoff)

Why Wealth Doesn't Carry Over:

This is a crucial design choice. If Wealth accumulated across rounds, players could hoard and make lump purchases later. By forcing "use it or lose it," we ensure:

  • Early asset purchases compound
  • Wealthy players reinvest efficiently
  • Poor players face hard choices every round
  • The gap widens consistently

Starting Conditions: Birth Circumstance Cards

The key mechanical innovation. Instead of rolling for starting position, players are dealt cards.

Part 11: The Event System

Events provide narrative texture and demonstrate how identical circumstances affect different wealth levels differently. They're not the primary teaching mechanism — compounding is — but they create concrete scenarios that resonate with real-world experience.

Event Design Principles

Principle 1: Same event, different impact

Every event should affect wealthy and poor players differently, even when rules are identical. A medical emergency costing 5 Wealth is a nuisance to someone with 15 Wealth and devastating to someone with 6 Wealth.

Principle 2: Choice reveals constraint

Events should create choices that reveal structural constraint. The wealthy player chooses between good options. The poor player chooses between bad ones.

Principle 3: Real-world resonance

Events should evoke recognizable life circumstances: healthcare costs, education opportunities, economic downturns, lucky breaks. The parallel to reality should be unmistakable.

Principle 4: Brief resolution

Events shouldn't bog down play. Quick to resolve, clear in effect, minimal debate needed.

Event Categories

Category 1: Universal Costs Everyone pays the same flat amount. Wealthy shrug it off; poor are devastated.

Category 2: Opportunities Something good is available — for a price. Wealthy can seize it; poor must watch it pass.

Category 3: Crises Something bad happens. Pay to avoid or suffer consequences. Wealthy pay; poor suffer.

Category 4: Windfalls Something good happens to everyone. Wealthy extend their lead; poor can't catch up anyway.

The Event Deck (24 Cards)

Universal Cost Events (6 cards)

"Tax Season" The government needs its share. All players pay 2 Wealth. (If you cannot pay the full amount, pay what you can.)

"Cost of Living Increase" Everything got more expensive this decade. All players pay 3 Wealth. (If you cannot pay the full amount, pay what you can.)

"Infrastructure Assessment" Your community needs new roads and utilities. All players pay 2 Wealth. (If you cannot pay the full amount, pay what you can.)

"Economic Recession" Hard times affect everyone. All players pay 3 Wealth. Assets produce 1 less Wealth next round (minimum 0 per Asset). (If you cannot pay the full amount, pay what you can.)

"Family Obligations" Weddings, funerals, reunions — family costs money. All players pay 2 Wealth. (If you cannot pay the full amount, pay what you can.)

"Professional Expenses" Licenses, certifications, equipment — staying employed isn't free. All players pay 2 Wealth. (If you cannot pay the full amount, pay what you can.)

Opportunity Events (6 cards)

"Investment Opportunity" A promising venture is seeking investors. Any player may pay 6 Wealth to gain 1 Asset. (This is slightly worse than normal Asset purchase — the "opportunity" isn't actually better, it just sounds like one.)

"Education Scholarship" A competitive scholarship is available. The player with the most Assets may pay 4 Wealth to gain 1 Asset. (Only the already-advantaged can access this "scholarship.")

"Real Estate Boom" Property values are rising. Any player may pay 8 Wealth to gain 2 Assets. (Same as Education purchase — accessible only to wealthy.)

"Networking Event" The right connections can change everything. Any player may pay 3 Wealth to look at the top 3 Event cards and rearrange them in any order. (Information advantage for those who can afford it.)

"Entrepreneurship Program" Start your own business — if you have capital. Any player may pay 10 Wealth to gain 2 Assets and 1 Victory Point. (Excellent deal — if you have 10 Wealth to spare.)

"Career Advancement" A promotion is available for those with the right credentials. Any player who has purchased Education this game may pay 4 Wealth to gain 1 Asset. (Rewards previous privilege.)

Crisis Events (8 cards)

"Medical Emergency" Someone in your family needs expensive treatment. Each player must either pay 5 Wealth OR lose 1 Asset. (The wealthy pay cash. The poor lose productive capacity.)

"Legal Trouble" You need a lawyer. Each player must either pay 4 Wealth OR lose 1 Asset. (Justice costs money.)

"Home Repair Crisis" Major repairs can't wait. Each player must either pay 4 Wealth OR lose 1 Asset. (Maintenance costs money. Neglect costs more.)

"Job Loss" Economic restructuring eliminates your position. Each player loses 3 Wealth. Players with 0 Assets lose 1 Victory Point instead (if they have any). (Everyone suffers, but the poor have less buffer.)

"Market Crash" Your investments take a hit. Each player loses 1 Asset (if they have any) OR 5 Wealth (if they have no Assets). (The wealthy lose investments. The poor lose savings.)

"Family Health Crisis" Ongoing care is needed. Each player must pay 2 Wealth now AND 2 Wealth next round (mark with a token). (Recurring costs are devastating to tight budgets.)

"Identity Theft" Someone stole your information. Each player loses 3 Wealth. Players with Emergency Fund may ignore this event. (Insurance helps — if you could afford it.)

"Unexpected Child Expenses" Children are expensive. Each player must pay 3 Wealth OR skip their next spending phase. (The poor can't buy their way out of lost opportunity.)

Windfall Events (4 cards)

"Economic Boom" Good times for everyone. All players gain 2 Wealth. (Sounds equal. In practice, wealthy can invest it while poor use it to survive.)

"Tax Refund" Money back from the government. All players gain 3 Wealth. (Same dynamic — equal gift, unequal impact.)

"Community Support" Your community comes together. All players gain 1 Wealth. The player with the fewest Assets gains an additional 2 Wealth. (A small catch-up mechanism — not enough to change trajectories.)

"Inheritance" A distant relative left you something. All players gain 1 Wealth per Asset they own (minimum 1 Wealth). (The rich inherit more. Obviously.)

Event Distribution

For a 6-round game, use 6 events (one per round).

Recommended distribution:

  • Rounds 1-2: Mix of Windfall and Universal Cost (establishing normalcy)
  • Rounds 3-4: Crisis Events (testing resilience, revealing disparity)
  • Rounds 5-6: Mix of Opportunity and Crisis (wealthy pulling ahead, poor falling behind)

Facilitator guidance:

  • Shuffle all 24 cards and draw 6, OR
  • Curate a specific sequence for desired arc, OR
  • Draw 2 from each category and select 6 for variety

How Events Teach

"Medical Emergency" — The wealthy player says "I'll pay 5" without hesitation. The poor player agonizes, then loses their only Asset. Same rules. Different impact. Everyone sees it happen.

"Education Scholarship" — The wealthy player, already ahead, gets cheaper access to more advantage. The poor player watches. This is the "higher education gate" made tangible.

"Economic Boom" — Everyone gets 2 Wealth. Sounds fair. The wealthy player invests it immediately. The poor player uses it to pay off last round's crisis. The gap widens despite equal treatment.

The events don't need to be explained. Players see the dynamics unfold. The parallel to real life becomes unavoidable.

Part 12: Level 6 — Presentation Architecture

Game Title: Life Stages

The name is descriptive and neutral. It doesn't announce "this is about inequality" — that would undercut discovery. "Life Stages" simply describes the structure: you play through stages of life.

Alternative titles considered:

  • "Head Start" — too revealing of the mechanism
  • "The Gap" — too on-the-nose
  • "Born Lucky" — spoils the lesson
  • "Fair Rules" — ironic, but might confuse

"Life Stages" lets the experience speak for itself.

Information Architecture

Fully visible to all players:

  • Everyone's Birth Circumstance card (inequality is public from the start)
  • Everyone's Asset count (piles of tokens visible)
  • Everyone's Victory Points (Luxury tokens visible)
  • Everyone's approximate Wealth (tokens visible unless deliberately hidden)
  • Current round
  • Event being resolved

Why full visibility:

Hidden information creates suspense but obscures learning. We want players to see the gap growing throughout the game. The tension comes from watching inequality compound, not from surprise reveals.

When the Working Class player sees the Wealthy Family player buying their third Asset while they're still trying to afford their first, the lesson is happening. That requires visibility.

Part 14: The Outro

The outro transforms mechanical outcomes into felt understanding. It's where numbers become lives, where the pattern crystallizes into insight.

Why the Outro Matters

The game teaches through experience: players feel inequality compound across six rounds. But experience alone isn't sufficient. Players need a moment to articulate what happened — to move from "I felt stuck" to "the system made me stuck."

The outro provides that moment without being preachy. It doesn't explain the lesson. It creates conditions for players to discover the lesson themselves.

The Outro Sequence

Step 1: Final Count (1 minute)

All players simultaneously count their final positions:

  • Total Victory Points (Luxuries purchased)
  • Current Wealth
  • Current Assets

Go around the table. Each player announces their totals. Note the winner (most Victory Points).

Keep this factual. No commentary yet. Just numbers.

Step 2: The Eulogies (4-5 minutes)

The facilitator reads (or paraphrases):

"Each of you lived a life. Six decades of choices, challenges, and circumstances. Now we remember those lives — but not our own. You'll speak for someone else.

"Look at the player to your left. In a moment, you'll deliver their eulogy — a brief summary of their life. What happened to them? What did they achieve? What did they struggle with? What do they leave behind?

"Take thirty seconds to think, then we'll go around the table. Start with whoever is eulogizing the winner."

Give players a moment to collect thoughts. Then go around the table. Each player delivers a brief verbal eulogy for the player on their right.

What happens:

The wealthy player hears their life summarized by someone who struggled: "They never faced a challenge they couldn't handle. Every opportunity was within reach. They leave their children wealth and security."

The working class player hears their life summarized by someone who succeeded: "They worked hard every round. They faced the same challenges but couldn't afford the solutions. They leave their children... not much. The same position they started in."

Why cross-eulogies work:

Writing your own eulogy invites self-justification. Writing someone else's requires perspective-taking. The wealthy player must articulate the poor player's struggle. The poor player must articulate the wealthy player's ease.

Step 3: The Final Question (2-3 minutes)

After all eulogies are delivered, the facilitator pauses. Then:

"One last question. Think honestly before you answer.

"What would you have done differently?"

Go around the table. Each player answers.

What happens:

The working class player searches for an answer. "I could have... maybe if I'd saved in Round 2... but then the medical emergency..." They trail off. The honest answer is: "Nothing. I couldn't have changed this."

The wealthy player searches too. "I guess I made some mistakes in Round 4, but it didn't really matter..."

The question assumes agency. The experience denied it. The gap between question and answer is the lesson landing.

Allow silence. When someone says "I don't know" or "Drawn a different card at the start" — that's the moment. Don't fill it with explanation.

Step 4: The Pivot (Optional — 1-2 minutes)

If the group has energy and interest, one more question:

"What would need to change — about the game, not about your play — for your answer to be different?"

This explicitly invites systemic thinking. "What would I have done differently?" becomes "What would need to be different about the rules?"

The answer is: starting conditions. The rules were identical. The start wasn't.

This pivot is optional. For some groups, the first question is sufficient. For others, the pivot crystallizes the insight. Facilitator discretion.

The core game as specified serves the primary teaching goal for the widest audience. But different facilitators have different contexts. This section provides clearly-labeled options with guidance on when to use them.

Option: Compressed Game (4 Rounds)

Use when: Time-constrained sessions (45-50 minutes), younger players with shorter attention spans, when the lesson needs to land faster.

Changes:

  • Rounds represent larger life chunks (Childhood, Early Career, Peak Years, Legacy)
  • Use only 4 Event cards
  • Reduce starting Wealth by ~20% to maintain pressure

Use when: Players are skeptical that "small" differences matter, or you want the lesson to be starker.

Changes: Increase the starting gap:

Trade-off: Less "even small differences compound" and more "large differences are insurmountable." Starker but less subtle.

Option: Income Variance (Dice)

Use when: You want some randomness in income, simulating job market uncertainty.

Changes: Instead of deterministic income (3 + Assets), roll 1d6 and add Assets.

  • Base income: 1d6 (average 3.5)
  • Asset bonus: +1 per Asset

Trade-off: Introduces luck into income, which may cause some players to attribute outcomes to dice rather than structure. Can obscure the lesson. Use cautiously.

Option: Written Eulogies

Use when: More reflective groups, want a takeaway artifact, have extra time.

Changes: Instead of verbal eulogies, players write 3-4 sentences about the player to their left. Then read aloud.

Trade-off: Slower, but creates physical artifact. Some players prefer writing to speaking. Others resist writing tasks.

Option: Self-Eulogies

Use when: Cross-eulogies feel too socially risky for your group.

Changes: Each player writes/speaks their own character's eulogy.

Trade-off: Loses perspective-taking benefit. Players may self-justify rather than articulate systemic reality. Less powerful but safer.

Option: Collective Action Module

Use when: Longer sessions (add 10-15 minutes), players who want agency, groups comfortable with negotiation, facilitator comfortable managing mild conflict.

Do not use when: Time-constrained, first-time facilitators, younger players, when clean "structural trap" lesson is priority.

How it works:

At the start of Round 4 (Middle Age), before the normal phases, a special Collective Action phase occurs.

"The workers may organize."

Eligibility: Players in the bottom half of total Wealth + Assets may participate.

Process:

  1. Proposal: Any eligible player may say "I propose we organize."

  2. Cost: To organize, each participating player must commit 3 Wealth to a "solidarity fund." This is lost regardless of outcome.

  3. Secret commitment: Eligible players secretly decide: Commit (risk 3 Wealth) or Decline. Use face-down tokens or slips of paper.

  4. Wealthy response: Before revealing, wealthy player(s) may offer "reforms" — publicly offer to redistribute some Wealth to the eligible players to prevent organizing.

  5. Reveal: All commitments revealed simultaneously.

  6. Resolution:

    • If ALL eligible players committed AND reforms weren't accepted: Collective action succeeds. Wealthy player(s) lose 1 Asset each. Solidarity fund is lost.
    • If any eligible player declined OR reforms were accepted: Collective action fails. Solidarity fund is still lost by those who committed.

What this teaches:

  • If collective action succeeds: "Organizing works but costs everyone something."
  • If someone defects: "Collective action is fragile — individuals can be scared off or bought off."
  • If wealthy offer reforms: "Those with power offer minimal concessions to prevent structural change."
  • If nobody organizes: "When action seems too risky, people accept unfair systems."

Trade-off: Adds complexity and time. May create interpersonal tension. May let wealthy player feel victimized rather than implicated. May dilute the "trapped by structure" lesson by offering an escape valve.

Warning: This module changes the game's tone significantly. It introduces negotiation, potential conflict, and explicit political framing. Some groups will love this. Others will find it uncomfortable. Know your audience.

Part 16: Complete Rules Reference

This section provides the complete, printable rules for Life Stages.

LIFE STAGES

A Game About the Lives We're Dealt

Players: 3-6 Time: 60-75 minutes Components: Birth Circumstance cards, Event cards, Reference card, tokens for Wealth/Assets/Victory Points, Round tracker

OVERVIEW

Life Stages is a game about living through six decades, from young adulthood to retirement. Players receive different starting positions and navigate identical rules. The player with the most Victory Points at the end wins.

SETUP

  1. Deal Birth Circumstances: Shuffle the Birth Circumstance cards and deal one to each player, face up. Everyone can see everyone's starting position.

  2. Distribute Starting Resources: Each player takes Wealth tokens and Asset tokens as shown on their card.

  3. Prepare Events: Shuffle the Event deck. Draw 6 cards and set them face down in a pile, or let the facilitator curate a set.

  4. Set Round Tracker: Place marker on Round 1 (Childhood/Young Adult).

  5. Designate First Player: Oldest player, random selection, or Working Class player (thematic choice).

ROUND STRUCTURE

Each round represents approximately one decade of life. Play 6 rounds total.

PHASE 1: INCOME

All players simultaneously gain Wealth:

  • Base Income: 3 Wealth
  • Asset Bonus: +1 Wealth per Asset owned

Example: A player with 2 Assets gains 3 + 2 = 5 Wealth.

PHASE 2: EVENT

Draw and reveal one Event card. Read it aloud. All players resolve the Event according to its text. Events may require payments, offer opportunities, or affect Assets.

If a player cannot pay a required cost, they pay what they can. Some Events have alternative consequences for those who can't pay.

PHASE 3: SPENDING

Starting with the First Player and going clockwise, each player may make purchases. A player may make multiple purchases if they can afford them.

PurchaseCostEffect
Asset5 WealthGain 1 Asset (produces +1 Wealth per round)
Education8 WealthGain 2 Assets
Luxury10 WealthGain 1 Victory Point
Emergency Fund3 WealthMay cancel one negative Event effect this game

Players are not required to purchase anything.

PHASE 4: END OF ROUND

  • All unspent Wealth is LOST. Wealth does not carry between rounds.
  • Advance the Round tracker.
  • Pass First Player marker clockwise.
  • If Round 6 is complete, proceed to End Game. Otherwise, begin next round.

END GAME

After Round 6 spending is complete:

  1. Count Victory Points: Each player counts their Luxuries purchased.

  2. Declare Winner: Player with the most Victory Points wins.

  3. Tiebreaker: If tied, the player with more Assets wins. If still tied, shared victory.

  4. Proceed to Outro: See Outro section for the closing sequence.

IMPORTANT RULES

Wealth does not carry over. At the end of each round, unspent Wealth is lost. You must spend it or lose it.

Assets are permanent. Once purchased, Assets remain for the rest of the game, generating income each round.

Events affect everyone (unless specified otherwise). When an Event says "all players," it means all players.

Purchases are optional. Players may choose not to buy anything.

Information is public. All players can see everyone's Wealth, Assets, and Victory Points at all times.

OUTRO SEQUENCE

After the game ends mechanically, the Outro creates space for reflection.

Step 1: Final Count Each player announces their final total: Victory Points, Wealth, Assets.

Step 2: Eulogies Each player delivers a brief verbal eulogy for the player on their left. What happened to them? What did they achieve? What did they struggle with? What do they leave behind?

Start with whoever is eulogizing the winner.

Step 3: The Final Question The facilitator asks: "What would you have done differently?"

Each player answers honestly.

Step 4: (Optional) The Pivot If desired, the facilitator asks: "What would need to change about the game for your answer to be different?"

QUICK REFERENCE

Income: 3 + Assets = Wealth gained

Purchases:

  • Asset: 5 Wealth → +1 future income
  • Education: 8 Wealth → +2 Assets
  • Luxury: 10 Wealth → +1 Victory Point
  • Emergency Fund: 3 Wealth → Cancel one negative Event

Round End: Unspent Wealth is lost.

Win Condition: Most Victory Points after 6 rounds.

Part 18: Validation

Before considering the design complete, we verify it meets our success criteria.

Testable Hypotheses

Hypothesis 1: Inequality emerges visibly by Round 3-4

Test: Observe Asset counts and Wealth levels mid-game.

Success: Wealthy player has 2+ more Assets than Working Class player by Round 4.

If fails: Increase starting gap or reduce Working Class starting resources.

Hypothesis 2: Working Class player cannot catch up with optimal play

Test: Simulate or playtest with Working Class player making optimal choices.

Success: Working Class wins < 5% of games even with perfect play.

If fails: Reduce Working Class starting position or increase purchase costs.

Hypothesis 3: Players attribute outcomes to system, not luck

Test: Post-game interviews. Ask "Why did you win/lose?"

Success: >70% reference starting position, system structure, or Birth Circumstance card.

Failure indicators: References to dice rolls, "bad luck," personal mistakes.

If fails: Ensure deterministic income (remove dice variant); emphasize Birth Circumstance framing.

Hypothesis 4: Rules perceived as fair, outcomes perceived as unfair

Test: Post-game survey: "Were the rules fair?" and "Were the outcomes fair?"

Success: >80% say rules were fair; <30% say outcomes were fair.

If fails: Check if any rules seem to target specific players; ensure identical rules for all.

Hypothesis 5: Session completes in 60-75 minutes

Test: Timed playtests with target audience.

Success: 80%+ of sessions complete in range.

If fails: Reduce rounds (use Compressed variant) or simplify Events.

Hypothesis 6: Rules learned in under 10 minutes

Test: Time from rules explanation start to first round beginning.

Success: 80%+ of groups ready in 10 minutes.

If fails: Simplify presentation; reduce Purchase options; provide better reference cards.

Playtest Protocol

First playtest wave: Designer or close collaborators. Looking for broken mechanics, unclear rules, timing issues.

Second playtest wave: Target audience (teenagers/young adults) with skilled facilitator. Looking for engagement, pacing, comprehension.

Third playtest wave: Target audience with novice facilitator (someone who just read the rules). Looking for facilitator burden, unclear instructions, edge cases.

Fourth playtest wave: Varied contexts (classroom, game night, workshop). Looking for context-specific issues.

Data to collect:

  • Session duration
  • Time to learn rules
  • Asset/Wealth distribution at end of each round
  • Winner by Birth Circumstance
  • Post-game responses to "What would you have done differently?"
  • Any moments of confusion or rules disputes

Known Limitations

The game teaches one lesson clearly. It does not teach:

  • Why inequality exists (only that it compounds)
  • What to do about inequality (only that individual action is insufficient)
  • Historical context for inequality
  • Policy solutions

The game may create discomfort. Players who "lose" may feel frustrated. The outro is designed to transform frustration into understanding, but some players may remain upset. This is partially intentional — the discomfort is pedagogically valuable — but facilitators should be prepared.

The game is one-shot. It doesn't benefit from repeated play. Once you've experienced the lesson, playing again offers diminishing returns. This is by design but limits commercial viability.

The Collective Action module changes the lesson. Adding it shifts from "individual action is insufficient" to "collective action is difficult." Both are true, but they're different lessons. Facilitators should choose intentionally.

Part 19: What We Didn't Build

The Long Game

Our fourth initial idea — the deep structural simulation — remains unbuilt. Here's what it would require:

Core insight: Teach not just that inequality compounds, but why systems produce and maintain it.

Required elements:

  • Role asymmetry (some players are elites, some aren't)
  • Multiple capital types (economic, social, educational, political)
  • Rule modification mechanics (lobbying, voting, policy)
  • Longer session (90-120+ minutes)
  • Higher cognitive load (7-12 trackable elements)

Target audience: Adults comfortable with complexity; university seminars; dedicated workshops.

Why we didn't build it here:

  • Exceeds our session constraint (60-75 minutes)
  • Exceeds teenage cognitive tolerance
  • Requires more facilitator skill
  • Higher design risk (unproven architecture)

The Long Game remains a valid design direction. It would serve different goals for a different audience. A future chapter or separate project could develop it fully.

Other Paths Not Taken

Cooperative variant: All players work together against "the system." Would teach different lesson about collective struggle. Changes competitive dynamics to cooperative dynamics. Interesting but different.

Digital implementation: Computer handles calculations, enables hidden information, allows adaptive difficulty. Could reach broader audience but loses physicality and social observation. Different medium, different affordances.

Campaign mode: Multiple sessions building a longer narrative. Characters persist, society evolves, policies change. Rich but requires ongoing commitment. Different temporal architecture entirely.

These aren't better or worse — they're different designs serving different purposes. The framework helps us choose; it doesn't declare one choice correct.

Part 20: Chapter Summary

What the Framework Provided

Systematic evaluation of candidates. Instead of pursuing each idea until hitting dead ends, we mapped requirements first and evaluated against them. Monopoly's problems were structural, not parametric. The Long Game exceeded our constraints. Simple compounding worked but Life Stages improved on it. We identified this through analysis, not trial and error.

Explicit tradeoffs. Life Stages vs. The Long Game isn't "good vs. bad." It's breadth vs. depth, accessibility vs. sophistication, teenagers vs. adults only. The framework made these tradeoffs visible before we invested development time.

Efficient derivation. From aesthetic goal (revelatory Discovery) to required dynamics (gradual revelation, visible gap) to mechanical specification (Birth Circumstance cards, compounding Assets) to complete playable design — in a structured sequence, not random exploration.

Justified decisions. Every design choice traces back to aesthetic goals:

  • Why no spatial mechanics? They don't serve the teaching goal.
  • Why is Wealth lost each round? To force the rich/poor divide in spending patterns.
  • Why cross-eulogies? To force perspective-taking.
  • Why that final question? Because the answer reveals the lesson.

A playable game. The chapter produces something real — printable, playable, complete. Theory that doesn't produce games is worthless.

The Design: Life Stages

Players: 3-6 Duration: 60-75 minutes Components: Printable cards, household tokens

Premise: Players live through six decades, starting with unequal birth circumstances. Through identical rules, initial advantages compound into systemic inequality.

Teaching goal: Players experience how structural inequality emerges from apparently fair rules, making abstract knowledge visceral.

Core mechanics:

  • Birth Circumstance cards set starting positions (dealt, not rolled)
  • Assets generate compound returns (+1 Wealth/round each)
  • Events affect rich and poor differently despite identical rules
  • Outro creates reflection without preaching

The lesson: The rules were the same for everyone. The outcomes were not.

For Designers

When facing multiple ideas:

  1. Establish context constraints (Level 0) — What's fixed before you design?

  2. Define aesthetic goals and audience (Level 1) — What experience for whom?

  3. Derive required dynamics (Level 2) — What must players do and feel?

  4. Evaluate candidates against requirements — Which ideas serve these needs?

  5. Choose and commit — Make the tradeoffs explicit; pick one path.

  6. Continue derivation through remaining levels — Temporal architecture, balance paradigm, mechanics, presentation, parameters.

  7. Validate — Playtest against measurable hypotheses.

The framework doesn't replace creativity or iteration. It structures the process so creativity is directed and iteration refines rather than discovers. You arrive at playtesting with a design that's structurally sound, needing parameter tuning rather than architectural rework.

The Instinct to Combine

We started with four ideas. The instinct was to combine them: Monopoly's property + Life Stages' framing + The Long Game's politics + ...

This instinct is almost always wrong.

Each mechanic adds cognitive load. Each system requires learning, tracking, deciding. A game that tries to teach five lessons teaches none.

The framework's discipline is choosing. Life Stages teaches one lesson clearly. It does not teach everything about inequality. It teaches that structural advantages compound under fair rules. That's enough. That's more than most games achieve.

Scope is not ambition's enemy. A focused game that delivers one lesson powerfully serves players better than an ambitious game that delivers five lessons weakly.

Conclusion

We began with a brief: teach economic inequality to teenagers and young adults. Make them feel it, not just know it.

We ended with Life Stages: a complete, printable board game that accomplishes this goal within 75 minutes, using only household components, runnable by a facilitator who read the rules that morning.

Along the way, we rejected Monopoly (broken foundation), set aside The Long Game (different audience, different constraints), and evolved from simple compounding to Life Stages (better teaching efficacy through Birth Circumstance framing).

Every decision traced from aesthetic goals through the derivation chain. Nothing is arbitrary. Nothing is "because it seemed fun." Every element serves the experiential purpose.

The game is not the point. The point is the process. Given a teaching goal and constraints, the framework produces a coherent design efficiently. Another designer with different goals would produce a different design — but using the same process.

Chapter 8 showed the derivation chain analyzing an existing game (Rimworld). This chapter showed the chain creating a new game from competing ideas. Chapter 10 will show the chain applied to yet another medium: the tabletop roleplaying game.

The framework flexes. The process holds. The games are different; the thinking is the same.
