History-list

Excellent idea - looking at evolution reveals WHY design choices were made, not just WHAT they are. Let me suggest systems to analyze.

Criteria for Selection

We want systems that:

  1. Have multiple editions/versions (can see evolution)
  2. Serve different purposes (not all tactical combat)
  3. Made significant changes (not just tweaks)
  4. Are well-documented (we can research why changes happened)
  5. Include at least one video game (cross-medium comparison)

Proposed Systems to Analyze

TTRPG Systems

1. D&D (OD&D → 5e)

  • Why: Most documented evolution, reveals industry assumptions
  • Versions: OD&D (1974), AD&D (1977), 2e (1989), 3e (2000), 3.5e (2003), 4e (2008), 5e (2014)
  • Major shifts: Wargame → RPG, systemization (3e), tactical board game (4e), accessibility (5e)
  • What it reveals: How designers react to player behavior, market forces, competing with video games

2. Powered by the Apocalypse (Apocalypse World → many descendants)

  • Why: Intentional design for specific experiences, rapid evolution through descendants
  • Versions: Apocalypse World (2010) → Dungeon World, Monsterhearts, The Sprawl, Masks, etc.
  • Major shifts: Same core engine, radically different implementations for different genres
  • What it reveals: How purpose drives mechanics, modularity vs coherence

3. Call of Cthulhu (1981 → 7e)

  • Why: Horror system, different purpose than D&D, relatively conservative evolution
  • Versions: 1e-7e, Pulp Cthulhu variant
  • Major shifts: Gradual refinement, not revolution
  • What it reveals: When stability serves purpose, incremental improvement

Video Game Systems

4. Civilization (Civ I → Civ VI)

  • Why: Sid Meier's signature series, clear evolution, well-documented design philosophy
  • Versions: Civ I (1991) → Civ II, III, IV, V, VI (2016)
  • Major shifts: Complexity management, UI evolution, multiplayer considerations, "one more turn"
  • What it reveals: Complexity creep vs accessibility, player psychology, 4X strategy evolution

5. XCOM (Original → Enemy Unknown → 2)

  • Why: Tactical combat focus (like D&D), reboot reveals modern design thinking
  • Versions: UFO Defense (1994) → Enemy Unknown (2012) → XCOM 2 (2016)
  • Major shifts: Streamlining complexity, action economy changes, risk/reward tuning
  • What it reveals: When to simplify vs add depth, modernizing old systems

Alternative Suggestions

Instead of/in addition to above:

6. World of Darkness (Old → Chronicles/New)

  • Different purpose (social horror vs dungeon crawl)
  • Radical reimagining between editions
  • Resource management focus (blood, willpower, etc.)

7. FATE (Fudge → FATE Core → Accelerated)

  • Narrative-focused system evolution
  • Shows different design philosophy than D&D
  • Modularity and toolkit approach

8. Dark Souls series (I → III)

  • Video game with clear resource management
  • Difficulty as core design principle
  • Environmental storytelling vs mechanical systems

9. Divinity: Original Sin (I → II)

  • CRPG that's essentially TTRPG digitized
  • Shows what computers can handle that humans can't
  • Environmental interactions, complex state

10. Magic: The Gathering (editions/formats)

  • Not RPG but fascinating system evolution
  • Balance through ban lists vs design
  • Complexity budgets

My Recommended Set (5 systems)

Core Analysis Set:

1. D&D (OD&D → 5e) - Industry standard, most documented 2. Powered by the Apocalypse (AW → descendants) - Purpose-driven design 3. Civilization (I → VI) - Strategy game evolution, Sid Meier philosophy 4. XCOM (Original → Reboot) - Tactical combat modernization 5. FATE (Fudge → Accelerated) - Narrative systems, different philosophy

Why This Set:

Covers different purposes:

  • Tactical combat (D&D, XCOM)
  • Narrative focus (PbtA, FATE)
  • Strategic thinking (Civilization)

Covers evolution types:

  • Gradual refinement (D&D mostly)
  • Radical reimagining (XCOM reboot)
  • Modular adaptation (PbtA descendants)
  • Philosophical shift (FATE evolution)
  • Complexity management (Civilization)

Covers mediums:

  • TTRPGs that rely on human adjudication
  • Video games that computerize everything
  • Systems at the boundary (FATE could be either)

What We'll Analyze For Each

Evolution Questions:

  1. What changed between versions?

    • Mechanics (resolution, resources, pacing)
    • Scope (what's included/excluded)
    • Complexity (more/less rules)
    • Philosophy (design principles)

  2. Why did it change?

    • Player feedback/behavior
    • Market forces
    • Designer philosophy shifts
    • Technology changes (especially video games)
    • Competition/influence from other games

  3. What stayed the same?

    • Core identity
    • Target audience
    • Key experience/purpose
    • Sacred cows (things they won't change)

  4. What does evolution reveal about design?

    • What problems were they solving?
    • What didn't work in previous version?
    • What assumptions were challenged?
    • What trade-offs were made?

Specific Lenses:

For resource management:

  • What resources were added/removed/changed?
  • How did resource complexity evolve?
  • What resources became too fiddly?
  • What resources proved essential?

For system coherence:

  • Did system become more or less unified?
  • What subsystems were integrated or split off?
  • How did modularity change?

For accessibility vs depth:

  • Simpler or more complex?
  • Entry barrier changes?
  • Skill ceiling changes?
  • Teaching/onboarding evolution?

For purpose alignment:

  • Did purpose shift?
  • Did mechanics better serve purpose?
  • Where did purpose and mechanics drift apart?

Analysis Structure

For each system:

Version History Map

  • Timeline of releases
  • Major changes each version
  • Context (what else was happening in gaming)

Deep Dive: 2-3 Key Transitions

Pick the most revealing transitions (e.g., D&D 3e→4e, Civ IV→V)

  • What changed and why
  • What was the design goal?
  • Did it work?
  • Player reception
  • Lessons learned

Pattern Extraction

  • What does this evolution teach us about game design?
  • What mistakes were made?
  • What successes can be replicated?
  • How does LLM change what's possible here?

Cross-System Comparison

After analyzing all systems:

  • Common evolution patterns
  • Different approaches to similar problems
  • Medium-specific constraints (TTRPG vs video game)
  • Universal principles

My Strong Recommendation:

Start with 3 systems:

  1. D&D (OD&D → 3.5e → 4e → 5e) - Focus on 3.5→4e→5e transitions
  2. Civilization (IV → V → VI) - Recent, well-documented, Sid Meier
  3. PbtA (Apocalypse World → 3 descendants) - Shows how purpose drives everything

Why 3 not 5:

  • Deep analysis better than shallow breadth
  • 3 gives us TTRPG standard (D&D), video game (Civ), narrative TTRPG (PbtA)
  • Can add XCOM or FATE later if patterns aren't clear

Then decide: Do we need XCOM (tactical video game) or FATE (narrative mechanics) to complete picture?
