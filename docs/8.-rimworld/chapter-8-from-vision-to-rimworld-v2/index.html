Chapter 8: From Vision to Rimworld

The derivation chain we've built across previous chapters is a tool. Like any tool, understanding it conceptually differs from using it fluently. This chapter walks through a complete derivation from initial vision to playable parameters, demonstrating how each level constrains the next and how creative decisions emerge naturally from systematic thinking.

Our subject is a game resembling Rimworld—the colony survival story generator created by Tynan Sylvester. We're not reverse-engineering Rimworld or claiming this is how it was actually designed. Instead, we're showing that given similar starting inputs, the framework derives toward similar solutions. This validates the chain while revealing the design space around any particular solution.

Along the way, we'll encounter decision points where multiple valid paths exist. We'll explore some alternatives to show the framework doesn't produce singular "correct" answers—it produces coherent answers that trace back to their origins. A designer who makes different choices at key junctures will arrive at different but equally valid destinations.

Let's begin where all derivations begin: with constraints that exist before design decisions.

Level 0: Context

Context encompasses everything fixed before creative work starts. As discussed in Chapter 2, this includes medium, platform, scope, setting, and archetype. These aren't limitations to resent—they're the foundation that makes subsequent decisions meaningful.

Medium and Platform

Medium: Video game

Platform: PC (primary), with potential console ports later

The choice of video game over tabletop or other media has immediate implications. Video games can simulate complex systems continuously. They can track dozens of variables per entity without burdening players with bookkeeping. They can run time forward, creating pressure and urgency. They can also present information visually in ways that would be impossible on paper.

PC as primary platform means we can assume:

  • Complex UI is viable (mouse, keyboard, screen real estate)
  • Sessions can be long (players sit at desks, not on buses)
  • Processing power supports simulation depth
  • Modding community is possible (PC culture expects it)

Scope and Constraints

Team size: Solo developer initially, small team eventually

Development timeline: Long-term, iterative (early access model)

Business model: Premium (single purchase), with potential expansions

Solo development is a profound constraint. It eliminates certain approaches entirely:

  • Authored content at scale (can't write thousands of quests)
  • Voice acting, motion capture, cinematics
  • Hand-crafted levels requiring dedicated level designers
  • Massive asset production

What solo development favors:

  • Systemic design (create rules, not content)
  • Procedural generation (let algorithms create variety)
  • Emergent complexity (simple rules producing complex outcomes)
  • Community involvement (modding extends content without developer effort)

This constraint will echo through every subsequent level. When we face choices between authored and emergent approaches, the answer is almost always emergent.

Setting

Setting: Sci-fi frontier—crash survivors on a distant, hostile planet, far from civilization

Setting is where the abstract becomes tangible. As Chapter 2 discusses, setting can be load-bearing (mechanically essential) or cosmetic (reskinnable without changing play). We should know which before proceeding.

Applying the Space Coffee Test: Could we reskin this to fantasy survivors in a hostile wilderness? Largely yes—the core dynamics of survival, scarcity, and social tension don't require science fiction. But sci-fi provides specific affordances:

  • Technology that can break (maintenance pressure with clear cause)
  • Communication equipment (can be damaged, restored, upgraded)
  • Escape goal with concrete requirements (build ship, not "find magic portal")
  • Mixed tech levels feel natural (crashed high-tech survivors using primitive tools)
  • Factions with different tech levels (tribals, pirates, advanced empires)

Setting is somewhat cosmetic but provides natural vocabulary and intuitive constraints. We'll treat it as chosen rather than derived—a creative decision that accelerates subsequent derivation.

What sci-fi frontier immediately suggests:

  • Isolation (no rescue coming, far from civilization)
  • Hostile environment (the planet itself is dangerous)
  • Technology requiring maintenance (things break, need power, need parts)
  • Strangers thrown together (crash survivors, not established community)
  • Escape as possible goal (return to civilization)
  • Other survivors with varied intentions (not alone on this world)

Archetype

Archetype: Colony Sim + Survival + Emergent Narrative

Chapter 2 introduced archetypes as structural patterns—the mechanical skeleton beneath setting's skin. Our game combines three:

Colony Sim: Manage a group in a persistent location. Build structures, assign work, develop over time. Examples: Dwarf Fortress, Caesar, Banished.

Survival: Harsh environment, resource scarcity, threat of death. Stay alive against pressure. Examples: Don't Starve, The Long Dark, Frostpunk.

Emergent Narrative: Systems produce stories without authorship. Characters with internal states create drama through interaction. Examples: The Sims, Crusader Kings, Dwarf Fortress.

The combination is unusual. Colony sims typically emphasize optimization and growth. Survival games emphasize resource management and threat response. We're adding Emergent Narrative as the primary experiential goal—the other two archetypes serve it.

This hierarchy matters. We're not making a colony sim that happens to generate stories. We're making a story generator that uses colony sim and survival mechanics as its engine.

Terminology Note

With setting and archetype established, we can adopt clear language. Instead of "persistent distinguishable entities," we can say "colonists." Instead of "constructed shelter locations," we can say "base" or "colony." Instead of "external hostile agents," we can say "raiders" or "hostile factions."

This isn't just convenience—it's cognitive efficiency. Abstract language creates distance. Setting-appropriate language engages intuition. When we say "Rosa is starving," we immediately understand stakes that "Agent 3's hunger resource is depleted" obscures.

Going forward, we'll use:

  • Colonists (or survivors, pawns): The persistent agents players care about
  • Colony (or base, settlement): The place being built and defended
  • Raiders (or pirates, hostiles): External human threats
  • The Rim (or the planet, the world): The hostile environment

With context established, we know what kind of thing we're building and what constraints we're working within. Now we can ask: what experience should it create?

Level 1: Aesthetic Profile

What experience are we creating? Not what mechanics exist or what players do—what do they feel? What do they take away?

Rather than mapping immediately to standard aesthetic categories from Chapter 1, let's articulate the vision in plain language first. Tynan Sylvester has spoken about Rimworld's origins in interviews and talks. He wasn't inspired by playing Dwarf Fortress (he found the interface impenetrable). He was inspired by reading stories people wrote about their Dwarf Fortress experiences—tales like Boatmurdered and Gemclod where emergent events created genuine drama.

The target experience, in his words and ours:

"I want players to have stories worth telling. Stories where characters they care about struggle through adversity, where each playthrough is unique, where the drama feels real because it emerged rather than was scripted."

Decomposing the Target Experience

Attachment—Players treasure specific colonists. When Rosa dies, it hurts. When she survives against odds, it feels earned. She's not "colonist #3"—she's Rosa, the surgeon with a bad back who's afraid of the dark, who saved Tom's life twice and hasn't spoken to Maria since the incident.

Drama—Meaningful events occur. Betrayals, sacrifices, close calls, tragic ironies, unexpected heroism. The raid happens while the doctor is sick. The fire starts in the food storage. The prisoner you recruited becomes your best fighter. Things happen that feel significant.

Emergence—Stories aren't written by anyone. They arise from collision of systems, colonists, and circumstance. The designer didn't script Rosa's sacrifice. The player didn't plan it. It emerged from Rosa's traits, her relationships, the raid timing, and a dozen other factors converging. This makes it feel true in a way authored narrative can't match.

Adversity—Struggle is constant. The Rim pushes back. Survival isn't guaranteed. There's never quite enough food, quite enough medicine, quite enough fighters. Easy victories feel empty; hard-won survival feels meaningful.

Memorability—Sessions produce moments worth recounting. "Let me tell you about the time..." Players remember specific colonists by name years later. They share stories online. They start new colonies because they want new stories, not because they want to optimize better.

What This Is Not

Clarity about what we're not making is equally valuable.

Not primarily Challenge. Players aren't trying to "beat" the game through skill mastery. Difficulty exists to create drama, not to test player ability. Losing can be as satisfying as winning if it makes a good story.

Not primarily Mastery. Deep system knowledge isn't required for the core experience. Min-maxers will find depth, but casual players can enjoy stories without understanding every mechanic.

Not primarily Progression. There's no power curve that's the point. No leveling from 1 to 60. Colonists improve, colonies grow, but this serves story potential, not progression satisfaction.

Not authored Narrative. No writer scripted these events. No dialogue trees, no branching paths, no "save the princess" quest lines. The game provides possibility space; stories emerge from play.

Player Profile

Investment level: Midcore to Hardcore

Session length: 1-4+ hours

Complexity tolerance: High (willing to learn systems)

Engagement style: Observational and reactive, not twitch or puzzle-solving

This audience will:

  • Accept learning curves if payoff is sufficient
  • Appreciate emergent complexity over authored content
  • Value unique experiences over polished ones
  • Spend hours watching their colony without "accomplishing" anything
  • Tell stories about their games to others

Success Criteria

How do we know if we've achieved the target experience?

  • Players tell stories about their colonies to others
  • Colonist deaths feel significant (players express emotion)
  • No two playthroughs feel the same
  • Players remember specific colonists by name
  • Sessions produce "that time when..." moments
  • Players start new colonies for new stories, not just optimization
  • Community shares stories, screenshots, tales of woe and triumph

With our aesthetic target clear, we can ask: what must actually happen during play to create this experience?

Level 2: Required Dynamics

Dynamics are the bridge between mechanics (rules) and aesthetics (feelings). As Chapter 6 established, players don't experience rules directly—they experience behaviors that emerge when rules engage. Level 2 asks: what behaviors must occur for our aesthetic targets to be hit?

This level also asks how each system function (from Chapter 2) will serve the aesthetic. We'll specify how those functions are implemented at Level 6, but we need to know what role each plays now.

Dynamics for Attachment

What creates attachment? Not game-specific attachment—human attachment generally. We attach to things that are:

  • Distinguishable (not identical to others)
  • Persistent (exist over time)
  • Vulnerable (can be lost)
  • Invested in (we put effort/attention toward them)

This suggests required dynamics:

Individuation—Each colonist must feel distinct. Not "worker #7" but "Rosa, the neurotic surgeon who hates the dark." Players need handles to differentiate and remember.

Investment accumulation—Time spent with a colonist builds attachment. The colonist who survived three raids and lost an arm means more than one who arrived yesterday.

Vulnerability—Colonists can be hurt, suffer, die permanently. Attachment requires stakes. If colonists are replaceable or immortal, no attachment forms.

Legible interiority—Players need windows into what colonists feel, want, fear. Not just "hungry" but moods, relationships, breaking points.

Dynamics for Drama

Drama requires:

  • Change (something different happens)
  • Stakes (the change matters)
  • Causation (the change happened because of something)

Required dynamics:

Consequential state changes—Events happen that matter and persist. The raid that killed Jorge shapes the colony forever.

Causal chains—Events happen because of prior states. Rosa broke down because Maria insulted her because they've hated each other since Maria let Rosa's husband die on the operating table.

Conjunction—Systems collide at inopportune moments. The raid happens while the doctor is sick. The fire starts in the food storage. Drama requires unfortunate (or fortunate) timing.

Dynamics for Emergence

Emergence requires:

  • Multiple interacting systems (not one monolithic system)
  • Non-predetermined outcomes (designer didn't script what happens)
  • Player-interpretable patterns (not just noise)

Required dynamics:

System interaction—Multiple systems affect each other. Weather affects crops affects food affects mood affects behavior. Isolated systems don't produce emergence.

Autonomous agents—Colonists make decisions based on their own states, not just player commands. They refuse tasks, break down, fall in love, pick fights. The story partly writes itself.

Legibility—Players can reconstruct why things happened. Emergence they can't understand feels arbitrary. "Of course the pyromaniac started a fire during the siege" is emergent. "Rocks fell, everyone died" is just random.

Dynamics for Adversity

Adversity requires:

  • Pressure (something pushing against player goals)
  • Scarcity (not enough of something needed)
  • Possibility of failure (pressure can win)
  • Possibility of success (pressure can be overcome)

Required dynamics:

Resource pressure—Never quite enough of something. Food, medicine, materials, safety, time.

External threats—The world attacks. Raiders, wildlife, weather, disease. The colony doesn't fail from boredom but from assault.

Degradation—Things break, wear out, run low. Entropy is constant. Just maintaining equilibrium requires effort.

Recovery possibility—Adversity must be survivable (sometimes). Pure doom isn't struggle, it's inevitability. Players need hope.

Dynamics for Memorability

Memorable experiences have:

  • Peaks (some moments more significant than others)
  • Specificity (this particular thing happened)
  • Narrative shape (situation, complication, outcome)

Required dynamics:

Variable intensity—Not everything equally important. Some events matter more, structuring memory around peaks.

Character-centered events—Memorable moments involve someone. "The time Rosa crawled back with both legs broken" not "the time resources were low."

Narrative coherence—Events chain into something story-shaped. Beginning, middle, end. Or at least: situation, complication, outcome.

System Function Requirements

How will each system function serve our aesthetic?

Adjudication: Clear and traceable. Players need to know why things happened—not to optimize, but to understand the story. "Rosa died because she tried to rescue Tom and got caught in the crossfire" requires clear cause-and-effect. Hidden calculations are fine; hidden causation breaks emergence legibility.

Information Management: Rich but not overwhelming. Players need windows into colonist internal states (mood, relationships, memories) to understand characters. But information should invite observation, not demand spreadsheet management. Show behavioral cues, allow inspection of detail.

Content Generation: Procedural and combinatorial. Events emerge from system interaction, not authored scripts. Content pools (traits, backstories, event types) multiply into unique situations. The designer creates ingredients; emergence creates meals.

State Persistence: Comprehensive. Consequences must persist—injuries, relationships, deaths, construction, history. The colony accumulates story. Agent memories should exist and fade naturally, affecting mood and behavior.

Feedback: Emotionally weighted. Deaths should land harder than task completions. Feedback should guide emotional response without dictating it. Let players feel what they feel.

Cognitive Framing: Agents as characters, not units. UI language, visual presentation, and information architecture should consistently frame colonists as people with inner lives, not resources to optimize.

Critical Derivation: What Do We Attach To?

We've said attachment requires "persistent distinguishable entities." But what should these entities be? The choice isn't obvious.

Options:

Individual characters—Humans, creatures, personalities. Attachment through names, traits, history. Examples: The Sims, XCOM soldiers, Rimworld colonists.

A place—Base, city, territory. Attachment through building, customizing, history. Examples: SimCity, Minecraft bases, Dwarf Fortress fortresses.

A lineage—Generations, dynasty, continuity through time. Attachment through legacy, inherited traits. Examples: Crusader Kings, Rogue Legacy.

A system—Ecosystem, economy, machine. Attachment through understanding, tuning, watching it work. Examples: Factorio, SimLife.

Evaluating against "stories worth telling":

Stories need specificity. "My city got big" is less tellable than "My surgeon killed my best fighter on the operating table because she was drunk."

Humans have specialized neural architecture for modeling other minds. We evolved to predict what other humans want, feel, will do. This is why we cry when fictional characters die, feel tension when they're in danger, tell stories about people doing things.

We can care about places, lineages, and systems—but that attachment is usually mediated through people. "The city where my grandmother lived." "The dynasty my favorite king founded." The emotional weight traces back to individuals.

Derived: The primary attachment entities are individual colonists with internal states.

Places matter (the colony they built together), but their significance derives from the people who built them and what happened there.

Critical Derivation: Do Colonists Need Autonomy?

If colonists are fully player-controlled (player gives commands, colonists execute exactly), what happens?

  • All actions are player decisions
  • Outcomes are predictable (player chose them)
  • "Emergence" is just player plans playing out
  • Stories are player-authored, not system-generated

For our target experience—stories that emerged rather than were scripted—this fails. The player becomes sole author. Rosa's sacrifice means less if the player explicitly ordered it.

Additionally, consider cognitive load. Managing 3-7 colonists with direct control means constant attention: tracking what each is doing, deciding what each should do next, issuing commands, monitoring execution, correcting when circumstances change.

This is viable for short sessions in games where control is the point (RTS games, turn-based tactics). For 1-4 hour sessions where the point is watching stories unfold, it's exhausting. Players would be too busy commanding to observe, reflect, or appreciate.

Derived: Colonists must have autonomy. They act on their own states. Player sets priorities and direction, colonists interpret and execute. Player can intervene at key moments but doesn't micromanage.

This creates interesting space: Player wants Rosa to haul food. Rosa is exhausted and heartbroken. Rosa collapses instead. Player must adapt to who colonists actually are.

Synthesized System Requirements

From our dynamics analysis, the game needs:

  1. Individual colonists with persistent, distinct identities (traits, skills, relationships, history)
  2. Internal states that affect colonist behavior (needs, mood, relationships)
  3. Colonist autonomy—they act on their states, not just player commands
  4. Multiple interacting systems whose combinations aren't predetermined
  5. Pressure that creates scarcity and threatens what players value
  6. Permanent consequences—death, injury, loss persist and shape future
  7. Variable intensity—not constant pressure, not constant calm
  8. Legible causation—players can understand why things happened

Level 3: Temporal Architecture

When do dynamics occur? At what rhythm? For how long? Temporal architecture structures time across multiple scales.

Defining Story

Before structuring time, we need clarity on what we're structuring time for. Our target is "stories worth telling." What makes something a story versus a sequence of events?

Minimum story structure:

  • Situation: A state of affairs
  • Complication: Something changes, creates tension
  • Resolution: The complication is addressed (successfully or not)

"Rosa was hungry. She found food. She ate."—barely a story.

"Rosa was starving. The only food was across the frozen river. She tried to cross and fell through the ice. She nearly drowned but dragged herself out. She got the food but lost two fingers to frostbite."—a story.

The difference: stakes, obstacle, cost, character persistence. For our systems to generate stories, they must generate this pattern—not author it, but create conditions where situation → complication → resolution emerges.

Sources of Complications

Complications arise when current approach stops working:

  • Depletion: Resource exhausts, state degrades past threshold
  • Intrusion: Something external arrives
  • Eruption: Internal state crosses threshold (colonist breaks down, relationship snaps)
  • Collision: Systems interact badly (fire starts during siege)

These are all state transitions that create pressure. They emerge from systems, not authored content.

Loop Scales

Following the temporal architecture framework from Chapter 5:

Micro loops (5-30 seconds): Individual colonist actions, small state changes. Rosa walks to stockpile, picks up food, walks to kitchen, cooks meal. Player observes, occasionally adjusts. Low intensity, ambient. The simulation ticking along.

Meso loops (10-30 minutes): Episodes: complication → tension → resolution. A raid arrives and is repelled. A disease spreads and is contained. A colonist breaks down and is comforted. These are complete small stories. Drama lives here.

Macro loops (1-4 hours): Sessions: multiple episodes forming a chapter. The player sits down, plays through several meso events, reaches natural stopping point with some closure. "This session, we survived first winter, lost Tom, Rosa became our best fighter, ended with uneasy stability."

Meta loops (days-weeks): The full story of this colony across multiple sessions. From crash landing to eventual fate—escape, thriving, or extinction. This is where long-term attachment lives.

Deriving Temporal Constraints

For attachment: Attachment requires investment time. Players need 30-60+ minutes with a colonist before loss becomes meaningful. This means colonists must persist across multiple meso loops. Early death before attachment forms should be rare.

For drama: Drama requires rhythm. Major events every 20-45 minutes, not constant or absent. Minor complications more frequently (every 5-10 minutes). Calm periods between for recovery, investment, anticipation.

For emergence: Systems need different timescales. Fast (moment-to-moment actions), medium (needs accumulating over minutes), slow (relationships over hours). Emergence happens when timescales intersect—the slow-building resentment explodes during a fast crisis.

For memorability: Sessions need 2-4 peak moments surrounded by lower intensity. Peaks might last 5-15 minutes. If everything is equally intense, nothing is memorable.

The Pacing Problem

Pure emergence doesn't guarantee good rhythm. If complications arise only from system states:

  • Complications could cluster (multiple crises hitting simultaneously = overwhelming)
  • Complications could drought (extended calm = boring)
  • No guaranteed structure (some sessions might have no peaks)

Players might quit during boring droughts or exhausting clusters. The target experience requires reliable rhythm even though content is emergent.

Options evaluated:

Pure emergence (no regulation): Maximum emergence, but risks terrible rhythm. Player might have four raids in an hour or nothing for two hours.

Designed cycles (seasons, day/night): Creates some rhythm but doesn't adapt to player state. Struggling colony gets same pressure as thriving one.

Random event injection: Breaks monotony but feels arbitrary. Events happen to the simulation, not from it.

Threshold triggers ("when wealth > X, attract threats"): Logical but gameable. Players learn to stay poor.

Adaptive regulation: Meta-system observes play state, modulates when complications emerge. Ensures rhythm without authoring content.

The adaptive approach best serves our needs. It maintains emergence (doesn't author what happens) while ensuring rhythm (regulates when pressure arrives).

The AI Storyteller

This derivation naturally produces the concept of an AI Storyteller—a meta-system that:

Observes:

  • Time since last significant complication
  • Current pressure level (active threats, unmet needs, mood states)
  • Recent colonist deaths or major losses
  • Colony stability indicators (wealth, population, defenses)

Modulates:

  • Probability of external complications arriving
  • Intensity of complications when they occur
  • Recovery window length after major events
  • Whether potential complications are triggered or deferred

Critically: The Storyteller doesn't create content. It creates conditions for complications to emerge from existing systems. It might speed up resource depletion, increase chance of mood breaks, make raiders more likely to notice a wealthy colony. The what remains emergent. The when is regulated.

This concept wasn't assumed from Rimworld—it was derived from requirements. Any story generator with emergent content and target rhythm will likely need something similar.

Storyteller Variants

If the Storyteller targets "good story rhythm," different rhythm preferences create different experiences. This suggests variants:

Variant axes:

  • Trajectory: Does pressure rise, stay flat, wave, or decline over time?
  • Variance: Is pacing predictable, moderate, or chaotic?
  • Recovery: Are breaks between crises harsh, moderate, or generous?

Stopping Points

Good stopping points need:

  • Immediate crisis resolved
  • State simplified (not mid-complex-situation)
  • Progress persisted (nothing lost by stopping)
  • Clear situation to return to

Natural stopping points should occur every 20-40 minutes, aligned with meso loop (episode) completion. Sessions end as complete chapters.

Story Endings

Does the meta-story have an ending? Options:

  • Endless: Play until you stop or everyone dies
  • Goal-oriented: Escape the planet, build the ship
  • Organic: Story ends when it ends

Our design: Organic with optional goal. Escape exists as possible objective for players wanting narrative shape. But sandbox play is equally valid. The story ends when the colony falls, escapes, or the player decides this story is complete.

Level 4: Balance Paradigm

How does the game maintain appropriate challenge, fairness, and engagement? Chapter 6 introduced three paradigms: Consistency, Framework, and Adaptive. Which serves our target experience?

What Needs Balancing

Our balance concerns aren't traditional:

  • Not "is option A as powerful as option B"
  • Not "is PvP fair"
  • Not "is difficulty appropriate to skill level"

Our concerns:

  • Story rhythm (peaks and valleys, not flat or chaotic)
  • Pressure calibration (hard enough for adversity, not hopeless)
  • Attachment protection (colonists survive long enough to matter)
  • Recovery possibility (setbacks can be overcome)
  • Session sustainability (playable for hours without exhaustion)

Paradigm Evaluation

Consistency (fixed rules, player learns patterns):

Pros: Learnable, fair, emergence is pure (no hidden hand) Cons: Doesn't adapt to player state, can't ensure rhythm, experts trivialize while newcomers drown

Verdict: Essential for simulation but insufficient for pacing.

Framework (human judgment fills gaps):

Requires GM or adjudicator. We're making a video game.

Verdict: Doesn't apply to our medium.

Adaptive (system adjusts to maintain target experience):

Pros: Ensures rhythm, adapts to player state, maintains drama across skill levels Cons: "Invisible hand" might feel manipulative if visible

Verdict: Required for pacing regulation.

Hybrid Paradigm

We need both:

Consistency for simulation: Fire spreads according to fire rules. Cold hurts according to cold rules. Hunger grows according to hunger rules. Always. Player can learn these rules and plan around them. This is essential for emergence—if rules were arbitrary, outcomes would feel random, not emergent.

Adaptive for pacing: The Storyteller modulates when pressure arrives without changing what rules govern it. A wealthy colony attracts raiders not because the rules change, but because the Storyteller makes raiders more likely to arrive.

The player experiences consistent simulation with well-paced drama. They can learn how fire works and plan around it. They can't predict exactly when the next raid arrives, but they know roughly what rhythm to expect from their chosen storyteller variant.

Paradigm Boundaries

Critical constraint: The adaptive layer must not violate simulation consistency.

Valid Storyteller actions:

  • Adjust probability of external events
  • Modulate intensity of events that occur
  • Control timing of potential complications
  • Provide longer/shorter recovery windows

Invalid Storyteller actions:

  • Change how fire spreads
  • Make colonists more/less hungry than rules specify
  • Alter combat damage calculations
  • Override colonist behavior rules

The line: Storyteller affects when and whether things happen. Never how things work once they're happening.

Level 5: Mechanical Specification

Before committing to specific mechanics, we explore alternatives. Then we specify.

Level 5a: Mechanical Alternatives

Given our paradigm, what different mechanical approaches could serve our aesthetic?

Alternative A: "The Long Return"

Emphasis: Fixed crew, escape goal, technology-focused

  • Starting crew has pre-crash relationships and history
  • Defined goal: repair ship or signal rescue
  • Macro structure is project progress toward escape
  • Technology repair and maintenance central

Evaluation:

  • Attachment: Strong (know characters from start)
  • Drama: Strong (will we escape?)
  • Emergence: Reduced (arc is predetermined)
  • Replayability: Reduced (same goal, similar structure)
  • Solo dev viable: Yes

Alternative B: "Strange World"

Emphasis: Alien discovery, learning an unknown planet

  • Planet is truly alien with unknown rules
  • Core loop: explore → discover → adapt
  • Drama from discovery and adaptation
  • Attachment partially to world itself

Evaluation:

  • Emergence: High (unknown rules create surprise)
  • Replayability: High (different discoveries)
  • Attachment: Split (world competes with characters)
  • Solo dev viable: No (requires massive alien content)

Alternative C: "Endless Frontier"

Emphasis: Sandbox, emergent character drama, optional endpoints

  • Random colonists, anyone can arrive or die
  • No required goal (escape optional)
  • Story emerges from character interactions and external events
  • Each playthrough is unique cast with unique story

Evaluation:

  • Attachment: Strong (characters are sole focus)
  • Emergence: High (no predetermined arc)
  • Replayability: High (always different)
  • Solo dev viable: Yes (systems over content)

Level 5b: Mechanical Specification

Now we specify concrete systems.

The Colonist System:

Each colonist has:

ComponentContents
BodyHealth, needs (hunger, rest, temperature), physical capabilities, persistent injuries
MindMood (aggregate of factors), stress threshold, memories, permanent traits
RelationshipsOpinion of each other colonist (-100 to +100), relationship type, shared history
CapabilitiesSkills, knowledge, equipment

Colonist Autonomy:

Colonists make decisions based on:

Need priority: When hunger crosses threshold, colonist prioritizes food. When exhaustion peaks, colonist seeks rest. Player sets work priorities, but critical needs override.

Mood-based behavior: Low mood increases chance of breakdowns (berserk, catatonic, fleeing, etc.). High mood enables positive interactions. Mood emerges from needs met, relationships, environment, memories.

Trait-based variation: Traits modify responses. "Lazy" colonist resists work. "Brave" colonist won't flee combat. "Pessimist" has lower mood baseline. Creates individuation—same situation, different responses.

Relationship-driven interaction: Colonists seek out friends, avoid rivals. Proximity + low mood + negative relationship = conflict risk. Bonds affect willingness to rescue, share, help.

Environment System:

ComponentFunction
ResourcesFood, materials, components, medicine, power—gathered, processed, consumed
ConditionsWeather, temperature, seasons, day/night
SpaceTerrain to build on, explore, defend
TimeDay/night cycle, seasons, continuous progression

Threat System:

Environmental threats: Weather extremes (cold snaps, heat waves), fire, disease, resource depletion.

External agent threats: Raiders (human enemies, various factions), wildlife (predators, manhunters), mechanoids.

Internal threats: Colonist breakdowns, relationship conflicts (fights, sabotage), accidents.

Threats follow consistent rules. Their arrival timing is modulated by Storyteller.

Work/Production System:

Work types: Gather, build, craft, farm, tend, cook, heal, fight, haul, research, clean.

Player role: Designate what should be done (mark trees, zone construction), set priority levels per work type per colonist, assign colonists to roles.

Colonist execution: Find highest-priority available task they can do, execute based on skill (speed, quality), interrupt for needs, crises, or higher priorities.

Colony Structure:

Derived from interdependence requirement:

  • Colonists share resources (common stockpiles)
  • Colonists share space (common rooms, defenses)
  • Colonists share fate (raid affects everyone)

The colony is: place + interdependent colonists + shared resources + accumulated history.

Storyteller System:

Observes: Colony wealth, population, time since last major event, current pressure level, recent deaths/losses.

Modulates: External threat probability (raids, manhunters), environmental event probability (weather, disease), opportunity probability (traders, wanderers), event intensity, recovery windows.

Variant parameters: Pressure trajectory over time, variance bounds, recovery generosity, intensity scaling with wealth.

Level 6: Presentation Architecture

How the game communicates is as designable as what the game does. This level implements the system function requirements we established at Level 2.

UI is the game. The same mechanics can produce radically different player experiences based on how they're presented. A colony simulation with full information transparency creates a different game than the same simulation with limited visibility. Decisions made here can shift your viable audience, change which dynamics actually emerge, and determine whether your aesthetic goals are achieved.

Adjudication: How Outcomes Are Determined and Communicated

Task success: Skill-based probability modified by health, mood, tools, environment.

Skill LevelSimple TaskHard Task
0 (untrained)70%10%
10 (skilled)98%60%
20 (master)99%95%

Failure consequences: Wasted materials, lower quality output, treatment failure, accidents.

Combat: Base 50% hit chance, modified by skill (+3%/level), distance, cover, movement.

Quality tiers: Awful, Poor, Normal, Good, Excellent, Masterwork. Determined by skill + mood + randomness.

Communication: Outcomes are clear and traceable. Player can understand why Rosa died, why the surgery failed, why the fire spread. Not through visible probability percentages during play, but through readable cause-and-effect after the fact.

Information Management: What Players Know and When

Always visible:

  • Colonist locations and basic states (mood icon, health indicator)
  • Resource stockpile levels
  • Structure status
  • Current weather, time, season

Revealed through interaction:

  • Colonist deep states (click to see full mood breakdown, memories, relationships)
  • Unexplored map areas (fog of war until explored)
  • Detailed relationship information

Warning system:

ThreatWarning TimeHow
Raid (on foot)1-2 minutesMap edge detection
Raid (drop pod)10-20 secondsBrief warning
Weather event1-3 minutesSky changes, notification
Colonist breakdown10-30 secondsMood warning

Hidden:

  • Storyteller internals (current tension, next event timing)
  • Exact future events
  • Precise probability calculations

Event log: Persistent record of major events for narrative reconstruction. "Rosa had a mental break. Final straw: Ate without table."

Colonist memory: Visible list of memories affecting mood, duration until fading. Creates character history players can read.

Content Generation: What Fills the Systems

Event taxonomy:

  • External threats (raids, manhunters, sieges)
  • Environmental (weather, disease, infestation)
  • Opportunity (traders, wanderers, cargo pods)
  • Internal (breakdowns, social fights, romances)

Colonist generation:

  • Random name from pool
  • Random backstory (childhood, adulthood)
  • 2-4 traits from pool
  • Skills derived from backstory + randomness
  • Random appearance

Traits pool (examples): Hard worker, Lazy, Optimist, Pessimist, Brave, Coward, Socialite, Loner, Pyromaniac, Teetotaler, Gourmand, Psychopath, Kind...

50+ traits creates millions of combinations. Individuation emerges from combination.

World generation: Procedural terrain, resource distribution, faction placement, wildlife appropriate to biome.

State Persistence: What the Game Remembers

Everything relevant persists:

  • All colonist stats (health, skills, relationships, memories)
  • All structures and modifications
  • All stored resources
  • Faction relationships
  • Time and season
  • World state

Colonist memory persistence: Memories have duration. Most fade (1-60 days). Some permanent (death of close relationship, major trauma).

Save system: Full state saved, can resume exactly.

Feedback: How the Game Signals What's Happening

Visual feedback:

  • Mood icons above colonists
  • Health bars when damaged
  • Task labels/animations
  • Construction progress visualization
  • Weather particles and sky color
  • Threat indicators (red flash, distinct appearance)

Audio feedback:

  • Task completion sounds
  • Combat sounds (hits, misses, impacts)
  • Alert sounds (threat, breakdown warning)
  • Ambient audio (weather, time of day)
  • Colonist pain/death sounds

Notifications:

PriorityEventsPresentation
CriticalDeath, raid, firePause option, flash, popup
ImportantBreakdown, major eventSound, popup
NormalTask completion, minorLog only

Emotional weighting: Deaths get more prominent feedback than task completions. The game signals what matters through feedback intensity.

Cognitive Framing: How Players Learn and Understand

Onboarding:

  • Crash landing narrative frame
  • Guided first tasks (cut trees, build shelter)
  • Gentle first events (squirrel attack, not raid)
  • Complexity revealed progressively

UI hierarchy:

  • Always visible: selected entity panel, key resources, time, alerts
  • One click: colonist list, work priorities, research
  • Two clicks: full colonist details, faction relations, statistics

Role framing:

  • Language: "Your colonists will..." not "Command your units"
  • Colonists presented as characters (names prominent, personalities visible)
  • Stories framed as emergent ("The story that unfolds...")

Autonomy legibility: Colonist panel shows current priority ("Eating—hungry"), refusal reasons ("Won't do: Cleaning"), breakdown causes ("Final straw: Ate without table").

Alternative Presentations: The Same Mechanics, Different Games

The specification above represents one set of choices. Different presentation choices would create different experiences with identical mechanics.

Chapter 2 introduced Build/Suggest/Omit as a framework for worldbuilding decisions—what to show explicitly, what to imply, what to leave out entirely. The same principle applies to presentation architecture. Information can be Built (shown explicitly), Suggested (implied through behavior or context), or Omitted (hidden entirely).

Alternative: "The Opaque Colony"

Same mechanics. Different information management and feedback.

AspectStandardOpaque
Colonist moodIcon + number + breakdownBehavior only (moping, smiling, withdrawn)
Colonist healthBar + injury listVisible wounds, limping, bandages
RelationshipsOpinion numbersObservation (who eats together, who avoids whom)
NeedsBarsBehavior (going to food, yawning, shivering)
Event notificationPopup announcementMust notice yourself

This alternative Suggests internal states through behavior rather than Building them explicitly in UI. Players must watch colonists like real people. "Rosa seems off lately" rather than "Rosa: 23 mood."

Effect: Deeper engagement for invested players, higher barrier for casual players. Same systems, different experience.

Alternative: "The Dark Colony"

Same mechanics. Radically different information architecture.

Core change: Player only knows what colonists collectively know.

Vision: Each colonist has vision range. Player sees only what at least one colonist currently sees. Unexplored areas are black. Previously visited areas fade to memory (greyed, potentially outdated).

Communication range: Colonists within colony radius share perception. Beyond range, colonist is alone. If they're incapacitated, player loses that viewpoint entirely.

The silence: When a colonist goes beyond range and doesn't return—did they die? Are they captured? Are they lost? You don't know until someone finds out.

This alternative Omits information no colonist perceives. It's a much more aggressive use of information restriction.

Effect: Genuine uncertainty. Expeditions are scary. "Rosa went hunting three days ago and hasn't come back" is a story in itself. Much narrower audience—horror-adjacent experience.

Coherence Check

Before proceeding to parameters, verify the design hangs together.

Do presentation choices serve Level 1 player profile?

Level 1: Midcore-hardcore, 1-4 hour sessions, complexity-tolerant.

Our specification: Information is available but requires some inspection. Autonomy legibility helps players understand colonist behavior. Progressive disclosure manages initial complexity. UI hierarchy prioritizes common actions.

Assessment: Coherent. Midcore players can engage; hardcore players have depth.

Do presentation choices serve Level 1 aesthetic targets?

Attachment: Colonist memory system, trait-based personality, backstories—supported. Drama: Event notifications, combat feedback, death presentation—supported. Emergence: Hidden storyteller internals preserve surprise—supported. Memorability: Event log, persistent consequences—supported.

Assessment: Coherent. All aesthetic targets have supporting presentation architecture.

Cognitive load check:

Tracking: 3-5 colonists × visible states + resources + threats = 6-10 simultaneous trackables.

For hardcore: Within budget. For midcore: High end. Mitigation: Agent autonomy, alerts, progressive disclosure.

Assessment: Acceptable with mitigations.

Audience shift check:

The alternative presentations (Opaque Colony, Dark Colony) would shift viable audience toward hardcore/niche. If we chose those paths, we'd need to revisit Level 1 and acknowledge the change. Our standard specification maintains the midcore-hardcore target.

Coherence confirmed. Proceed to parameters.

What We Didn't Cover

A complete specification would require additional derivation for:

Technology progression: What research exists, what it unlocks, how long each takes, dependencies. This would follow from asking "what capabilities should unlock over time and why"—serving pacing (new options keep long games fresh), gating (some content is late-game), and goal structure (escape requires advanced tech).

Building specifics: Complete list of structures, their functions, costs, placement rules. This would follow from asking "what spatial configurations serve our dynamics"—sleeping needs beds, eating needs tables, defense needs walls.

Job assignment mechanics: Exactly how priority systems work, schedule details, restriction options. This would follow from asking "how much control does player need while maintaining colonist autonomy."

Faction system: Other groups in the world, reputation mechanics, diplomacy. This would follow from asking "what external social dynamics create interesting complications."

Specific event content: The actual events in each category, their requirements, their effects. This would follow from asking "what specific complications serve our episode structure."

Each of these could be derived through the same chain: what does our aesthetic require, what dynamics serve it, what mechanics create those dynamics, what presentation communicates those mechanics, what parameters tune those mechanics. We've focused on core systems to demonstrate the method; extension follows the same pattern.

What This Example Teaches Us

The Chain Works

Starting from "I want players to have stories worth telling about characters they care about," following the derivation chain produced a game closely resembling Rimworld. This isn't because we reverse-engineered Rimworld—it's because we started from similar inputs and followed the logic.

This validates the framework: given inputs, derivation produces outputs. Different inputs would produce different outputs. The chain isn't circular or arbitrary.

Constraints Enable Creativity

Notice how often constraints clarified decisions:

  • "Solo developer" eliminated authored content approaches
  • "1-4 hour sessions" required sustainable pacing
  • "Stories worth telling" demanded characters over systems
  • "Emergent" required autonomy over control

Constraints aren't limits on creativity—they're what makes decision-making possible. Without constraints, everything is equally valid and nothing is indicated.

Alternatives Exist at Every Level

At each level, we noted other paths:

  • Level 5a: Three different mechanical approaches
  • Level 6: Three different presentation architectures
  • Level 4: Different balance paradigms considered
  • Level 3: Different pacing approaches evaluated

The framework doesn't produce "the" answer. It produces "an" answer that traces back through justified decisions. Another designer making different choices at key points would arrive somewhere else—equally valid, differently suited.

Creativity Lives in the Derivation

The framework might seem mechanical, but creativity pervades it:

  • Choosing which aesthetic targets to prioritize
  • Deciding what dynamics serve those targets
  • Envisioning how mechanics create dynamics
  • Balancing trade-offs between approaches

The chain provides structure for creative work, not replacement for it. It ensures creative decisions connect to each other and to experiential goals. It doesn't make the decisions for you.

Presentation Architecture Matters

It would be tempting to think game design is mainly about mechanics (Level 5). But our exploration showed:

  • Level 6 (Presentation Architecture) dramatically affects player experience
  • Same mechanics can produce vastly different games through presentation choices
  • Information architecture is a design decision as important as mechanics

"What players know and how they learn it" isn't an afterthought—it's fundamental to the experience they have. UI is the game.

Iteration Is Expected

We didn't arrive at the final design in one pass. The derivation revealed:

  • Autonomy requirement emerged from thinking through attachment and cognitive load
  • Storyteller emerged from thinking through pacing requirements
  • Coherence check caught potential audience drift

Real design involves revisiting earlier levels when later levels reveal problems. The chain provides structure for iteration: you know which level to revisit based on what kind of problem you've found.

Conclusion

We've walked through a complete derivation, from "stories worth telling" to specific parameters for need rates and damage values. Along the way, we derived concepts like the AI Storyteller from requirements rather than assuming them, explored alternatives at key decision points, and saw how Level 6 choices can fundamentally reshape player experience.

The result resembles Rimworld because we started from Rimworld's inputs. A designer starting from "competitive tactical challenge" or "meditative building flow" or "horror survival" would follow the same chain to very different destinations.

The framework doesn't design games for you. It ensures that your designs cohere—that your mechanics serve your dynamics, your dynamics serve your aesthetics, your presentation serves your mechanics, your parameters serve your temporal targets. It makes the invisible connections visible and the implicit choices explicit.

Whether you're building a colony sim, a roguelike, a puzzle game, or something that doesn't exist yet, the chain applies. Start with what experience you want to create. Derive what must happen to create it. Derive when it happens and at what rhythm. Derive how the game maintains engagement. Derive concrete systems. Derive how they communicate to players. Derive specific numbers.

And when something isn't working, trace it back up the chain to find where the derivation went wrong.
