# Context Design Validation Framework
## A Playtesting Guide for Designers

**Purpose:** This framework helps you test whether your context design decisions actually create the experiences you intended. It provides concrete metrics, observation protocols, and diagnostic tools to validate archetype, setting, perspective, and detail decisions through playtesting.

**Who this is for:** Designers who have made context design decisions (following Chapter 2's framework) and need to validate those decisions before full production.

**What you'll get:** Specific tests, metrics, and questions that reveal whether your context is working as intended—whether it's space coffee or load-bearing design.

---

## Part 1: Pre-Playtest Preparation

Before testing, document your context design intentions clearly. This creates the baseline against which you'll measure reality.

### Document Your Context Hypothesis

Complete this template for your game:

**Game Title:** _________________

**Primary Aesthetic Goal:** _________________
- What experience should players have?
- What should players FEEL?

**Secondary Aesthetic Goals:** _________________

**Chosen Archetype:** _________________
- Investigation / Tactical Combat / Survival Horror / Roguelike / 4X Strategy / Cozy Simulation / etc.
- Why this archetype serves your aesthetic

**Chosen Setting:** _________________
- Gothic horror / Sci-fi / Fantasy / Noir / Cyberpunk / etc.
- Why this setting reinforces your aesthetic

**Chosen Medium:** _________________
- Video game / TTRPG / Board game / Mobile
- Why this medium enables your archetype

**Defined Perspective:** _________________
- The emotional lens through which players interpret everything
- Horror: "Unknowable threat" / Mystery: "Solvable puzzle" / Cozy: "Safe and pleasant" / etc.

**Space Coffee Test Prediction:** _________________
- What integration level do you expect? (1-5)
- Which systems should be load-bearing vs. cosmetic?

**Build/Suggest/Omit Strategy:** _________________
- What gets built explicitly?
- What gets suggested through hints?
- What gets deliberately omitted?

**Expected Player Behavior:** _________________
- How should context shape player decisions?
- What should players prioritize because of context?
- What dynamics should emerge from contextual framing?

**Success Criteria:** _________________
- How will you know context is working?
- What observable behaviors indicate success?
- What would indicate failure?

**Example - Completed Template:**

**Game Title:** Shadows of Ashcroft Manor

**Primary Aesthetic Goal:** Tension (adrenaline, high stakes, psychological pressure)

**Secondary Aesthetic Goals:** Discovery (uncovering secrets), Narrative (unfolding story)

**Chosen Archetype:** Investigation (gothic horror investigation)
- Why: Investigation naturally creates Discovery aesthetic through clue-finding
- Combined with horror elements creates Tension through threat and vulnerability

**Chosen Setting:** Gothic horror (Victorian mansion, supernatural elements, isolation)
- Why: Gothic setting naturally reinforces Tension through atmosphere
- Familiar conventions help players understand threat without extensive exposition

**Chosen Medium:** Video game
- Why: Enables atmospheric presentation, state tracking, branching investigation paths
- Can automate complex clue relationships

**Defined Perspective:** "Vulnerable investigators facing unknowable supernatural threat"
- Players are mortal, threatened
- Supernatural elements mysterious and dangerous
- Deaths serious and consequential
- No comedy, no sympathetic framing of threat

**Space Coffee Test Prediction:** Level 4 (Generative)
- Investigation mechanics emerge from gothic setting (discovering house history, supernatural clues)
- Could not be easily reskinned to sci-fi or modern without losing core mechanics
- Setting generates specific types of clues and threats

**Build/Suggest/Omit Strategy:**
- Build: House layout, visible clues, investigation rules, threat capabilities
- Suggest: House history, previous inhabitants' fates, supernatural nature
- Omit: Complete explanation of supernatural force, full backstory of mansion

**Expected Player Behavior:**
- Cautious exploration (Tension from threat)
- Deliberate clue gathering (Discovery through investigation)
- Theory formation about house history (Narrative engagement)
- Risk assessment before entering dangerous areas
- Resource conservation (limited light sources, health)

**Success Criteria:**
- Players move cautiously, check corners, express nervousness
- Players discuss clues and form theories about what happened
- Players express dread when encountering supernatural elements
- Players make risk/reward decisions about investigating dangerous areas
- Players respect the threat (don't treat as comedy)
- Players care about uncovering the mystery

## Part 2: Observation Protocol During Playtesting

### General Observation Guidelines

**DO:**
- ✅ Watch how players interpret elements (do they take context seriously?)
- ✅ Note emotional responses to contextual elements (fear, curiosity, humor, boredom)
- ✅ Record when players reference context in decisions ("The threat is too dangerous to face")
- ✅ Listen for spontaneous comments about setting/theme ("This place is creepy", "I don't trust this NPC")
- ✅ Observe whether context shapes behavior (caution in horror, aggression in power fantasy)

**DON'T:**
- ❌ Interrupt to explain lore (note confusion, fix through better presentation)
- ❌ Ask leading questions ("Isn't this scary?" tells them how to feel)
- ❌ Defend context choices during play (you're testing, not justifying)
- ❌ Only watch for intended responses (unintended responses reveal problems)

### Context-Specific Observation Checklist

**For ARCHETYPE Validation:**

□ **Archetype Recognition:** Do players understand what type of game this is?
- Failure signal: Players confused about what they're supposed to do
- Success signal: Players articulate game type correctly ("This is an investigation game")

□ **Required Mechanics Present:** Does archetype include necessary mechanics?
- Investigation needs: Evidence gathering, clue analysis, theory formation
- Tactical combat needs: Positioning, action economy, resource optimization
- Survival horror needs: Threat avoidance, resource scarcity, vulnerability
- Failure signal: Players say "How do I [archetype-required action]?"
- Success signal: Players use archetype-appropriate mechanics naturally

□ **Archetype-Aesthetic Alignment:** Does archetype create intended aesthetic?
- Failure signal: Players experiencing wrong emotion (humor in horror game)
- Success signal: Players expressing intended aesthetic ("This is tense", "I feel clever solving this")

□ **Behavioral Emergence:** Are archetype-appropriate dynamics emerging?
- Investigation: Players forming theories, debating clues, testing hypotheses
- Tactical: Players analyzing positions, optimizing turns, adapting strategies
- Horror: Players moving cautiously, conserving resources, expressing fear
- Cozy: Players relaxing, exploring at own pace, expressing contentment
- Failure signal: Wrong behaviors (aggressive play in horror, cautious play in power fantasy)
- Success signal: Context-appropriate behaviors without prompting

**Observation Prompt Questions (ask AFTER play):**
- "What kind of game would you say this is?"
- "What were you trying to accomplish during play?"
- "How did the game want you to approach challenges?"
- "Did the game structure make sense for what it was trying to do?"

**For SETTING Validation:**

□ **Setting Recognition:** Do players understand where/when this takes place?
- Failure signal: Players confused about basic setting facts
- Success signal: Players describe setting correctly without prompting

□ **Aesthetic Reinforcement:** Does setting amplify intended aesthetic?
- Failure signal: Setting contradicts tone (comedic setting in horror game)
- Success signal: Players comment on how setting enhances experience

□ **Cosmetic vs. Load-Bearing Test:** Could setting be reskinned?
- Test: Ask "Could this be [different setting] without changing?"
- If players say YES easily: Setting is cosmetic (is this intentional?)
- If players say NO: Setting is load-bearing (good for most games)

□ **Setting Consistency:** Is setting coherent across all elements?
- Failure signal: Elements feel out of place ("Why is there modern tech in medieval fantasy?")
- Success signal: Everything feels like it belongs

□ **Recognition Without Over-Explanation:** Do players grasp setting without exposition dumps?
- Failure signal: Players need extensive explanation to understand basics
- Success signal: Environmental storytelling conveys setting effectively

**Observation Prompt Questions:**
- "Where/when did you feel like this story was taking place?"
- "Did the setting make sense for what you were doing?"
- "Were there any elements that felt out of place?"
- "If this was [different setting], would it feel the same?"

**For PERSPECTIVE Validation:**

□ **Perspective Maintenance:** Is emotional lens consistent?
- Horror: Do players treat threat seriously? Or laugh at it?
- Mystery: Do players believe puzzle is solvable? Or feel it's arbitrary?
- Cozy: Do players feel safe? Or stressed?
- Challenge: Do players see difficulty as fair? Or unfair?
- Failure signal: Players interpret elements through wrong lens (comedy in horror)
- Success signal: All elements interpreted through intended lens

□ **No Perspective Breaks:** Are there elements that violate perspective?
- Failure signal: Players laugh when they should be scared
- Failure signal: Players frustrated when they should be challenged
- Failure signal: Players stressed when they should be relaxed
- Success signal: Emotional consistency throughout

□ **Perspective Over Realism:** Do "realistic" elements serve perspective?
- Test: When players say "But realistically...", does it enhance or break perspective?
- Failure signal: Realism breaks intended emotion (pratfall alien in horror)
- Success signal: Realism serves perspective (alien learning behavior increases threat)

□ **Element Filtering:** Do all elements pass perspective test?
- For each major element, ask: Does this serve perspective?
- Failure signal: Some elements work against intended feeling
- Success signal: Everything reinforces same perspective

**Observation During Play:**
- Horror perspective: Watch for fear responses (hesitation, caution, nervousness)
- Mystery perspective: Watch for puzzle engagement (theory formation, clue discussion)
- Cozy perspective: Watch for relaxation (casual pace, experimentation, contentment)
- Challenge perspective: Watch for skill engagement (learning, adaptation, satisfaction)

**Observation Prompt Questions:**
- "What was the overall feeling you got from this game?"
- "Were there any moments that felt tonally inconsistent?"
- "Did anything break the mood or feeling?"
- "What emotion were you experiencing most of the time?"

**For BUILD/SUGGEST/OMIT Validation:**

□ **Build: Necessary Information Provided:** Do players have what they need?
- Failure signal: Players can't make informed decisions due to missing information
- Failure signal: Players constantly ask "How does [mechanic] work?"
- Success signal: Players understand core mechanics and rules

□ **Suggest: Engagement Through Mystery:** Does suggested content create interest?
- Failure signal: Players ignore hints, don't form theories
- Failure signal: Players frustrated by vagueness ("Just tell me!")
- Success signal: Players discuss implications, form theories, express curiosity

□ **Omit: Strategic Restraint Working:** Does omission serve experience?
- Failure signal: Players need omitted information to proceed
- Failure signal: Players feel information missing arbitrarily
- Success signal: Mystery enhances engagement (horror) or reduces complexity (cozy)

□ **Ratio Appropriate for Archetype:** Does Build/Suggest/Omit match archetype needs?
- Investigation: High Suggest working? Players forming theories from hints?
- Tactical: High Build working? Players understand all relevant tactical information?
- Horror: High Omit working? Unknown creating fear, not frustration?
- Cozy: High Build + Low Suggest working? Clarity without mystery or confusion?

□ **No Over-Explanation:** Is anything explained that shouldn't be?
- Failure signal: Players bored by exposition dumps
- Failure signal: Mystery killed by complete explanation
- Success signal: Players appreciate restraint, enjoy theorizing

□ **No Under-Explanation:** Is critical information missing?
- Failure signal: Players stuck because don't know how to proceed
- Failure signal: Players feel puzzle is arbitrary, not solvable
- Success signal: Players have sufficient information to strategize

**Observation Prompt Questions:**
- "Was anything confusing or unclear?"
- "Were there mysteries you enjoyed theorizing about?"
- "Did you feel like you had enough information to make decisions?"
- "Was anything over-explained or boring?"
- "Were there things you wished were explained more?"

**For SPACE COFFEE TEST Validation:**

□ **Integration Level Check:** Test each major system
- Could this system work in completely different setting?
- Does setting create unique mechanics here?
- Would removing setting break this system?

□ **Load-Bearing vs. Cosmetic:** Which systems are which?
- Level 1-2 (Cosmetic): Is this intentional? Is this appropriate?
- Level 3 (Constrained): Do constraints create interesting problems?
- Level 4-5 (Generative/Inseparable): Does setting generate unique gameplay?

□ **Player Perception of Integration:** Do players notice setting matters?
- Failure signal (when high integration intended): "This could be any setting"
- Success signal: "This wouldn't work in [other setting]"

**Test During Play:**
- Mentally reskin game to different setting
- What breaks? What stays identical?
- Document which systems are load-bearing vs. cosmetic

**Observation Prompt Questions:**
- "Could you imagine this game in a different setting? Like [suggest alternative]?"
- "What would change if it was [different setting]?"
- "Does the setting matter to how you play?"
- "What would break if we changed the setting?"

## Part 3: Aesthetic-Specific Validation Metrics

Different aesthetics require different context validation approaches.

### TENSION AESTHETIC Validation

**Behavioral Signals (observe during play):**

✅ **Success Signals:**
- Players move cautiously, checking surroundings
- Players express nervousness or dread
- Players make risk/reward decisions ("Is it worth going in there?")
- Players conserve resources (anticipating danger)
- Players discuss threat and survival strategies
- Players show physical tension (leaning forward, gripping controller)

❌ **Failure Signals:**
- Players rush through carelessly (no threat perceived)
- Players laugh at threat (comedy perspective, not horror)
- Players express boredom (not engaged)
- Players say "This isn't scary" or "I'm not worried"
- Players never discuss risk or danger

**Validation Questions:**
- "How did you feel while playing?"
- "What were you worried about?"
- "Were there moments you felt unsafe?"
- "Did you ever feel genuinely threatened?"

**Diagnostic Tests:**
□ Threat presence: Do players acknowledge danger exists?
□ Vulnerability: Do players feel they could fail/die?
□ Caution: Do players behave conservatively?
□ No comedy: Do players take everything seriously?

### DISCOVERY AESTHETIC Validation

**Behavioral Signals:**

✅ **Success Signals:**
- Players actively investigate environment
- Players form theories about mysteries
- Players discuss clues and connections
- Players express satisfaction at revelations
- Players say "I wonder..." or "Maybe this means..."
- Players document findings (taking notes)

❌ **Failure Signals:**
- Players ignore optional content
- Players don't discuss clues
- Players confused about what they've learned
- Players say "I don't understand what's going on"
- Players express frustration at mysteries

**Validation Questions:**
- "What did you learn or discover?"
- "What theories did you form?"
- "What are you still curious about?"
- "Did investigating feel rewarding?"

**Diagnostic Tests:**
□ Mystery engagement: Do players care about secrets?
□ Theory formation: Do players form hypotheses from clues?
□ Revelation satisfaction: Do discoveries feel rewarding?
□ Appropriate restraint: Is mystery maintained or over-explained?

### SUBMISSION AESTHETIC Validation

**Behavioral Signals:**

✅ **Success Signals:**
- Players appear relaxed, not stressed
- Players experiment without fear of failure
- Players progress at comfortable pace
- Players express contentment or calm
- Players multitask (play while doing other things)
- Players say "This is relaxing" or "This is chill"

❌ **Failure Signals:**
- Players express stress or anxiety
- Players worried about making mistakes
- Players feel pressured by time or difficulty
- Players say "This is stressful" or "I'm worried"
- Players can't relax into flow state

**Validation Questions:**
- "How did this game make you feel?"
- "Were you able to relax?"
- "Did you ever feel stressed or pressured?"
- "Could you play this to wind down?"

**Diagnostic Tests:**
□ No stress: Players never express anxiety?
□ Forgiveness: Mistakes don't feel costly?
□ Comfortable pacing: No pressure or urgency?
□ Pleasant atmosphere: Setting reinforces calm?

### CHALLENGE AESTHETIC Validation

**Behavioral Signals:**

✅ **Success Signals:**
- Players analyze situations carefully
- Players learn from failures
- Players adapt strategies
- Players express satisfaction at victory
- Players say "Let me try again" after failure
- Players discuss optimal approaches

❌ **Failure Signals:**
- Players give up in frustration
- Players say difficulty is "unfair"
- Players succeed without effort (too easy)
- Players don't see patterns to learn
- Players express anger, not determination

**Validation Questions:**
- "Did difficulty feel fair?"
- "Could you see why you failed?"
- "Did you feel you improved over time?"
- "Did victory feel earned?"

**Diagnostic Tests:**
□ Fair rules: Players understand why they fail?
□ Skill growth: Players improve with practice?
□ Pattern recognition: Challenge is learnable?
□ Earned victory: Success feels satisfying?

### MASTERY AESTHETIC Validation

**Behavioral Signals:**

✅ **Success Signals:**
- Players discuss optimal strategies
- Players experiment with builds/compositions
- Players analyze systems deeply
- Players express desire to replay/optimize
- Players compare different approaches
- Players discover synergies and interactions

❌ **Failure Signals:**
- Players find dominant strategy immediately
- Players say "There's only one way to play"
- Players uninterested in optimization
- Systems too shallow for deep engagement
- Players exhaust content quickly

**Validation Questions:**
- "What strategies did you try?"
- "Did you discover interesting combinations?"
- "Do you want to try different approaches?"
- "How deep do the systems feel?"

**Diagnostic Tests:**
□ Depth perception: Players see optimization space?
□ Synergy discovery: Players find combinations?
□ Build diversity: Multiple viable approaches?
□ Replayability: Players want to master systems?

### NARRATIVE AESTHETIC Validation

**Behavioral Signals:**

✅ **Success Signals:**
- Players care about story outcomes
- Players discuss character motivations
- Players make choices based on narrative not mechanics
- Players express emotional investment
- Players talk about story after playing
- Players remember character names and events

❌ **Failure Signals:**
- Players skip dialogue/cutscenes
- Players don't remember story beats
- Players make choices based on mechanics only
- Players say story is "boring" or "in the way"
- Players express no emotional connection

**Validation Questions:**
- "What did you think of the story?"
- "Which characters did you care about?"
- "Did your choices feel meaningful?"
- "What moments stood out emotionally?"

**Diagnostic Tests:**
□ Character investment: Players care about characters?
□ Story engagement: Players follow narrative?
□ Choice meaning: Decisions feel consequential?
□ Emotional response: Story creates feelings?

## Part 4: Integration Validation

Context doesn't exist in isolation—it must integrate with mechanics, resources, balance, and pacing.

### Context-Mechanics Integration Check

□ **Do mechanics serve archetype?**
- Investigation archetype has clue-gathering mechanics?
- Tactical archetype has positioning/action-economy mechanics?
- Survival horror has resource-scarcity/threat-avoidance mechanics?
- Failure: Mechanics don't match archetype (space coffee warning)

□ **Does context frame mechanics meaningfully?**
- Same mechanic, different context = different meaning?
- "Depleting buffer" feels different as HP vs. Sanity vs. Social Standing?
- Failure: Context is just paint on generic mechanics

□ **Do mechanics emerge from setting?**
- Level 4-5 integration: Mechanics wouldn't exist without setting?
- Level 3 integration: Setting constrains mechanics interestingly?
- Level 1-2 integration: Mechanics ignore setting (is this intentional?)

**Test:** Remove setting mentally. Do mechanics still make sense? If YES to everything, you have space coffee.

### Context-Resources Integration Check

□ **Are resources appropriate for archetype?**
- Investigation: Evidence trackers, investigation points?
- Tactical: Action points, positioning resources?
- Horror: Scarce limiters (ammo, health, saves)?
- Cozy: Abundant currencies, collection trackers?
- Failure: Resource types don't match archetype needs

□ **Does scarcity match aesthetic?**
- Tension aesthetic: Scarcity creates pressure?
- Submission aesthetic: Abundance prevents stress?
- Challenge aesthetic: Strategic scarcity creates decisions?
- Failure: Scarcity level contradicts intended emotion

□ **Does context frame resource meaning?**
- Same resource type feels different based on context?
- HP in combat vs. Sanity in horror vs. Reputation in social game?
- Failure: Resources feel generic regardless of context

**Cross-reference:** Chapter 3 (Resources) validation framework for detailed resource testing.

### Context-Cognitive Load Integration Check

□ **Does archetype complexity match target audience?**
- Complex archetypes (4X, grand strategy) for hardcore players?
- Simple archetypes (cozy simulation, idle) for casual players?
- Failure: Complexity mismatch for audience

□ **Does Build/Suggest/Omit manage cognitive load?**
- Omitting complexity for casual games?
- Building all tactical information for challenge games?
- Suggesting mysteries without overwhelming?
- Failure: Information density wrong for archetype

□ **Does setting add or reduce cognitive load?**
- Familiar settings reduce load (players know conventions)?
- Original settings increase load (must learn everything)?
- Is this appropriate for target audience?

**Cross-reference:** Chapter 4 (Cognitive Load) validation framework for detailed complexity testing.

### Context-Pacing Integration Check

□ **Does archetype create appropriate pacing?**
- Horror: Slow, tense pacing?
- Action: Fast, aggressive pacing?
- Cozy: Player-controlled pacing?
- Investigation: Deliberate, thoughtful pacing?
- Failure: Pacing contradicts archetype

□ **Does perspective maintain across pacing changes?**
- Horror stays tense during exploration AND encounters?
- Mystery stays investigative during action AND dialogue?
- Failure: Perspective breaks during certain pacing states

□ **Does setting support pacing needs?**
- Isolated settings support slow pacing (horror)?
- Action-oriented settings support fast pacing?
- Comfortable settings support flexible pacing (cozy)?

## Part 5: Common Context Problems and Diagnostic Tools

### Problem 1: Space Coffee (Setting Doesn't Matter)

**Symptoms:**
- Playtesters can easily imagine game in different setting
- Setting feels like paint on generic mechanics
- Setting never mentioned in strategic discussions
- Could reskin completely without changing experience

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- "Could this be [different setting] without changing anything?"
- "Does the setting affect how you play?"
- "What would break if we changed the setting?"

**If answers reveal space coffee:**
- Determine: Is this intentional? (Abstract games, this is fine)
- If NOT intentional: Need to increase integration level
- Options: Add setting-derived mechanics (Level 4) or constrain mechanics with setting (Level 3)

**Fix Validation:**
- Retest with reskin thought experiment
- Should now have clear "NO, setting matters" answer

### Problem 2: Archetype-Aesthetic Mismatch

**Symptoms:**
- Players experiencing wrong emotion
- Wanted tension, getting boredom
- Wanted discovery, getting frustration
- Mechanics don't create intended aesthetic

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- "What experience did you have?"
- "How did this make you feel?"
- Compare to intended aesthetic—match?

**If mismatch confirmed:**
- Determine: Wrong archetype chosen, or wrong implementation?
- Test: Would different archetype serve aesthetic better?
- Options: Switch archetype (major redesign) or fix implementation (adjust perspective, Build/Suggest/Omit)

**Fix Validation:**
- Retest focusing on emotional response
- Should now match intended aesthetic

### Problem 3: Perspective Breaks

**Symptoms:**
- Horror becomes comedy (players laugh at threat)
- Mystery becomes arbitrary (players feel solution is unfair)
- Challenge becomes frustration (players feel game is cheap)
- Cozy becomes stressful (players express anxiety)

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- "Were there any moments that felt tonally inconsistent?"
- "Did anything break the mood?"
- "What made you [laugh/get frustrated/feel stressed]?"

**If perspective breaks identified:**
- Identify: Which specific elements break perspective?
- Test: Remove or reframe those elements
- Validate: Perspective over realism (serve aesthetic, not accuracy)

**Fix Validation:**
- Retest with problem elements removed/reframed
- Players should maintain intended perspective throughout

### Problem 4: Build/Suggest/Omit Imbalance

**Symptoms:**
- Over-Built: Boring exposition dumps, mystery killed
- Under-Built: Players confused, can't strategize
- Wrong ratio for archetype: Investigation too vague, Tactical too mysterious

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- "Was anything confusing?"
- "Was anything over-explained?"
- "Did you have enough information to play?"

**If imbalance confirmed:**
- Determine: What needs to shift category?
- Build more: Critical tactical information, core mechanics
- Suggest more: Optional depth, mysteries, backstory
- Omit more: Non-essential complexity, irrelevant lore

**Fix Validation:**
- Retest information density
- Should now match archetype needs

### Problem 5: Setting-Aesthetic Contradiction

**Symptoms:**
- Setting fights intended aesthetic
- Comedic setting in horror game
- Grimdark setting in cozy game
- Peaceful setting in action game

**Diagnostic Questions:**
- "Did the setting match the experience?"
- "Was anything about the setting jarring?"

**If contradiction confirmed:**
- Options: Change setting to match aesthetic, or own the contrast intentionally
- Test: Would different setting reinforce better?

**Fix Validation:**
- Retest with new setting
- Should now amplify aesthetic, not fight it

## Part 6: Metrics and Measurement

### Quantitative Metrics

**Pacing Metrics:**
- Average time spent per investigation/encounter/session
- Does pacing match archetype expectations?
- Horror: Slow exploration (players cautious)?
- Action: Fast progression (players aggressive)?

**Decision Metrics:**
- Time spent on decisions influenced by context
- Investigation: Players debate which lead to follow?
- Tactical: Players analyze contextual advantages?
- Horror: Players assess risks before acting?

**Engagement Metrics:**
- Content interaction rate: What percentage of optional content do players engage with?
- Discovery: High interaction (players investigate everything)?
- Challenge: Lower interaction (focused on core challenge)?

**Completion Metrics:**
- What percentage of players complete?
- Does archetype difficulty match target audience?

### Qualitative Metrics

**Emotional Response Recording:**
- Document specific moments of intended emotion
- Horror: When did players express fear?
- Discovery: When did players express curiosity?
- Challenge: When did players express determination?
- Submission: When did players express relaxation?

**Spontaneous Comments Analysis:**
- Track unprompted player statements
- Positive: "This is creepy", "I'm curious", "This is relaxing"
- Negative: "This isn't scary", "I'm bored", "This is stressful"
- Categorize by aesthetic alignment

**Theory Formation Tracking (for Discovery):**
- How many theories do players generate?
- Are theories based on evidence or random?
- Do players enjoy theorizing process?

**Strategic Discussion Analysis:**
- When do players reference context in strategic discussions?
- Investigation: "Let's investigate the study first"
- Tactical: "We should use cover"
- Horror: "It's too dangerous to go there"
- Never: Context isn't affecting decisions (space coffee)

## Part 7: Playtesting Protocols by Aesthetic

### Horror/Tension Playtesting Protocol

**Setup:**
- Dim lighting if possible (environmental contribution)
- Minimize distractions (fear needs focus)
- Ideally solo play (social interaction reduces fear)

**Observe:**
- Physical responses: Tension, jumping, hesitation
- Verbal responses: Nervous laughter, expressions of dread
- Behavioral: Cautious movement, resource conservation

**Questions:**
- "What were you feeling?"
- "What made you nervous?"
- "Did anything break the tension?"
- "Was there anything that felt too safe/too threatening?"

**Success Criteria:**
- Players express genuine nervousness
- Players move cautiously
- Players respect threat
- No unintended comedy

### Discovery Playtesting Protocol

**Setup:**
- Provide note-taking materials (players often document clues)
- Allow collaboration (theory formation is social)
- Don't interrupt theories (even if wrong)

**Observe:**
- Theory formation: Do players generate hypotheses?
- Evidence discussion: Do players reference clues?
- Revelation response: Satisfaction or disappointment at answers?
- Documentation: Do players take notes?

**Questions:**
- "What did you figure out?"
- "What are you still wondering about?"
- "Did you enjoy the investigation process?"
- "Were clues fair and findable?"

**Success Criteria:**
- Players form theories from evidence
- Players express curiosity
- Revelations feel earned
- Some mysteries remain interesting

### Submission Playtesting Protocol

**Setup:**
- Comfortable environment (couch, relaxed setting)
- Allow distractions (test if game works while multitasking)
- Longer sessions (test sustained relaxation)

**Observe:**
- Stress indicators: Any anxiety or frustration?
- Pacing: Do players proceed comfortably?
- Experimentation: Do players try things without fear?
- Body language: Relaxation vs. tension

**Questions:**
- "How did this make you feel?"
- "Could you relax?"
- "Was anything stressful?"
- "Could you play this to unwind?"

**Success Criteria:**
- Players appear relaxed
- No expressions of stress
- Players describe as "chill" or "relaxing"
- Can play while multitasking

### Challenge Playtesting Protocol

**Setup:**
- Fair environment (no external pressure)
- Allow retries (test learning curve)
- Vary skill levels (test accessibility)

**Observe:**
- Learning: Do players improve with practice?
- Failure response: Determination or frustration?
- Strategy discussion: Analytical approach?
- Victory response: Satisfaction or underwhelmed?

**Questions:**
- "Did you understand why you failed?"
- "Could you see how to improve?"
- "Did victory feel earned?"
- "Was difficulty fair?"

**Success Criteria:**
- Players learn from failures
- Difficulty feels fair
- Victory feels earned
- Players want to retry

## Part 8: Integration Playtesting

Test how context works with full game systems.

### Full Integration Test Protocol

**Phase 1: Isolated Context Test (Sessions 1-3)**
- Test context elements in isolation
- Focus: Does archetype work? Does setting reinforce? Does perspective maintain?
- Minimal other systems (just enough to test context)

**Phase 2: Context + Resources Test (Sessions 4-6)**
- Add resource systems
- Focus: Do resources match archetype? Does scarcity match aesthetic?
- Test: Resource-context alignment

**Phase 3: Context + Mechanics Test (Sessions 7-9)**
- Add full mechanics
- Focus: Do mechanics serve archetype? Does context frame mechanics meaningfully?
- Test: Mechanics-context integration

**Phase 4: Full System Test (Sessions 10-15)**
- All systems together
- Focus: Does context maintain coherence with everything integrated?
- Test: No system contradicts context

**Phase 5: Scale Test (Sessions 16-25)**
- Multiple players, various playstyles
- Focus: Does context hold across different approaches?
- Test: Robustness to edge cases

### Context-Specific Integration Concerns

**Investigation + Combat:**
- Does combat break investigation perspective?
- Is balance right (investigation primary, combat secondary)?
- Test: Players should still feel like investigators, not warriors

**Horror + Resource Management:**
- Do scarce resources create tension or frustration?
- Is survival balance right?
- Test: Dread from scarcity, not rage from impossibility

**Cozy + Progression:**
- Does progression pressure break relaxation?
- Is advancement optional or required?
- Test: Players should progress comfortably, not grind stressfully

**Tactical + Narrative:**
- Does story enhance tactics or distract?
- Is balance right (which is primary)?
- Test: Narrative should contextualize tactics, not interrupt them

## Part 9: Diagnostic Decision Trees

### When Players Express Wrong Emotion

**Is this a perspective problem?**
- YES: Elements violating perspective (comedy in horror, unfairness in challenge)
- Solution: Apply perspective filter, cut/reframe violations

**Is this an archetype mismatch?**
- YES: Wrong archetype for intended aesthetic
- Solution: Change archetype or accept aesthetic mismatch

**Is this a Build/Suggest/Omit problem?**
- YES: Wrong information density (over/under-explained)
- Solution: Adjust ratios to match archetype

**Is this a setting problem?**
- YES: Setting fights aesthetic (contradictory tone)
- Solution: Change setting or own contradiction intentionally

### When Players Say "This Could Be Any Setting"

**Is this intentional?**
- YES: Abstract game, mechanics-first design, Level 1-2 integration planned
- Solution: Confirm this matches design goals, no action needed

**Is this unintentional?**
- YES: Intended higher integration, getting space coffee
- Diagnose: Which systems are cosmetic that should be load-bearing?
- Solution: Add setting-derived mechanics or setting-constrained mechanics

**Test fix:**
- Reskin thought experiment should now show setting matters

### When Players Are Confused

**Is necessary information missing?**
- YES: Under-built, need to Build more explicitly
- Solution: Build critical mechanics/rules clearly

**Is information density overwhelming?**
- YES: Over-built, or wrong complexity for target audience
- Solution: Omit non-essential complexity, or simplify archetype

**Is information contradictory?**
- YES: Internal consistency problem
- Solution: Align all information sources

**Is perspective unclear?**
- YES: Players don't understand emotional framing
- Solution: Clarify perspective through all elements

### When Players Rush (Should Be Cautious)

**Is threat not perceived?**
- YES: Horror not scary, players don't respect danger
- Solution: Increase threat visibility, consequence weight

**Is perspective broken?**
- YES: Comedy or power fantasy instead of horror
- Solution: Cut comedy elements, reduce player power

**Is pacing wrong?**
- YES: Game encouraging speed when should encourage caution
- Solution: Adjust pacing incentives

### When Players Are Stuck (Should Be Progressing)

**Is information missing?**
- YES: Investigation too vague, over-omitted
- Solution: Build more core clues explicitly

**Is difficulty too high?**
- YES: Challenge overwhelming, not appropriate for audience
- Solution: Adjust difficulty or add assist options

**Is puzzle unfair?**
- YES: Mystery unsolvable with available information
- Solution: Add necessary clues, ensure logical path exists

## Part 11: Iteration Guidelines

### When to Tune vs. When to Redesign

**TUNE (Small Changes) When:**
- Core context is working but needs refinement
- Perspective maintained but occasional breaks
- Build/Suggest/Omit ratios slightly off
- Setting works but needs consistency cleanup
- Mechanics present but need balancing

**Small iteration examples:**
- Cut specific elements that break perspective
- Build more explicit information for confused players
- Adjust pacing through resource recovery rates
- Polish setting consistency issues

**REDESIGN (Major Changes) When:**
- Fundamental archetype-aesthetic mismatch
- Wrong archetype chosen for intended aesthetic
- Space coffee when integration intended
- Persistent perspective breaks across all elements
- Target audience completely wrong

**Major iteration examples:**
- Change archetype entirely (e.g., investigation to survival horror)
- Complete setting replacement (fantasy to sci-fi)
- Redefine perspective from scratch
- Target different audience

### Iteration Priority Order

**Priority 1: Archetype-Aesthetic Alignment (Most Critical)**
- If archetype doesn't create intended aesthetic, nothing else matters
- Test first, fix first if broken
- May require complete redesign

**Priority 2: Perspective Maintenance**
- If perspective breaks, intended emotion destroyed
- Usually fixable with targeted cuts/reframes
- Rarely requires full redesign

**Priority 3: Build/Suggest/Omit Balance**
- Information density problems
- Usually tuning, not redesign
- Adjust ratios based on feedback

**Priority 4: Setting Polish**
- Consistency and reinforcement
- Almost always tuning
- Rarely requires setting change

**Priority 5: Space Coffee Level**
- Only address if integration level wrong
- May require significant mechanics work
- Consider if worth effort (Level 2 fine for some games)

### Iteration Phases

**Phase 1: Concept Validation (1-2 sessions)**
- Goal: Does basic archetype-aesthetic-setting combination work?
- Players: Designers or close colleagues
- Success: Some intended aesthetic response observed
- Failure: Complete mismatch → Redesign archetype or aesthetic

**Phase 2: Perspective Validation (3-5 sessions)**
- Goal: Can perspective be maintained consistently?
- Players: Target audience representatives
- Success: No major perspective breaks, intended emotions present
- Failure: Persistent breaks → Identify and remove violating elements

**Phase 3: Detail Validation (5-10 sessions)**
- Goal: Are Build/Suggest/Omit ratios right?
- Players: Varied skill/experience within target audience
- Success: Information density appropriate, no confusion or boredom
- Failure: Adjust ratios, add/remove information

**Phase 4: Integration Validation (10-15 sessions)**
- Goal: Does context work with full systems?
- Players: Full target audience spectrum
- Success: All systems coherent, no contradictions
- Failure: Identify conflicting systems, redesign or remove

**Phase 5: Scale Validation (15-25 sessions)**
- Goal: Does context hold across many players?
- Players: Broad testing including edge cases
- Success: Robust to different playstyles
- Failure: Add safeguards or redesign weak points

## Conclusion: Context as Foundation

Context validation is the foundation of all other validation. If your context isn't working—if archetype doesn't serve aesthetic, if perspective breaks, if setting is just space coffee—then no amount of mechanical polish will save the game.

**The validation process:**

1. **Document** - Clear context hypothesis (archetype, setting, perspective, Build/Suggest/Omit)
2. **Test** - Playtest with specific observation goals
3. **Measure** - Behavioral and emotional responses
4. **Diagnose** - Identify what works and what's broken
5. **Iterate** - Targeted changes (tune or redesign as appropriate)
6. **Repeat** - Until validated

**Remember:**
- Context shapes everything downstream
- Archetype-aesthetic alignment is most critical
- Perspective must maintain consistently
- Setting should reinforce, not contradict
- Space coffee only acceptable if intentional
- Test early and often

**The goal isn't perfect context—it's context that reliably creates your intended experience.**

When playtests consistently show:
- Players understand archetype
- Players experience intended aesthetic
- Perspective maintained throughout
- Setting matters (if intended to matter)
- Context integrates with all systems
- Target audience enjoys experience

Then your context is validated and ready for full production.

**Context is not decoration. It's the foundation that transforms mechanics into meaning.**
